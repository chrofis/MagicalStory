## 1. Geography — The Village at the End of the Valley

- Kandersteg sits at **1,174 meters** above sea level in the upper **Kander valley** (Kandertal), in the **Frutigen-Niedersimmental** administrative district of the canton of Bern
- The village lies on a high, flat plain at the **southern end of a hanging valley** carved out by Ice Age glaciers, surrounded on three sides by towering peaks of the **Bernese Alps**: the **Blüemlisalp** (3,661 m), **Doldenhorn** (3,643 m), **Balmhorn** (3,698 m), and **Altels** (3,629 m)
- For centuries, Kandersteg was a **dead-end settlement** — the road simply stopped at the mountain wall. The only way onward was on foot, over the high passes into Valais
- The village grew up as a **trailhead** for travellers crossing into the Valais via the **Gemmi Pass** (2,269 m) and the **Lötschen Pass** (2,690 m), part of the ancient trade route from Italy over the **Simplon Pass**
- Population (2020): approximately **1,288** inhabitants — it remains one of the smallest and most unspoiled mountain villages in the Bernese Oberland

## 2. Village History — From Pass Station to Mountain Resort

- The poor farmers of the Kander valley built the **village church** around **1510**, partly to give travellers a place to pray before and after the dangerous Alpine crossings. The **church bell**, cast in **1541**, is still rung to mark the New Year
- In **1850**, Kandersteg separated politically from the larger municipality of **Frutigen**, forming an independent commune together with Kandergrund. In **1909**, Kandersteg separated again from Kandergrund to become its own municipality
- Tourism began some two centuries ago after a visiting doctor wrote about the beauty of the area and the healthiness of its inhabitants — this account, combined with improved access, drew the first visitors
- Between **1855 and 1890**, five hotels opened; by **1913** (when the Lötschberg Tunnel opened), there were **19 hotels**
- Today, the village is characterised by **centuries-old wooden chalets** and traditional Bernese Oberland architecture — a remarkably intact Alpine settlement

## 3. The Oeschinensee — The Jewel of the Alps

- **Oeschinensee** (Oeschinen Lake) lies at **1,578 meters** above Kandersteg, covering **1.1 km²** with a maximum depth of **56 meters**
- The lake was formed approximately **3,200 years ago** when massive rockslides broke off the **Doldenhorn** at an altitude of 2,100 meters, creating a natural dam with a volume of over **100 million cubic metres** of debris
- The dam material remains porous — the lake drains **underground**, with the Oeschibach only surfacing several hundred metres below the lake
- An earlier, even larger geological event occurred around **8,000 years ago**: a huge landslide from the **Birre** sealed off the entire Kandersteg valley basin, temporarily damming the Kander river into a lake, until a debris flow reopened the valley
- In the **17th and 18th centuries**, **sulfur was mined** on the Oeschinenalp above the lake
- A **chairlift** from Kandersteg to the lake opened in **1948** — for 60 years, visitors rode in an open two-seater chair with direct views of the Doldenhorn. In **2008**, it was replaced by a modern **eight-seater gondola** reaching the upper station at **1,682 meters**
- The lake has been part of the **Swiss Alps Jungfrau-Aletsch UNESCO World Heritage Site** since the area's extension in **2007**

## 4. The Blausee — The Lake of the Weeping Maiden

- The **Blausee** (Blue Lake) sits at **887 meters** in the Kandertal, a small, crystal-clear mountain lake surrounded by dense forest
- The lake was created approximately **15,000 years ago** by a rockslide that formed a natural dam
- Its extraordinary **turquoise-blue colour** comes from mineral-rich mountain spring water fed by an underground source — the water is so transparent that tree trunks, rocks, and a stone sculpture on the lake bed appear to lie just below the surface
- **The legend**: A maiden with deep blue eyes fell in love with a shepherd. When he died in the mountains, she wept every day beside the lake. Her tears transformed the water to an eternal turquoise blue
- In memory of this legend, sculptor **Raphael Fuchs** placed a **stone statue of the maiden** on the lake bed, visible through the crystal water
- In **1878**, Zurich merchant **Johann Caspar Leemann-Boller** purchased the lake and adjacent lands, built a hotel, and established an **Alpine trout farm** — one of the oldest in Switzerland
- In **1925**, the property was acquired by **Oscar Kambly**, founder of the famous Kambly biscuit company, who developed it into a nature park
- Today the **20-hectare traffic-free nature park** includes an organic trout farm, a lakeside restaurant, and glass-bottom rowing boats — but only the trout are allowed to swim in the lake

## 5. The Lötschberg Tunnel — Piercing the Mountain Wall

- The **Lötschberg Tunnel** is a **14.6 km** railway tunnel through the Bernese Alps, connecting **Kandersteg** (canton of Bern) with **Goppenstein** (canton of Valais)
- Construction began on **15 October 1906**; breakthrough came on **31 March 1911**; the line opened to the public on **15 July 1913**
- At its peak, approximately **1,700 workers** (mostly Italian) and **220 engineers and supervisors** (principally Swiss and French) worked on the tunnel
- **February 1908**: An avalanche destroyed a workers' hotel, killing **13 people**
- **24 July 1908**: The most devastating disaster — tunnellers broke into a deep, water-filled glacial fissure beneath the **Gasterntal** valley. The tunnel flooded with water and glacial sediment, killing **25 workers** (all but three of the shift). **1,554 meters** of tunnel had to be permanently abandoned and sealed, replaced by a curved bypass. The engineers of the era did not understand they had struck a glacial overdeepening some **170 meters** below ground
- The tunnel's highest point is **1,240 meters** above sea level — the highest point on the main Swiss railway network
- The tunnel transformed Kandersteg from a remote dead-end village into a **through-route** connecting the Bernese Oberland to the Rhone valley and onwards to Italy
- A **car shuttle** (Autoverlad) still carries vehicles through the tunnel between Kandersteg and Goppenstein in about **20 minutes**
- In **2007**, the **Lötschberg Base Tunnel** (34.6 km) opened far below the original, carrying high-speed and freight trains at lower altitude

## 6. The International Scout Centre — Baden-Powell's Dream

- In **1921**, **Walther von Bonstetten**, Chief Scout of Switzerland, visited Kandersteg on holiday and discovered an empty chalet built in **1908** to house construction workers during the Lötschberg Tunnel project, abandoned after the tunnel's completion in 1913
- Von Bonstetten saw it as the perfect location for the **permanent international meeting place** that **Robert Baden-Powell**, founder of the Scout Movement, had long envisioned — a "Permanent Mini Jamboree" where Scouts from all nations could gather
- On **12 February 1923**, the Scouts International Home Association was established; on **12 April 1923**, the chalet and adjacent land along the River Kander were purchased for **CHF 15,100**
- The Centre was **inaugurated on 21 May 1923**
- Baden-Powell himself visited Kandersteg in **1922** (before the official founding) and again in **1930** for a World Scout Committee meeting — a hill at the centre was named **"B.P.'s Hill"** in his honour
- The **1st World Rover Moot** was held in Kandersteg in **1931**, attracting **2,500 Rover Scouts from 23 countries**
- During **World War II**, the centre housed troops and interned French soldiers — one of its only closures
- The **5th World Rover Moot** (1953) brought **4,168 Rovers from 22 countries**; the **9th World Scout Moot** (1992) drew nearly **2,500 from over 50 countries**
- In **1977**, it was officially renamed the **Kandersteg International Scout Centre (KISC)**
- In **1989**, staff adopted distinctive **pink uniforms**, earning the affectionate nickname **"the Pinkies"**
- In **2023**, KISC celebrated its **centenary** with **88,000 overnight stays** and engagement from more than **110 nationalities** — it remains the World Organization of the Scout Movement's official **World Scout Centre**

## 7. The Gasterntal — The Wild Valley

- The **Gasterntal** is a secluded, roughly **10-kilometre-long** trough valley behind Kandersteg, accessible only through the dramatic **Klus Gorge** (Chluse) where the young Kander river has carved through limestone
- The valley is a textbook **glacial trough**: steep rock faces rising hundreds of metres on either side, fed by waterfalls cascading from the **Doldenhorn**, **Altels**, **Balmhorn**, and surrounding peaks
- At its upper end lies the **Kander Glacier** (Kanderfirn) — the source of the **44-kilometre-long Kander river**, which flows wild and untamed through the valley, shaping a rare **alpine floodplain landscape** that is now protected
- Towards the end of the **1700s**, **50 year-round inhabitants** lived in **12 households** in the valley; today it is only seasonally occupied by Alpine farmers
- Very rare and protected species thrive here, including the **lady's slipper orchid** (Frauenschuh)
- The Gasterntal is part of the **Swiss Alps Jungfrau-Aletsch UNESCO World Heritage Site**
- It was beneath this valley, at a depth of **170 meters**, that the catastrophic **1908 Lötschberg tunnel flood** occurred

## 8. The Gemmi Pass — Ancient Crossing to Valais

- The **Gemmi Pass** (2,269 m) connects Kandersteg on the north side with **Leukerbad** on the south side in the canton of Valais — one of the oldest and most frequented north-south routes across the Bernese Alps
- **Already the Romans** used this path from the main Valais valley northward; it was later traversed by soldiers, pilgrims, and traders for centuries
- Wealthy travellers were once carried across in the famous **"Gemmi-Wägeli"** — two-wheeled carriages hauled by mules along the vertiginous trail
- The pass is the **only well-known Alpine pass in Switzerland** that is walkable even in winter — in the past, muleteers carried goods over the snow-covered route year-round
- In **1863**, **Thomas Cook** offered a guided walk over the Gemmi as one of the **first organised hiking tours in Switzerland**
- The **Schwarenbach Inn**, located halfway across the pass, became one of the most famous mountain inns in literature:
  - **Mark Twain** crossed the Gemmi on **23 August 1878** and described the inn as sitting "on a lonely spot among the peaks" in **"A Tramp Abroad"** (1880)
  - **Guy de Maupassant** set his chilling short story **"L'Auberge"** (The Inn) at the Schwarenbach
  - **Zacharias Werner** wrote **"Der vierundzwanzigste Februar"** (February 24), a tale of murder at the inn
  - **Arthur Conan Doyle** featured the pass in a Sherlock Holmes context
  - **Jules Verne** signed the inn's guest book in **1873**; **Pablo Picasso** crossed in **1933**; **Lenin** also walked the route
- Today, the **Sunnbüel cable car** from Kandersteg (to 1,936 m) provides the first stage of the crossing, and the **Gemmibahn** from Leukerbad ascends the steep 900-metre south face

## 9. The Blüemlisalp Legend — When the Alp Turned to Ice

- The **Blüemlisalp** (3,661 m) — meaning "flowering alp" — is the great massif towering over Kandersteg and the Oeschinensee
- In **1707**, physician and natural scientist **Johann Jakob Scheuchzer** published the legend of the Blüemlisalp, one of the most famous tales in Swiss Alpine folklore
- **The legend**: The Blüemlisalp was once the most lush and fertile alp in the mountains — so rich that cows had to be milked **three times a day**. A vain and wasteful herdsman lived in abundance while the people in the valley below starved. He refused to share, and even mocked the suffering valley dwellers. He let his sweetheart **bathe in milk** and paved the paths with **wheels of cheese**
- As divine punishment, the thriving meadow was transformed into a **wasteland of rocks and ice** — the glacier that now covers the mountain
- The legend exists in many variations across the Alpine region; Scheuchzer recognised its moral function — a parable about **pride, greed, and the consequences of refusing to share**
- The story also contains an echo of real climate history: the **Little Ice Age** (roughly 14th–19th centuries) saw Alpine glaciers advance dramatically, swallowing pastures that had once been productive

## 10. The Kander River and the Kander Correction

- The **Kander** is a **44-kilometre** river rising at the Kander Glacier in the Gasterntal and flowing northward through Kandersteg, Frutigen, and onward to Lake Thun
- Originally, the Kander bypassed Lake Thun entirely and joined the **Aare** between Thun and Uttigen — but its frequent flooding devastated the low-lying **Thun Allmend** plain
- In **1698**, affected communities petitioned the Bernese government to divert the river into Lake Thun
- **Samuel Bodmer** (1652–1724), a trained baker who had gained engineering knowledge as an artillery officer, designed the diversion plan. The Bern council approved it on **11 February 1711**
- Construction began with **150 workers** digging a channel through the **Strättlig hill** that separated river and lake. When progress stalled, city architect **Samuel Jenner** proposed a **300-metre tunnel** through the hill, completed by late **1713**
- On **16 July 1714**, the tunnel collapsed under water pressure, sweeping away **five people**. The river carved its own path, creating the dramatic **Kanderschlucht** gorge visible today
- The diversion increased Lake Thun's water inflow by **60%**, causing severe flooding in the city of Thun for decades. Bodmer was blamed, received death threats, and fled his home by **1717**
- The **Kander Correction** is considered the **first river engineering project of such scale** in Swiss history

## 11. Alpine Farming and Winter Sports

### Alpine Farming (Alpwirtschaft)
- For centuries, Kandersteg's economy was based on **subsistence farming and herding** — cattle were driven to high Alpine pastures (Alpen) each summer in a practice known as **transhumance**
- The **Alpaufzug** (ascent, in spring/early summer) and the **Alpabzug** (descent, in September/October) remain celebrated traditions: cows are decorated with enormous bells, handmade leather collars, and flower wreaths, led by a **queen cow** (Leitkuh) wearing the most impressive headdress
- The Gasterntal, Oeschinenalp, and surrounding high valleys still support summer grazing today

### Ice Climbing
- Kandersteg is one of **Europe's premier ice-climbing destinations** — when winter freezes the valley's countless waterfalls, they become towering pillars and curtains of ice
- Notable routes include **Rübezahl** (also called Staubbach) — a **215-metre, 7-pitch** frozen waterfall first climbed by **Xavier Bongard and Peter Gobet in 1988**, graded WI6
- The annual **Kandersteg Ice Climbing Festival** is one of the top events of its kind in the Alps, drawing the international ice-climbing community for competitions, training, and gatherings

### Nordic Skiing
- Kandersteg is one of the **largest cross-country skiing areas in Switzerland**, with approximately **60 kilometres** of groomed trails, including a spectacular high-altitude circuit at **Sunnbüel** (1,936 m) at the foot of the Altels

## 12. Notable Historical Facts

- **Roman era**: The Gemmi Pass was already in use as a north-south route across the Alps
- **1510**: The village church was built, serving travellers crossing the passes
- **1541**: The church bell was cast — still rung today on New Year's Eve
- **1707**: Scheuchzer published the Blüemlisalp legend
- **1714**: The Kander Correction diverted the river into Lake Thun — Switzerland's first major river engineering project
- **1850**: Kandersteg became an independent municipality, separating from Frutigen
- **1878**: Mark Twain crossed the Gemmi Pass and stayed in Kandersteg
- **1906–1913**: Construction of the Lötschberg Tunnel, with catastrophic disasters in 1908
- **1923**: The International Scout Centre was founded, becoming a global symbol of peace and youth fellowship
- **1931**: First World Rover Moot held in Kandersteg with 2,500 Scouts from 23 countries
- **2007**: Oeschinensee and the Gasterntal inscribed as part of the UNESCO World Heritage Site

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The lake that fell from the mountain** — 3,200 years ago, a child watches the Doldenhorn crack and roar as a colossal rockslide dams the valley, creating Oeschinensee. The child befriends the new lake's spirit and learns that even destruction can create beauty
2. **The maiden of the Blausee** — A shepherd's child discovers the stone maiden on the lake bed and, diving into the crystal water, enters the old legend: they must help the blue-eyed maiden and her lost shepherd find each other before the lake's colour fades forever
3. **The tunnel and the flood** — July 1908: an Italian tunneller's child in Kandersteg feels the ground shake as the Lötschberg tunnel breaks into the glacial abyss. The Kander river spirit, disturbed from its underground sleep, must be calmed before it drowns the mountain
4. **The pink scarves of Kandersteg** — A child arrives at the International Scout Centre and finds a dusty trunk in the original 1908 chalet containing Baden-Powell's lost field journal. Following its clues leads to a treasure hunt across the Kander valley, uniting Scouts from five continents
5. **The herdsman who would not share** — Retelling of the Blüemlisalp legend: a child from the starving valley climbs to the impossibly lush alp and confronts the proud herdsman. When the ice comes, the child must race down the mountain carrying the last wheel of cheese to save the village
6. **The Gemmi crossing** — A child joins a mule caravan crossing the Gemmi Pass in winter and meets the ghosts of all who have walked the ancient path — Roman soldiers, medieval pilgrims, Mark Twain with his notebook, Picasso with his sketchbook — each leaving a mark on the Schwarenbach Inn's guest book
7. **The river that changed its mind** — 1714: young Samuel Bodmer watches in horror as the tunnel collapses and the Kander carves a wild new gorge overnight. The river asks the child: "Where should I go?" — and the child must decide between the old path and the new
8. **The wild valley's last family** — In the 1790s, a child lives with the last 50 inhabitants of the Gasterntal. As winter closes in and the hay runs out, the Kander glacier whispers secrets of the valley's ancient past, and the child must decide whether to stay or follow the others down to Kandersteg
