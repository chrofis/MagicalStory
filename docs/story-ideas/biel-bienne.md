## 1. Geography — At the Crossroads of Languages and Landscapes

- Biel/Bienne sits at the **northeastern end of Lake Biel** (Bielersee / Lac de Bienne), at the **foot of the Jura mountains**, in the canton of Bern
- The city straddles the **Röstigraben** — the cultural and linguistic boundary between German-speaking and French-speaking Switzerland, running north-to-south from the Jura through the Three Lakes region
- Surface area: **21.21 km²**; population: **58,158** (December 2024), making it Switzerland's **tenth-largest city**
- The urban agglomeration reaches about **100,000 inhabitants**
- The **Schüss** (La Suze) river flows through the city into Lake Biel, carving the famous **Taubenlochschlucht** gorge on its way
- Elevation ranges from **434 m** at the lake to the heights of **Magglingen/Macolin** (approx. 900 m) above the city
- The **Three Lakes region** (Drei-Seen-Land / Région des Trois-Lacs) — Lakes Biel, Neuchâtel, and Murten — stretches to the south and west

## 2. Switzerland's Largest Bilingual City

- Biel/Bienne is the **only officially bilingual city in Switzerland** where German and French hold **fully equal status**
- Since **1 January 2005**, the official double name **"Biel/Bienne"** has been enshrined
- Language split (2024): approximately **55.6% German-speaking**, **44.4% French-speaking**; the city also has significant Italian-speaking (10%) and multilingual populations
- A century ago, roughly **67% spoke German** — the French proportion has steadily grown, driven by immigration from the Jura and Romandie
- The **"Biel Model"** (since the 1980s): in conversation, the language is chosen by **whoever speaks first**, and the other person adapts — a form of **"consensual bilingualism"** (konsensuale Zweisprachigkeit)
- Bilingual education is available **from nursery school through high school** (Gymnasium / Gymnase)
- The city hosts the **Forum du bilinguisme / Forum für die Zweisprachigkeit** — a research institute dedicated to bilingualism
- All official signage, documents, and street names appear in **both languages**
- A comparative study praised Biel/Bienne's bilingual coexistence as **"a role model for the whole of Switzerland"**
- The bilingualism has its roots in the **mid-19th century watch industry boom**, when French-speaking Jura watchmakers (horlogers) migrated to the city in large numbers; local authorities offered **French-language schooling** to attract them

## 3. Medieval Origins — The Prince-Bishop and the Town

- In **999**, King **Rudolph III of Burgundy** granted the lands around Lake Biel to the **Bishopric of Basel** (Bistum Basel / Évêché de Bâle)
- The town was probably founded by Bishop **Heinrich II von Thun** between **1225 and 1230**
- The **Church of St. Benedict** (Stadtkirche / Église de la Ville) was first mentioned in **1228**; the current late-Gothic building dates from **1451–1470** and is regarded as the **second most important late-Gothic building** in the Canton of Bern after the Berner Münster
- In **1275**, King of Germany **Rudolph of Habsburg** granted Biel a **town charter** (Stadtrecht)
- **1279**: First alliance with **Bern** — later made an **eternal alliance** in **1352** as Bern joined the Old Swiss Confederacy
- Biel officially remained under the **jurisdiction of the Prince-Bishop of Basel** from the 11th to the 18th century, but the town council repeatedly clashed with the bishop's representative (the Meier / Maire)
- Contradictory loyalties to both the bishop and Bern led to **war in 1367**: Biel was burned and the **bishop's castle destroyed**
- The city adopted the **Protestant Reformation** in **1528**, following Bern's lead after the famous Bern Disputation
- The tension between episcopal authority and Bernese alliance shaped Biel's identity for over **400 years** — the town was never fully independent of either power

## 4. The Old Town — Altstadt / Vieille Ville

- The **Ring** (place du Ring) is the central medieval square, named for the days when community leaders sat in a **semicircle** (Ring) to pass judgment on accused citizens
- Leading from the Ring is the **Burggasse**, home to the **stepped-gabled Rathaus** (Town Hall / Hôtel de Ville), built in **1534**
- The **Obergasse** (rue Haute) and surrounding narrow lanes feature **guild halls** (Zunfthäuser), painted shuttered houses, and Renaissance-era fountains
- The **Stadtkirche St. Benedikt** dominates the old town, with notable **15th-century stained glass**
- The **Schwab Museum** (now part of the Neues Museum Biel / Nouveau Musée Bienne, NMB) houses artifacts from **La Tène** Iron Age lake-dwelling sites discovered nearby — the La Tène culture itself is named after a site on the shores of Lake Neuchâtel
- Charming **fountains** (Brunnen / fontaines) punctuate the lanes, following the Swiss-German tradition of public water supply as civic art
- Despite its medieval core, the Old Town today hosts restaurants, boutiques, and the lively **First Friday** monthly cultural event

## 5. Napoleonic Interlude and Joining Canton Bern

- On **6 February 1798**, French troops marched through the open city gate while the population **celebrated their arrival** — Biel was incorporated as the **"Canton de Bienne"** into the département du Mont-Terrible of the First French Republic
- In **1800**, the city was transferred to the **département du Haut-Rhin**
- After the collapse of Napoleon's empire, Biel sent **Georg Friedrich Heilmann** to the **Congress of Vienna** (1814–1815) to push for an **independent Canton of Biel** — but his bid was unsuccessful
- On **20 March 1815**, the Congress granted most of the former Prince-Bishopric of Basel to the Canton of Bern, including Biel — as **compensation** for territory Bern had lost during the Napoleonic reshuffling
- Biel joined the **Canton of Bern** as part of the **Oberamt of Nidau**, ending over 500 years of episcopal governance

## 6. Watch Capital of the World — Omega, Swatch, Rolex

- Biel/Bienne is known as the **"world capital of watchmaking"** (Uhrenmetropole / métropole horlogère), home to three of the world's most famous watch brands:

### Omega
- Founded by **Louis Brandt** in **La Chaux-de-Fonds** in **1848** as a pocket-watch workshop
- After Brandt's death in **1879**, his sons **Louis-Paul** and **César** moved production to **Bienne** in **1880–1882**
- In **1894**, they developed the legendary **Omega 19-ligne calibre**, designed for interchangeable parts — so successful that the company adopted the name **Omega** in **1903**
- Omega's entire corporate and production operation remains in Biel (movements made in Villeret)
- The **OMEGA Museum** at the headquarters showcases **175+ years** of watchmaking history

### Swatch & The Swatch Group
- **Swatch** was born in Biel in **1983**, created to fight the **"quartz crisis"** that had devastated the Swiss watch industry against Japanese competition
- Founded through the merger of the struggling **SSIH** and **ASUAG** groups by Lebanese-Swiss entrepreneur **Nicolas G. Hayek** (1928–2010)
- The **Swatch Group**, headquartered in Biel, is the **world's largest watch group** (~$7.5 billion revenue), comprising **17 brands** including Omega, Longines, Tissot, Rado, and Hamilton
- The striking new Swatch headquarters — a curving timber "snake" building — was designed by Pritzker Prize-winning architect **Shigeru Ban**
- The **Federation of the Swiss Watch Industry** (FH / Fédération de l'industrie horlogère suisse) also has its offices in Biel

### Rolex
- **Hans Wilsdorf** founded Rolex in London in **1905** and partnered with **Hermann Aegler** in Biel to produce miniaturized wristwatch movements
- **Jean Aegler** had opened a movement factory in Biel in **1878**; the family firm supplied Rolex for nearly a century
- The **first Rolex watches were made in Biel**, though corporate headquarters later moved to Geneva
- In **2004**, Rolex Geneva acquired the Biel movement factory from the Borer family (Aegler's descendants) for a reported **CHF 1 billion**
- Today, Rolex is **Biel's largest employer** with approximately **3,000–3,500 workers**; major expansion is underway
- The NMB (Neues Museum Biel) houses the **largest publicly accessible vintage Rolex collection**: 46 timepieces

### Swatch Internet Time
- On **23 October 1998**, Swatch introduced **Swatch Internet Time** (.beat) — a timezone-independent system dividing the day into **1,000 .beats** (each 86.4 seconds)
- The reference meridian: **Biel Mean Time (BMT)**, putting the small Swiss city at the centre of a proposed new world time standard

## 7. The Jura Water Correction — Juragewässerkorrektion / Correction des eaux du Jura

- The **largest river engineering project ever undertaken in Switzerland**, designed to end centuries of catastrophic flooding in the Seeland (Grosses Moos) region
- Before correction, the Aare river caused devastating **annual floods**, turning the flat land between the three lakes into marshland and destroying harvests

### First Correction (1868–1891)
- Designed by engineer **Richard La Nicca** (1794–1883)
- Key canals built: the **Hagneck Canal** (Hagneckkanal) diverting the Aare into Lake Biel; the **Nidau-Büren Canal** regulating outflow; the **Broye Canal** connecting Lake Murten to Lake Neuchâtel; and the **Zihl Canal** (Canal de la Thielle) linking Lake Biel to Lake Neuchâtel
- On **16 August 1878**, the waters of the Aare first flowed into Lake Biel through the Hagneck Canal
- The Hagneck Canal crossed the **Seerücken Hills** in a cutting **900 metres long** and **34 metres deep**, requiring the excavation of **one million cubic metres** of material
- Lake levels dropped by approximately **2.5 metres**, draining vast marshlands and creating fertile agricultural land
- **St. Peter's Island** (Île Saint-Pierre / St. Petersinsel) lost its island status, connected to the mainland by a **4.5 km land bridge** to Erlach

### Second Correction (1962–1973)
- Expanded, deepened, and widened the existing canals
- Built a new **hydropower plant at Flumenthal**
- Central regulation now managed from the **Regulierwehr Port** (Port dam) near Biel

- The three lakes now function as a **unified reservoir** operating on the principle of compensating bodies of water — a triumph of Swiss hydraulic engineering

## 8. Lake Biel and St. Peter's Island — Rousseau's Paradise

### Lake Biel (Bielersee / Lac de Bienne)
- The **tenth-largest lake in Switzerland**, stretching about **15 km** between Biel and Erlach
- The **northern shore** between **Twann** (Douanne) and **Ligerz** (Gléresse) is one of Switzerland's most celebrated **wine regions** — vineyards have existed here since at least **866 AD** (documented in a papal letter)
- The small villages of Schafis, Ligerz, Twann, and Vingelz retain centuries-old winemaking traditions; the **Rebbaumuseum** in Ligerz (housed in a 16th-century building) documents the viticulture heritage

### St. Peter's Island (St. Petersinsel / Île Saint-Pierre)
- Until **1868**, it was **Switzerland's largest island**; now a peninsula connected to Erlach after the Jura Water Correction lowered the lake
- In the **early 11th century**, the **Cluny Order** (Cluniac monks) established a **priory** on the island; the Romanesque church and monastery ruins survive
- In **1530**, after the Reformation, the monastery was handed to Bern and the church was destroyed
- **Archaeological treasures**: Bronze Age **pile-dwelling settlements** on the island's shores, later a **Roman temple** (2nd–3rd century) and a **Merovingian burial site**

### Rousseau's Stay (1765)
- **Jean-Jacques Rousseau** fled to St. Peter's Island in autumn **1765**, escaping persecution over his controversial writings
- He later wrote: **"Nowhere have I felt so truly happy as on St. Peter's Island in the middle of Lake Biel"**
- His **Fifth Walk** in the *Reveries of the Solitary Walker* (Les Rêveries du promeneur solitaire) is an immortal meditation on happiness and solitude, inspired by his weeks on the island
- The stay brought **European fame** to the island; notable visitors who followed included **Goethe**, **Captain Cook**, **Empress Josephine Bonaparte**, and the **Kings of Prussia, Sweden, and Bavaria**
- Today, **Rousseau's room** can be visited at the island's hotel-restaurant

## 9. Magglingen/Macolin — The Funicular and Swiss Sport

- **Magglingen** (German) / **Macolin** (French) sits on the Jura ridge above Biel at approximately **900 m** elevation
- The **Biel–Magglingen Funicular** (Standseilbahn / funiculaire) opened in **1887**, originally built by **Pümpin, Herzog & Cie** of Bern as a **water-powered line** with a Riggenbach rack rail for braking
- Rebuilt by **Von Roll** in **1923** (still water-powered), then converted to **electric operation** in **1954**
- Completely renovated in **2001** with new **panoramic cars** — the ride takes about **10 minutes**
- In the late 19th century, Magglingen became a fashionable **spa town** (Kurort), with a spa built in **1876–1877**
- On **3 March 1944**, the Swiss Federal Council authorized the creation of a **school for gymnastics and sport** in Magglingen
- Today, the village is home to the **Swiss Federal Office of Sport** (BASPO / OFSPO) and the **Swiss Federal Institute of Sport Magglingen** (EHSM), where elite Swiss athletes train
- Hundreds of students, athletes, and staff commute daily on the funicular

## 10. The Taubenlochschlucht — A Gorge at the City's Edge

- The **Taubenlochschlucht** (Gorges du Taubenloch / Gorges de la petite Colombe) lies between **Bözingen** (a suburb of Biel) and **Frinvillier**, stretching about **2 km**
- Described as **"the only gorge in Europe located in the immediate vicinity of a town"**
- The **Schüss** (La Suze) river carved the gorge through **150 million years** of limestone over geological time
- The elevation drops from **517 m** (Frinvillier) to **447 m** (Bözingen); the walk takes about **35 minutes**
- **The legend**: the name "Taubenloch" honours a beautiful young bride called **"the little turtledove"** (la petite Colombe). The cruel knight **Ingelram of Rondchâtel Castle** attacked her wedding party and killed the groom. Rather than face capture, the girl threw herself into the gorge. It has borne her name ever since
- From the gorge, hikers can ascend the **Bözingenberg** (930 m), Biel's local mountain, with panoramic views over the Three Lakes and Bernese Alps
- Free access, no opening hours — a beloved escape on hot summer days for the refreshing coolness of the canyon

## 11. Red Biel and Bauhaus Architecture

- In **1921**, Social Democrat **Guido Müller** became mayor (Stadtpräsident), ushering in the era of **"Red Biel"** (Rotes Biel / Bienne la Rouge) — a period of progressive social-democratic governance
- In **1923**, the railway station was relocated to its current position, freeing over **90,000 m²** of prime building land
- Under Müller's vision, this land was developed into an entire **modernist neighbourhood** in the **Neues Bauen** (New Building / Bauhaus) style
- The **Volkshaus** (Maison du Peuple / People's House), designed by architect **Eduard Lanz** and built **1930–1932**, is the icon of this era: a striking complex with a one-storey **rotunda restaurant**, an eight-storey **high-rise tower**, and a multipurpose hall
- Biel has the **highest concentration of Bauhaus-style architecture outside of Tel Aviv**: smooth facades, concrete balconies, glass-walled staircases, round elevator annexes, geometric window patterns
- In **July 1918**, a demonstration of starving workers had erupted into **street riots** requiring military intervention — the workers' movement was a powerful force in the city
- **Robert Grimm** (1881–1958), the socialist leader of the **1918 Swiss General Strike** (Landesstreik), was a prominent figure in the political movement connected to the city; the strike demanded the **48-hour work week**, old-age pensions, and **women's suffrage**

## 12. Notable People Connected to Biel/Bienne

- **Jean-Jacques Rousseau** (1712–1778): philosopher, spent his happiest weeks on St. Peter's Island in **1765**
- **Nicolas G. Hayek** (1928–2010): Lebanese-Swiss businessman, co-founder and CEO of the **Swatch Group**, headquartered in Biel; credited with **saving the Swiss watch industry** from the quartz crisis
- **Roger Federer** (born 1981): tennis legend, trained at the **Biel tennis academy** as a junior; in **2016**, the street where he trained was renamed **"Allée Roger Federer"**. His youthful temper — including legendary racket-throwing episodes — is part of academy lore
- **Nemo Mettler** (born 1999): Swiss musician born in Biel/Bienne, performing as **Nemo**; won the **Eurovision Song Contest 2024** with the song *"The Code"* and **591 points** — the first openly **non-binary artist** to win the contest
- **Hans Wilsdorf** (1881–1960): founded Rolex and sourced his first movements from Biel's Aegler workshop
- **Louis Brandt** (1825–1879): founder of the workshop that became **Omega**, moved to Biel by his sons in **1880**
- **Eduard Lanz**: architect of the iconic **Volkshaus** and several other Bauhaus-era buildings in Biel

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The watchmaker's apprentice and the two languages** — A child arrives in Biel and can't decide if they're in a German or French city. Guided by a bilingual clockmaker, they discover that every tick of a watch speaks both languages at once — and that the "Biel Model" means you never have to choose

2. **Rousseau's island secret** — A child finds an old leather journal on St. Peter's Island and is transported back to **1765**, meeting a lonely philosopher who talks to the trees and the lake. Together they discover the island's Bronze Age pile-dwellings hidden beneath the water

3. **The girl in the gorge** — Retelling of the Taubenlochschlucht legend: the brave "little turtledove" (petite Colombe) outwits the cruel knight Ingelram, escaping through the canyon with the help of the Schüss river's voice — transforming a tragedy into a tale of courage

4. **The flood that moved a river** — 1878: a child watches engineer Richard La Nicca's team dig the Hagneck Canal, wondering what will happen when the mighty Aare changes course. On **16 August 1878**, the waters rush in — and the island where Rousseau once dreamed becomes part of the mainland

5. **The funicular to the clouds** — A child rides the 1887 water-powered funicular up to Magglingen and discovers a secret sports school in the Jura mist, where young athletes from all of Switzerland gather to train. But one athlete has lost something on the journey up — and only the funicular's old water mechanism holds the clue

6. **The second hand that saved Switzerland** — A child in the Swatch factory watches **Nicolas Hayek** assemble the very first Swatch in **1983** — a plastic watch that must convince the world that Swiss time still ticks. A race against the Japanese quartz invasion, told through the eyes of the tiny second hand

7. **Two fountains, two names** — A treasure hunt across the Old Town: every fountain has a German name and a French name, and each pair of names is a riddle that leads to the next. At the end, in the Ring square, the child discovers why the medieval judges sat in a circle — and what justice looks like when two languages must agree

8. **The vine and the lake** — A child helps tend the ancient vineyards above Twann on the north shore of Lake Biel, where grapes have grown since **866 AD**. When an autumn storm threatens the harvest, the child must race along the vineyard trail to Ligerz to warn the winemakers — passing through 1,000 years of history along the way
