## 1. Geography — Between Jura, Lake, and Plain

- Yverdon-les-Bains sits at the **southern tip of Lake Neuchâtel** (Lac de Neuchâtel), Switzerland's largest lake entirely within the country
- The town lies in the **canton of Vaud**, in the district of **Jura-Nord vaudois** (created in 2006 when the former Yverdon District was dissolved)
- Geography shaped by three elements: the **Jura mountains** to the northwest, the **Plaine de l'Orbe** (Orbe plain) to the south, and the **Collines de la Broye** (hills of the Broye) to the east
- The river **Thièle** (formed by the confluence of the **Orbe** and **Talent** rivers) flows through the town and enters Lake Neuchâtel here — making Yverdon a natural junction point between waterways
- Elevation: approximately **435 meters** above sea level
- Population: around **30,000** — the second-largest town in the canton of Vaud after Lausanne
- The town's full name, "les-Bains" (the Baths), reflects the thermal springs that have defined it for millennia

## 2. Neolithic Origins — The Menhirs of Clendy (Les Menhirs de Clendy)

- On the southern lakeshore near Yverdon stands **the most important megalithic site in Switzerland** — and one of the most significant in all of Western Europe
- **45 standing stones** (menhirs), erected between **4500 and 4000 BCE** during the Middle Neolithic period
- The stones are **Alpine erratic blocks** — boulders carried by the ancient Rhône glacier and later cut into anthropomorphic shapes by Neolithic people
- Many display **carved human features**: a head and two shoulders are clearly visible, making them statue-menhirs rather than simple boulders
- The largest stones reach over **4 meters** in height and weigh close to **five tonnes**
- The menhirs were **hidden underwater** for millennia: they only reappeared after the **Jura Waters Correction** (Correction des eaux du Jura, 1868–1891) lowered Lake Neuchâtel by approximately **2.7 meters**
- Engineer **Charles de Sinner** first described the site in **1887** and recognized the stones had been deliberately aligned by humans — but his claim was not scientifically confirmed until **1975** by geologist Jacques-Henri Gabus
- **Astronomical significance**: The two rows of stones are aligned at angles of **222° and 246°**, forming sightlines that mark the moon's position at its southernmost extremes during **major and minor lunar standstills** — events repeating every **18.6 years**. This suggests the site functioned as a **prehistoric astronomical calendar**
- Small stones were replaced with concrete copies to prevent theft; the originals are displayed in the **Musée d'Yverdon et région**

## 3. Roman Eburodunum — Fortress and Thermal Town

- The Celtic **Helvetii** settled here from around the **2nd century BCE**, naming the place **Eburodunum** (Celtic: likely "yew-tree fortress" or "fortress of Eburos")
- After Julius Caesar's conquest of Helvetia in **58 BCE**, the Romans developed a **vicus** (civilian settlement) beginning around **25 BCE** — with soldiers, craftsmen, boatmen, shipbuilders, officials, and magistrates
- Eburodunum controlled critical trade routes: the **Geneva–Aventicum** road connecting the Rhône and Rhine basins, and lake navigation on Neuchâtel
- The Romans already exploited the **sulphurous thermal springs** — archaeological evidence confirms a thermal bath complex (thermes) from the **1st century CE**
- After Alemanni invasions destroyed the civilian settlement (mid-3rd century), Emperor **Constantine** ordered the construction of a massive **castrum** (military fortress) around **325 CE**
- The **Castrum Ebredunense** covered approximately **2 hectares** (5 acres / 20,000 m²), defended by **gigantic ramparts and 15 stone towers** — making it the **second-largest Roman fortress in Switzerland**
- Only two civilian buildings survived the military restructuring: the **thermal baths** and an **audience hall** converted for military use
- In **401 CE**, Emperor Honorius ordered the withdrawal of Roman troops; the castrum was abandoned by **405 CE** — less than 100 years after construction
- Today, scattered ruins survive, and two remarkable **Gallo-Roman boats** were discovered: a 10-meter vessel (found 1984) and a 20-meter barge (found 1971), both displayed in the castle museum

## 4. The Château d'Yverdon — Savoy's Square Fortress

- Around **1235**, **Amédée III de Montfaucon-Montbéliard**, lord of Orbe, built a large circular tower (donjon) on the site
- In **1260**, he sold Yverdon to **Pierre II de Savoie** (Peter II, Count of Savoy), who immediately began fortifying the town
- Peter II commissioned a quadrangular castle in **1259–1260**, designed by master masons **Jean and Jacques de Saint-Georges** (father and son) — the younger Jacques later became famous as **Master James of St. George**, the architect of Edward I's great castles in Wales (Conwy, Harlech, Caernarfon, Beaumaris)
- The castle was completed by approximately **1265–1270** and is recognized as **the first known "carré savoyard"** (Savoyard square) — a revolutionary lowland fortress design with a square courtyard and **round towers at the four corners**
- This design — perfected here — would later be exported to Wales by James of St. George, making Yverdon Castle an ancestor of some of the most famous medieval castles in Britain
- **Bernese period (1536–1798)**: After the Bernese conquered the Pays de Vaud, the castle served as the residence of Bernese **baillis** (bailiffs). Three rooms still preserve original **Bernese wall and ceiling paintings** from the 16th–18th centuries
- **1798**: The castle became national property after the fall of the Ancien Régime
- **Since 1914**: Houses the **Musée d'Yverdon et région**, displaying 8,000 years of regional history, including a rare **Celtic wooden statue** (c. AD 50) — one of only three from this period found in Switzerland — and 25 late-18th-century **Amerindian artifacts** (among the oldest in Switzerland, connected to Frederick Haldimand's Canadian governorship)

## 5. Medieval Yverdon under Savoy and Bern

- Under Savoy rule (1260–1536), the town prospered. The Savoy counts granted generous **franchises et libertés** (charters and liberties) to the burghers of Yverdon
- Peter II of Savoy also fortified the town with **walls and gates**, making it an important regional center
- The **Burgundian Wars** (1474–1477) disrupted Savoy's control of the region
- In **1536**, during the Protestant Reformation, **Bernese troops conquered the Pays de Vaud** (Waadtland), including Yverdon. The castle became the seat of a Bernese **bailliage** (bailiwick)
- Bernese rule lasted **262 years** (1536–1798), during which Protestant worship was established and the town was administered in German-speaking Bernese fashion — despite being a French-speaking community
- The **Vaud Revolution of 1798**, inspired by the French Revolution, ended Bernese rule. The canton of Vaud joined the Helvetic Republic and later the Swiss Confederation (1803)

## 6. The Thermal Baths — Les Thermes (Bains Thermaux)

- Yverdon's **sulphurous thermal springs** have been used since at least the Roman era, and likely since Celtic times — the springs may have served as a **sacred site** with wooden structures before the Romans built stone baths
- The thermal water rises from deep underground at a natural temperature of **29°C**, rich in **sulphur and magnesium** — beneficial for rheumatic, respiratory, and neurological conditions
- Medieval documents attest to a **spa centre** as early as **1429**, with several associated buildings
- After a period of decline, the **18th century** brought a renaissance: in **1728**, the town authorities commissioned a new spa building, and Yverdon's reputation as a thermal destination grew rapidly
- In **1982**, prospecting for additional thermal springs was successful, and a new outdoor pool was added the following year
- Today, the **Centre thermal d'Yverdon-les-Bains** (Grand Hôtel & Centre Thermal) is the **most important spa resort in western Switzerland** (la plus importante station thermale de Suisse romande)
- The town's designation as "les-Bains" was officially added in **1981** to distinguish it and honour its thermal heritage

## 7. Pestalozzi — The Castle School (L'Institut Pestalozzi)

- **Johann Heinrich Pestalozzi** (1746–1827), the Zurich-born educator considered the **father of modern pedagogy**, established his most famous school in the Château d'Yverdon
- He arrived in late **1804** with three fellow teachers and began setting up the institute. Many students and staff transferred from his previous school in Münchenbuchsee
- **Peak years (1807–1809)**: The institute housed **165 pupils, 31 teachers, and 32 trainee teachers** — approximately 250 people living and learning in the medieval castle
- Students came from across Europe; famous visitors included the philosopher **Johann Gottlieb Fichte**, the educator **Friedrich Froebel** (who later invented the kindergarten), **Johann Friedrich Herbart**, and the geographer **Carl Ritter**
- **Teaching methods** were revolutionary: approximately **60 hours of instruction per week** (triple the standard of the time), with no comparative testing, no marks, no reports — assessment based on individual merit
- Lessons were frequently held **outdoors**: students observed plants, animals, and geological formations in nature. Physical education included swimming, ice-skating, and Alpine hiking tours lasting weeks
- Practical skills were integral: **gardening, carpentry, printing, and bookbinding** — students learned to use saws, hammers, lathes, and worked in the institute's own printing press
- One-third of pupils attended **free of charge**; teachers received no wages, only room and board — the institute ran on idealism more than money
- **Decline**: Internal conflicts between staff members — particularly between Pestalozzi's right-hand man **Joseph Schmid** and philosopher **Johannes Niederer** — destroyed the institute's reputation. By 1816, sixteen teachers departed. A public rupture during a church service in 1817 sealed the decline
- The institute **closed in 1825**. Pestalozzi died two years later in Brugg
- Today, the **Centre Pestalozzi** within the castle honours his legacy, and Pestalozzi's pedagogical ideas — learning by doing, respect for the child, education of head, heart, and hand — remain foundational to modern education worldwide

## 8. The Maison d'Ailleurs — House of Elsewhere (Musée de la science-fiction)

- The **Maison d'Ailleurs** (House of Elsewhere) is the **only public museum in the world** dedicated exclusively to **science fiction, utopia, and extraordinary voyages** (voyages extraordinaires)
- Founded in **1976** when **Pierre Versins** (born Jacques Chamson, 1923–2001), a French science-fiction scholar and encyclopedist, donated his lifetime collection to the city of Yverdon-les-Bains
- Versins was the author of the monumental **Encyclopédie de l'Utopie, des Voyages extraordinaires et de la Science-Fiction** (1972) — a 1,000-page reference work that won a Special Award at the 1973 World Science Fiction Convention in Toronto
- The museum was initially housed in a three-story townhouse; in **1991**, it moved to its current location: a **former prison built in 1806** in the centre of town
- The collection contains approximately **70,000 documents**: books, artworks, toys, magazines, and memorabilia related to science fiction and utopia — including pieces dating back to the **16th century**
- In **2003**, collector **Jean-Michel Margot** donated one of the world's most important private collections dedicated to **Jules Verne**, further enriching the museum
- The museum has hosted exhibitions featuring the work of **H.R. Giger** (the Swiss artist who created the Alien creature design) and many other science-fiction artists
- Versins served as curator until his death and was honoured with the **Pilgrim Award** (1991) by the Science Fiction Research Association

## 9. The Grande Cariçaie and Pro Natura Centre Champ-Pittet

- The **Grande Cariçaie** is Switzerland's **largest lakeside wetland** — a 40-kilometre-long strip of marshes, fens, alluvial forests, and reed beds stretching along the southern shore of Lake Neuchâtel
- It covers nearly **3,000 hectares** and shelters approximately **800 plant species** and over **10,000 animal species** — making it one of Switzerland's most biodiverse areas
- The wetland appeared largely as a result of the **Jura Waters Correction** (1868–1891), which lowered the lake level and exposed new shoreline
- The **Pro Natura Centre Champ-Pittet**, inaugurated in **1985**, serves as the visitor gateway to the Grande Cariçaie. It is housed in an **18th-century manor** (château) on the lakeshore
- Pro Natura has owned the Champ-Pittet estate since **1979**
- The centre features a **2-kilometre marsh trail** built on pontoons, passing through reed beds, marshy meadows, and white water-lily ponds, with an **ornithological observation post** for birdwatching
- Four nature paths traverse the **40-hectare estate**, mostly accessible by stroller and wheelchair
- The Grande Cariçaie is home to reintroduced **beavers** (castors) and is a critical habitat for migratory birds

## 10. The Encyclopédie d'Yverdon — A Protestant Rival to Diderot

- In the 18th century, Yverdon became an unlikely centre of the European Enlightenment thanks to **Fortunato Bartolomeo De Felice** (1723–1789), an Italian nobleman who emigrated to Switzerland in 1757 and settled in Yverdon in 1762
- De Felice edited and published the **Encyclopédie d'Yverdon** (Encyclopédie, ou dictionnaire universel raisonné des connoissances humaines) — **58 volumes** published between **1770 and 1780**
- Unlike Diderot and d'Alembert's famous Paris Encyclopédie, the Yverdon version was **less hostile to religion** and more sympathetic to Protestantism — earning it the nickname **"l'encyclopédie protestante"** (the Protestant encyclopedia)
- De Felice wrote over **800 articles** himself and assembled more than **30 international collaborators**, including the mathematician **Leonhard Euler**, the naturalist **Albrecht von Haller**, and the astronomer **Joseph Jérôme de Lalande**
- The encyclopedia was widely distributed across **Northern Europe** and remains an important document of Enlightenment thought outside France

## 11. Expo.02 — The Arteplage of Yverdon-les-Bains

- In **2002**, Yverdon-les-Bains hosted one of the five **Arteplages** (exhibition sites) of **Expo.02**, Switzerland's 6th national exposition (held 15 May – 20 October 2002)
- The theme of the Yverdon Arteplage was **"Moi et l'Univers"** (Me and the Universe)
- The centrepiece was a spectacular **artificial cloud** floating above the lake, generated by **31,140 water nozzles** and **80 pumps** — an iconic and surreal installation
- On land, enormous **artificial dunes** six metres high were covered in mass plantings of flowers in vivid colours and psychedelic patterns, with deliberately disorienting fragrances
- Expo.02 attracted over **10 million visits** across all sites
- The Expo catalysed major urban renewal in Yverdon, including the renovation of the **Place de la Gare** and the creation of a new **lakeside park** reconnecting the old town to the waterfront
- This transformation contributed directly to the town winning the prestigious **Prix Wakker** (Wakker Heritage Prize) in **2009**, awarded by the Swiss Heritage Society (Patrimoine suisse) for exemplary urban development and the successful reconnection of the medieval town centre to Lake Neuchâtel

## 12. Notable People Connected to Yverdon-les-Bains

- **Sir Frederick Haldimand** (1718–1791): Born in Yverdon, served as **Governor of the Province of Quebec** (1778–1786) during the American Revolution. After service in Prussian and Dutch armies, he transferred to the British army in 1756. He oversaw the settlement of Loyalist refugees in Canada and was knighted in 1785. He died in his native Yverdon. The Amerindian artifacts he sent home are now displayed in the castle museum
- **Johann Heinrich Pestalozzi** (1746–1827): Though born in Zurich, his most famous school operated in the Château d'Yverdon (1804–1825). Considered the **father of modern pedagogy** — his ideas on child-centred education, learning by doing, and developing "head, heart, and hand" (Kopf, Herz und Hand) transformed schooling worldwide
- **Fortunato Bartolomeo De Felice** (1723–1789): Italian-born scholar who settled in Yverdon and produced the 58-volume Encyclopédie d'Yverdon, a major Enlightenment work
- **Robert Piguet** (1898–1953): Born in Yverdon, became a renowned **Parisian couturier** who trained both **Christian Dior** and **Hubert de Givenchy**. Dior later said: "Robert Piguet taught me the virtues of simplicity." Piguet's archive of 3,000 original designs is held by the **Musée suisse de la Mode** (Swiss Fashion Museum), housed in the Château d'Yverdon
- **Pierre Versins** (1923–2001): French-born science-fiction scholar who donated his collection to Yverdon, creating the Maison d'Ailleurs — the world's only public science-fiction museum

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The moon watchers of Clendy** — A Neolithic child helps the village elders position the last menhir at exactly the right angle to track the moon's great cycle; 6,000 years later, a modern child touches the same stone and sees the ancient sky
2. **The last legionary of Eburodunum** — In 405 CE, the last Roman soldier leaves the castrum; a local child must decide whether to follow the legion south or stay and guard the thermal springs the Romans left behind
3. **The castle that crossed the sea** — Young James of St. George learns to build his first castle at Yverdon alongside his father; years later, a child discovers the same square-tower design in a Welsh castle and must figure out how it got there
4. **Pestalozzi's wildest lesson** — A child arrives at the Château school expecting strict discipline but instead is taken outdoors to draw flowers, build a chair, and swim in the lake — discovering that learning means using head, heart, and hand
5. **The House of Elsewhere** — A child wanders into the Maison d'Ailleurs and finds that one of the 70,000 books is actually a portal; they must navigate a science-fiction world using clues from Jules Verne to find their way home
6. **The cloud over the lake** — During Expo.02, a child gets lost inside the artificial cloud floating above Lake Neuchâtel and meets fantastical characters representing the theme "Me and the Universe"
7. **The Protestant encyclopedia** — In 1770s Yverdon, a printer's apprentice must deliver the final volume of De Felice's Encyclopédie across the Jura before a rival publisher can steal its secrets
8. **The beavers of the Grande Cariçaie** — A child follows a family of beavers along the 40-kilometre marshland, discovering the 10,000 species of the wetland and learning how lowering a lake by three metres created a whole new world
