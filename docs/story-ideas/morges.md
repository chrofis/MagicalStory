## 1. Founding by Louis of Savoy (1286)

- In **1286**, **Louis I of Savoy**, Baron of Vaud, founded the town of Morges on a flat stretch of lakefront pasture where a gallows had previously stood — a deliberate act of urban planning to create a new commercial and administrative centre on **Lac Léman** (Lake Geneva)
- Louis granted the town a **charter of freedoms** (*franchises*) in **1293**, modelled on the rights already enjoyed by nearby Savoyard towns, attracting settlers with promises of market rights, self-governance, and tax advantages
- The town was laid out in a planned medieval grid: two longitudinal avenues — the **Grand-Rue** and the **Rue Louis-de-Savoie** — run parallel to the lakeshore, connected by several perpendicular cross-streets, a design that survives intact today
- Morges rapidly developed into an important **market town** (*ville de marché*) and a **transshipment hub** for goods moving between the lake and the hinterland — travellers and merchants arrived by boat and continued overland toward Bern, Fribourg, and the interior of the Pays de Vaud
- The entire old town (*vieille ville*) is part of the **Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites** of national significance

## 2. The Castle of Morges (Château de Morges)

- Louis of Savoy built the **Château de Morges** simultaneously with the town's founding in **1286**, placing it at the southern edge of the town square to guard the harbour and the lakefront
- The castle follows the **"carré savoyard"** (Savoyard square) plan: a square floor plan with **four round corner towers**, a design inspired by the earlier **Château d'Yverdon** and characteristic of Savoyard military architecture in the 13th century
- A unique feature was a **fortified kitchen** (*cuisine fortifiée*) built outside the castle walls on the lake side — the only example of its kind in Switzerland
- After the **Bernese conquest of the Pays de Vaud in 1536**, the castle became the residence of the **Bernese bailiff** (*bailli bernois*), a role it served for **262 years** until the **Vaudois Revolution of 1798**
- In **1803**, the castle became the **cantonal armoury** (*arsenal cantonal*); additional utility buildings were added in **1836-39**
- Since **1932**, the castle has housed museums; today it contains **four museums** under one roof: the **Musée militaire vaudois** (Vaudois Military Museum), the **Musée suisse de la figurine historique** (Swiss Museum of Historical Figurines, with **10,000 figurines** in nearly 50 dioramas from the Raoul Gérard collection), the **Musée de l'artillerie** (Artillery Museum, covering 500 years of Swiss artillery from Burgundian War culverins to Cold War shot simulators), and the **Musée de la gendarmerie vaudoise** (Vaud Gendarmerie Museum)
- The castle is a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**

## 3. Geography — Lake Geneva, La Côte, and the Alps

- Morges lies on the **northern shore of Lac Léman** (Lake Geneva), at an elevation of approximately **380 metres**, about **10 km west of Lausanne** and **50 km east of Geneva**
- The town occupies a gently sloping site between the lakeshore and the first terraces of the **La Côte** wine region, with the **Jura mountains** rising to the north and the panorama of the **French and Swiss Alps** — including **Mont Blanc** — visible across the lake
- Several prehistoric **pile-dwelling settlements** (*stations lacustres*) have been found along the Morges lakefront; the largest, **Grande-Cité**, dates from the **late Bronze Age**, with wooden objects dendrochronologically dated to **1031 BC**; the settlement **Les Roseaux** dates from the **Early Bronze Age** and is rich in bronze axe-edge strips and fine ceramics — both sites are part of the **UNESCO World Heritage** serial property "Prehistoric Pile Dwellings around the Alps"
- The **CGN** (Compagnie Générale de Navigation sur le lac Léman), founded in **1873**, serves the Morges landing stage with both modern vessels and historic **Belle Époque paddle steamers**; the CGN even named one of its ships **"Morges"**
- During the second half of the **19th century**, the town's economy boomed thanks to the **steamship port** and the connection to the railway network; Morges station opened in **1855** on the Lausanne–Geneva mainline

## 4. The Parc de l'Indépendance and the Tulip Festival

- The **Parc de l'Indépendance** stretches along the lakeshore between the château and the Morges river, covering about **16,000 m²** of land that was progressively **reclaimed from the lake** starting in **1845** — an encircling wall was built and the space filled in over approximately twenty years
- The park was renamed **Parc de l'Indépendance** on **24 January 1898**, during celebrations marking **100 years of Vaudois independence** from Bernese rule; a monument to three local patriots — **Jean-Jacques Cart**, **Jules Muret**, and **Henri Monod** — was inaugurated that day
- Chestnut trees planted as early as **1750** along the lakeside form the beginning of a promenade developed from **1837**; the park contains more than **50 species of trees**
- Since **1971**, the park has hosted a spring floral exhibition that became the **Fête de la Tulipe** (Tulip Festival) — conceived to mark the **50th anniversary** of the *Société vaudoise d'horticulture* (Vaud Horticultural Society)
- Today approximately **150,000 tulips, daffodils, and hyacinths** of some **350 varieties** bloom each spring in a free, open-access spectacle that draws nearly **300,000 visitors** annually, earning comparisons to the **Keukenhof** in the Netherlands
- In **2024**, the festival won **Best Public Festival** at the **World Tulip Summit** in The Hague

## 5. Major Davel — The Vaudois Patriot

- **Jean Daniel Abraham Davel** (1670–1723), known as **Major Davel**, is the great patriotic hero of the Canton de Vaud; his story is intimately connected to the Morges military museum, which holds **Davel memorabilia** (*Souvenirs du Major Davel*)
- Born the son of a Protestant minister, Davel studied in Lausanne, became a notary and land commissioner in the nearby town of **Cully**, and began a distinguished military career in **1692** serving Prince Eugene of Savoy
- In **1717**, the Bernese appointed him **Major** of the Vaud militia of the district of **Lavaux**, but Davel harboured a vision of Vaudois liberation: he claimed to have been called since **1691** by a mysterious figure he named **"La belle inconnue"** ("the beautiful unknown woman") to free his country from Bernese tyranny
- On **31 March 1723**, using the pretext of an Easter military parade, Davel marched **600 troops** into Lausanne while the Bernese bailiffs were absent; he presented a manifesto of grievances to the municipal council at the city hall
- Davel was arrested the next day, interrogated five times, tortured twice, and **beheaded on 24 April 1723** at Vidy near Lausanne — maintaining throughout that his mission was divinely inspired and that he acted alone
- The **Vaudois** regard Davel as a proto-independence martyr; his sacrifice is commemorated every **24 April** with a procession from the Château Saint-Maire to the execution site at Vidy

## 6. Paderewski and Morges — The Pianist-Statesman

- **Ignacy Jan Paderewski** (1860–1941), the virtuoso Polish pianist, composer, and statesman, made Morges his principal home for over **four decades**
- In **1897**, Paderewski rented the **Villa Riond-Bosson**, a 26-room estate on the outskirts of Morges overlooking Lac Léman and Mont Blanc — once the property of **Fouché**, Napoleon's Minister of Police; he purchased it the following year
- In May **1899**, he married **Helena Gorska** (Baroness von Rosen) at Riond-Bosson, and the villa became the centre of his private life between gruelling international concert tours
- In **1919**, Paderewski became **Prime Minister and Minister of Foreign Affairs** of newly independent Poland, leading the Polish delegation to the **Paris Peace Conference at Versailles** — one of the most extraordinary dual careers in modern history
- He returned to Morges after his political career; during **World War II**, at age 80, he left Riond-Bosson for a final concert tour in support of Polish war relief, and died in New York on **29 June 1941**
- Today the **Musée Paderewski**, located within the Château de Morges, preserves photos, letters, original documents, scores, furniture, and personal objects from his life; the museum and the **Société Paderewski** publish the periodical *Annales Paderewski*

## 7. Audrey Hepburn and Tolochenaz

- The legendary actress **Audrey Hepburn** (1929–1993) lived for thirty years at **La Paisible** ("The Place of Peace"), an 18th-century Swiss farmhouse in the village of **Tolochenaz**, just **2 km from Morges**
- Hepburn and her first husband, actor **Mel Ferrer**, purchased the property in **1963** and named it La Paisible; the estate became her refuge from Hollywood, a place where she tended her vegetable garden, visited the **Morges market** on the Grand-Rue, and raised her family
- She passed away at La Paisible on **20 January 1993** and was laid to rest at the **cemetery of Tolochenaz**; her funeral at the village church drew worldwide attention
- The Morges tourist office offers a walking itinerary called **"In the Footsteps of Audrey Hepburn"** (*Sur les traces d'Audrey Hepburn*), linking sites in Morges and Tolochenaz associated with her daily life
- La Paisible remains private property (listed for sale in **2025** for approximately CHF 20 million), but the village church and cemetery are publicly accessible

## 8. La Côte — The Wine Region

- Morges sits at the heart of the **La Côte AOC** wine region, the **largest wine-producing appellation** in the Canton de Vaud, covering approximately **2,000 hectares** of terraced vineyards stretching from Nyon to Lausanne along the northern shore of Lac Léman
- La Côte contributes **40%** of all Vaud wine production; about **70%** of vineyards are planted with **Chasselas**, the signature white grape of the region (wines made from Chasselas are called **Dorin** or, for top quality, **Terravin**); reds include **Pinot Noir** (15%) and **Gamay** (12%)
- Twelve communes hold **Grand Cru** status, including **Morges** itself — the largest Grand Cru area in La Côte — alongside Féchy, Mont-sur-Rolle, Vinzel, Luins, and others
- The **Maison des Vins de La Côte** in Morges serves as a showcase for regional wines and offers tastings
- Wine walks (*balades viticoles*) through the vineyards above Morges offer panoramic views over the lake to the Alps — the tourist office organises a **winetouristic stroll** (*balade oenotouristique*) through the surrounding communes

## 9. The Old Town and Key Landmarks

- The **Grand-Rue**, the main artery of the old town, is a cobblestoned pedestrian street approximately **1 km long**, pedestrianised in **1993**; it hosts the **Morges market** on Wednesday and Saturday mornings, flanked by colourful cafés and shops
- The **Hôtel de Ville** (Town Hall), built around **1515–20** from two private buildings acquired by the municipality in **1510**, is the **oldest example of communal architecture** in the Canton de Vaud; it features a striking **polygonal stair tower** added in **1682** and represents the transition from **Gothic to Renaissance** architecture
- The **Temple de Morges** (Reformed church), built in the **1770s**, stands overlooking the Grand-Rue in a **late Baroque** style with elements of early **French classicism**
- The **Auberge de la Croix-Blanche** (White Cross Inn) is listed as a Swiss heritage site of national significance, along with several houses on the Grand-Rue (nos. 54 and 94)
- The **Place de la Navigation** and the lakeside promenade (*Promenade du Littoral*) connect the old town to the harbour and park, offering an unbroken waterfront walk

## 10. Transport — The BAM Railway and the Lake

- The **Bière–Apples–Morges** (BAM) railway, a **metre-gauge** (1,000 mm) narrow-gauge line, connects Morges to the villages of **Apples** and **Bière** in the Vaudois hinterland, with a branch from Apples to **L'Isle**
- The line was first discussed on **26 September 1875** among local communities seeking to connect the lakeside to the expanding **Swiss army barracks at Bière**; after years of debate over five possible routes, construction began in **1894** and the line **opened on 1 July 1895**; the branch to L'Isle followed on **12 September 1896**
- Originally steam-powered, the system was **electrified in 1943**; the operating company was later renamed **Transports de la région Morges-Bière-Cossonay (MBC)** to reflect its expanded bus services
- Since **2017**, the line has been served by modern **Stadler ABe 8/12** trainsets nicknamed **"Le Joran"** (after the cold north wind off the Jura)
- The railway also carries **military traffic** on a short 2 km branch serving Swiss army installations near Bière, including tanks transported on standard-gauge flat wagons mounted on metre-gauge carrying trucks
- Morges mainline station, on the **Lausanne–Geneva SBB line**, opened in **1855** and provides fast connections to both cities

## 11. Events and Traditions

- The **Swiss Classic British Car Meeting**, held annually since the early 1990s, brings approximately **1,500 classic British cars** (all at least 35 years old) to the quays and parks of the Morges lakefront each October — free to both exhibitors and visitors, emphasising fun and relaxation over competition
- The **Fête de la Tulipe** (April–May) transforms the Parc de l'Indépendance into a sea of colour with 150,000 blooms — Switzerland's largest flower festival and an event of international stature
- The weekly **Morges market** on the Grand-Rue (Wednesday and Saturday mornings) has been a fixture of town life since the medieval charter of **1293**, offering local produce, flowers, cheeses, and wines from the La Côte vineyards
- **Vaudois independence** is commemorated in the park's very name: the Parc de l'Indépendance was renamed in **1898** to celebrate **100 years** since the **Vaudois Revolution of 24 January 1798**, when the region threw off Bernese rule and briefly formed the **République lémanique** before joining the Helvetic Republic

## 12. Notable People Connected to Morges

- **Louis I of Savoy** (c. 1250–1302), Baron of Vaud, founder of Morges in **1286** and builder of the castle — his urban planning vision gave Morges its enduring medieval grid
- **Major Abraham Davel** (1670–1723), the Vaudois patriot whose memorabilia are held in the Morges castle museum; his failed 1723 uprising against Bernese rule inspired the later Vaudois independence movement
- **Ignacy Jan Paderewski** (1860–1941), Polish pianist, composer, and Prime Minister, who made his home at **Riond-Bosson** near Morges for over 40 years; his museum occupies rooms within the château
- **Audrey Hepburn** (1929–1993), film icon and UNICEF Goodwill Ambassador, who lived at **La Paisible** in Tolochenaz (2 km from Morges) for 30 years and is buried in the village cemetery
- **General Henri Guisan** (1874–1960), Commander-in-Chief of the Swiss Army during World War II, is closely associated with the Vaud region; the Morges military museum chronicles his role alongside that of Major Davel

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The tulip that remembered** — During the Fête de la Tulipe, a child touches a golden tulip in the Parc de l'Indépendance and is transported back to 1971, when the very first bulbs were planted; they must help the gardeners arrange 100,000 flowers before dawn, or the festival will never exist

2. **The castle's four museums** — Each of the four museums inside the Château de Morges holds a guardian: a tiny tin soldier from the figurine collection, a Burgundian War cannon that whispers, a gendarme's kepi that floats through the halls, and a knight's sword that glows at midnight; a child must unite all four to save the castle from a storm rolling across Lac Léman

3. **Paderewski's lost melody** — A child visiting the Musée Paderewski discovers a half-finished musical score hidden behind a portrait; when they play the first notes on a piano, the ghost of Paderewski appears at Riond-Bosson and asks them to help complete the melody before the moon sets over Mont Blanc

4. **Major Davel's 600 soldiers** — A child finds a tin figurine of Major Davel in the castle museum; it comes alive and tells the story of his march on Lausanne in 1723 with 600 troops; the child must decide: help Davel deliver his manifesto, or warn him of the betrayal that awaits

5. **The narrow-gauge adventure** — A child boards the little BAM train at Morges station, but instead of arriving in Bière, the 1895 steam locomotive appears and carries them through time: each stop — Apples, L'Isle, the army barracks — reveals a different era of the Vaudois countryside

6. **Audrey's garden** — A child picking vegetables in a Tolochenaz garden finds a hidden gate that leads to La Paisible as it was in the 1970s; Audrey Hepburn is there, tending her roses and preparing for a trip to the Morges market; together they walk the Grand-Rue, and the child learns that the greatest adventures are sometimes the simplest ones

7. **The lake dwellers of Grande-Cité** — Swimming off the Morges shore, a child dives underwater and surfaces in **1031 BC**, among the Bronze Age pile dwellings of Grande-Cité; they must help the lake dwellers cast a bronze axe and trade fine ceramics before the water rises and the village must be moved to higher ground

8. **The ship named Morges** — A child boards the CGN paddle steamer "Morges" for a routine lake crossing, but the Belle Époque vessel sails into a fog and emerges in different centuries at each port: medieval Morges under Louis of Savoy, revolutionary Morges in 1798, tulip-festival Morges in full spring bloom — and the child must find their way back to the present before the last whistle blows
