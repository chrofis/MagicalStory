## 1. Geography — A Valley Ringed by Giants

- Grindelwald lies at **1,034 m** above sea level in the **Lütschine Valley** (Lütschental), southeast of Interlaken in the Bernese Oberland, Canton of Bern
- The village sits in a dramatic basin enclosed by some of the most famous peaks in the Alps: the **Eiger** (3,967 m), **Mönch** (4,107 m), **Jungfrau** (4,158 m), and **Wetterhorn** (3,690 m) to the south; the **Faulhorn** range to the north
- The **Schwarze Lütschine** (Black Lütschine) river runs through the Grindelwald valley, joining the **Weisse Lütschine** (White Lütschine) from the Lauterbrunnen valley at **Zweilütschinen** to form the Lütschine proper
- Two major mountain passes connect the valley: the **Grosse Scheidegg** to the east (towards Meiringen) and the **Kleine Scheidegg** to the southwest (towards Lauterbrunnen/Wengen)
- Between the peaks flow two famous ice streams — the **Upper** and **Lower Grindelwald glaciers** — which once reached into the valley floor itself
- The **Mettenberg** ridge separates the two glaciers

## 2. Founding and Medieval History

- First documentary mention: **1146** as **"Grindelwalt"**, when King **Conrad III** granted estates in Grindelwald to the **Augustinian monastery of Interlaken**
- The name derives from Old High German **"grindel"** (bar, bolt, barrier) and **"walt"** (forest) — meaning "barred forest" or "enclosed woodland," a fitting description for a valley hemmed in by ridges and moraines
- According to legend, two monks from the Interlaken Monastery explored the Lütschine valleys and reported back about **"Grinden"** (stones) and **"Wald"** (forests)
- From the **13th century**, the monastery purchased rights and land in the valley and gradually pushed out local nobles
- The valley was historically governed through **seven mountain communities (Bergschaften)**: Bussalp, Holzmatten, Bach, Grindel, Scheidegg, Wärgistal, and Itramen — cooperatives operating under a **valley charter** dating from **1404/1538** (updated as recently as 2002)
- In **1528**, Bern suppressed the Interlaken Monastery during the Reformation, and the inhabitants gained their **freedom** from monastic rule

## 3. Pioneer of Alpine Tourism — The English Discover the Alps

- Grindelwald was one of the **first mountain resorts** in the Bernese Oberland, attracting visitors from the **late 18th century** thanks to its dramatic glaciers and mountain panorama
- Early travelers — primarily **English** — were initially housed in the **parsonage** or private homes
- The first hotel, the **Schwarzer Adler** (Black Eagle), opened in **1820**
- The first **road** connecting Grindelwald to the outside world was completed in **1860**
- In **1888**, Grindelwald opened to **winter tourism** — a pioneering move that transformed it into a year-round resort with sleigh rides, ice skating, curling, and early skiing
- The **Bernese Oberland Railway** commenced operations to Grindelwald in **1890**
- Hotel growth exploded: from **10 establishments in 1889** to **33 by 1914**, with proprietors installing central heating and electric lighting
- The outbreak of **World War I in 1914** abruptly ended the golden age of foreign tourism and buried ambitious plans for casinos and further mountain railways

## 4. The Great Fire of 1892

- On **August 18, 1892**, a devastating fire broke out at the **Hotel Bär**
- Driven by strong **Föhn winds** and a prolonged dry period, the flames spread rapidly through the wooden village
- **44 residential houses** and **72 other buildings** were destroyed, including the newly built railway station
- **412 inhabitants** were left homeless within two hours
- Sparks carried by the wind ignited fires as far as the hamlet of **Schwendi** down the valley
- The disaster led to a complete **reorganization of the fire brigade** and the construction of additional **fire ponds** as water reserves throughout the valley

## 5. The Eiger North Face — Mordwand

- The **Eigernordwand** (Eiger North Face) is one of the **three great north faces of the Alps** — a sheer wall of rock and ice rising **1,600 to 1,800 meters** (over a mile) above the valley
- The face earned the grim nickname **"Mordwand"** (Murder Wall), a pun on Nordwand (North Wall) — since **1935**, at least **64 climbers** have died on it
- The north face is visible directly from Grindelwald and from the terrace at **Kleine Scheidegg**, where spectators famously watched early climbing attempts through telescopes

### The 1936 Tragedy
- In July **1936**, four climbers — Bavarians **Andreas Hinterstoisser** and **Toni Kurz**, and Austrians **Willy Angerer** and **Edi Rainer** — attempted the first ascent
- On the ascent, Hinterstoisser pioneered a daring tension traverse (now called the **Hinterstoisser Traverse**), but the technique could not be used in reverse during retreat
- Caught by severe weather and forced to descend, an **avalanche** swept Hinterstoisser to his death; Angerer was killed hitting the rock face; Rainer was strangled by the rope
- **Toni Kurz**, age 23, survived the avalanche but hung suspended on the rope. Swiss guides attempted rescue from the **Eigerwand railway station** window but could not reach him. After hours of desperate effort, Kurz died **within sight of his rescuers** — one of mountaineering's most haunting tragedies

### The 1938 First Ascent
- On **July 21-24, 1938**, four climbers succeeded: Germans **Anderl Heckmair** and **Ludwig Vörg**, and Austrians **Heinrich Harrer** and **Fritz Kasparek**
- The two teams climbed separately at first; Heckmair and Vörg caught up with Harrer and Kasparek and the four joined forces
- Heckmair led the critical upper pitches through three days and nights of storms, avalanches, and extreme cold
- Their success made international headlines and drew a personal congratulation from Adolf Hitler — forever entangling the achievement with the politics of the era

## 6. The Jungfrau Railway — Engineering Marvel to the "Top of Europe"

- On **August 27, 1893**, textile and railway entrepreneur **Adolf Guyer-Zeller** conceived the idea during a hike above Mürren — he sketched the railway layout that night in **room 42** of the Mürren spa hotel
- He received his construction license on **December 21, 1894**
- **Groundbreaking**: July 27, **1896**
- The railway was built as an **electrically powered rack railway**, running mostly inside a **tunnel** bored through the Eiger and Mönch mountains — protecting the line from snow, avalanches, and extreme weather
- **Key milestones**: first section to Eigergletscher opened September **1898**; Eigerwand station summer **1903**; Eismeer station July **1905**; final breakthrough to Jungfraujoch **February 21, 1912**
- The first passenger train reached the summit in **July 1912** — **16 years** after construction began
- **30 construction workers died**, mostly in blasting accidents; workers struck **six times**; construction management changed **eight times**
- The complete route spans **9.3 km** from Kleine Scheidegg (2,064 m) to the **Jungfraujoch** (3,454 m) — the **highest railway station in Europe**, earning the name **"Top of Europe"**
- Power stations were built in **Lauterbrunnen** and **Burglauenen** near Grindelwald to supply the electric system
- The railway uses a rare **three-phase alternating current** system, one of only four such lines in the world

## 7. The Wetterhorn Elevator — Switzerland's First Aerial Cableway

- Designed by **Wilhelm Feldmann**, a German engineer who had moved to Bern in the early 1900s (he previously worked on the Wuppertal suspension railway)
- Feldmann tragically died on **June 2, 1905**, at age 52, before witnessing the opening
- Construction began in **1905** on the first stage up to **Enge station** (1,670 m)
- **Inaugurated July 27, 1908** — Switzerland's **first public aerial cableway** for passenger transport and one of the first modern aerial cableways in the world
- The journey over the **Upper Grindelwald Glacier** took approximately **9 minutes**, climbing **420 meters** in altitude
- Called an "elevator" rather than a cableway due to its extreme **116% gradient**
- Safety features included **two independent braking systems**, automatic power interruption for overspeed, and **45-millimeter-thick steel cables**
- In its first operating season (through September 1908): **1,880 trips**
- Cost: CHF 3.50 one-way, CHF 5 round-trip
- **Ceased operations during World War I** due to the collapse of tourism; the 20-year concession was never renewed, and a rockfall eventually destroyed the lower station

## 8. The Grindelwald Glaciers — Ice, Art, and Commerce

- The **Upper Grindelwald Glacier** (Oberer Grindelwaldgletscher) and **Lower Grindelwald Glacier** (Unterer Grindelwaldgletscher) once extended far into the valley, reaching close to the village itself
- Around **1820**, the Lower Grindelwald Glacier reached its **first maximum extent** of the Little Ice Age — Swiss landscape artist **Samuel Birmann** captured it in detailed drawings
- The **Bisson Brothers** photographed the glacier at its **second maximum** around **1855**
- **Grindelwald marble** was discovered in the frontal region of the Lower Glacier around **1730** and quarried between **1740 and 1760** — the stone was used for furniture panels and fireplace surrounds; some pieces later decorated the **Swiss Parliament building**
- **Glacier ice export** (1863-1914): The Bernese company **Schegg & Bohlen** received the concession for commercial ice harvesting in **1863**. Ice blocks were transported by road to Interlaken and then by train as far as **Paris** for use as refrigerant. In **1864 alone**, **17,473 hundredweight** of ice were extracted. The local brewer **Christian Indermuhle** (Rugenbrau) was a major client. The trade ended with the invention of electric refrigeration and the outbreak of WWI
- The **Glacier Gorge** (Gletscherschlucht), carved by the Lower Grindelwald Glacier, is today a dramatic tourist attraction with rock galleries and tunnels
- **Dramatic modern retreat**: The Lower Grindelwald Glacier was **8.3 km** long in 1973 but had shrunk to just **6.2 km** by 2015, with **1.9 km** of retreat occurring since 2007 alone

## 9. Mountain Railways and Viewpoints

### Wengernalp Railway (1893)
- Opened **June 20, 1893**, connecting Lauterbrunnen to Grindelwald via Wengen and **Kleine Scheidegg**
- At **19.11 km**, it is the **world's longest continuous rack-and-pinion railway**
- Electrified in **1909-1910**; steam operation ceased in 1912

### Mannlichen Gondola (1978)
- The **Grindelwald-Mannlichen gondola cableway** opened in **1978** with a length of **6,239 m** — at the time, the **longest passenger-carrying gondola cableway in the world** (record held until 1995)
- Replaced in **2019** with a new, faster system as part of the V-Bahn project
- The **Mannlichen** summit offers a **360-degree panorama** of the Eiger, Monch, and Jungfrau, with the famous **Panorama Trail** descending to Kleine Scheidegg (4.68 km, easy walking)

### Pfingstegg Cable Car (1967)
- Opened **July 15, 1967**, ascending **360 meters** from the valley (1,027 m) to Pfingstegg (1,387 m) in **3.5 minutes**
- Starting point for hikes to the **Glacier Gorge** and the Upper Grindelwald Glacier area
- Cost approximately **CHF 2.4 million** to build; original retro cabins were lovingly refurbished in **2013**

## 10. The V-Bahn and Eiger Express — 21st-Century Engineering

- The **V-Bahn** project was Grindelwald's most ambitious infrastructure investment since the Jungfrau Railway
- A local vote in **2014** initially rejected the plans, but a revised proposal was approved in **early 2015**
- Construction began **April 2018** after resolving 17 formal objections
- The project included: a new **Grindelwald Terminal** station, the replacement of the old Mannlichen gondola, and the construction of the **Eiger Express**
- The **Eiger Express** opened **December 5, 2020** — a state-of-the-art **3S (tricable) gondola** with **44 cabins** seating **26 passengers** each
- Route: **6,483 meters** from Grindelwald Terminal to **Eigergletscher** station in just **15 minutes**, traveling at **8 m/s**
- The gondolas pass within **1,800 meters** of the Eiger North Face — a dramatic visual experience
- Reduced travel time to the Jungfraujoch by **47 minutes** compared to the old route
- Only **7 support towers** along the entire route thanks to the tricable system

## 11. First Cliff Walk by Tissot

- Opened in **September 2015** on the west side of the **First** summit (approximately 2,168 m)
- A suspended metal walkway and single-rope hanging bridge bolted to the sheer rock face, with a viewing platform jutting **45 meters out into the void**
- **Jungfrau Railways** invested over **CHF 1.5 million** in the construction
- Sponsored by Swiss watchmaker **Tissot** (naming rights), making it **free of charge** for all visitors who reach the summit by gondola
- Offers dramatic views of the Grindelwald valley, the Eiger, Monch, Jungfrau, and the Schreckhorn
- One of the first summit attractions at Grindelwald-First, later joined by the First Flyer zip line, Mountain Cart, and Trottibike

## 12. The Velogemel — Grindelwald's Unique Snow Bicycle

- Invented in **1911** by **Christian Buhlmann**, a graduate of the local woodcarving school and sawmill manager
- Buhlmann was physically disabled and found it difficult to move around the village in winter — he built a **bicycle-like wooden sled** as a practical solution
- The frame is made of **ash wood**, with skid mount, handlebars, and saddle of **maple**
- On **April 1, 1911**, he patented his "single-track steerable sport sled" at the Federal Office of Intellectual Property in Bern
- The name combines Swiss German **"Velo"** (bicycle) and **"Gemel"** (local dialect for sled)
- In **1917**, he built a child's version for his daughter and later a tandem model called **"Hanibal"**
- Manufacturing occurs **exclusively in Grindelwald**, with **30 to 50 units** produced annually and sold internationally
- Since **1996** (the village's **850th anniversary**), Grindelwald has hosted the annual **Velogemel World Championship** every February
- Recognized as a **living tradition** of Switzerland (UNESCO intangible cultural heritage list)

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The last ice harvesters of Grindelwald** — In 1863, a child helps the ice cutters at the glacier, loading blocks onto sleds bound for Paris. But the glacier is alive and has something to say about losing its ice
2. **The Eiger's secret window** — A child discovers a hidden door inside the Eigerwand railway station that leads into the mountain itself, where the Eiger tells the stories of all the climbers who challenged its north face
3. **The Velogemel race** — A shy child builds a Velogemel with old Christian Buhlmann's ghost, enters the world championship, and discovers that the sled steers itself when you believe in it
4. **Ride to the Top of Europe** — A child boards Adolf Guyer-Zeller's first train in 1912, traveling through the Eiger tunnel as construction workers' lanterns flicker in the dark, emerging at the Jungfraujoch into blinding sunlight
5. **The glacier that remembers** — The Lower Grindelwald Glacier is shrinking and forgetting its own past. A child walks the Glacier Gorge and collects the glacier's scattered memories — the marble, the ice trade, the Little Ice Age — before they melt away forever
6. **The flying elevator of the Wetterhorn** — In 1908, a child is the first passenger on Wilhelm Feldmann's miraculous aerial cableway, soaring 420 meters above the glacier in a tiny cabin while the inventor's spirit rides alongside
7. **Forty-five meters into nothing** — A child afraid of heights must cross the First Cliff Walk to deliver a message to the alpine choughs nesting on the other side, discovering that the mountains hold you up when you trust them
8. **The seven Bergschaften** — Each of Grindelwald's seven mountain communities holds a piece of an ancient key. A child must visit Bussalp, Holzmatten, Bach, Grindel, Scheidegg, Wargistal, and Itramen to assemble the key and unlock the secret of the "barred forest"
