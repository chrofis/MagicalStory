## 1. The Castle on the Bay — Schloss Spiez

- **Schloss Spiez** sits on a small peninsula jutting into Lake Thun, making it one of the most photographed castles in Switzerland
- The site has been inhabited since the **Bronze Age** — burial objects from **1750–1500 BC** and graves from the **4th–2nd century BC** have been found on the castle peninsula
- A fortified residential settlement likely occupied the peninsula by the **7th century AD**
- The **first written mention** dates to **762**, when Bishop **Heddo of Strasbourg** bequeathed "the church and tithes of Spiez" to the Alsatian monastery of Ettenheim
- The massive **square keep** was built around **1245** (dated by a 2019 archaeological investigation) and was heightened several times, with its final construction phase in **1600**
- Portions of the curtain walls also date to the **12th century**; by the **13th century**, the town of Spiez had grown outside the castle walls
- Three great families shaped the castle over **700 years**: the **Barons von Strättligen** (to 1338), the **Lords von Bubenberg** (1338–1516), and the **von Erlach** patrician family (1516–1875)
- Today the castle is a **museum** run by the Spiez Castle Foundation, established in **1927**, which purchased the property in **1929** with funds raised by a public lottery of **115,000 Swiss francs**
- The castle is a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**

## 2. The Barons von Strättligen — Minnesingers and First Lords

- The **Barons von Strättligen** were the first documented noble family at Spiez, first mentioned in **1175**
- **Heinrich III von Strättligen** appears in records as **"dominus de Spiez"** (Lord of Spiez) in **1250**
- In the 13th and early 14th centuries, the family served as **bailiffs of Wimmis**, made donations to the **cloister at Interlaken**, and pursued strategic marriages with powerful aristocratic families
- Their most culturally notable member was the **minnesinger Heinrich von Strättligen**, who composed three songs — courtly love laments called **"planh"** — that were included in the famous **Codex Manesse**, the great early-14th-century collection of minnesingers' poetry (now in Heidelberg)
- According to the **Strättliger Chronik** by Elogius Kiburger, **King Rudolph II of Burgundy** built the castle in **933**, after which the Strättligen family settled there
- A crushing burden of debt forced **Johann IV von Strättligen** to sell the entire barony to the Bubenberg family in **1338** for **5,600 pounds Bernese currency** — including six villages
- The male line of the Strättligen expired before **1350**

## 3. Adrian von Bubenberg — Hero of the Battle of Murten

- **Adrian von Bubenberg** (c. **1431**–August **1479**) was born at Spiez Castle, the son of **Heinrich IV von Bubenberg**, Schultheiss (chief magistrate) of Bern and Lord of Spiez
- He succeeded his father as Lord of Spiez in **1465**
- During a pilgrimage to Jerusalem in **1466**, he was dubbed a **Knight of the Holy Sepulchre** and inscribed his name and emblem on the wall of David's Tomb
- He served as **Schultheiss of Bern** three times: **1468–69**, **1473–74**, and **1477–79**
- As a partisan of the feudal aristocracy, he initially opposed the Swiss alliance with France against Burgundy and sought peaceful relations with **Charles the Bold**
- In April **1476**, he was appointed **commander of the garrison at Murten** (Morat). There he withstood a **twelve-day siege** by Charles the Bold's Burgundian army, which outnumbered his militia **10 to 1**
- On **June 22, 1476**, Swiss Confederate forces arrived and routed the Burgundians in the **Battle of Murten** — one of the most decisive victories in Swiss history
- Von Bubenberg died in **1479** and was buried in the choir of the **Berner Münster**. He was posthumously banned for debts in 1481, but the Bernese government refused Pope Sixtus IV's demand that he be exhumed
- In **1897**, Bern honored him with a bronze statue on Christoffelplatz, which was renamed **Bubenbergplatz**
- The Bubenberg line ended in **1506** with the death of **Adrian II**; the castle passed to the **von Erlach** family in **1516**

## 4. The Von Erlach — Nine Generations of Baroque Splendor

- **Ludwig von Erlach** purchased Schloss Spiez in **1516**, beginning a dynasty that would hold it for **nine generations** until **1875**
- The most prominent Erlach lord was **Franz Ludwig von Erlach** (1575–1651), Baron of Spiez, Lord of Schadau, Bümpliz and Oberhofen, who served as **Mayor of Bern** from **1629 to 1651**
- In **1614**, Franz Ludwig converted the castle's great hall (Palas) into a magnificent **Baroque banquet hall**, decorated with early Baroque stucco work by **Antonio Castelli of Ticino** — it remains one of the finest interiors in the Bernese Oberland
- His son **Sigismund von Erlach** substantially modified the castle church interior in **1670/76**, adding Baroque stucco and new windows
- It was an earlier Erlach lord, **Rudolf von Erlach** (Schultheiss of Bern), who commissioned the famous **Spiezer Chronik** in 1484 (see below), cementing the Erlach family's connection to the cultural memory of Spiez
- The last Erlach owner, **Ferdinand Rudolf Albrecht von Erlach** (1821–1884), lost his investments and faced financial ruin; the castle was **auctioned in 1875**, ending 359 years of Erlach ownership

## 5. The Castle Church — One of Switzerland's Oldest Buildings

- The **Schlosskirche** (Castle Church of St. Laurentius) next to the castle is one of the **oldest buildings in Switzerland**
- Parts of the **crypt** and lower tower sections are **pre-Romanesque**, dating to approximately **700 AD**
- Archaeological excavations in **1949/50** uncovered remains of a small **Carolingian single-nave church** with lateral annexes and an apse, the earliest structure on the site
- The current building was erected around **1000–1050 AD** in the **Lombardian Romanesque** style: a **three-nave, flat-ceilinged pillar basilica** with four bays, a vaulted chancel, a raised middle apse over a large crypt, and two lateral apses
- The three **round apses** on the lakeside are the church's most distinctive external feature
- **Romanesque frescoes** (c. 1200) survive in the choir barrel vault: **Christ enthroned on a rainbow**, transported to heaven by four angels, with the **Virgin Mother** and **Evangelist symbols** in the apse
- A figure of **St. Christopher** (c. 1300) and a **Late Gothic plaster pulpit** (c. 1518/22) are later additions
- Together with the churches at **Einigen** and **Scherzligen**, the Spiez church is among the **oldest missionary churches** in the Bernese Oberland
- The church at **Einigen** (also part of the municipality of Spiez) is considered the **mother church** of the twelve 1000-year-old churches along the **Way of St. James** on Lake Thun

## 6. "The Most Beautiful Bay in Europe"

- Spiez lies on the **south shore of Lake Thun** (Thunersee) in a sheltered, south-facing bay with views of the Bernese Alps
- In the **1970s**, plans were drawn up to build houses and a road through Spiez Bay, threatening its natural character
- A citizens' group called **"Grüne Bucht Spiez"** (Green Bay Spiez) formed and successfully fought the development for two decades
- In **1990**, at a ceremony where the Swiss cultural heritage protection association honored the group's conservation work, the famous environmentalist **Franz Weber** of Montreux declared: **"Spiez has the most beautiful bay in Europe"**
- The phrase **"Die schönste Bucht Europas"** became the town's iconic slogan and is still used in all tourism materials today
- The unique panorama — **lake, vineyards, castle, and Alpine peaks** in a single view — is what gives the claim its power
- The bay is also a stop on the **Grand Tour of Switzerland** photo route

## 7. Wine Growing — A Thousand-Year Tradition at Altitude

- Spiez is home to one of the **highest vineyards north of the Alps**, at approximately **700 meters** above sea level
- The first documented mention of viticulture dates to **994**, when the varieties **Elbling** and **Thunrebe** (now called Räuschling) were cultivated
- This makes the Spiezer wine tradition over **1,000 years old**
- Today the vineyards cover approximately **11 hectares** of south-facing slopes above the lake
- Annual production: about **80,000 bottles** of wine
- Major grape varieties: **Riesling-Sylvaner** (Müller-Thurgau), **Blauburgunder** (Pinot Noir), Cabernet Jura, Malbec, Chardonnay, Gewürztraminer, Muscat, and the historic Elbling
- The climate is made possible by **Lake Thun's moderating influence** and the warm **Föhn** wind from the south
- A **vineyard sensory adventure trail** runs through the vines above the bay, combining wine education with panoramic views

## 8. The Niesen — The Swiss Pyramid

- The **Niesen** (2,362 m / 7,749 ft) rises directly south of Lake Thun as an almost perfectly **pyramid-shaped peak** — earning it the nickname **"die Schweizer Pyramide"** (the Swiss Pyramid)
- The **Niesenbahn** funicular railway, opened **July 15, 1910**, climbs from the village of **Mülenen** (693 m) to just below the summit (2,336 m) in **30 minutes**
- The line is divided into **two sections**: Mülenen to **Schwandegg** (2,111 m long, max. gradient **66%**) and Schwandegg to **Niesen Kulm** (1,388 m long, max. gradient **68%**)
- Construction began on **August 26, 1906**, with over **200 workers** at peak activity, using manual labor and wooden scaffolding rather than modern machinery
- The service stairway alongside the funicular track has **11,674 steps** and is listed in the **Guinness Book of World Records** as the **longest stairway in the world**
- The staircase is normally **closed to the public** (maintenance crews only), but once a year in June, up to **500 runners** compete in the **Niesenlauf** (Niesen Stairway Run)
- The men's record stands at approximately **1 hour 2 minutes**; places must be reserved nearly a year in advance
- From the summit, the panorama encompasses **Lake Thun**, the entire **Bernese Alps** chain (Eiger, Mönch, Jungfrau), and on clear days stretches to the **Jura**, **Vosges**, and **Black Forest**

## 9. The Spiezer Chronik — A Medieval Masterpiece

- The **Spiezer Schilling** (also called the Spiezer Chronik or "Privater Schilling") is a richly illuminated manuscript chronicle completed in **1484/85** by **Diebold Schilling the Elder**, court scribe of Bern
- It was commissioned by **Rudolf von Erlach**, who had served multiple terms as Schultheiss of Bern and was lord of Spiez — hence the chronicle's name
- Across **806 pages** (407 leaves, 37 x 26 cm), written in **Bastarda libraria** script in Schilling's own hand, it chronicles the **history of Bern from its legendary founding through 1465**
- The manuscript contains **339 colored illustrations** with ink frames, including **16 full-page miniatures**, **323 smaller miniatures**, and **24 animal illustrations**
- It was Schilling's **final and most artistically accomplished work** before his death in **1486**
- The chronicle is today held in the **Burgerbibliothek Bern** (Mss.h.h.I.16) and can be viewed digitally on the **e-codices** platform (Virtual Manuscript Library of Switzerland)
- It is named after Spiez because it remained at the castle for centuries as an Erlach family treasure

## 10. The Lötschberg Railway — Gateway Through the Alps

- **Spiez** is the northern terminus of the **Lötschberg line**, connecting the Bernese Oberland with the **Valais** and onward to Italy via the Simplon
- The original **Lötschberg Tunnel** (14.6 km) was built between **1907 and 1913** by the **Bern-Lötschberg-Simplon railway** (BLS), linking Kandersteg (Bern) with Goppenstein (Valais)
- Construction was marked by tragedy: in February **1908**, an avalanche destroyed workers' quarters, killing **13**; in July **1908**, the tunnel broke into a glacial fissure beneath the Gasterntal, flooding the workface and killing **25** — **1,554 meters** of tunnel had to be abandoned and sealed, replaced by a curved bypass
- The line opened on **July 15, 1913**, with electrification from the start — a technical milestone
- The **Lötschberg Base Tunnel** (34.6 km), part of the **NEAT AlpTransit** project, opened on **December 9, 2007**, running **400 meters below** the original tunnel
- The BLS **control centre in Spiez** monitors all traffic through both tunnels, extending as far south as **Domodossola** in Italy
- Spiez also serves as a junction for the lines to **Interlaken** (since 1893) and to **Zweisimmen–Montreux** (since 1897–1905), making it one of the most important **railway junctions** in the Bernese Oberland

## 11. Lake Thun and the Paddle Steamer Blümlisalp

- **Lake Thun** (Thunersee) stretches **18 km** between Thun and Interlaken, with Spiez on its southern shore
- The lake has been a transportation route since antiquity; today, scheduled boats operated by **BLS Schifffahrt** connect Spiez with Thun, Interlaken, and lakeside villages
- The crown jewel of the fleet is the **paddle steamer Blümlisalp**, built in **1906** by **Escher, Wyss & Co.** in Zurich for **375,000 Swiss francs**
- Named after the **Blüemlisalp** mountain massif, she was the largest and most elegant steamer on Lakes Thun and Brienz
- Decommissioned on **August 1, 1971**, and slated for scrapping, the Blümlisalp languished for two decades moored in the delta of the **Kander River**
- The cooperative **"Vaporama"** rescued her: after over two years of restoration, the steamer's **second maiden voyage** took place on **May 22, 1992**
- In **2008**, ICOMOS (International Council on Monuments and Sites) awarded the restoration its **Special Award** for heritage preservation
- The Blümlisalp now operates regular summer cruises, passing directly through the Spiez bay with its castle backdrop

## 12. Geography — Gateway to the Oberland

- Spiez (population c. **13,000**) sits at **558–852 m** above sea level on the south shore of Lake Thun, with **13 km of lake access**
- The municipality comprises five districts: **Spiez**, **Spiezwiler**, **Hondrich**, **Faulensee**, and **Einigen**
- Spiez is a natural **gateway**: the **Kandertal** (leading to Kandersteg, Lake Oeschinen, and the Lötschberg) and the **Simmental** (leading to Zweisimmen and Gstaad) both branch off from the town
- This strategic position made Spiez a vital **transportation hub** from the railway era onward
- The combination of lake, castle, vineyards, and the Alpine backdrop — with the Niesen pyramid to the south and the Bernese high Alps beyond — creates a landscape often described as one of the most beautiful in Switzerland

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The minnesinger's last song** — A child finds a hidden melody in the castle walls; following it leads back to Heinrich von Strättligen composing his final love lament for the Codex Manesse, with the castle and lake as his muse
2. **Adrian's oath at Murten** — Adrian von Bubenberg's horse carries a young squire from Spiez Castle to the besieged walls of Murten; the child must deliver a secret message before Charles the Bold's army breaks through
3. **11,674 steps to the sky** — A child enters the forbidden Niesen staircase and finds each landing opens a window to a different era of Spiez's history, from Bronze Age settlers to the funicular's construction workers
4. **The Blümlisalp's second life** — The old paddle steamer, rusting in the Kander delta, tells a child her story and asks for help; together they rally the town to save her before she is scrapped
5. **The vine that remembered** — A thousand-year-old grapevine root in the Spiezer vineyard whispers stories of every harvest since 994; the child must help it survive a frost that threatens the highest vines in the Alps
6. **The chronicle painter's secret** — A child discovers a hidden 340th illustration in the Spiezer Chronik that reveals a treasure map of the castle; following the clues through the real castle rooms and church
7. **The bay they saved** — Set in the 1970s, a child joins the "Green Bay" movement to stop bulldozers from paving over the most beautiful bay in Europe; Franz Weber arrives with a legendary speech
8. **The tunnel that broke through** — Deep inside the Lötschberg tunnel in 1908, a child worker hears water rushing behind the rock face; a race against time to warn the crew before the glacial flood
