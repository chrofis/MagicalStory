## 1. Geography — The Highest City on the Jura Plateau

- La Chaux-de-Fonds sits at **992 metres** above sea level on the **Jura plateau** (Haut-Plateau neuchâtelois), in the canton of **Neuchâtel** — just a few kilometres from the French border
- It is the **highest city in Switzerland** with more than 30,000 inhabitants, and one of the highest in Europe
- Population: approximately **36,900** (French-speaking / francophone)
- The name derives from Franco-Provençal dialect: **"Chaux"** (high open pastureland) and **"Fonds"** (valley bottom) — literally "the high meadow in the valley"
- The climate is **harsh and continental**: long, snowy winters with temperatures dropping to **-6 °C** in January, heavy snowfall accumulating up to **680 mm** of snow per month
- The surrounding Jura landscape of **coniferous forests and limestone ridges** made the area ill-suited to agriculture — very little settlement occurred here before the late Middle Ages
- First mentioned circa **1350** as "la Chaz de Fonz"; in **1531** the hamlet had only about **35 inhabitants**

## 2. The Great Fire of 1794 (Le Grand Incendie)

- On **5 May 1794**, a devastating fire swept through the village of La Chaux-de-Fonds, destroying **two-thirds of all buildings**
- Damage was estimated at over **1 million livres** (pounds), including 700,000 for buildings alone
- The fire was a turning point: rather than rebuild the old medieval village of narrow, winding lanes, the authorities decided to create an entirely **new urban plan**
- The **King of Prussia** (the Neuchâtel region was then a Prussian principality) donated **24,000 livres** toward reconstruction
- Three local citizens — **Charles Simon-Sandoz, Fredrich Sandoz, and Moyse Perret-Gentil** — embarked on a 54-day horseback tour across Switzerland to raise funds, visiting Basel, Bern, Solothurn, and Frauenfeld
- Within three years, the population had returned to its pre-fire level — the community's resilience and solidarity were remarkable
- A small eastern section of the city was **spared by the fire** and preserves the older, irregular street pattern to this day

## 3. The Grid City — Urbanisme Horloger (Watchmaking Town Planning)

- In **1835**, engineer **Charles-Henri Junod** (an inspector of the Ponts-et-Chaussées / public works) drew up a revolutionary urban plan for the rebuilt city
- Junod was a pupil of the Swiss pedagogue **Johann Heinrich Pestalozzi** and was inspired by the **"Sonnenstadt"** (City of the Sun), an ideal town designed in 1824 by Dr Bernhard Christoph Faust, in which all houses face south to receive maximum sunlight
- The plan created a strict **grid of parallel streets** running east-west, with each row of buildings followed by a row of **gardens** — ensuring every workshop received maximum **natural daylight** (lumière du jour), essential for the precision work of watchmakers
- Streets were made **wide** and buildings were **limited in height** — both for fire prevention and for light
- The central avenue, **Avenue Léopold Robert**, forms the main axis of this geometric plan
- Karl Marx described La Chaux-de-Fonds in **Das Kapital** (1867) as a "huge factory-town" (eine grosse Fabrikstadt) where the entire city functioned as "a single watch manufactory" — his classic example of the **division of labour** in manufacturing
- In **2009**, UNESCO inscribed **La Chaux-de-Fonds / Le Locle, Watchmaking Town Planning** (urbanisme horloger) as a **World Heritage Site** (criterion iv) — recognising the towns as outstanding examples of mono-industrial cities whose urban planning was shaped entirely by a single craft

## 4. Watchmaking Capital — L'Horlogerie

- Watchmaking arrived in the Neuchâtel mountains in the **late 17th century**, likely brought by **Huguenot** refugees fleeing France after the revocation of the Edict of Nantes (1685)
- **Daniel JeanRichard** (1665–1741) of La Sagne (near La Chaux-de-Fonds) is considered the **father of Neuchâtel watchmaking**: according to tradition, as a 15-year-old blacksmith's apprentice, he repaired a broken English pocket watch brought by a horse trader, and within a year had built his first watch from scratch
- JeanRichard trained many apprentices and pushed for mechanised toolmaking, giving birth to over **100 specialist professions**: movement-blank producers, gem-cutters, escapement-makers, profile turners, and many others
- By the 18th century, La Chaux-de-Fonds had developed the **établissage** system (système de l'établissage): a decentralised network of home workshops (ateliers), each specialising in one component, with an **établisseur** coordinating assembly — up to **3,000 small workshops** operating simultaneously
- The city became the **world capital of watchmaking** by the 19th century, its entire economy and urban fabric built around horology
- Today, major watch brands with roots in La Chaux-de-Fonds include **Girard-Perregaux**, **Ebel**, **Corum**, and **TAG Heuer** (headquarters)

## 5. Musée International d'Horlogerie (MIH)

- In **1865**, the Watchmaking School of La Chaux-de-Fonds (École d'horlogerie) began assembling a collection of old clocks and watches for didactic purposes
- In **1902**, watch industrialist **Maurice Picard** championed the creation of a proper museum; on **24 March 1902**, the town council signed the foundation deed of the Watchmaking Museum
- The current building opened in **1974** as the **Musée International d'Horlogerie** — with avant-garde underground architecture
- The collection is the **largest in the world** devoted to the history of time measurement: approximately **10,000 objects** including Swiss and international watches, clocks, automata, tools, machines, engravings, and iconographic works from the 16th century to the present
- Features a working **antique watchmaking restoration centre** built entirely of glass, where visitors can observe watchmakers at work on weekdays

## 6. Le Corbusier — L'Enfant du Pays (The Local Son)

- **Charles-Édouard Jeanneret** — later known as **Le Corbusier** — was born in La Chaux-de-Fonds on **6 October 1887** into a family of watchmakers and musicians
- He studied at the **École d'Art** (Art School) under the influential teacher **Charles L'Éplattenier** (1874–1946), whom he later called "my only teacher" (mon seul maître)
- L'Éplattenier taught him drawing, art history, and the naturalist aesthetics of Art Nouveau; he also decided that the young Jeanneret should become an architect
- Le Corbusier designed **six buildings** in La Chaux-de-Fonds before leaving for Paris:
  - **Villa Fallet** (1906–1907): his first building, co-designed with architect René Chapallaz for watchmaker Louis Fallet — a manifesto of the **Style sapin** (fir tree style), with geometric motifs drawn from Jura conifers
  - **Villas Stotzer and Jaquemet** (1908): two houses in a similar regional style
  - **Villa Jeanneret-Perret / Maison Blanche** (1911–1912): his **first fully independent project**, built for his parents — a white-plastered house with four central pillars foreshadowing his later theories of the **plan libre** (free plan) and **pilotis**
  - **Cinéma La Scala** (1916): a 1,000-seat cinema built into a natural slope
  - **Villa Schwob / Villa Turque** (1916–1917): built for watch industrialist **Anatole Schwob** (owner of Cyma Watches) — a bold departure toward the modernism that would define his career
- The **Maison Blanche** was acquired by the Association Maison Blanche in 2000 and restored in 2004–2005; it is open to the public

## 7. Art Nouveau and the Style Sapin

- La Chaux-de-Fonds is one of Switzerland's richest cities for **Art Nouveau** (Jugendstil) architecture, sitting at the crossroads where French/Belgian curvilinear Art Nouveau meets the more geometric German/Austrian Sezessionstil
- Art Nouveau was imported at the end of the 19th century under the influence of **watch-making patrons** and their international trade representatives
- **Charles L'Éplattenier** and his students at the École d'Art developed an original local variant: the **Style sapin** (fir tree style, 1905–1914) — an artistic language drawing on the Jura's native flora, especially the **sapin** (fir tree), with its branches, cones, and needles abstracted into geometric ornament
- Notable Art Nouveau buildings include the **Crematorium** (1909–1910), considered the masterpiece of the Style sapin period, with richly decorated facades featuring natural motifs
- Decorative elements appear throughout the city: painted tiles, stained glass, stucco work, wrought iron, carved wood — on apartment buildings, villas, and public buildings alike
- La Chaux-de-Fonds is a member of the **Réseau Art Nouveau Network**, a European network of cities with significant Art Nouveau heritage

## 8. Louis Chevrolet — From Watchmaker's Son to Motor City

- **Louis-Joseph Chevrolet** was born in La Chaux-de-Fonds on **25 December 1878**, the second child of **Joseph-Félicien Chevrolet**, a watchmaker, and **Marie-Anne Angéline Mahon**
- In **1887**, the family moved to **Beaune, France**, where Louis developed his mechanical skills and a passion for bicycle racing
- He emigrated to the **United States** from France in **1900**
- In **1905**, in his first automobile race, he defeated the legendary driver **Barney Oldfield** — his mile time of **52.8 seconds** was considered remarkable
- On **3 November 1911**, Louis co-founded the **Chevrolet Motor Car Company** with **William Crapo Durant** (former head of General Motors), his brother Arthur Chevrolet, and investors William Little and Dr Edwin R. Campbell
- Louis and Durant clashed over the car's design; in **1915**, Louis sold his stake to Durant, who the following year merged Chevrolet into **General Motors**
- Cars he designed won the **Indianapolis 500** twice: in **1920** (driven by his brother Gaston) and **1921** (driven by Tommy Milton)
- He later pursued motorboat racing and worked for Stutz Automobile Company before rejoining General Motors in **1936**
- He died on **6 June 1941** in Detroit — the watchmaker's son from the Jura who gave his name to one of America's most iconic car brands

## 9. Workers' Movements and the Jura Anarchists

- The watchmakers of La Chaux-de-Fonds were not only skilled artisans but also **politically radical**: their independent, home-based workshop system (établissage) fostered egalitarian, anti-authoritarian values
- In **1866**, two sections of the **International Workingmen's Association** (First International) were established by watchmakers in the region
- **Mikhail Bakunin**, the Russian anarchist, travelled to the Jura in **1869** and found a receptive audience among the literate, independent watchmakers — "in their houses in the canton of Jura they not only assembled intricate miniature mechanisms, but also read a lot"
- The **Fédération jurassienne** (Jura Federation) became the geographic centre of the anarchist wing of the First International, opposing Marx's centralised vision
- The **1871 Sonvilier Circular** (Circulaire de Sonvilier), issued from the neighbouring village, criticised Marx's concentration of power and cemented the Jura's reputation worldwide as a cradle of libertarian socialism
- The **Congress of Saint-Imier** (1872) was attended by delegates from Italy, Spain, France, and Russia — a founding event for the **international anarchist movement**
- In **1880**, a congress of the Jura Federation held in La Chaux-de-Fonds adopted **"anarchist communism"** (communisme anarchiste) as its doctrine
- Key figures included **James Guillaume**, **Adhémar Schwitzguébel** (who later founded the Federation of Watch Industry Workers), and **Élisée Reclus**
- Friedrich Engels praised the radical disposition of the working people of La Chaux-de-Fonds; the tension between the old artisanal autonomy and the new factory discipline (exemplified by **Ernest Francillon's** mechanised Longines factory, opened **1867**) fuelled decades of labour activism

## 10. The Salle de Musique — A Jewel of Acoustics

- The **Salle de Musique** (Concert Hall) was built in **1951** and inaugurated in **June 1955**, designed by architects **René Chapallaz** and **Hans Bieri**
- The hall seats approximately **1,100** concertgoers
- Its interior walls are covered with **walnut wood panelling** assembled using **lutherie** techniques (the craft of violin-making) — this is widely considered the secret of its extraordinary acoustic properties
- More than **450 recordings** for prestigious music labels have been made in the hall
- Chilean pianist **Claudio Arrau** famously said: "From the point of view of sound, the most beautiful piano in the world is in La Chaux-de-Fonds, in a delightful little concert hall. I made my best recordings there" — Arrau recorded there repeatedly between **1978 and 1991**
- Since **2010**, the Salle de Musique has been listed in the **Swiss Inventory of Cultural Property of National Importance** (Inventaire suisse des biens culturels d'importance nationale)
- The nearly **130-year-old Société de Musique** organises the concert seasons, reflecting the city's long tradition of supporting classical music alongside its industrial identity

## 11. Notable Historical Facts

- **1350**: First documented mention of the locality as "la Chaz de Fonz"
- **1656**: La Chaux-de-Fonds formally chartered (date often cited as the city's founding)
- **Late 17th century**: Watchmaking arrives in the Neuchâtel mountains, transforming the economy
- **1794**: The Great Fire destroys two-thirds of the village
- **1835**: Charles-Henri Junod designs the revolutionary grid plan
- **1867**: Karl Marx cites La Chaux-de-Fonds in Das Kapital as "a single watch manufactory"
- **1872–1880**: The Jura becomes the cradle of international anarchism
- **1887**: Le Corbusier born; Louis Chevrolet's family departs for France
- **1902**: Watchmaking Museum founded
- **2009**: UNESCO World Heritage inscription for "urbanisme horloger"
- The city's motto could well be "light and precision" — its entire urban form, from street width to building height to garden spacing, was designed to serve the **craft of measuring time**

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The girl who rebuilt the city of light** — After the Great Fire of 1794, a child helps architect Junod design the new grid plan by showing him how sunlight falls through her father's watchmaking loupe onto the workshop table
2. **The watchmaker's 3,000 tiny workshops** — A lost watch gear rolls through La Chaux-de-Fonds, passing from workshop to workshop (gem-cutter, escapement-maker, profile turner) — the child must follow it through the établissage system to complete the watch before midnight
3. **Le Corbusier's first house** — Young Charles-Édouard builds a house for his parents (Maison Blanche) and keeps redesigning it; the child helps him understand that a home needs heart, not just pillars and plans
4. **The Christmas boy who raced the world** — Young Louis Chevrolet, born on Christmas Day to a watchmaker father, dreams of speed instead of springs; the child follows his journey from bicycle races in Beaune to defeating Barney Oldfield in America
5. **The piano in the wooden hall** — A child sneaks into the Salle de Musique at night and discovers the walnut walls were built like a giant violin; the hall itself begins to sing, and the child must conduct the music of the city
6. **The anarchist watchmakers** — A child in 1872 overhears Bakunin speaking to a room full of watchmakers; the gears of the watches begin turning backwards, and the child must decide: does time belong to everyone, or to the factory owner?
7. **The fir tree style** — Teacher L'Éplattenier takes his students into the Jura forest to study fir trees; young Jeanneret sees architecture in the branches, and the child discovers that every Art Nouveau building in the city hides a secret pattern from nature
8. **The city where time stands still** — On the coldest winter night at 992 metres altitude, all the clocks in the Musée International d'Horlogerie stop at once; a child must wind the great carillon clock to restart time before the snow buries the grid city forever
