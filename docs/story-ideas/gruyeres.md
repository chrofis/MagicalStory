## 1. Geography — The Fribourg Pre-Alps

- Gruyères sits at **810 meters** altitude on top of an **82-meter-high hill** overlooking the Saane valley (French: vallée de la Sarine) in the canton of **Fribourg** (German: Freiburg)
- The municipality has a population of approximately **2,282** — but the medieval hilltop village itself is tiny, essentially a single cobblestone lane
- The dominant peak of the region is **Le Moléson** (2,002 m / 6,568 ft), the symbol of the Fribourg Pre-Alps (Préalpes fribourgeoises), accessible by funicular and cable car from the village of Moléson-sur-Gruyères
- The **Sarine river** (German: Saane) flows through the valley below, and the artificial **Lac de la Gruyère** (Lake of Gruyère) stretches along the valley floor
- From the summit of Moléson, visitors enjoy a **360-degree panorama** spanning from Lake Geneva (Lac Léman) through the Bernese Oberland (Eiger, Mönch, Jungfrau) to the Jura mountains
- The region lies between the Swiss Plateau (Mittelland) and the Alps proper — rolling green pastures, forested hills, and dramatic limestone peaks define the landscape

## 2. The Crane Legend — Founding Myth (La légende de la grue)

- According to legend, Gruyères was founded around **400 BC** by **Gruerius**, a Vandal king
- Gruerius saw a **crane** (French: grue) flying across the crimson evening sky and decided to build his town on that very spot
- He captured the crane and chose it as his **heraldic animal**, giving the settlement its name — **Gruyères** derives directly from "grue"
- The **coat of arms** (les armoiries) of Gruyères depicts a **silver crane on a red field** — in heraldic terms: "Gules, a Crane rising Argent"
- The blazon has remained unchanged for centuries, and the crane symbol appears throughout the town and region to this day
- No historical record confirms the existence of Count Gruerius — the legend is a medieval founding myth — but the first documentary mention of **"de Grueri"** appears around **1138–1139**
- The legend elegantly ties together the town's name, its heraldic identity, and its enduring sense of place

## 3. The Counts of Gruyères — A Noble Dynasty (Les Comtes de Gruyère)

- The **Counts of Gruyères** were one of the leading noble families of western Switzerland during the Middle Ages
- The first documentary evidence of the dynasty appears in a **1115 charter** issued by **Count Guillaume I**
- For almost **five centuries**, from their hilltop castle, the counts ruled large tracts of land on either side of the **Sarine river**, including lordships of Palézieux, Oron, and Aubonne
- The counts were vassals of the **House of Savoy**, and their castle was built in the **carré savoyard** (Savoyard square) military design
- Approximately **19 counts** ruled in succession from the castle
- The dynasty ended with **Count Michel** (ruled 1539–1554), who was beset by mounting debts, familial extravagance, and failed loans
- In **1554**, Count Michel declared **bankruptcy** (la faillite), and his properties were confiscated by his principal creditors: the cantons of **Bern** and **Fribourg**
- Bern and Fribourg divided his former lands between them — Fribourg took possession of the Gruyères region and installed **bailiffs** (baillis) in the castle
- Between **1555 and 1798**, more than **fifty bailiffs** drawn from leading Fribourg families administered the region from the castle, dispensing justice, managing finances, and collecting taxes

## 4. Château de Gruyères — Eight Centuries of History

- First mentioned in archives in **1244**; the current structure was built between **1270 and 1282** following the Savoyard square plan
- The castle sits on the hilltop at the far end of the village, commanding views of the entire valley
- In the **15th century**, Count Louis initiated extensive reconstruction, transforming the fortified castle into a stately residence — elegant stone galleries (galeries de pierre) replaced wooden structures, wide windows were added, a staircase tower was built, and a chapel was installed in a former defensive tower
- After the **1554 bankruptcy** and the **bailiff period** (1555–1798), the castle passed through various hands
- In **1848**, the prefecture relocated to Bulle; the castle was put up for sale
- In **1849**, the **Bovy brothers** (including painter Daniel Bovy) and the **Balland family** purchased it at auction and established an **artists' colony** (colonie d'artistes)
- Beginning in **1850**, Daniel Bovy invited friends including the celebrated French painter **Jean-Baptiste Camille Corot** and **Barthélemy Menn** (a Geneva drawing teacher) to create decorative works throughout the castle rooms
- **Corot** resided at the castle in June **1852** and **1857**, painting four landscape medallions on the wood panelling — the room still bears his name ("Salle Corot")
- The castle houses **three capes of the Order of the Golden Fleece** (Ordre de la Toison d'or), captured as **war booty** by Swiss Confederate troops (including soldiers from Gruyères) at the **Battle of Morat** (Murten) on **22 June 1476** against **Charles the Bold, Duke of Burgundy**
- In **1938**, the canton of Fribourg repurchased the castle, converted it into a **museum**, and opened it to the public in **1939**
- Today the castle displays collections spanning the Middle Ages to the 19th century, including the **Chapel of St. John the Baptist** with stained-glass windows from **1482**

## 5. Le Gruyère AOP — The World-Famous Cheese

- The region of Gruyère has been known for cheese production since the **12th century** — the earliest documentation of cheesemaking dates to **1115**
- Medieval chronicles record that inhabitants turned their herds' milk into full-fat cheese (fromage gras), which was exported as far as **France and Italy** — wheels were transported to Vevey and shipped onward to Lyon
- The **17th century** brought both official recognition of the cheese's name and a boom in exports; early origin-protection measures through marking emerged
- The term "gruyère" entered the **Dictionnaire de l'Académie française** by **1762**
- Production follows strict traditional methods: cows are fed only **natural forage** — fresh grass in summer, hay in winter — with **no silage or additives** permitted
- Each wheel requires approximately **400 liters of milk**, is pressed for about **20 hours** with up to **900 kg** of applied force, and weighs roughly **35 kg**
- Aging: minimum **5 months** for "Classique," up to **18+ months** for "Réserve" — the flavor deepens from mild and nutty to complex and crystalline
- Annual production: approximately **29,000 tonnes** across village dairies in the cantons of Fribourg, Vaud, Neuchâtel, Jura, and parts of Bern
- **2001**: Gruyère received **Controlled Designation of Origin** (AOC) at the national level
- **2011**: Achieved **Protected Designation of Origin** (AOP — Appellation d'Origine Protégée) across Europe

## 6. La Maison du Gruyère — The Demonstration Dairy

- On **24 June 1969**, Switzerland's first demonstration cheese dairy (fromagerie de démonstration) was inaugurated in **Pringy**, at the foot of the Gruyères hill, with Federal Councillor Hans Schaffner in attendance
- Over **100,000 visitors** came during its inaugural year
- In **May 2000**, the facility was extensively renovated and reopened as **La Maison du Gruyère** (renovation cost: CHF 9.7 million)
- Today the dairy produces **48 wheels of Gruyère AOP per day** using four copper vats of 4,800-liter capacity each
- The aging cellars store up to **7,000 wheels** at controlled temperature and humidity
- Visitors follow an interactive tour guided by "Cerise" the cow, engaging all senses — cowbells, Alpine hay scent, and cheese tastings at three stages of ripening
- In **2019**, La Maison du Gruyère welcomed over **670,000 guests**, making it one of the most visited attractions in the Fribourg region

## 7. HR Giger Museum & Bar — Biomechanical Art in a Medieval Village

### The Museum (Le Musée HR Giger)
- **Hans Ruedi Giger** (5 February 1940, Chur — 12 May 2014) was a Swiss artist renowned for his **biomechanical** (biomécanique) style, blending human forms with machine elements in haunting airbrush paintings
- In 1979, he designed the **Xenomorph** creature for Ridley Scott's film *Alien*, inspired by his painting *Necronom IV*
- He won the **Academy Award for Best Visual Effects** in **1980** for his work on the film
- After numerous visits to Gruyères, Giger fell in love with the medieval village; on **11 September 1997**, he purchased the **Château Saint-Germain** to house his art
- The **museum opened on 21 June 1998**, preceded by a hugely successful **1990 retrospective** at the Château de Gruyères celebrating his 50th birthday, which drew **110,000 visitors**
- The collection spans from the early 1960s to the present, including seminal works such as *The Spell* and the *Passages* series, plus film designs for *Alien*, *Alien 3*, *Dune*, *Species*, and *Poltergeist 2*
- Giger was inducted into the **Science Fiction and Fantasy Hall of Fame** in **2013**

### The Giger Bar (Le Museum HR Giger Bar)
- Opened on **12 April 2003**, located across from the museum in the Château Saint-Germain
- Giger personally designed every element: **ceiling, walls, floors, fittings, tables, and chairs** are modelled in his biomechanical style
- The interior resembles a cavernous skeletal structure — **double arches of vertebrae** crisscross the vaulted ceiling, evoking the sensation of sitting inside a prehistoric fossilized creature
- The contrast is surreal and deliberate: a **medieval Swiss village** housing a bar that looks like the interior of an alien organism

## 8. The Medieval Hilltop Village — A Jewel of the Pre-Alps

- Gruyères is one of Switzerland's **most beautiful villages** (un des plus beaux villages de Suisse), a perfectly preserved medieval settlement
- The village is essentially a **single cobblestone lane** (une seule ruelle pavée) enclosed within ancient **fortification walls** and a gate
- The street is **car-free** (sans voitures) — visitors arrive on foot from the train station or parking areas below
- Facades of historic houses, shops, hotels, and restaurants line both sides, many decorated with wrought-iron signs and flower boxes
- A central **fountain** (fontaine) stands in the middle of the main street, flanked by the historic buildings
- At the far western end stands the **castle**; the village stretches along the hilltop ridge to the east
- Four museums within this tiny village: the **Château de Gruyères**, the **HR Giger Museum**, the **Tibet Museum** (Fondation Alain Bordier, opened 2009, housing ~300 Buddhist art objects from the 6th–18th century), and **La Maison du Gruyère** at the foot of the hill

## 9. Bénichon — The Harvest Festival (La fête de la Bénichon)

- **La Bénichon** is a centuries-old harvest festival unique to the canton of Fribourg
- The name derives from the Latin **"benedicto"** (blessing) — originally a religious feast celebrating the consecration (bénédiction) of the local church
- Over time, it evolved into a secular folk festival marking the **end of the harvest** and the **return of cattle herds** from the Alpine pastures (la désalpe)
- Celebrated in the lowlands on the **second Sunday of September** and in the mountainous regions on the **second Sunday of October**
- The traditional Bénichon meal is a legendary multi-course feast lasting **six hours or more**:
  - **Cuchaule** (AOP since 2018): a golden saffron-infused brioche bread, the "queen of the Bénichon," first mentioned in **1558**
  - **Moutarde de Bénichon**: a sweet-spicy mustard spread made with mustard powder, white wine, vin cuit, cinnamon, and star anise
  - **Poire à botzi**: caramelized pears, a regional specialty
  - Smoked ham, Fribourg sausage, cabbage soup, lamb stew
  - **Meringues with double cream** (meringues à la crème double) for dessert
  - **Bricelets**: delicate wafer-type biscuits; **cuquettes**: flaky pastries
- Music, dancing, parades, and brass bands accompany the feast

## 10. La Désalpe — The Cattle Descent (La Désalpe de Charmey)

- The **désalpe** (German: Alpabzug) is the annual autumn descent of cattle from their high Alpine summer pastures (alpages) down to the valley farms for winter
- This tradition has roots stretching back thousands of years — an essential rhythm of Alpine pastoral life
- The most famous désalpe in the Gruyère region takes place in **Charmey**, every **fourth Saturday of September**
- The cows descend wearing enormous **bells** (sonnailles/cloches) hanging from their necks and brightly colored **floral headdresses** (couronnes de fleurs)
- The herders — called **armaillis** — dress in the traditional **bredzon** (a dark blue smock), and the women wear **dzaquillons** (traditional festive dress)
- Alphorn players, flag throwers (lanceurs de drapeaux), and brass bands accompany the procession
- The milk from these cows — fed on rich Alpine grasses all summer — is used to make **Gruyère AOP** cheese
- The désalpe is both a working agricultural event and a beloved folk celebration, drawing thousands of spectators

## 11. Maison Cailler — Chocolate from Broc

- **François-Louis Cailler** founded Switzerland's oldest surviving chocolate brand in **Corsier-sur-Vevey** in **1819**
- In **1898**, his grandson **Alexandre-Louis Cailler** opened the factory in **Broc**, just a few kilometers from Gruyères, drawn by the region's abundant fresh milk and the hydroelectric power of the **Jogne river**
- The Jogne's waters were diverted through a **one-kilometer tunnel** to generate **2,000 horsepower** of electricity — enough to power the entire village of Broc
- In **1912**, the **Bulle–Broc–Fabrique railway** was inaugurated, replacing horse-drawn convoys
- The Cailler family was connected to chocolate history through marriage: François-Louis Cailler's daughter married **Daniel Peter**, who invented **milk chocolate** in **1875** using condensed milk from his neighbor **Henri Nestlé**
- In **1929**, the Peter-Cailler-Kohler company merged with **Nestlé**, and Cailler became a Nestlé brand
- Today, **Maison Cailler** in Broc is an interactive chocolate museum and working factory, one of the most visited attractions in the region

## 12. Alpine Culture — Chalets, Alphorns, and Mountain Traditions

- The **chalet** (from the Arpitan/Franco-Provençal word for "herder's hut") is the quintessential building of the Gruyère landscape — heavy timber construction with gently sloping roofs, wide eaves, and carved wooden balconies
- Originally, chalets were seasonal Alpine farms (chalets d'alpage) where herders lived during the summer months, producing butter and cheese from their cows' milk
- The **alphorn** (cor des Alpes) was first documented in Switzerland in the **mid-16th century** — used by shepherds to call cows from pasture and communicate across valleys
- The alphorn experienced a renaissance in the **19th century** with Romanticism and the revival of Swiss folklore, becoming a national symbol
- In the Gruyère region, the alphorn tradition remains alive — local groups perform at festivals, the désalpe, and cultural events
- The **inalpe** (montée à l'alpage) in spring and the **désalpe** in autumn mark the seasonal rhythm of transhumance — the ancient practice of moving herds between lowland and highland pastures
- Traditional crafts include woodcarving, embroidery, and the making of **taillé** (patterned wooden butter molds)

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The crane who named a kingdom** — Retelling of the founding myth: a child follows a silver crane across the evening sky to the hilltop where Gruyères will be born, and the crane whispers the town's name
2. **The last count's secret** — Count Michel's debts are mounting; a child discovers a hidden room in the castle containing the original crane banner, and must choose between saving the treasure or letting the old world pass
3. **The cheese wheel that rolled away** — A freshly pressed wheel of Gruyère escapes from La Maison du Gruyère and rolls through the village, past the castle, down the hill — child chases it, learning about cheesemaking at each stop
4. **Alien in the castle** — A child enters the Giger Bar thinking it is a medieval dungeon, discovers the biomechanical world, and must figure out how the strangest art museum in Switzerland ended up in the most charming village
5. **The golden bread of Bénichon** — At the harvest festival, a child learns to bake cuchaule with a mysterious old woman who claims the saffron was a gift from the crane on the coat of arms
6. **The cows come home** — A shy child joins the armaillis for the désalpe from the high pastures above Charmey; the lead cow's enormous bell has a magical tone that makes everyone who hears it dance
7. **Corot's lost painting** — In 1852, painter Corot left an unfinished landscape medallion hidden in the castle walls; a child follows clues through the Salle Corot to find the missing work
8. **The four museums mystery** — A treasure hunt linking clues across all four Gruyères museums: a medieval cloak in the castle, an alien sculpture in the Giger Museum, a Tibetan prayer bell, and a cheese mold at La Maison du Gruyère — together they reveal the crane's secret
