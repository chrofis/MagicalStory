## 1. Origins — From the Neolithic to the Celts and Romans

- The area around **Thun** has been inhabited since the **Neolithic age** (mid-3rd millennium BC), making the settlement approximately **5,000 years old**
- During the **early Bronze Age** (ca. **1750 BC**), a local chieftain was buried at **Thun-Renzenbühl** with one of the richest collections of early Bronze Age grave goods in Europe — six **torques** (neck rings), garment pins, a dagger, and a ceremonial **battle axe** decorated with **gold inlays** using a **damascening technique** (Tauschierung) also found in **Mycenaean Greece**, suggesting far-reaching trade networks connecting Lake Thun to the Mediterranean
- The name **"Thun"** derives from the **Celtic word *dūnon*** (also *dunum*), meaning **"fortified settlement"** or **"stockade"** — the same root found in many Celtic place names across Europe (e.g. Verdun, London/*Londinium*)
- The lake was recorded as ***lacus dunesis*** in a document from **660 AD**
- **58 BC**: The Romans conquered the region; Thun became one of the administrative centres under Roman rule
- Around **400 AD**: The **Burgundians** displaced Roman control and settled the region
- **1033**: The area became part of the **Holy Roman Empire** when Emperor **Conrad II** gained the title of King of Burgundy

## 2. The Zähringen Dynasty — Builders of Thun and Bern

- In the late 12th century, the Holy Roman Emperor entrusted the **Zähringen** (Zähringer) family with subduing the unruly Burgundian nobles of central Switzerland
- **Duke Berchtold V of Zähringen** (Herzog von Zähringen, ruled 1186–1218) built the **castle at Thun** between **1180 and 1190** and expanded the town
- The same Berchtold V founded the city of **Bern** in **1191** — Thun and Bern share the same dynastic founder
- The Zähringen never actually lived in Thun Castle, preferring **Burgdorf Castle** (Schloss Burgdorf) as their primary residence — the massive Thun keep served primarily as a **symbol of power** over the region and its trade routes
- When Berchtold V died in **February 1218** without heirs, the ducal line of the Zähringen became **extinct**
- The castle and town passed to the **House of Kyburg** (Grafen von Kyburg), who built the upper levels above the original Zähringen structure
- In **1264**, Thun received formal **town rights** (Stadtrecht)
- In **1384**, the city of **Bern** purchased Thun and Burgdorf, and the castle became the seat of Bernese regional administration — a role it held for over **400 years**

## 3. Schloss Thun — The Mighty Zähringen Keep

- The **donjon** (Bergfried/Wohnturm) built by Berchtold V is one of the **best-preserved Romanesque keeps** in Switzerland
- The walls are roughly **4 metres thick** up to the level of the Knights' Hall
- The centrepiece is the **Rittersaal** (Knights' Hall / Grosser Saal): a single massive room measuring approximately **19 × 12.5 metres** with a ceiling height of **14 metres** — one of the finest preserved **medieval representation halls** in Europe
- The Kyburg additions (post-**1218**) raised the tower further and added the distinctive **four corner turrets** that give the castle its iconic silhouette
- From the corner turrets, visitors enjoy a **360-degree panorama** over terracotta rooftops, the turquoise Aare, Lake Thun, and the distant **Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau**
- From the **17th century**, a **prison** operated within the donjon
- In **1888**, the **Historisches Museum** (historical museum) opened in the tower — it has been a museum ever since
- The castle is a **Swiss heritage site of national significance** (Kulturgut von nationaler Bedeutung)

## 4. The Obere Hauptgasse — Walking on Rooftops

- The **Obere Hauptgasse** is Thun's most distinctive street, famous for its **Hochtrottoirs** — uniquely elevated sidewalks that run along the rooftops of the shops below
- Medieval planners built **raised walkways** creating a **two-level shopping street**: ground-floor shops at street level, and a second row of shops, cafes, and entrances accessible from the elevated terrace above
- The lower level features **Lauben** (covered arcades), originally built in the **12th century** to protect goods from rain and snow — similar in concept to Bern's famous arcades, but with the added rooftop walkway unique to Thun
- The street terminates at the **Rathausplatz** (Town Hall Square), built around **1500**, which forms the historical heart of the old town
- The **Rathaus** (Town Hall) dates from **1500 AD** and anchors the square alongside colourfully painted medieval buildings
- This two-level design is found nowhere else in Switzerland and is one of Thun's most photographed features

## 5. The Stadtkirche and Scherzligen Church

### Stadtkirche Thun (City Church)
- Construction began in **1330** under the supervision of **Peter von Aarberg** and was completed in **1496** — a span of **166 years**
- The massive **octagonal bell tower** dates to approximately **1220**, predating the main church as part of a Romanesque predecessor building, and rises to about **50 metres**
- The tower hall features **paintings from around 1430** with three pointed-arch openings
- Originally a Catholic church dedicated to **Saints Maurice and Augustine**, it became **Reformed (Protestant)** after Bern adopted the **Reformation in 1528**
- The current **nave** was added in **1738** in Baroque style
- Interior highlights: **cross-vaulted ceilings** with biblical frescoes, a Renaissance limestone **choir screen** (Lettner) depicting the Last Supper, and a modern **organ by Metzler Orgelbau** (1981) with approximately **3,500 pipes**

### Scherzligen Church (Kirche Scherzligen)
- The **oldest church** in the Thunersee region, with origins around **500 AD**
- First documented mention: **760/761 AD**, when Bishop **Heddon of Strasbourg** donated it to the Ettenheim convent in Baden-Württemberg
- The Carolingian **tower** dates from the **9th century**, the Romanesque **nave** from the **10th century**, and the Gothic **choir** from the **1380s**

## 6. Lake Thun — Gateway to the Bernese Oberland

- **Thunersee** (Lake Thun) is the largest Swiss lake entirely within a single canton (Canton of Bern)
- Dimensions: **17.5 km** long, **3.5 km** wide, surface area **48.3 km²**, maximum depth **217 metres**, surface elevation **558 m** above sea level
- After the last glacial period, Lake Thun and **Lake Brienz** (Brienzersee) were once a single body of water called the **Wendelsee** ("Lake Wendel"); they separated after the **10th century** as sediment from the **Lütschine** river filled the gap at Interlaken
- The **Aare** river flows out of Lake Thun at its northern end, passing through Thun before continuing toward Bern
- Thun sits at the point where the Aare exits the lake, making it the historic **"Tor zum Berner Oberland"** (Gateway to the Bernese Oberland) — travellers heading to **Interlaken**, the **Jungfrau region**, and the high Alps all passed through Thun
- The **Blüemlisalp** (also spelled Blümlisalp) mountain massif (3,661 m) dominates the southern horizon across the lake

### The Blümlisalp Paddle Steamer
- The **DS Blümlisalp** is a historic **Belle Époque paddle steamer** built in **1906** by **Escher, Wyss & Co.** in Zurich at a cost of **375,000 Swiss francs**
- It was the largest and most elegant vessel on Lake Thun
- Decommissioned on **1 August 1971** and left moored in the Kander delta for nearly 20 years
- A cooperative called **"Vaporama"** undertook its restoration; the second maiden voyage took place on **22 May 1992**
- Today operated by **BLS Schifffahrt AG**, the restored steamer offers scheduled cruises from April to October with its lovingly maintained **open engine room** visible to passengers

## 7. Thun as a Military Town — The Waffenplatz

- The **Waffenplatz Thun** (Military Training Ground Thun) is the **oldest and largest military training ground** in Switzerland
- Established in **1819** following a decision by the **Tagsatzung** (Swiss Diet / legislative assembly) to unify and standardize military training
- The site was chosen for its central location and the suitability of the **Thuner Allmend** (Allmend = common grazing land) as an artillery training ground
- **Colonel Jost Göldlin von Tiefenau** served as the first director of the communal military school
- From **1828**, the school trained officers from the general staff, artillery, engineering, infantry, cavalry, and snipers
- The **Artillery Testing Facility** (Artillerieversuchsstation) was built in **1893**
- The **Panzermuseum Thun** (Tank Museum) preserves the military heritage, displaying Swiss and international armoured vehicles
- A notable visitor: **Louis-Napoléon Bonaparte** (the future Emperor Napoleon III) spent time in Thun during his Swiss exile in the **1830s**, training at the military school and even obtaining Swiss citizenship — Schloss Thun's museum includes exhibits about his connection to the town

## 8. Schadau Park and Schloss Schadau

- The **Schadau** estate was first mentioned in **1348**
- The current **Schloss Schadau** was built between **1846 and 1854** for the Neuchâtel banker **Abraham Denis Alfred de Rougemont**
- Designed by Swiss architect **James Victor Collin** and French architect **Pierre-Charles Dusillon** (trained at the École des Beaux-Arts in Paris)
- Architectural style: a historicist mix of **Tudor Gothic** and **Neo-Renaissance**, inspired by the **Loire Valley** castles of France — from the rich sandstone ornamentation to the exquisite interior decoration
- Since **1925**, the castle has belonged to the **city of Thun**
- The façade was restored by the city's own **quarrymen** (Steinmetze) between **1972 and 1992**, and a comprehensive renovation ran from **2017 to 2019**
- The castle is a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**
- Today it operates as a **boutique hotel** (Swiss Historic Hotels) with a restaurant
- The surrounding **Schadau Park** covers **3.6 hectares**, laid out as an **English landscape garden** and open to the public since **1860** — with direct views across the lake to the **Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau**

## 9. The Wocher Panorama — World's Oldest Surviving Cyclorama

- The **Thun-Panorama** houses the **oldest surviving cyclorama** (Rundbild / circular panoramic painting) in the world
- Painted by **Marquard Wocher** (1760–1830), a Basel-based artist who specialized in miniature paintings
- In the **summer of 1809**, Wocher climbed to a rooftop in the middle of Thun's old town and sketched a 360-degree view of the town and its alpine surroundings
- He then spent **five years** (1809–1814) translating the sketches into a monumental painting in his studio in Basel
- Dimensions: approximately **38 metres** in circumference and **7.5 metres** in height
- First displayed in **1814** in a purpose-built rotunda in Basel — cycloramas were the "first mass medium" of the early 19th century, drawing huge crowds
- After Wocher's death, the painting fell into obscurity and was only **rediscovered in the 1950s**
- Restored in **1958–1959** by conservator **Hans Fischer** with the support of the **Gottfried Keller Foundation** (Gottfried Keller-Stiftung)
- Installed in a new rotunda in **Schadau Park** and opened to the public in the **summer of 1961**
- A second comprehensive restoration was completed in the **summer of 2014** by **Michael Fischer**
- The painting offers a remarkably detailed snapshot of daily life in Thun around **1814** — figures, animals, buildings, market activity, and the surrounding alpine landscape

## 10. The Aare and the Wooden Sluice Bridges

- The **Aare** (Aare river) flows out of Lake Thun and splits the city into three parts: the northern bank, the southern bank, and the **Bälliz** island in the middle
- The **Bälliz** is a long, narrow island created by the river forking around it — today a lively **pedestrian shopping zone** with cobblestone streets and colourful buildings
- The **Untere Schleuse** (Lower Lock / Mühleschleuse) is a **wooden covered bridge** near Mühleplatz, connecting the Bälliz to the historic centre — first constructed in the **18th century** (documented dates include 1724 and 1788)
- Both the Untere Schleuse and the **Obere Schleuse** (Upper Lock / Scherzligschleuse, built **1818**) regulate the water level between lake and river, preventing flooding and facilitating navigation
- The rapid water flowing through the sluice gates creates **standing waves** that have made Thun a popular spot for **river surfing** (Flusssurfen) — a modern addition to the city's identity
- The **turquoise-green colour** of the Aare (fed by glacial meltwater) is one of Thun's signature visual features

## 11. Panoramic Views — Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau

- From Thun's elevated positions — the castle turrets, the Stadtkirche tower, the Schadau Park lakeshore — visitors enjoy one of the most celebrated **alpine panoramas** in Switzerland
- The three iconic peaks visible to the south:
  - **Eiger** (3,967 m) — "The Ogre," famous for its deadly **Nordwand** (North Face)
  - **Mönch** (4,107 m) — "The Monk," flanked by the Eiger and the Jungfrau
  - **Jungfrau** (4,158 m) — "The Maiden," forming the trio known as the **"White Giants"** (Weisse Riesen)
- These peaks form the dramatic southern wall of the **Bernese Oberland**, and their visibility from Thun — some **50 km** away — is a testament to the city's unique geographic position between the **Swiss Plateau** (Mittelland) and the **high Alps**
- The **Blüemlisalp** massif (3,661 m) and the **Niesen** (2,362 m, the "Swiss Pyramid") are also prominent in the panorama

## 12. Notable Historical Events and Facts

- **1798**: During Napoleon's **French invasion of Switzerland**, the old Bernese order was shattered. The Bernese Oberland was separated from Bern, forming the short-lived **Canton of Oberland** with **Thun as its capital** — a status it held during the Helvetic Republic until **1803**, when the old cantonal structure was largely restored
- **1819**: Establishment of the **Waffenplatz**, transforming Thun into Switzerland's premier military town
- **1830s**: **Louis-Napoléon Bonaparte** (future Napoleon III) trained at the Thun military school during his Swiss exile and obtained **Swiss citizenship**
- **1888**: Opening of the **museum** in Thun Castle's donjon, and connection to the **Swiss telephone network**
- **1906**: Launch of the **DS Blümlisalp** paddle steamer on Lake Thun
- **Population**: approximately **43,000** (as of mid-2020s), making Thun the **11th-largest city** in Switzerland and the second-largest in the Canton of Bern after the capital
- **Elevation**: approximately **565 metres** above sea level (city centre), rising to **1,170 m** on the eastern municipal boundary
- Post-WWII curiosity: Lake Thun contains **discarded military munitions** dumped after the Second World War — a fact that periodically prompts environmental debate

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The King of Renzenbühl** — A Bronze Age child discovers a glittering gold-inlaid axe in a chieftain's grave; the axe whispers stories of trade routes stretching to faraway Mycenae and the warm Mediterranean Sea
2. **Walking on rooftops** — A child gets lost in Thun's old town and discovers the Hochtrottoirs of the Obere Hauptgasse; climbing from street level to the elevated sidewalks, each level reveals a different century of the town's history
3. **The painter on the rooftop** — Marquard Wocher invites a curious child to help him sketch the 360-degree panorama of Thun from a precarious rooftop in 1809; together they capture every detail of daily life below
4. **The sleeping steamboat** — The abandoned Blümlisalp paddle steamer, rusting in the Kander delta, is awakened by a child's wish; the old ship takes one last ghostly voyage across moonlit Lake Thun before its restoration begins
5. **The knight who never came home** — Schloss Thun's Rittersaal echoes with the footsteps of a ghostly knight from the Zähringen era; a child must solve a riddle in the 14-metre-high hall before the knight can finally rest
6. **The Aare surfer and the sluice keeper** — A child discovers that the wooden sluice bridges hide a secret: at midnight the water spirit of the Aare teaches river surfing to anyone brave enough to ride the standing waves
7. **Gateway to the giants** — A child boards a boat at Thun and sails across the Thunersee; at each stop (Spiez, Oberhofen, Beatushöhlen) a different alpine legend comes to life, culminating in a meeting with the Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau personified as ancient guardians
8. **Napoleon's schoolboy summer** — Young Louis-Napoléon arrives at the Waffenplatz in the 1830s, homesick and uncertain; a local child befriends him and shows him Thun's secret corners, not knowing this shy boy will one day become an emperor
