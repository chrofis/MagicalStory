## 1. Founding — The Kyburgs, Reichenau, and the Field of Our Lady

- The earliest documented reference to the area is from **853/854**, when **Erichinga** (the predecessor settlement) appears in St. Gallen monastery records as a judicial assembly site
- In **888–889**, King **Arnulf** transferred the Erchingen estate to **Reichenau Abbey**, the powerful Benedictine monastery on Lake Constance
- Around **1230–1240**, the **Counts of Kyburg** built a fortified **castle tower** on the site — dendrochronological analysis of preserved wooden beams dates the tower to approximately **1230**
- A settlement grew up around the castle on land belonging to the Abbot of Reichenau, forming a planned community with a **rectangular street grid** still visible today
- First documented mention of the name **"Frauenfeld"**: **1246**, in Zurich records
- **1264**: When the Kyburg dynasty died out, the **Habsburgs** inherited control of Frauenfeld
- **1286**: Frauenfeld received formal **city rights**; the chapel of **St. Nicholas** was established the same year
- **1331**: The city adopted its first **constitution**

## 2. The Name — "Field of Our Lady"

- The name derives from the old German genitive singular of "Frau" — literally **"Feld der Frau"** ("Field of the Woman")
- The official city interpretation: the "Frau" refers to the **Virgin Mary**, patron saint of **Reichenau Abbey**, which owned the land on which the city was founded — meaning "on Our Lady's Field"
- An alternative theory connects the name to a **noblewoman** of the Kyburg family who may have owned the plot
- A third (less accepted) theory links it to the **Fraumünster** in Zurich, but the Reichenau connection is considered most plausible by historians
- The city's **coat of arms** depicts a **rearing lion led on a chain by a woman in a headscarf** — the lion likely represents the Habsburgs, while the woman symbolizes the Virgin Mary in secular form
- The red and white colors match those of **Reichenau Abbey's** heraldry

## 3. Schloss Frauenfeld — The Castle on the Murg

- The **massive tower** dates to around **1230** and was built from **glacial fieldstones** (Findlinge) — an unusual construction material that gives the tower its distinctive rough appearance
- The Kyburgs built the castle as an administrative center for the surrounding territory
- After the **1460 conquest** of Thurgau by the Swiss Confederation, the castle became the seat of the **eidgenössische Landvogt** (federal governor/bailiff) — from **1532** onward, the Landvogt resided permanently in the castle
- For over **250 years** (1532–1798), a rotating series of governors from the ruling cantons administered Thurgau from this castle
- Today the castle houses the **Historisches Museum Thurgau** — the cantonal history museum, with **free admission**
- The museum's exhibition focuses on the **turbulent 15th century**, when Thurgau's borders were formed after the Council of Constance (1414) and during the Reformation
- Notable artifacts include the **Kreuzlingen mitre**, the **Diessenhof Minneliedblatt** (the earliest paper document from the region), and a **knight's sword from around 1300**
- The museum spans three locations: the castle itself, the **Schaudepot St. Katharinental**, and the **Alte Zeughaus** (old arsenal)

## 4. Thurgau — A Subject Territory of the Confederation

- In **1460**, the **Old Swiss Confederacy** seized the Thurgau from the Habsburgs during the conflict with Duke Sigismund of Austria
- Thurgau became a **Gemeine Herrschaft** (common lordship / condominium) — a subject territory jointly administered by **seven Swiss cantons**: Zurich, Lucerne, Uri, Schwyz, Unterwalden, Zug, and Glarus
- Unlike full cantons, the people of Thurgau had **no representation** in the Confederation and were governed by outside bailiffs
- The **Landvogt** rotated every two years, each time appointed by a different ruling canton — governing from Frauenfeld Castle
- This arrangement lasted **over 300 years**, from 1460 until **1798**, when Napoleon's Helvetic Republic abolished the old system
- In **1803**, under Napoleon's **Act of Mediation**, Thurgau finally became a **full canton** of Switzerland, with Frauenfeld as its capital
- Since **1832**, the cantonal parliament (Grosser Rat) has split its sessions between **Frauenfeld** (April–September) and **Weinfelden** (October–March) to balance regional interests

## 5. The Tagsatzung — From Baden to Frauenfeld

- The **Tagsatzung** (Federal Diet) was the assembly of delegates from all Swiss cantons — the closest thing to a national parliament before 1848
- From **1421**, the Tagsatzung met regularly in **Baden** (AG), which served as neutral ground with its famous thermal baths
- As early as **1500/1515**, Frauenfeld occasionally hosted Tagsatzung sessions
- The turning point came in **1712**: after the **Second Villmergen War**, the Protestant cantons defeated the Catholic cantons. Catholic Baden had sided with the losers, and the victorious Protestants refused to meet there again
- From **1712**, the Tagsatzung alternated between **Frauenfeld and Baden**
- In **1742**, Frauenfeld was chosen as the **permanent seat** of the Tagsatzung
- The current **Rathaus** (town hall) was built in **1791–1793**, featuring a grand **Tagsatzungssaal** (Diet hall) specifically designed for the assembly
- The Tagsatzung met in Frauenfeld until **1798**, when the Helvetic Republic centralized governance
- This period (1712–1798) was Frauenfeld's **political golden age** — the town hosted Swiss diplomacy at the highest level

## 6. The Great Fires and the Rebuilt Old Town

- **1771**: A devastating fire destroyed much of the medieval city
- **1788**: A second catastrophic fire struck just 17 years later, destroying almost all remaining structures
- Only three buildings survived both fires: the **13th-century castle**, **one house**, and the **Evangelical church** (consecrated 1645), which contains rare **14th-century stained-glass windows**
- The **entire old town** visible today dates from the **late 18th century** — rebuilt in a uniform late Baroque / early Classicist style after the double devastation
- The **Rathaus** (1791–1793) was built during this reconstruction period
- The **Promenadestrasse** was laid out in **1813–1816** on the site of the demolished fortification walls and a filled-in city moat, at the initiative of industrialist **Bernhard Greuter**
- The entire old town of Frauenfeld is listed in the **Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites**

## 7. Kartause Ittingen — The Charterhouse Near Frauenfeld

- Founded in **1150** as a monastery for **Augustinian Canons Regular**
- In **1461**, the premises were sold to the **Carthusians**, who established a charterhouse (Kartause)
- **The Ittinger Sturm (1524)**: During the early Reformation, the Catholic bailiff in Frauenfeld arrested a Reformed pastor in Stein am Rhein. The enraged population — some **3,000 people** — redirected their fury at the monastery. On **July 18, 1524**, the crowd stormed Kartause Ittingen, plundering it for two days, destroying images and books, draining the fish ponds, and setting the complex ablaze
- Three alleged ringleaders were sentenced to **death** in Baden on September 28, 1524
- The prior and nearly all monks fled; reconstruction took approximately **30 years** — the monastery church was not reconsecrated until **1553**
- **1620**: A major donation from **Ludwig Pfyffer von Altishofen** funded six new monks' cells, beginning a period of renewed prosperity
- **1848**: The monastery was **dissolved** following political changes affecting religious houses across Switzerland
- **1867**: Purchased by the **Fehr family**, who operated it as a model farm until **1977**
- **1977–1983**: The charitable **Stiftung Kartause Ittingen** took over and comprehensively restored the complex
- Today: a major **cultural and conference center** housing the **Kunstmuseum Thurgau** (cantonal art museum), the **Ittinger Museum**, a seminar center, a hotel, a restaurant, a farm shop, and gardens — with over **850 years of history**

## 8. "Mostindien" — Thurgau, the Apple Kingdom

- Thurgau is nicknamed **"Mostindien"** — a play on "Most" (cider/apple juice) and "Ostindien" (East Indies), plus the canton's shape, which supposedly resembles India
- The nickname dates to the **19th century** and reflects Thurgau's dominance in Swiss apple production
- Thurgau produces approximately **one-third** of Switzerland's eating apples and nearly **half** of all juicing apples — roughly **140,000 tonnes** per year
- **Mosterei Möhl** in Arbon, founded around **1895**, has been pressing apples for five generations and is one of only two large-scale apple juice producers in Switzerland
- The company operates the **MoMö Museum** (Schweizer Mosterei- und Brennereimuseum) in Arbon, opened in **2018**, documenting the region's juicing and distilling traditions
- The gentle, rolling landscape of Thurgau — between the **Thur** and **Bodensee** (Lake Constance) — provides ideal conditions for fruit growing
- Traditional products include **Süssmost** (sweet cider), **Saurer Most** (hard cider), apple brandy, and numerous juice varieties

## 9. The Murg River and Industrialization

- The **Murg** is a **34.1 km** tributary of the Thur, flowing from Canton St. Gallen through Thurgau to join the Thur near Warth
- The river cuts through the center of Frauenfeld, and its **medieval water channels** (Murgkanäle) powered the city's early industry
- As early as **1349**, multiple **mills** were documented along the Murg in Frauenfeld
- In **1805**, **Bernhard Greuter** established textile manufacturing operations along the right bank of the Murg Canal — marking the beginning of Frauenfeld's industrialization
- Key industrial sites along the river: the **Walzmühle** (rolling mill) and **Eisenwerk** (ironworks), which pioneered metalworking in the region
- In **1916/1917**, **Ferdinand Sigg** relocated his aluminium goods factory from Biel to Frauenfeld, taking over the former rolling mill. **SIGG** produced pots, pans, and (from **1920**) the iconic **cylindrical aluminium bottle** still sold worldwide today
- After the devastating **1876 flood**, the Murg was channeled into a straightened bed, and the old floodplain was repurposed as a military training ground
- In **2015**, the **Murg-Auen-Park** opened: a pioneering urban revitalization project that freed the river from its rigid embankment, restoring a natural floodplain forest with the old factory canal preserved as an industrial monument
- The project won the **2017 Schulthess Garden Prize** from the Swiss Heritage Foundation

## 10. The Frauenfeld-Wil-Bahn

- A **1,000 mm metre-gauge** railway connecting Frauenfeld (Canton Thurgau) to Wil (Canton St. Gallen), following the **Murg valley**
- Line length: **17 km**
- Plans for the route date to the **early 1850s**; the line opened on **23 November 1887**
- **Electrified in 1921**
- Originally served primarily **freight transport** — milk, wood, and livestock — alongside passenger service
- The railway gained importance during major events like the **Eidgenössisches Schützenfest** (Federal Shooting Festival) in 1890 and the **Swiss Agricultural Exhibition** in 1903, both held in Frauenfeld
- In **2013**, five new **Stadler Rail ABe4/8 low-floor trains** entered service, replacing the old rolling stock
- In **2021**, the Frauenfeld-Wil-Bahn company merged into **Appenzeller Bahnen**; the line now operates as **S15** of the St. Gallen S-Bahn
- The railway connected Frauenfeld to the broader northeast Swiss rail network via the **Winterthur–Romanshorn line** (first connected in **1855**)

## 11. Notable People from Frauenfeld

- **Conrad Dasypodius** (c. 1530–1600): Mathematician and astronomer, professor at the University of Strasbourg, who designed the famous **Strasbourg astronomical clock** (built 1572–1574 by Isaac and Josias Habrecht) — one of the greatest mechanical masterworks of the Renaissance
- **Julius Maggi** (1846–1912): Born October 9, 1846 in Frauenfeld. Founded the **Maggi** company, invented the world's first **instant soup** (1886) and the famous **Maggi seasoning sauce**. The company merged with **Nestle** in 1947 and remains a global household name
- **Heinrich Häberlin** (1868–1947): Lawyer and politician from Frauenfeld, elected to the **Swiss Federal Council** in 1920. Served as head of the Justice and Police Department until 1934; was **President of the Swiss Confederation** in 1926 and 1931
- **Walter Rudolf Hess** (1881–1973): Born March 17, 1881 in Frauenfeld, son of a physics professor. Won the **1949 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine** for discovering how the **hypothalamus** coordinates the functions of internal organs — research conducted using pioneering brain stimulation techniques at the University of Zurich

## 12. The Bechtelistag — Frauenfeld's Medieval Feast Tradition

- **Bechtelistag** is Frauenfeld's unique local celebration, held on the **third Monday in January**
- The tradition traces back to shortly after the city's **13th-century founding**, when nobles formed a social drinking club called the **"Constables' Association"** (Gesellschaft der Constaffel)
- In **1430**, tradesmen created a rival association called **"Zum wilden Mann"** ("The Savage Man")
- In **1640**, the two associations merged into one, managing property, granting loans, and collecting interest
- Members met at the start of each year for **account presentations** followed by feasting
- Since **1811**, the feast has followed a set menu: **Salzisse** (a type of sausage), bread, and wine
- The formal banquet begins at **6 PM** in the town hall, with speeches and music, and ends around 11 PM
- Then comes the **Bechtelisnacht**: the town hall opens to all ages, locals don costumes, confetti fills the streets, and most restaurants stay open through the night
- The Bechtelistag is recognized as part of Switzerland's **living traditions** (Lebendige Traditionen der Schweiz)

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The woman on the field** — A child discovers a mysterious lady tending a meadow beside the castle; she reveals she is the guardian spirit of "Our Lady's Field" and tells the tale of how Frauenfeld got its name, leading the child through the coat of arms coming to life (the lion, the chain, the woman)
2. **The governor's lost key** — A Landvogt from one of the seven cantons loses the key to Frauenfeld Castle in 1650; a local child must solve riddles across the old town to find it before the next canton's governor arrives
3. **The night the monks fled** — Set during the Ittinger Sturm of 1524: a young novice at Kartause Ittingen must save the monastery's most precious book from the approaching mob, escaping through secret passages to Frauenfeld Castle
4. **The Tagsatzung comes to town** — After the 1712 Villmergen War, Frauenfeld suddenly becomes the most important town in Switzerland; a baker's child must prepare food for delegates from all 13 cantons, each with impossible demands
5. **Julius Maggi's magic soup** — Young Julius experiments in his father's mill to invent a soup that can feed everyone; ingredients come alive and must be convinced to work together, with a stirring spoon that reveals flavors from around the world
6. **The apple train of Mostindien** — A child rides the narrow-gauge Frauenfeld-Wil-Bahn through orchards where the apple trees whisper stories; each station along the Murg valley reveals a different chapter of Thurgau's history
7. **The Nobel brain** — Young Walter Rudolf Hess explores a fantastical version of the brain as a vast underground city beneath Frauenfeld, where different districts (the hypothalamus, the cortex) control different functions, and he must restore harmony between them
8. **The Bechtelisnacht costume mystery** — During the annual costume night, a child discovers that one of the costumed figures is a real medieval citizen from 1430, escaped from the "Zum wilden Mann" tavern through a crack in time; together they must return him before dawn
