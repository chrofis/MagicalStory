## 1. Origins — From Prehistoric Settlers to Langatun

- Archaeological evidence shows **settlements around 4000 BC** in the Langenthal area — a **Hallstatt-era necropolis** with twelve grave mounds was discovered at Unterhard, along with remnants of **two Roman villae**
- First documented mention: **861**, as **"marcha in Langatun"** in a St. Gallen charter, when a man named Theathard transferred his property to the **monastery of St. Gallen**
- The Old High German name **Langatun** likely combines a hydronym (*langa-*, referring to the Langete stream) with the Gaulish element **dunum** ("fort" or "hill fort")
- The settlement grew along the **Langete**, a tributary of the Murg, with farming estates scattered along the valley
- In the **12th century**, Langenthal belonged to the territory of the **lords of Langenstein**, a noble family whose castle complex controlled the Oberaargau region

## 2. Geography — The Heart of the Oberaargau

- Langenthal sits on the **Swiss Plateau** (Mittelland) in the **Oberaargau** district of the canton of Bern, roughly halfway between **Bern** (40 km southwest) and **Olten** (25 km northeast)
- The **Langete** river flows through the town center — historically both a lifeline and a threat, flooding the streets on average **twice per year** before modern flood control
- The streets of the inner town were designed with **elevated pavements** acting as canals, channelling floodwaters through the town during high water — a distinctive urban design visible to this day
- The surrounding landscape of the Oberaargau features rolling hills, lush fields, and the **Wässermatten** (water meadows) — an ingenious irrigation system dating back to the **13th century**, created by Cistercian monks of **St. Urban's Abbey**
- These Wässermatten are a **nationally significant cultural landscape**, where meadow irrigation has been practised for roughly a thousand years
- Population: approximately **16,000 residents**; Langenthal attained **city status in 1997** and serves as the economic and cultural center of the Oberaargau, with a catchment area of around **80,000 people**

## 3. Medieval Lords and St. Urban's Abbey

- In **1194**, the Freiherr of Langenstein founded **St. Urban's Abbey** (Kloster St. Urban), a Cistercian monastery, and endowed it with lands in Langenthal
- The abbey brought **agricultural innovation** to the region, most notably the construction of the elaborate **Wässermatten irrigation system** of ditches and channels throughout the Langete valley
- After the **extinction of the Langenstein family in 1212**, the abbey inherited additional lands
- The **lords of Grünenberg** rose to prominence: in **1279**, the abbey was forced to cede the low court and a fortified house in Langenthal to the **Freiherr of Grünenberg** as a fief
- The three castles — **Grünenberg, Schnabelburg, and Langenstein** — formed the center of noble power in the Oberaargau during the High Middle Ages (12th–15th centuries)
- A **church** in Langenthal is first recorded in **1197** as a private church of the lords of Grünenberg; in **1224**, church patronage was transferred to St. Urban's Abbey
- On **22 December 1392**, Bishop **Hermann von Klingenberg** of Konstanz consecrated the church altar

## 4. The Reformation and the Reformierte Kirche

- In **1528**, Bern adopted the **Reformation** following the Bern Disputation, and its entire territory — including Langenthal — became Reformed Protestant
- The **Abbot Sebastian Seemann** of St. Urban resisted for nearly a decade, creating a jurisdictional conflict between Catholic and Reformed authorities
- In **1538**, after negotiations at a council meeting in Willisau involving representatives from Bern and Lucerne, the **Kirchgemeinde Langenthal** (parish of Langenthal) was officially founded as an independent Reformed parish, incorporating **Schoren** and **Untersteckholz**
- The mother parish of **Thunstetten** symbolically gifted Langenthal the **middle church bell** for the new community
- The current church on the **Geissberg** was built in **1676–1677** by architect **Abraham Dünz** as a representative **Baroque hall church** for Reformed preaching
- In **1680**, a devastating village fire swept through Langenthal but spared the newly built church
- In **1768–1772**, the tower was improved and a new organ installed by the Geneva builder **Schärer**
- The church underwent comprehensive renovation in **1957–1958**, creating its current interior appearance

## 5. The Linen Trade — Langenthal's First Golden Age

- From **1640**, Langenthal and neighbouring Langnau became **centers of linen canvas production**, exporting to **France, Italy, Spain, and Portugal**
- In **1704**, Langenthal became the seat of the **Oberaargau Canvas Dealers' Guild** (Leinwandhändlergesellschaft), uniting weavers, shopkeepers, and traders to protect their commercial interests
- The linen trade brought enormous prosperity: by **1756** the town had grown to **189 households**, three taverns, various administrative buildings, and a new warehouse (built **1748**)
- The **Marktgasse** (market street) was paved in **1730** and became the commercial spine of the town; the row of houses on its eastern side was built directly **over the Langete river** during the 18th-century expansion of the "Innere Allmend" area
- In **1613**, a **Kaufhaus** (market hall) was built for the weekly markets; it was reconstructed between **1790 and 1808** by architect **Joseph Purtschert** as the striking late-Baroque **Choufhüsi**, featuring a distinctive clock tower
- The Choufhüsi contained a market hall, restaurant, cloth hall, guard room, meeting rooms, a prison, and a library — a true civic multipurpose building

## 6. Industrialisation — Textiles, Machines, and Porcelain

- Langenthal's location on the road from Bern to Zurich (completed **1760**) and on the **Swiss Central Railway** line (inaugurated **1857**) fuelled industrial development
- **1886**: Friedrich Baumann and Albert Brand founded **Brand & Baumann** in Langenthal for the production and sale of linen and half-linen goods, initially supplying **cheesemakers in the Emmental**; this company eventually split and evolved into **Création Baumann** (interior textiles) and **Lantal Textiles** (transportation textiles)
- **Lantal Textiles**: founded **1886** as a cheese-linen producer; shifted focus to **aircraft interiors in 1954** and became a global leader in transportation textiles, still headquartered in Langenthal
- **Création Baumann**: Friedrich Baumann's sons Willy and Fritz took over in **1930**; after WWII the enterprise split, with Fritz developing the **Création Baumann** brand — today a leading Swiss textile company for interior furnishing
- **1869**: **Jakob Ammann** founded a millwright operation in nearby Madiswil; in **1896**, his son **Ulrich Ammann** relocated the company to Langenthal to access the railway, transitioning into **road construction machinery** — today the **Ammann Group** employs around 3,000 people worldwide
- **1917**: **Arnold Bucher** began manufacturing the cleaning agent REX in Bützberg near Langenthal; in **1947** it became **Motorex**, now a global lubricant manufacturer still headquartered in Langenthal, run by the 3rd and 4th generation of the Bucher family

## 7. The Porzellanfabrik Langenthal — "The Porzi"

- Founded on **4 July 1906** by entrepreneur **Arnold Spychiger** with initial capital of **500,000 Swiss francs** among 47 shareholders — the first Swiss porcelain factory in over a century
- Switzerland had **no kaolin deposits and no skilled porcelain workers**: raw materials were imported from the **Karlsbad region** (now Czechia), and **35 of the initial 87 workers** were recruited from **Bohemia**
- The first vessels were fired on **17 January 1908**, shortly after a fire in 1908 required rebuilding
- **Rudolf Münger**, a Bernese painter, was appointed art director in **1909**; **Fernand Renfer** joined management in **1920**
- Technological milestones: electric **Blondel tunnel kiln** installed **1936–1937**; gas-fired kilns added **1964–1965**
- The factory's most successful years were **1950–1970**, peaking at **950 employees in 1964**
- For decades, the "Langenthal" name became **synonymous with Swiss crockery** — the distinctive tableware was found in virtually every Swiss household, hotel, and restaurant across all language regions
- Decline began in the **1980s** due to foreign competition; in **1988** the company merged with **Keramik Holding Laufen AG**
- By **1998**, porcelain production in Langenthal ceased entirely; only the painting workshops remained
- In **2003**, the Czech **G. Benedikt Group** purchased the company — today all "Langenthal" porcelain is manufactured in the **Czech Republic**, though the **Suisse Langenthal** brand continues for professional hotel and restaurant ware
- The former factory site, the **Porzi-Areal**, has been reimagined as a mixed-use urban quarter — one of the developments cited in Langenthal's **2019 Wakker Prize**

## 8. The Railway Junction

- The **Swiss Central Railway** (Schweizerische Centralbahn) reached Langenthal in **1857** on the **Olten–Bern line** (Mittellandlinie), connecting Langenthal to the main east-west axis across the Swiss Plateau
- The station became a true **junction** with the addition of the **Langenthal–Huttwil line** (BLS AG, standard gauge), connecting to the Emmental
- On **26 October 1907**, the metre-gauge **Langenthal–Oensingen** line opened (Langenthal-Jura-Bahn), linking Langenthal northward via Niederbipp
- On **6 October 1917**, the metre-gauge **Langenthal–Melchnau** line opened, serving the hinterland to the south
- These narrow-gauge lines are now operated by **Aare Seeland mobil** (asm)
- The railway transformed Langenthal from a market town into an **industrial hub**, enabling the growth of textile factories, the porcelain works, and machine manufacturers

## 9. SC Langenthal — Ice Hockey Tradition

- **SC Langenthal** was founded in **1946** and plays its home games at the **Eishalle Schoren**, built in **1961** and roofed in **1980**, with a capacity of approximately **4,300 spectators**
- The club achieved promotion to the **National League B** (now Swiss League, the second tier of Swiss professional ice hockey) in the **2001–02 season** after finishing first in Swiss Division 1
- SC Langenthal's golden era: the club won three **Swiss League championships** in **2012**, **2017**, and **2019**, becoming one of the most successful second-tier clubs in Swiss hockey
- Following the **2022–23 season**, the club voluntarily withdrew from professional hockey due to **financial constraints** and now competes in the **MyHockey League** (third tier)
- Ice hockey remains deeply embedded in Langenthal's identity — the Eishalle Schoren is a community gathering point and the club's history a source of local pride

## 10. Cultural Life — Kunsthaus, Museum, and Stadttheater

- The **Kunsthaus Langenthal** has been housed since **1992** in the historic **Choufhüsi** (the former 1790–1808 Kaufhaus); the conversion was initiated by the **Kunstverein Oberaargau**
- The Kunsthaus occupies the first and second floors and presents up to **eight temporary exhibitions per year** of contemporary art, with a focus on current Swiss art alongside international positions
- The institution is a member of ICOM, the Swiss Art Association, and the Association of Swiss Contemporary Art Institutions
- The **Museum Langenthal** (Bahnhofstrasse 11) presents the history and geology of Langenthal and the Oberaargau on its upper floor, with a focus on the **building ceramics of St. Urban** and regional development; admission is **free**
- The **Stadttheater Langenthal** was built **1914–1916** and opened in **December 1916**; it was extensively renovated in **2016–2017**, gaining a second hall ("Theater 49" with 200 seats) alongside the main **400-seat hall**, and has maintained a supra-regional cultural reputation for over a century

## 11. The Wakker Prize 2019 — Recognition of Urban Quality

- In **2019**, Langenthal received the prestigious **Wakker Prize** from the **Swiss Heritage Society** (Schweizer Heimatschutz), awarded annually to a Swiss municipality for exemplary architectural development
- The Heritage Society praised Langenthal for surviving the industrial crisis following the porcelain factory closure "thanks to a return to local quality and the courage to innovate"
- Specifically cited: the thoughtful treatment of **factory areas, worker settlements, villa complexes, and public buildings**; the redevelopment of the **Porzi-Areal**; investments in **public spaces** in the town center; renovations of **school buildings and the theater**
- Inventory lists of heritage structures were systematically catalogued as **anchor points for future development**
- The Heritage Society described Langenthal as "confidently saying that it wants to be a **lively, urban centre** of a larger rural region"

## 12. Notable People from Langenthal

- **Friedrich Born (1903–1963)**: ICRC delegate in Budapest during WWII who saved an estimated **11,000–15,000 Hungarian Jews** from deportation. Born distributed **Schutzbriefe** (protection letters), established **60 safe houses** for more than 7,000 children, and recruited some 3,000 Jews as ICRC workers, granting them protected status. He was posthumously honored as **Righteous Among the Nations** by Yad Vashem in **1987**
- **Heinz Holliger (born 1939)**: world-renowned **oboist, composer, and conductor**, widely considered the greatest oboist of the 20th century. Won international competitions in Geneva (1959) and Munich (1961); dozens of major composers — including Berio, Henze, Lutoslawski, and Stockhausen — wrote works specifically for him. Received the **Ernst von Siemens Music Prize** in **1991**
- **Pedro Lenz (born 1965)**: one of Switzerland's most prominent contemporary authors, known for novels and spoken-word performances in **Bernese German dialect**. His debut novel **Der Goalie bin ig** (2010) became a bestseller and was adapted into a **feature film in 2014**
- **Karl Jaberg (1877–1958)**: Swiss linguist and dialectologist, co-author of the monumental **Sprach- und Sachatlas Italiens und der Südschweiz** (Linguistic Atlas of Italy and Southern Switzerland)
- **Fritz Scheidegger (1930–1967)**: Swiss motorcycle racer who won two **Sidecar World Championships** (1965, 1966)

## 13. The Langatun Distillery — From Beer to Whisky

- In **1857**, master brewer **Jakob Baumberger** returned from Munich and founded a brewery and distillery in Langenthal, later acquiring the **Mumenthaler-Marti brewery** (established **1785**)
- During WWII, the Swiss government **banned grain distillation** for food security reasons, halting whisky production for decades
- The ban was repealed in **1999**, and fourth-generation descendant **Hans Baumberger III** revived the family tradition by creating **Langatun single malt whisky** — named after the town's original **861 Celtic name**
- In **2014**, the distillery relocated to the historic **Kornhaus in Aarwangen** and expanded; Langatun whisky has been marketed **worldwide since 2017** and has won multiple international awards

## 14. The Langete — Taming the River

- The **Langete** (also Langeten) gave Langenthal its name and shaped its history — but it was also the town's greatest natural threat
- Before modern flood control, the inner-city streets with their elevated pavements acted as **overflow channels**, flooding on average twice yearly
- In **1903**, an additional drainage system into the **Hardwald** forest was built, but its capacity was limited to **20 m3/s**
- The catastrophic **floods of 1975** caused property damage of at least **60 million Swiss francs** in the region
- In response, a flood protection association built a **7,531-metre relief tunnel** between **1988 and 1991**, capable of diverting up to **58 m3/s** from below Madiswil directly into the **Aare** at Bannwil
- Since the tunnel's completion in **1992**, the town center has been protected from the devastating floods that shaped its urban design for centuries

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The linen weavers' secret** — A child discovers a hidden loom in a Marktgasse cellar that weaves stories into cloth; each fabric tells the tale of a family from 1640s Langenthal, when linen sailed to Spain and Portugal
2. **The last plate from the Porzi** — A porcelain plate from the Langenthal factory in the 1960s refuses to break; a child follows its journey from Bohemian kaolin to a Swiss grandmother's Sunday table, discovering why 950 people once worked to make it
3. **The Langete's revenge** — Before the relief tunnel, the Langete floods the streets again; a child must navigate the canal-like elevated pavements in a tiny boat, racing to warn the town — discovering why the streets were built like rivers
4. **The monks' meadow magic** — A child meets a Cistercian monk from St. Urban's Abbey in the 13th century who is building the Wässermatten irrigation system; together they channel water through meadows and learn how patience shapes a landscape
5. **The Choufhüsi clock strikes thirteen** — The clock tower of the 1808 Kaufhaus strikes an impossible thirteenth hour, opening doors to the building's past lives: market hall, cloth hall, prison, library, and finally an art gallery full of living paintings
6. **The ice hockey ghost of Schoren** — On a foggy night at the Eishalle Schoren, a young player sees the ghost of a 2012 championship team skating on the ice; the ghost teaches the child that winning is not about the league you play in
7. **The train that connected everything** — In 1857, the first train arrives in Langenthal; a child rides the locomotive and sees how the railway junction transforms a quiet market town into a bustling industrial center, meeting weavers, porcelain painters, and machine builders along the way
8. **The bear on the Grünenberg** — A child explores the ruins of Grünenberg Castle and awakens a medieval knight who tells the tale of the three castles that once ruled the Oberaargau — and the monks who quietly outlasted them all
