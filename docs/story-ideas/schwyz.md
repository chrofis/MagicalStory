## 1. The Town That Named a Nation

- **Schwyz** is a small town of about **16,000 inhabitants** (2023) in central Switzerland, the capital of the canton of the same name — and the place that gave the entire country its name
- The toponym was first attested in **972** as Old High German **"Suittes"**; modern scholars derive it from *swedan* ("to burn"), referring to forest cleared by burning
- As early as **1320**, the name of the canton was applied to the whole of the Swiss Confederation, because Schwyz had taken a leadership role in the alliance
- It was only in **1803**, however, that **"Schweiz"** (derived from "Schwyz") became the **official name** of the country
- The German name *Schweiz*, the French *Suisse*, and the Italian *Svizzera* all trace back to this one small Alpine town
- The town sits at **516 meters** above sea level, at the foot of the dramatic Mythen peaks, between **Lake Lucerne** to the west and **Lake Lauerz** to the east

## 2. The Federal Charter of 1291 — Switzerland's Birth Certificate

- In early **August 1291**, the valley communities of **Uri, Schwyz, and Nidwalden** (Unterwalden) signed a pact of mutual defense — the **Bundesbrief** (Federal Charter)
- The charter was likely drafted shortly after the death of **King Rudolf I of Habsburg** on **15 July 1291**, to ensure legal certainty and self-governance during the power vacuum
- The first two paragraphs commit all three communities to **joint defense**; the remainder concerns judicial matters: arbitration of conflicts, rejection of foreign judges, the death penalty for murderers, and exile for arsonists
- This document is Switzerland's **oldest constitutional document**, though it was only recognized as the founding charter in the late **19th century** — previously, **1307** (from Aegidius Tschudi's chronicle) was the accepted founding date
- The **Federal Council** organized jubilee celebrations in **1891** (600th anniversary) and in **1899** declared **1 August** the Swiss National Day
- The original parchment document belongs to the **Canton of Schwyz's State Archives** and is permanently displayed at the **Bundesbriefmuseum** in Schwyz

## 3. The Bundesbriefmuseum — Shrine of the Nation

- The **Museum of the Swiss Charters of Confederation** (Bundesbriefmuseum) was built in **1936** at **Bahnhofstrasse 20, Schwyz**, specifically to house the Federal Charter
- Designed by architect **Joseph Beeler**, the building was conceived during the 1930s era of **"Geistige Landesverteidigung"** (Spiritual Defence) against fascism — the charter was displayed in a showcase called the **"Altar des Vaterlandes"** (Altar of the Fatherland)
- The museum also houses other original alliance documents, historical banners, and a collection of medieval pacts
- Notable artworks in the building include Heinrich Danioth's **"Fundamentum"** relief and Hans Brandenberger's **"Wehrbereitschaft"** sculpture
- After the social upheavals of the **1960s**, a **1979 renovation** repositioned the charter as one document among many, rather than a singular national relic
- Since **2014**, the permanent exhibition critically examines the **myth** surrounding the charter alongside the actual history of the Old Swiss Confederacy
- Open Tuesday to Sunday, 10:00–17:00; admission CHF 5.00 (children free)

## 4. The Rütli Oath — The Legendary Founding

- The **Rütli Oath** (Rütlischwur) is the legendary oath taken by representatives of Uri, Schwyz, and Unterwalden — traditionally dated to **1291** (or **1307** in older sources)
- The oath was supposedly sworn on the **Rütli meadow**, a small Alpine meadow above **Lake Uri** (the southern arm of Lake Lucerne), near Seelisberg
- The three legendary oath-takers: **Walter Fürst** (Uri), **Werner Stauffacher** (Schwyz), and **Arnold von Melchtal** (Unterwalden)
- **Werner Stauffacher** came from a prominent Schwyz family; members of the Stauffacher family held the office of **Landammann** (chief magistrate) of Schwyz throughout the 13th and 14th centuries
- His unnamed wife — later called **Gertrud** in Friedrich Schiller's **1804 play *Wilhelm Tell*** — is celebrated as the one who encouraged her husband to resist Habsburg tyranny after a bailiff threatened them for building a stone house
- A statue of the **Stauffacherin** stands in the chamber of the **National Council** in the Federal Palace in Bern, honored as the **"bearer of the idea"** of Swiss independence
- The Rütli Oath first appears in the **White Book of Sarnen** (written c. 1470, based on a source from c. 1420) — about a century after the purported event

## 5. The Battle of Morgarten (1315) — The First Great Victory

- On **15 November 1315**, troops of Schwyz, supported by allies from Uri and Unterwalden, ambushed a Habsburg army on the shores of **Lake Ägeri**, at the **Morgarten pass**
- **Duke Leopold I of Austria** led a force of heavily armored knights (estimates range from 2,000 to 9,000; likely around 2,000–3,000) marching from Zug to punish the Confederates
- The Swiss force of approximately **1,300 men from Schwyz**, 200 from Uri, and others from Unterwalden lay in wait above the narrow path between the lake and the steep **Figlenfluh** cliff
- The Confederates rolled **boulders and tree trunks** down the slope, then attacked on foot with **halberds** (a combination of spear, axe, and hook used to pull armored knights from their horses)
- The Austrians lost over **1,500 men** killed or drowned; Swiss losses were minimal — reportedly fewer than a dozen
- The victory proved that **dismounted commoners** could defeat armored knights — a revolutionary military lesson
- Within a month, on **9 December 1315**, the Confederates renewed and strengthened their alliance at **Brunnen** (the **Pact of Brunnen**), initiating a period of growth for the Confederation
- The prestige gained by **Schwyz** in this battle was so great that the entire Confederation came to be known by forms of the canton's name

## 6. The Banner of Schwyz — Origin of the Swiss Flag

- In **1240**, Emperor **Friedrich II** presented the **blood-red war banner** of the Holy Roman Empire to the warriors of Schwyz — a symbol of imperial immediacy (sovereignty within the Empire)
- For over a century, the banner was **plain red** — red symbolizing sovereignty and the power over life and death
- Over time, a **white cross** was added to the corner of Schwyz's red flag; the first solid evidence of the white cross on the Schwyz war banner dates from **1475**
- At the **Battle of Laupen (1339)**, Swiss soldiers from the combined Confederation sewed **white crosses** onto their clothing to distinguish themselves from Habsburg troops who wore the red cross of Saint George
- The Swiss flag — a **white cross on a red field** — was officially adopted in the **1848 federal constitution** and legally established on **12 December 1889**
- Switzerland and the Vatican are the only two sovereign states with **square national flags**

## 7. The Hauptplatz, Rathaus, and Parish Church

- The great fire of **20 April 1642** destroyed **47 buildings** in the center of Schwyz, including the Rathaus and the church
- The town was rebuilt with a new, larger **Hauptplatz** (main square), major roads radiating outward from it — the layout that defines the town center today
- The **Rathaus** (Town Hall) was built **1642–1645** and is now a Swiss heritage site of national significance
- In **1891**, for the **600th anniversary** of the Confederation, Munich-based historical painter **Ferdinand Wagner** was commissioned to paint scenes from Swiss history on the north and west facades
- The **west facade** depicts the Rütli Oath (1291), the Battle of Morgarten, and the Pact of Brunnen (1315); the **north facade** shows the Stauffacherin, the handover of the Schwyz Freedom Charter (1240), Saint Martin, and allegorical figures of justice and freedom
- The murals were painted using **Keim mineral paints** (patented 1878) and completed on **18 July 1891**, just in time for the celebrations
- The **Parish Church of St. Martin** stands slightly elevated on a natural terrace on the village square — a **late baroque hall church** (the seventh church on the site), built **1769–1774**

## 8. The Ital Reding-Hofstatt and Haus Bethlehem

- The **Ital Reding-Hofstatt** is a complex of three historic buildings, among the most valuable building groups in Central Switzerland
- The main mansion was built in **1609** on behalf of **Ital Reding**, a provincial governor (Landammann) of Schwyz
- The building blends **Gothic, Renaissance, and Baroque** elements — artfully painted windows, a saddleback roof with dormers, richly paneled state rooms with wooden ceilings, and a small **Baroque garden** with two shade pavilions
- Next to it stands **Haus Bethlehem** (House of Bethlehem), built in **1287** — four years before the Federal Charter — and confirmed by **dendrochronological dating** (tree-ring analysis) as **Europe's oldest surviving wooden dwelling**
- The house is a two-story **blockhouse** assembled entirely from interlocking wooden beams joined with wooden nails (metal was too expensive), with low ceilings (1.90 m), tiny windows (40 x 15 cm), and no chimney — smoke escaped through the windows and roof shingles
- The complex survived the 1642 fire that destroyed most of the village — one of the reasons for its extraordinary preservation
- Today the estate operates as a **museum** with exhibitions on 20th-century history and its connection to the Reding family

## 9. Einsiedeln Abbey — Europe's Great Pilgrimage Site

- **Einsiedeln Abbey**, about 25 km northeast of Schwyz, is Switzerland's largest pilgrimage site and one of the most important in Europe
- Founded by the Benedictine hermit **Saint Meinrad** around **835 AD**; Meinrad lived alone in the Dark Forest for 26 years, keeping two ravens as companions, before being murdered by two bandits in **861**
- A small Benedictine community was established at his hermitage, and the site soon became a pilgrimage destination
- The **Black Madonna** — a 15th-century wooden statue of the Virgin Mary — is housed in the **Chapel of Grace** (Gnadenkapelle) beneath a painted dome, and is the spiritual heart of the pilgrimage
- The current monastery is an imposing **Baroque edifice** with four inner courtyards, built in the **18th century**
- Between **150,000 and 200,000** Catholic pilgrims visit annually; with general tourists, the total exceeds **800,000** visitors per year
- In **1314**, men from Schwyz raided Einsiedeln Abbey in a dispute over grazing rights — this raid was the direct trigger for the **Habsburg invasion** that led to the **Battle of Morgarten**

## 10. The Mythen — Schwyz's Twin Peaks

- The **Mythen** are two striking rock pyramids that dominate the skyline east of Schwyz: the **Grosser Mythen** (1,898 m) and the **Kleiner Mythen** (1,811 m), plus the secondary peak **Haggenspitz** (1,761 m)
- The name derives from Latin **"meta"** (cone, pyramid) — not from the German word for "myth"
- The **Grosser Mythen** is a popular hiking destination, classified as **T3** (challenging mountain hike) — relatively steep but rewarded with a panoramic view of the Glarus and Uri Alps, Lake Lucerne, and the surrounding region
- The **Kleiner Mythen** is more dangerous and not suitable for normal hikers — it requires scrambling
- The twin peaks are the **landmark and symbol** of the town of Schwyz and appear on countless postcards, paintings, and logos associated with the region

## 11. The Goldau Bergsturz (1806) — Switzerland's Worst Landslide

- On **2 September 1806**, the south face of the **Rossberg** mountain collapsed, sending **40 million cubic meters** of rock thundering into the valley below in just **three minutes**
- The landslide buried the villages of **Goldau** and parts of **Lauerz**, destroying **111 homes, 220 stables, and 4 churches**
- **457 people** and **323 head of cattle** perished — making it the **largest historical natural disaster in Switzerland**
- An offshoot of the rock avalanche reached **Lake Lauerz** (Lauerzersee), 2.5 km away, triggering a **tsunami** on the lake that claimed further lives in the villages of Lauerz and Seewen
- Lake Lauerz lost **one-seventh of its surface area** in a single moment
- The catastrophe caused a sensation across Europe and marked a turning point: it was one of the first geological disasters to be studied with a **naturalistic, scientific approach** rather than attributed to divine punishment
- On the small island of **Schwanau** in Lake Lauerz, a medieval castle (ruins dating to c. 1200–1240) was completely destroyed by the resulting tidal wave; the ruins were restored and given a viewing platform in **2009**

## 12. Traditional Culture and Conservative Catholic Identity

- Schwyz is one of Switzerland's most **deeply Catholic and conservative** cantons — the faith has shaped its identity for centuries
- In **1531**, the Landsgemeinde passed a resolution specifically affirming the canton's special relationship with the **Catholic faith and the Pope**; Schwyz fought in the **Battle of Kappel (1531)**, in which Reformation leader **Huldrych Zwingli** was killed
- The **Landsgemeinde** (open-air assembly of all citizens) was the supreme political institution in Schwyz for centuries — meeting on the last Sunday of April to elect officials, pass laws, and reaffirm traditional rights; abolished at the cantonal level by the **1848 constitution** but still held in some districts
- In **1845**, Schwyz joined the **Sonderbund** — a separate alliance of Catholic cantons to protect cantonal sovereignty and Catholic religion against liberal federal centralization
- The town is also the home of the **Swiss Army knife**: **Karl Elsener** opened a knife forge in nearby **Ibach** in **1884**, founding what would become **Victorinox**; today over **25,000 Swiss Army knives** are produced daily in the Canton of Schwyz
- The region is renowned for its **cherry orchards** — cherry blossoms and cherry-based specialties (Kirsch brandy, cherry pastries) are a signature of Schwyz's agricultural heritage

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The parchment that built a country** — A child touches the Federal Charter in the Bundesbriefmuseum and is transported to August 1291; must help the three delegates from Uri, Schwyz, and Unterwalden find their way through a storm to sign the pact before dawn
2. **The red banner of Schwyz** — A young flag-bearer in 1315 must carry Schwyz's blood-red banner through the chaos of the Battle of Morgarten; the banner keeps the soldiers brave, and the child learns that courage isn't about being unafraid
3. **The house that remembers** — Haus Bethlehem (1287), Europe's oldest wooden house, whispers stories at night; a child discovers each beam remembers a different century of Schwyz's history, from before the Confederation to the present
4. **Gertrud Stauffacher and the stone house** — Retelling of the Stauffacherin legend: when a Habsburg bailiff threatens her family, a girl's mother inspires the whole valley to stand up for their rights — leading to the Rütli Oath
5. **The mountain that fell** — September 1806: a child and their dog sense the Rossberg is about to collapse and must warn the village of Goldau before 40 million cubic meters of rock thunder down in three minutes
6. **The Black Madonna's ravens** — A child follows two mysterious ravens (Saint Meinrad's companions) from Schwyz to Einsiedeln Abbey, where the Black Madonna grants a wish — but only to someone who asks for something for others, not themselves
7. **Climbing the Grosser Mythen** — A nervous child conquers fears one switchback at a time on the steep path up the Great Mythen; at the summit, the entire story of Switzerland is visible below — Lake Lucerne, the Rütli meadow, the path to Morgarten
8. **The painted walls of the Rathaus** — At midnight, the murals on the Schwyz Town Hall come alive: the Rütli oath-takers step off the wall, the Morgarten soldiers march, and the Stauffacherin speaks — the child must return them to the facade before sunrise
