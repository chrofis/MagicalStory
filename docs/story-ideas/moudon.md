## 1. Celtic Origins and Roman Minnodunum

- Founded by the **Celts**, the settlement was originally called **Minnodunum** (also written Minnidunum) — a name of Celtic origin with the suffix *-dunum* meaning "fortress" or "hill fort"
- During Roman times, Minnodunum was a **vicus** (secondary agglomeration) in the territory of the **Helvetii**, strategically located on the road from **Viviscus** (modern Vevey) to **Aventicum** (modern Avenches), the Helvetian capital
- The settlement grew considerably thanks to its position along the route connecting Avenches to the **Great St Bernard Pass**, the major Alpine crossing to Italy
- Archaeological evidence includes a **Roman necropolis** upon which parts of the medieval town were later built; the **Hôtel de Ville** (Town Hall, built 1835–1842) houses a **Roman votive altar** discovered locally
- The name Moudon evolved through the medieval period: documented as **Meldun** (around 1100, 1154, 1180), **Moudon** (1161), and **Meldunum** or **Mildunum** (1167)
- The vicus declined with the fall of the Roman Empire, but the site remained inhabited through the early medieval period under Frankish rule, two Burgundian kingdoms, and the Counts of Geneva

## 2. Geography — The Broye Valley and the Two Rivers

- Moudon lies at the **confluence of the Broye and the Mérine** rivers in the heart of the **Broye valley** (vallée de la Broye), in the canton of Vaud at an elevation of approximately **515 meters**
- The **Broye** is a 68 km river flowing through the cantons of Fribourg and Vaud; it changes direction near Moudon, turning northeast toward Lake Morat
- The town sits at the intersection of two historically important routes: one from **Italy to Burgundy** and the other between **Geneva and Bern** — this crossroads position made it a natural market and administrative centre
- The **Ville-Haute** (upper town) occupies a rocky ridge between the two rivers, while the **Ville-Basse** (lower town) extends along the Broye downstream
- The municipality covers **15.69 km²**, with 46.7% agricultural land and 35.1% forest; the highest point is the wooded hill of **Les Bourlayes** at 850 m
- Since 2006, Moudon has been part of the **district de la Broye-Vully**, formed by merging the former districts of Avenches, Moudon, Oron, and Payerne; population approximately **6,100**

## 3. The Savoy Conquest and Medieval Capital

- Around **1130**, the Count of Geneva fortified the hilltop settlement; around **1190**, the **Zähringen** dynasty further strengthened the defences, leaving the **Tour de la Broye** (Broye Tower), a 12th-century remnant still standing today
- In **1207**, **Thomas I of Savoy** conquered Moudon, though it nominally remained under the sovereignty of the Bishop of Lausanne — this marked the beginning of Savoyard expansion into the Pays de Vaud
- The **Traité de Burier** (Treaty of Burier, **1219**) with the Zähringen heirs confirmed Savoy's possession, marking the start of over three centuries of Savoyard rule in Vaud
- **Peter II of Savoy** ("Le Petit Charlemagne") established Moudon as the **seat of the bailliage de Vaud** (bailiwick of Vaud) around **1260**, making it the administrative and judicial capital of the entire region
- The town received **franchises** (municipal liberties) in **1285**, which promoted commerce, granted market rights, and secured legal protections for the bourgeois (citizens)
- The Counts of Savoy styled themselves **"Seigneurs de Moudon et Romont"** (Lords of Moudon and Romont), underscoring the town's pre-eminence
- Moudon developed rapidly during the 13th century, becoming the most important town in the Pays de Vaud — a status it held until the Bernese conquest in 1536
- The town's medieval prosperity is visible in its substantial stone buildings — unlike many Swiss towns that relied on timber construction, Moudon built in **local molasse sandstone** (grès de molasse), the same warm-toned stone that characterises Bern and Fribourg

## 4. The Église Saint-Étienne — Cathedral of the Broye

- The **Église réformée Saint-Étienne** (Reformed Church of Saint Stephen) was first mentioned in **1134–1143**, though it may have been built earlier
- Construction of the present Gothic structure began around **1281**, with the apse enclosure dating to that year; a bell tower was added in **1420**
- Major enlargements and renovations took place between **1495 and 1502**, giving the church its current form
- It is the **largest parish church in the canton of Vaud** and one of the finest examples of **Rayonnant Gothic** architecture in French-speaking Switzerland — locals nickname it the **"Cathédrale de la Broye"** (Cathedral of the Broye)
- The interior features **three naves** and a **polygonal choir** with **ribbed vaults** (voûtes d'ogives), rich painted decoration, and **16th-century choir stalls** (stalles) of exceptional quality
- The church houses the **oldest functioning organ in the canton**, dating from **1764**
- The fortification of the Ville-Basse around **1280** was contemporaneous with the church's construction, indicating that the Savoyard authorities conceived the lower town and its monumental church as a single urban project
- Listed as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance** (bien culturel d'importance nationale); the church also serves as a stop on the **Via Jacobi**, the Swiss section of the pilgrimage route to Santiago de Compostela

## 5. The Medieval Old Town — Ville-Haute and Ville-Basse

- The **Ville-Haute** (upper town) is the oldest quarter, occupying the rocky ridge between the Broye and Mérine rivers; its most distinctive feature is the way the **high rear walls of houses rise as extensions of the sandstone cliffs** (falaises de molasse) on which they stand — creating a dramatic, fortress-like silhouette
- The **Rue du Château** (Castle Street) is the oldest street, lined with arcaded houses and dominated by the **grande tour** (great tower), the surviving remnant of the medieval lord's fortress
- The **Ville-Basse** (lower town) developed during the early Savoyard period around two important hospices: the **Hospice de Saint-Jean-de-Jérusalem** (Knights of St John) and the **Hospice de Saint-Bernard**, flanking the **Pont Saint-Éloi** (Saint Eloi Bridge)
- The Ville-Basse was fortified around **1280**, simultaneously with the construction of Saint-Étienne
- The layout shares characteristics with other **Zähringen foundations** like Bern and Fribourg: a ridge-top main street with houses on both sides, rear walls dropping steeply to the river below — though Moudon predates the Zähringen involvement as a settlement
- The Ville-Haute is classified as a **site d'importance nationale** (townscape of national importance) in the Swiss ISOS inventory, and the medieval character of the ensemble has been compared to the old towns of Bern and Fribourg
- Heritage sites of national significance in Moudon include: the **Ancien Grenier** (old granary), the **Maison d'Arnay**, the **Maison Loys de Villardin**, the **Maison de Rochefort**, the **Maison Tacheron**, the **Fontaine de Moïse** (Moses Fountain), and the **Hôtel de Ville**
- The **Château de Carrouge**, built on medieval foundations in the old town and renovated in the 18th–19th centuries, served from 1869 as the **École cantonale pour enfants sourds** (Cantonal School for Deaf Children); the **Château de Billens**, a 17th–18th century stately residence outside the city walls, was the birthplace of Eugène Burnand

## 6. The Château de Rochefort and the Musée du Vieux-Moudon

- The **Château de Rochefort** stands at the highest point of the Ville-Haute, dominating the town; originally **14th-century merchants' stone houses** (maisons de marchands), they were transformed into a **seigneurial residence** in **1595**
- Around **1730**, a **municipal belfry** (beffroi) was added to the staircase tower, giving the building its definitive and picturesque silhouette — the belfry clock has structured daily life in Moudon ever since
- The **Musée du Vieux-Moudon** was founded in **1910** by the **Association du Vieux-Moudon**, at the initiative of **Auguste Burnand**, a Moudon native passionate about local history who worked in Bern as a federal translator
- In **1950**, the museum moved into the Château de Rochefort; after a thorough restoration between **1989 and 2000**, the collections were reinstalled with new museography
- The museum presents the everyday life of old Moudon: a **family kitchen**, a **notary's office**, **bourgeois salons**, agricultural tools, craftsmen's workshops, and historic pub signs — offering a vivid portrait of centuries of small-town life in the Broye

## 7. The États de Vaud and the Maison des États

- As the capital of the bailliage de Vaud, Moudon served as the regular meeting place for the **États de Vaud** (Estates of Vaud) — the assembly of nobles, clergy, and town representatives who advised the Savoyard bailli on taxation, justice, and military matters
- The **Maison des États du Pays de Vaud**, a small **late-Gothic building** (maison gothique tardive) on the Rue du Château, was built around **1500** and is traditionally identified as the hall where these assemblies gathered
- The building is listed as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**
- The États de Vaud were a rare example of representative assembly in the region, and their tradition helped shape a civic consciousness in the Pays de Vaud that persisted through centuries of Bernese rule and ultimately contributed to the creation of the canton of Vaud in **1803**
- The judicial structure under Savoy placed the **bailli de Vaud** at Moudon as the highest regional authority; local courts of first instance could be appealed to the bailiwick court, and major cases were brought before the Estates — making Moudon the judicial heart of the region

## 8. Bernese Rule and the Reformation (1536–1798)

- In **1476**, during the **Burgundian Wars**, Swiss Confederate forces captured and plundered Moudon after the defeats of Charles the Bold at Grandson and Morat — but the **Peace of Fribourg** restored Savoyard rule the same year
- In **January 1536**, Bernese troops conquered the entire Pays de Vaud from Savoy, bringing the **Reformation** (Réforme) by force; the Catholic mass was abolished and the Église Saint-Étienne became a Protestant church
- There was **little native enthusiasm** for the Reformation in Vaud; a Bernese ordinance of **December 1536** had to be issued to compel compliance with the new faith
- Under Bernese rule, Moudon lost its status as regional capital; the **bailli** (bailiff) was transferred to nearby **Lucens**, and Moudon became the head of a smaller administrative district
- The town nevertheless retained its judicial importance and its tradition of civic assemblies; a register of **mandats souverains** (sovereign mandates) kept by the **curial** (clerk) of Moudon survives for the period **1766–1796**
- Bernese rule also brought **Huguenot refugees** after the revocation of the **Edict of Nantes** (1685), who contributed skilled craftsmanship and intellectual life to the region
- In **1798**, the Vaudois enthusiastically welcomed French Revolutionary troops and proclaimed the **République lémanique** (Lemanic Republic); Moudon became part of the new **canton de Vaud** in **1803** under the Act of Mediation

## 9. The Fontaine de Moïse and Renaissance Heritage

- The **Fontaine de Moïse** (Moses Fountain), located in the Ville-Haute, has an iron basin ring dating from **1557**; the column and statue of Moses were sculpted by **Laurent Perroud** in **1559** — a period when Renaissance fountain sculpture flourished across Swiss towns
- The current **octagonal basin** dates from **1679** and follows the classic Swiss Renaissance model; it features two twisted water spouts emerging from **lion mouths**, cast by **Jacques Fardeau**, a founder from Fribourg
- Perroud also created Justice fountains in **Neuchâtel**, **Soleure**, and Moudon itself — placing Moudon's fountain in a broader tradition of civic sculpture symbolising good governance
- The fountain is listed as a **heritage site of national significance** and remains one of the most photographed landmarks in the old town

## 10. Les Brandons — Moudon's Traditional Carnival

- **Les Brandons de Moudon** is the town's traditional carnival, held on the first weekend of Lent — one of the major carnivals of western Switzerland (Suisse romande)
- The name comes from the German word **"Brand"** (ember/firebrand); a Bernese ordinance of **1597** mentions **"feux de mars à la façon des payens"** (March fires in the pagan manner) called "brandons" or "chafferoux"
- Originally, communities gathered around **bonfires** fuelled by wood scraps and hedge debris collected by young people; torch-lit processions, masked participants, and communal banquets were central to the celebration — the fires were believed to hold **propitiatory virtues** ensuring agricultural prosperity and announcing spring's arrival
- By the early 20th century, open bonfires had disappeared and Les Brandons became primarily a **children's celebration** with lamp-lit parades and confetti battles
- The carnival nearly died out in the **1960s**, but was revived in the **1980s** by a new municipal committee; the pivotal **1982 edition** introduced Swiss-German **Guggenmusiks** (brass carnival bands), transforming the festival
- Since **2000**, each edition has an annual theme; today Les Brandons features three days of fancy-dress processions, satirical floats, a satirical newspaper, competitions, and the symbolic **burning of the Winter Man** (Bonhomme Hiver)
- Classified as an event of **intangible cultural heritage** of the canton of Vaud

## 11. Notable People — From Chocolate to Royal Kitchens

- **Abraham-Louis-Rodolphe Ducros** (1748–1810): born in Moudon, one of the foremost watercolourists of his era; lived in Rome from 1777 to 1793, where he produced monumental watercolours of Roman landscapes and collaborated with engraver Giovanni Volpato on a celebrated series of 24 hand-coloured views of Rome; a key figure in the pre-Romantic movement
- **Daniel Peter** (1836–1919): born in Moudon on 9 March 1836, he invented **milk chocolate** in **1875** by combining chocolate with condensed milk developed by his neighbour **Henri Nestlé** in Vevey; the brand **Gala Peter** (from Greek *gala*, "milk") launched in 1887; in 1879 Peter and Nestlé formed a partnership that grew into the Nestlé company
- **Eugène Burnand** (1850–1921): born at the **Château de Billens** in Moudon, a celebrated naturalist painter and contemporary of Ferdinand Hodler and Albert Anker; his best-known work, *Les Disciples Pierre et Jean courant au sépulcre* (1898), hangs in the **Musée d'Orsay** in Paris; the **Musée Eugène Burnand** in Moudon (established 1960, renovated 1991) is the only museum in Romandy dedicated to a single painter
- **Gabriel Tschumi** (1882–1957): born in Moudon, emigrated to Britain in 1899 at age 16; became **Master Chef** to three British monarchs — Queen Victoria, Edward VII, and George V; his memoirs, *Royal Chef* (1954), recount decades of life in the royal kitchens
- **Gustave Roud** (1897–1976): the most beloved poet of **Suisse romande** and co-founder (with Charles-Ferdinand Ramuz) of modern French-language Swiss literature; lived from age 11 at a farm in **Carrouge** near Moudon until his death; his poetry celebrates the landscapes of the **Haut-Jorat**; also a prolific photographer with some 13,000 images; his most famous work is *Air de la solitude*
- **Philippe Jaccottet** (1925–2021): born in Moudon, one of the most acclaimed French-language poets and translators of the 20th century; mentored by Gustave Roud, who introduced him to Hölderlin, Novalis, and Rilke

## 12. Moudon Today — Heritage and Living Traditions

- Despite its small size, Moudon possesses an extraordinary concentration of heritage: **two museums** (Musée du Vieux-Moudon, Musée Eugène Burnand), **one of the canton's finest Gothic churches**, and a medieval townscape of national importance
- The town lies on the **Via Jacobi**, the Swiss section of the pilgrimage route to Santiago de Compostela, drawing hikers and pilgrims through its old streets
- The **Arsenal** (1774–1775), originally a grain store (grenier), and the **Malta Hospital** (Hôpital de Malte, 1556) on the Rue Mauborget are further witnesses to the town's layered history
- Les Brandons and the annual **Festival de musiques populaires** (Popular Music Festival, with nearly 100 concerts over a weekend) keep Moudon's tradition as a gathering place alive
- The **Château de Carrouge** site, once a school for deaf children, represents ongoing questions about adaptive reuse of historic buildings in small towns
- The challenge of preserving such a rich heritage while sustaining a small rural community is an ongoing theme — Moudon was recognised as one of the **plus beaux villages de Suisse** (most beautiful villages in Switzerland)
- Moudon's two millennia of continuous habitation — from Celtic oppidum to Roman vicus to Savoyard capital to quiet Vaudois market town — make it one of the most historically layered small towns in Switzerland

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The chocolate secret of the Broye** — Young Daniel Peter can't get his milk-and-chocolate mixture to work; a magical journey down the Broye river to Vevey, where he meets neighbour Henri Nestlé and discovers the secret of condensed milk
2. **The two towns on the hill** — A child living in the Ville-Basse discovers a hidden stairway that leads to the Ville-Haute after dark; each medieval house on the Rue du Château reveals a different century of Moudon's past, from Roman legionnaires to Savoyard knights
3. **The belfry clock strikes thirteen** — When the Château de Rochefort's belfry chimes an impossible thirteenth stroke, a child is transported to the year 1260, when Peter of Savoy arrives to make Moudon the capital of Vaud
4. **The painter and the firebrands** — During Les Brandons, a child follows a mysterious masked figure through the carnival; each float comes alive with scenes painted by Eugène Burnand, and the Bonhomme Hiver must be convinced that spring is truly coming
5. **Gustave Roud's lost photograph** — A child finds a forgotten photograph by Gustave Roud showing a landscape of the Haut-Jorat that no longer exists; following the poet's footsteps through meadows and farms near Carrouge, the child discovers what the land remembers
6. **The royal chef's first recipe** — Young Gabriel Tschumi, about to leave Moudon for England, must prepare one perfect dish from his mother's kitchen to prove he is ready to cook for a queen; ingredients come from Moudon's market stalls, and each vendor tells a story
7. **The pilgrims' fountain** — A child sitting by the Fontaine de Moïse meets a medieval pilgrim walking the Via Jacobi to Santiago de Compostela; together they explore the Église Saint-Étienne, where the ancient organ plays a melody that guides lost travellers home
8. **The Estates assemble** — In the Maison des États, a child accidentally sits in the seat reserved for the representative of the Pays de Vaud; transported to a 15th-century assembly, the child must speak for the townspeople and convince the Savoyard lords to keep the town's franchises
