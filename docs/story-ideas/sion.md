## 1. Prehistoric Roots — One of the Oldest Inhabited Places in Europe

- The alluvial fan of the river **Sionne** and the rocky hills of Valère and Tourbillon have been settled nearly continuously for over **7,000 years**
- The oldest trace of human settlement: **c. 6200 BC** (late Mesolithic)
- Around **5800 BC**, early Neolithic farmers from the Mediterranean established themselves in Sion
- By **4500 BC** (middle Neolithic), settlements increased sharply; farming and grazing spread throughout the Rhône valley
- The dead were buried in **Chablandes-type stone cists** (coffres en dalles) with engraved **anthropomorphic stelae** (stèles anthropomorphes) — upright stones carved with human features and copper daggers
- The **Le Petit-Chasseur** necropolis (nécropole du Petit-Chasseur), discovered in **1961**, is one of Switzerland's most internationally significant Neolithic-to-Bronze-Age sites: **three dolmens** dated 2900–2200 BC, roughly **30 carved stelae**, and nine cist graves, excavated systematically from 1961 to 1992
- These stelae — depicting armed warriors with triangular-bladed daggers — are unique in Europe and connect Sion to the **Bell Beaker culture** that spread across the continent

## 2. Roman Sedunum — Gateway to the Alps

- At the end of the 1st century BC, Sion was the capital of the **Seduni** (Sédunes), one of four Celtic tribes in the Valais
- The Romans conquered the Seduni in the **10s BC**; the settlement became known as **Sedunum**
- Grave stelae attest to the presence of **duumviri** (civic magistrates), **flamines** (priests of the imperial cult), a Roman knight, and a former consul
- Under the church of Saint-Théodule, a large **Roman bath complex** (thermes romains) was partially excavated
- Near La Sitterie and Sous-le-Scex, portions of several **villae suburbanae** (country villas) were found
- In **377 AD**, the provincial governor **Pontius Asclepiodotus** converted to Christianity in Sedunum — one of the earliest documented conversions in the region
- The Roman name "Sedunum" evolved through the centuries into the French **Sion** and the German **Sitten**

## 3. The Bishopric — Oldest Diocese in Switzerland

- The **Diocese of Sion** (diocèse de Sion) is the oldest in Switzerland and one of the oldest north of the Alps
- The first authenticated bishop: **St. Theodorus** (d. 391), who attended the **Council of Aquileia in 381** — recorded as "Theodorus Episcopus Octodorensis"
- The see was originally at **Octodurus** (Martigny); in **589**, Bishop **St. Heliodorus** transferred it to Sion because Octodurus was repeatedly flooded by the Rhône and the Drance
- St. Theodorus also founded the **Abbey of Saint-Maurice** — the oldest monastery north of the Alps — in honour of the martyred soldiers of the Theban Legion
- The **Cathédrale Notre-Dame-du-Glarier** preserves its magnificent **12th-century Romanesque bell tower** (clocher roman) with Lombard arches, while the main Gothic structure dates from the late 15th century

## 4. The Prince-Bishops — Spiritual and Temporal Lords

- In **999**, King **Rudolph III of Upper Burgundy** granted the **Countship of Valais** (comté du Valais) to Bishop **Hugo**, uniting spiritual and secular powers in one office
- This made the Prince-Bishop of Sion (prince-évêque de Sion) the most powerful ruler in the upper Rhône valley
- The bishops were simultaneously **prefects of the Holy Roman Empire** — exercising justice, collecting taxes, and commanding military forces
- Their temporal authority extended from the upper Valais to the confluence of the Trient and the Rhône, while their spiritual jurisdiction covered the entire valley above Lake Geneva
- The bishops faced constant opposition from petty feudal nobles, each perched in fortified castles on rocky heights, seeking to evade episcopal supremacy
- The temporal power gradually eroded: the **Seven Tithings** (Sept Dizains / Sieben Zenden) — the democratic communes of Upper Valais — began minting their own coins in the early 17th century
- Bishop **Hildebrand Jost** formally ceded most secular authority in **1630**
- The last Prince-Bishop, **Joseph Anton Blatter**, went into exile in 1798 when Napoleon incorporated Valais into the Helvetic Republic

## 5. The Twin Hills — Valère and Tourbillon

### Valère (la colline de Valère)
- The **Basilique de Valère** (Basilique Notre-Dame de Valère) was built between the **12th and 13th centuries** in Romano-Gothic style, perched on the eastern hill
- The Venerable Chapter of the Cathedral (Vénérable Chapitre de la Cathédrale) — a college of **secular canons** first mentioned in **1049** — resided within the fortified hilltop enceinte and held the privilege of nominating future bishops
- The basilica's belfry originally functioned as a **donjon** (defensive keep)
- Raised to the rank of **minor basilica** by Pope **John Paul II** in **1987**
- Today houses the **Musée d'Histoire du Valais** (Valais History Museum)
- The hill is a **protected natural area** featuring rare Mediterranean flora

### Tourbillon (le château de Tourbillon)
- Built at the turn of the **13th/14th century** by Bishop **Boniface de Challant** of Savoy, who made it his principal residence
- Served as the bishop's main dwelling until **1373**, when it was downgraded to a summer residence after the purchase of another castle
- **1417**: Burned down during the **Raron affair** (affaire de Rarogne) — a war between the people of Sion and the powerful Raron family
- Rebuilt by Bishop **Guillaume III de Rarogne**, who consecrated the **Chapelle Saint-Georges** in **1447**, adorning it with frescoes
- **1788**: Definitively destroyed in the Great Fire of Sion; never rebuilt
- The crenellated ruins remain one of Switzerland's most dramatic hilltop silhouettes

## 6. The World's Oldest Playable Organ — Valère

- The organ of Valère, dating to **c. 1431–1437**, is the **oldest playable organ in the world** (le plus vieil orgue jouable du monde)
- Its builder remains **unknown** — no records survive identifying the craftsman
- The painted wing panels are attributed to **Peter Maggenberg** (c. 1380–1463), the same artist who painted the basilica's murals, which allowed experts to date the organ
- Some **180 original pipes** from the 1430s still exist — unique in the world
- The organ was modified in the **18th century** for Baroque music but otherwise remains essentially unchanged
- **Survival**: It escaped the 1788 fire because the wind direction blew flames away from Valère; it survived the Reformation (which destroyed organs in Geneva and Zurich) partly because the difficult access to the hilltop protected it
- In **2005**, a major restoration was completed; in **2022**, the organ was retuned to **mean-tone temperament** (tempérament mésotonique), its historically accurate tuning
- The **Festival International de l'Orgue Ancien de Valère**, founded in **1969** by restorer **Maurice Wenger**, draws organists from across the globe every summer

## 7. The Great Fire of 1788 (le grand incendie de 1788)

- On **24 May 1788**, a devastating fire broke out just before noon on a **market day**
- According to local accounts, it started when a housewife left a meal unattended on the fire
- Conditions were catastrophic: the spring had been dry, houses were tightly packed, streets were narrow, and firefighting was rudimentary
- Strong **Foehn winds** (vents de foehn) spread the flames through the old town and up to **Tourbillon**, destroying the castle definitively
- Ashes carried by the wind reportedly reached the **Sierre** region, kilometres away
- Nearly **300 residents** were left homeless, but miraculously **no one was killed**
- The basilica of Valère was spared because the wind direction blew the fire away from its hill
- The bishop wished to rebuild Tourbillon, but political turmoil on the eve of the French Revolution prevented reconstruction; the stones were scavenged for building material before the ruins were stabilized in the 19th century

## 8. The Mazze — Medieval Instrument of Popular Revolt

- The **mazze** (also spelled Matze; from Italian *mazza*, "club" or "mace") was a unique Valaisan instrument of direct democracy — a carved **tree trunk with a human head** raised as a symbol of collective defiance
- First documented use: **1414**, when it was raised against the noble family of Raron
- When the mazze was erected in a public square, an orator would address the figure and invite it to "designate" the tyrant through nods or gestures; citizens would then drive **nails** into the trunk as a pledge of solidarity
- The most famous episode: in **1510**, **Georg Supersaxo** raised the mazze against Prince-Bishop **Matthäus Schiner** (c. 1465–1522), his former ally turned rival
- Schiner — who became a **cardinal** in 1511 and served as papal minister of war — excommunicated Supersaxo and had him arrested for high treason
- The feud brought Valais close to civil war; the **Peace of the Patriots** (1517) prevented Schiner's return to Valais until his death in Rome in 1522
- The mazze tradition represents one of the earliest forms of **organised popular protest** in Alpine Europe — a precursor to democratic movements

## 9. Viticulture — The Oldest Vineyards in Switzerland

- The Valais has the **oldest wine tradition** in Switzerland, with archaeological evidence of a ceramic wine bottle from a **2nd-century BC Celtic tomb** near Sembrancher
- Many vineyards on the steep south-facing terraces above the Rhône date to **Roman times**
- The **1313 Anniviers Register** provides the first mention of specific grape varieties in Valais: **Humagne** and **Rèze** — both still cultivated today as heritage grapes
- The oldest documented vineyard **dry stone walls** (murs en pierres sèches) appear in the **1341** accounts of the Saillon Chatellenie
- **Fendant** (le Fendant du Valais) — the iconic Valais white wine made from the **Chasselas** grape — was massively introduced from **1848** onward, when François-Eugène Masson established the **Domaine du Mont d'Or** on the southwest slope of the Montorge near Sion
- The arrival of the **railroad in Sion in 1860** sparked a commercial boom; vineyard area grew from **1,140 ha** (1877) to **3,520 ha** (1919) partly due to the phylloxera crisis elsewhere
- In **1966**, the Swiss Federal Court granted **exclusive use** of the appellation "Fendant" to Chasselas wine produced in Valais
- Today the terraced vineyards surrounding Sion — dramatically draped over sunny slopes between the twin castles — are an inseparable part of the city's identity

## 10. The Simplon Pass and Napoleon

- The **Col du Simplon** (Simplonpass, 2,005 m) connects **Brig** in the Valais with **Domodossola** in Piedmont, Italy
- A locally used passage existed for centuries; **Kaspar Jodok von Stockalper**, the "King of the Simplon" (Roi du Simplon), improved the route in **1635** and erected monumental buildings along it
- Between **1801 and 1805**, Napoleon ordered the construction of a true carriage road through the pass, built by engineer **Nicolas Céard** — primarily to transport artillery between the Rhône valley and Italy
- The resulting 63-kilometre route with **eight large bridges** and **seven rock galleries** became the **first true highway over the Alps** (première route carrossable à travers les Alpes)
- Napoleon also brought political upheaval: in **1798**, Valais was incorporated into the **République helvétique**; from 1802 to 1810, the Valais briefly became the independent **République du Valais** before Napoleon annexed it as the French **Département du Simplon** (1810–1813)
- Sion, as capital of the Valais, was at the centre of these transformations — its prince-bishops permanently lost temporal power during this era

## 11. The Maison Supersaxo and the Tour des Sorciers

### Maison Supersaxo
- Built in **1505** by **Georges Supersaxo** (Georg Auf der Flüe), the powerful and controversial Valaisan leader who feuded with Bishop Schiner
- The festival hall (salle d'apparat) on the second floor features an extraordinary **sculpted and painted ceiling** by **Jacobinus Malacrida** — considered one of the finest Late Gothic ceilings in Switzerland
- At its centre: a large rosette with a sculptured **Nativity scene** (Nativité), surrounded by smaller rosettes and Supersaxo's coat of arms at the four corners
- An **Italian-style staircase** (escalier à l'italienne) decorated with heraldic murals connects the floors
- Today used by the **municipality of Sion** as meeting rooms and offices — open to visitors

### Tour des Sorciers (Hexenturm / Witches' Tower)
- One of only **two surviving towers** from Sion's medieval fortification walls, dating to the **13th century**
- Originally a defensive tower with three floors and a surrounding moat
- Transformed into a **prison** in the 16th century during the witch trials (procès de sorcellerie) that swept through Valais from the 15th to 18th centuries
- The tower's grim name reflects Sion's role as a focal point for **inquisitorial tribunals** — it witnessed countless interrogations and acts of torture

## 12. Modern Sion — Climate, Olympics, and Identity

### Geography and Climate
- Capital of the canton of **Valais** (Wallis), population approximately **35,000** (Sédunois/es)
- Elevation: **512 metres**, on the right bank of the Rhône
- One of the **driest and sunniest cities** in Switzerland: only **82 days** of rain per year, **603 mm** of precipitation, and over **2,000 hours of sunshine** annually
- The sheltered position deep within the Rhône valley creates a near-continental microclimate ideal for viticulture

### The Olympic Bids
- Sion bid **three times** for the Winter Olympics: **1976**, **2002**, and **2006**
- The **2006 bid** was considered the favourite — backed by Switzerland's Olympic pedigree and proximity to IOC headquarters in Lausanne
- On **19 June 1999**, at the 109th IOC Session in Seoul, **Turin defeated Sion 53–36** in a surprise upset
- The loss was partly attributed to backlash against Swiss IOC member **Marc Hodler**, who had exposed the Salt Lake City bribery scandal shortly before the vote

### The Bisses (les bisses / Suonen)
- The **bisses** are ancient open-air irrigation channels unique to the Valais, carrying glacial meltwater to fields and pastures on the dry valley slopes
- Built from the **13th century** onward (first written mention: mid-13th century), though many likely predate written records
- At their peak in the early 20th century: over **200 bisses** totalling roughly **1,800 kilometres**
- A 2018 survey catalogued **188 bisses** over one kilometre still in existence
- Maintained by **consortages** — cooperative associations where members contributed labour proportional to their landholdings
- Today the bisses serve as popular **hiking trails** (sentiers des bisses) and are recognised as cultural heritage of the Valais

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The organ that remembers** — A child touches the keys of the Valère organ; each note opens a vision of a different century — the canons chanting in 1437, the fire of 1788, the Reformation that nearly destroyed all organs, the restorer who saved it in 1969
2. **The two castles who stopped talking** — Valère and Tourbillon, personified as sisters on twin hills, once watched over Sion together; after the 1788 fire destroyed Tourbillon, they haven't spoken — a child must mend their quarrel before a new storm arrives
3. **The stelae of Petit-Chasseur** — A child discovers a carved stone warrior in a Sion park; following its dagger-shaped carvings, they travel 5,000 years back to a Neolithic village on the Rhône and must help raise a dolmen before the solstice
4. **The nails of the mazze** — A child finds a carved wooden head at a market stall; when they drive a nail in, they're transported to 1510 and witness the showdown between Supersaxo and Schiner — and must carry the mazze through the streets of Sion
5. **The bisse that ran dry** — A mountain village above Sion has no water; a child must follow the ancient bisse path along cliff faces and through tunnels, repairing the channel and learning the names of every family in the consortage who once maintained it
6. **Napoleon's road** — A child's toy cannon rolls down a hill and lands on the old Simplon road; following it, they meet the engineers building the first highway over the Alps and must help a mule team navigate the seven rock galleries before winter closes the pass
7. **The Foehn wind and the forgotten fire** — On a hot, dry market day, a child smells smoke; reliving 24 May 1788, they race through narrow streets warning neighbours, rescuing a cat from Tourbillon, and watching in awe as the wind spares Valère
8. **The secret ceiling of Georges Supersaxo** — In the Maison Supersaxo, a Nativity figure in the rosette ceiling winks; the child is pulled into 1505, where the sculptor Jacobinus Malacrida needs help finishing the ceiling before Supersaxo's grand banquet
