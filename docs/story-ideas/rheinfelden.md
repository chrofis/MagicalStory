## 1. Founding — The Oldest Zahringer City

- **Around 1130**: Duke **Konrad von Zahringen** elevated Rheinfelden to town status, granting it urban rights and market privileges — making it **the oldest Zahringer city in present-day Switzerland** and the oldest city in the Aargau
- Around **1150**, Konrad also had the **first bridge built across the Rhine** between Konstanz and Strasbourg, establishing Rheinfelden as a vital river crossing
- The Zahringer dynasty died out in **1218** with the death of Berchtold V (the founder of Bern)
- Rheinfelden then became a **free imperial city** in **1225**, enjoying direct status under the Holy Roman Emperor
- The town celebrated its **850th anniversary in 1980**; ten Zahringer cities (including Bern, Fribourg, Murten, and Thun) donated commemorative plaques to the Rheinfelden town hall
- Like other Zahringer foundations, Rheinfelden features the characteristic **broad main street (Marktgasse)** serving as a market axis — a hallmark of Zahringer urban planning

## 2. Rudolf von Rheinfelden — The Anti-King

- **Rudolf von Rheinfelden** (c. 1025–1080) was Duke of Swabia from 1057 and resided at **Castle Stein** on the island (Inseli) in the Rhine
- He was brother-in-law to Emperor Henry IV, but turned against him during the **Investiture Controversy** — the great power struggle between Emperor and Pope over the right to appoint bishops
- On **15 March 1077**, Rudolf was elected **anti-king** at Forchheim — the **first anti-king in the history of the Holy Roman Empire**
- He promised to respect the elective character of the monarchy and to renounce royal control over church appointments
- Rudolf was crowned at Mainz but had to flee when the townspeople, loyal to Henry IV, rioted
- He was mortally wounded at the **Battle on the Elster** on **15 October 1080**, dying of his injuries
- His daughter **Agnes von Rheinfelden** (1065–1111) married Duke Bertold II of Zahringen and founded the **monastery of St. Peter in the Black Forest**, which became the burial site of the Zahringen dynasty — thus connecting Rheinfelden's history directly to the family that would later refound the city

## 3. The Twin City — Divided by Napoleon

- For nearly 700 years, Rheinfelden was **one single city** spanning both banks of the Rhine
- In **1330**, King Ludwig the Bavarian pledged Rheinfelden to the **Habsburgs**, beginning almost **500 years of Austrian rule** as part of **Vorderosterreich** (Further Austria)
- Rheinfelden was one of the four **Waldstadte** (riverside fortress towns) alongside Sackingen, Waldshut, and Laufenburg
- In **1802**, Napoleon fixed the border on the Rhine: the south bank became Swiss, the north bank became German
- On **20 February 1802**, Rheinfelden was made district capital of the new Canton of Fricktal; in August it joined the Helvetic Republic
- Since **19 March 1803**, Rheinfelden has belonged to the **Canton of Aargau**
- The German side became part of the Grand Duchy of Baden (today **Rheinfelden, Baden-Wurttemberg**)
- Despite the separation, the two Rheinfeldens remain **socially and economically intertwined** — residents cross the bridge daily for work, shopping, and leisure

## 4. The Rhine Bridge and the Inseli

- The **first bridge** at Rheinfelden dates to around **1150** — one of the oldest Rhine crossings between Konstanz and Strasbourg
- A **wooden covered bridge** served for centuries until it burned down on **12 June 1897**
- The current **stone bridge** was inaugurated in **1912**, designed by the renowned Swiss engineer **Robert Maillart** together with architects Joss & Klauser; it stretches **147 meters** in length
- A divided **border stone from 1810**, bearing the crests of Baden and Canton Aargau, marks the international boundary on the bridge
- Since 2008, the old bridge has been largely **car-free**, open mainly to pedestrians, cyclists, and buses
- The **Inseli** (little island) is a shell limestone rock in the Rhine that divides the bridge into two angled sections
- In the **10th century**, the Counts of Rheinfelden built **Castle Stein** on the island — residence of Rudolf von Rheinfelden and later the Zahringers and Habsburgs
- The castle was **razed to the ground in 1446** during the war between Austria and Basel
- An Austrian **artillery fortress** occupied the site from 1685 to 1745, when the French destroyed it
- Today the Inseli is an **idyllic public park** with views of the old town; it features the **St. Anna's Hole**, one of the deepest points in the Rhine, and memorial artworks by Christina Frey depicting Rheinfelden's rulers
- The Inseli was renovated in 2024–2025 at a cost of around **CHF 950,000**

## 5. The Medieval Old Town — Towers, Gates, and Walls

- Rheinfelden's old town preserves well-maintained remnants of its **medieval ring wall** with several notable towers

### Obertorturm (Upper Gate Tower)
- First documented in **1256** as the "Glunkins Gate" — the southeastern entrance to the old town
- Considered **the most important defensive tower** of Rheinfelden's fortifications
- Its clock has a famous quirk: it strikes **seven minutes early** — a deliberate design to warn farmers working outside the walls so they had time to return before the gates closed for the night
- This practice gave rise to the expression **"Torschlusspanik"** (gate-closing panic) — still used in German today to mean anxiety about missing a deadline

### Messerturm (Knife Tower)
- Stands at the **northeastern end** of the old town, directly on the Rhine
- Features a distinctive **triangular ground plan** jutting into the riverbed — its shape "cuts" the floodwaters
- Served as an **interrogation and torture chamber**; an upper room was accessible only from the battlements
- Local legend: a trapdoor led to a knife-lined shaft dropping victims into the Rhine — city guides add that the Rhine salmon downstream grew notably well-fed as a result

### Other Landmarks
- **Storchennestturm** (Stork's Nest Tower) — part of the northern wall
- **Kupferturm** (Copper Tower) — along the western fortification
- **Marktgasse** — the broad main street housing the town hall, the widest street in the old town
- **Albrechtsplatz** — a square named for the Habsburg connection
- The old town is today a **pedestrian zone**, vehicles only permitted on Marktgasse from 7:00–14:00

## 6. Churches and the Knights Hospitaller

### Stadtkirche St. Martin
- Dates from the **early 11th century** — around 1,000 years old
- Called the **"Schatzkammer des Fricktals"** (treasury of the Fricktal) for its extraordinary art spanning Romanesque through Baroque and Rococo periods
- In **December 1147**, the crusade preacher **Bernard of Clairvaux** stopped twice in Rheinfelden and reportedly performed miraculous healings in the church vestibule
- Served as a **canons' monastery from 1228 to 1870**
- In 1873, the congregation rejected the papal infallibility dogma and became **Christian Catholic** (Old Catholic)
- Houses a rare **17th-century Lenten cloth (Hungertuch)**, rediscovered by chance during cleaning work in the late 1990s

### Johanniterkapelle (Knights Hospitaller Chapel)
- Knight **Berchtold von Rheinfelden**, a servant of the Zahringen dynasty, founded the **Kommende** (commandery) in **1212** — originally outside the city walls as a hospital for pilgrims and the poor
- Around **1450**, the Kommende was attacked and destroyed by noble warriors disguised as pilgrims
- Commander **Johann Loesel** rebuilt it within the town walls; the chapel was completed **1456–1458** by the city's master builder Herman
- The chapel walls feature **frescoes from around 1500** in secco technique, including a Last Judgment attributed to a student of **Martin Schongauer**
- Dissolved by the canton in **1806**; used as a storage room for decades until its cultural significance was recognized in the late 19th century
- Since **1996**, the chapel has served as an exhibition and cultural space

## 7. Feldschlosschen — The Castle Brewery

- Founded on **8 February 1876** as "Kollektivgesellschaft Wuthrich & Roniger zum Feldschlosschen" by two partners:
  - **Theophil Roniger** — a brewer from nearby Magden who trained in German brewing cities
  - **Mathias Wuthrich** — son of a wealthy farmer from Olsberg who provided financial backing
- First-year production: **3,600 hectolitres**
- The brewery's distinctive **castle-style buildings** gave it its name: Feldschlosschen literally means "little castle in the field"
- **1882**: First expansion, doubling capacity from 15,000 to 30,000 hectolitres
- **1887**: New castle-style buildings raised capacity to 45,000 hectolitres
- **1890**: Converted to a public limited company with Fritz Brunner as first chairman
- **1892**: Acquired its first mechanical ice maker, ending dependence on natural ice (previously transported by cart and rail from Klontal in Canton Glarus)
- **1898**: Produced **100,000 hectolitres**, becoming **Switzerland's largest brewery** — a position it still holds
- **1909**: New **Art Nouveau brewhouse** completed, featuring the iconic twelve-copper configuration
- **1913**: Theophil Roniger died; his son **Adolf Roniger** took over and managed the company for 60 years
- **1974**: First year exceeding **1 million hectolitres** of annual output
- **1996**: Merged with **Hurlimann** (Zurich) to form Feldschlosschen-Hurlimann-Holding
- **2000**: Became part of the **Carlsberg Group** (Copenhagen, Denmark)
- The brewery grounds in Rheinfelden remain its headquarters and are open to visitors through the **Brauwelt** experience center

## 8. White Gold — The Salt Town

- In **1844**, salt deposits were discovered beneath Rheinfelden, transforming the town's fortunes
- Two salt works were established: **Rheinfelden** and **Riburg** (nearby)
- **1846**: The **Hotel Schutzen** opened as the first brine bath facility, launching Rheinfelden's spa tradition
- **1847**: Heinrich Dressler founded what became the Grand Hotel des Salines au Parc (today Parkresort Rheinfelden)
- **1864**: An **"Armensolebad"** (poor people's brine bath) was established, democratizing access to the healing waters
- **1874**: The saltworks of Riburg, Rheinfelden, and Kaiseraugst joined forces to compete with Schweizerhalle
- **1896**: A sanatorium was built outside the old town — predecessor of today's **Reha Rheinfelden** clinic
- **1909**: The three works merged to found **Swiss Saltworks on the Rhine**
- **1925**: Direct **brine pipelines** connected the salt works to the bathing hotels, eliminating manual transport
- The Rheinfelden saltworks eventually closed in favor of **Saline Riburg**, which remains one of Switzerland's three active salt extraction sites (alongside Schweizerhalle and Bex)
- Today, the **Rheinfelder Natursole** is a protected brand — one of Europe's strongest natural brines, pumped from 200 meters depth and piped to the **sole uno wellness center** where visitors float in 36 degrees C water with 12% salt content
- Rheinfelden holds official status as a **certified Wellness Destination** (since 2005) and brands itself **SaltCity**

## 9. The Hydroelectric Pioneer

- In **1898**, the **Rheinfelden hydroelectric power plant** was commissioned on the Rhine — the **first large-scale hydroelectric plant in Europe**
- Output: **12,500 kilowatts** (17,000 horsepower), later increased to 25,000 kW
- The engineers chose **three-phase alternating current at 50 cycles per second** — a decision that proved historic: the 50 Hz frequency became the standard adopted across most of the world
- The plant pioneered **cross-border power distribution** between Germany and Switzerland, from which the interconnected electrical network of continental Europe evolved
- Two original three-phase generators operated continuously for over a century
- The old plant was decommissioned in **2011** and replaced by a modern facility
- An **exhibition pavilion** at the power station tells the story of this engineering milestone, which received an **IEEE Milestone** designation

## 10. Fasnacht — Frau Fasnacht and the Rhine

- Rheinfelden's carnival tradition centers on **Frau Fasnacht** (Mrs. Carnival), a figure that embodies the spirit of the festival
- At the opening ceremony, Frau Fasnacht is **hung from the Obertorturm** — from that moment, she "rules the town" for four days
- The festival features parades, **Guggenmusik** (brass band cacophony), **Schnitzelbange** (satirical verse performances), a children's carnival with face painting, and a torchlight procession
- At the closing ceremony, participants wearing **white scarves** (symbolizing mourning for the season's end) accompany Frau Fasnacht from the Obertorplatz to the **Schifflande** on the Rhine
- There she is placed on a raft, **set alight, and surrendered to the Rhine** — a dramatic farewell to carnival and winter
- The **Fasnachtsgesellschaft Rheinfelden (FGR)** has organized the festival since 1973
- The tradition has medieval roots: before the 40-day Lenten fast, commoners would celebrate in disguise, playfully mocking the authorities

## 11. Geography and Modern Rheinfelden

- Rheinfelden lies **15 km east of Basel** on the southern bank of the **High Rhine**, which forms the border with Germany
- The municipality covers about **16 square kilometres** with elevations of 270–290 meters; to the south, the terrain rises into the **Tafeljura** (table-like plateaus of the Jura foothills)
- Population: over **13,000** (municipality), making it a mid-sized Aargau town
- The town is the **seat of the district of Rheinfelden** in the Fricktal region of northwestern Aargau
- In **2016**, Rheinfelden received the **Wakker Prize** from the Swiss Heritage Society — Switzerland's most prestigious award for urban heritage preservation
- The jury praised Rheinfelden's balance of growth with quality urban development, its "visionary policy" and "continuous planning," and its cooperation with the German sister city across the Rhine

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The anti-king's island** — Rudolf von Rheinfelden's daughter Agnes discovers a secret passage in Castle Stein on the Inseli; she must choose between loyalty to her father and peace with the Emperor, while the Rhine rises around them
2. **The clock that strikes too early** — A child visiting the Obertorturm discovers the clock still strikes seven minutes early; stepping through the gate at that "extra" moment, they land in medieval Rheinfelden and must help a farmer reach the gates before true closing time
3. **Frau Fasnacht's last ride** — During Fasnacht, the burning effigy of Frau Fasnacht on the Rhine raft winks at a watching child; she takes them on a wild night ride down the river past the Inseli, the Messerturm, and under the old bridge, showing Rheinfelden's history through carnival eyes
4. **The salt beneath the city** — A child follows a trail of salt crystals from the sole uno pool deep underground to the ancient salt deposits; they meet the "Salzkonig" (Salt King) who shows how Rheinfelden's "white gold" transformed a sleepy town into a spa destination
5. **Two Rheinfeldens** — A child walks across the old bridge and discovers that stepping past the border stone from 1810 takes them to a time before Napoleon split the city; they must reunite two friends — one Swiss, one German — before the border falls between them
6. **The knights' secret fresco** — In the Johanniterkapelle, a child notices a figure in the 1500 Last Judgment fresco is moving; the painted knight leads them through the story of Berchtold von Rheinfelden and the pilgrims' hospital, revealing a hidden message in the chapel wall
7. **The castle brewery's first brew** — Young Theophil Roniger and Mathias Wuthrich argue over recipes while building their "little castle in the field" in 1876; a child helps them through the crisis of the severe winter of 1877, when ice had to be hauled from distant Klontal by cart and rail
8. **The salmon of the Messerturm** — A child fishing on the Rhine catches an enormous, suspiciously well-fed salmon that tells the dark (but comical) legend of the Knife Tower's trapdoor; together they explore the old fortifications, discovering that truth is stranger than legend
