## 1. Geography — The Highest Town in Europe

- Davos lies at **1,560 meters** (5,120 ft) above sea level in the canton of **Graubünden**, in eastern Switzerland — making it the **highest town in Europe**
- The town stretches along the **Landwasser valley**, a high Alpine valley in the Rhaetian Alps between the Plessur and Albula mountain ranges
- Davos consists of two main centers: **Davos Platz** (the larger) and **Davos Dorf**, plus several smaller settlements (Frauenkirch, Glaris, Monstein)
- The **Landwasser river** flows through the valley and eventually joins the Albula, which feeds into the Rhine system
- **Lake Davos** (Davosersee) lies northeast of the village, below the Wolfgang Pass
- The surrounding peaks reach over 2,800 meters; the **Weissfluh** (2,844 m) above the Parsenn area is the most prominent
- Despite its small population (around 11,000), Davos holds town status and is one of the **largest municipalities by area** in Switzerland

## 2. The Walser Settlers — Medieval Colonists from Valais

- The first written mention of Davos appears in **1213** as **"Tavaus"** — a Romansh-speaking settlement
- The **Walser people** (Walliser) were German-speaking mountain farmers originally from the **Upper Valais** (the upper Rhone valley), who migrated outward from approximately **1150 to 1450** — one of the last great folk movements of the Middle Ages
- Factors driving the migration: **overpopulation** in the upper Rhone valley, the end of the **Medieval Warm Period** (c. 800–1300), agricultural pressure, and feudal rivalries over land
- From about **1280**, the **Barons of Vaz** invited Walser colonists to settle in the Davos valley, granting them the **Walserrecht** — feudal charters that conferred land in exchange for moderate taxes and military service
- In Davos, the Walsers received extraordinary privileges: the right to their own **judicial bodies**, full **freedom of individual movement**, and exemption from personal taxes
- In **1289**, a formal agreement between the people of Davos and the Baron of Vaz confirmed that Davos citizens would pay no personal taxes — only the community government owed a yearly amount of goods to the baron
- Davos became the **largest Walser settlement area** in eastern Switzerland
- The Walser legacy persists in the local dialect, farmhouse architecture, and place names throughout the valley

## 3. The League of Ten Jurisdictions — A Free Community

- In **1436**, the **League of the Ten Jurisdictions** (Zehngerichtebund) was founded in Davos — a political alliance of ten courts in the region
- A **1438 agreement** confirmed the League's rights and acknowledged the older 1289 treaty, exempting Davos from trade taxes within the territory of the Ten Jurisdictions
- The League was one of the **Three Leagues** (Drei Bünde) that governed Graubünden as a loose federation of free communities — remarkably democratic for medieval Europe
- Davos was under **Austrian overlordship** from **1477 to 1649**, though the community maintained substantial self-governance
- In **1803**, the Three Leagues were incorporated into the Swiss Confederation as the canton of Graubünden

## 4. Dr. Alexander Spengler and the Healing Climate

- **Alexander Spengler** (1827–1901) was a German physician who transformed Davos from a remote farming village into a world-famous health resort
- As a law student, Spengler fought at the barricades during the **1848 revolution** in the Grand Duchy of Baden; when it failed, he fled to Switzerland as a political refugee
- He studied medicine at the **University of Zurich**, became a Swiss citizen in **1855**, and traveled to Davos in **November 1853** to work as a country doctor
- Spengler quickly noticed something remarkable: **no villager in Davos suffered from tuberculosis** — the disease that was killing tens of thousands across Europe (Switzerland alone lost approximately 9,000 people to TB in 1900)
- He concluded that the **high altitude, dry air, and intense sunshine** of the Landwasser valley had a curative effect on lung disease
- His treatment methods were unconventional: long walks, up to **three liters of milk daily**, chest rubs with **marmot fat**, ice-cold showers, and extended stays in cowsheds (**"Stabulation"**)
- Colleagues dismissed his spa concept as **"sheer madness"** — but results proved him right
- **Winter 1865**: Two German patients, **Hugo Richter** and **Dr. Friedrich Unger**, experienced dramatic improvement, spreading the word
- By **1875**, Davos had more **winter guests than summer guests** for the first time
- Spengler partnered with the Dutch entrepreneur **Willem Jan Holsboer** to build hotels and push for a railway connection
- He founded the **Alexander House** for patients who could not afford private sanatorium care
- His sons **Lucius** and **Carl Spengler** both became leading tuberculosis therapists in Davos

## 5. The Sanatorium Era — Europe's Open-Air Hospital (1860s–1950s)

- After Spengler's pioneering work, sanatoriums multiplied: by **1940**, Davos had approximately **40 medical institutions** dedicated to tuberculosis treatment
- The sanatoriums had characteristic **long balconies** where patients lay on sun loungers in the fresh air for hours — the prescribed "Liegekur" (rest cure)
- Daily life was strictly regimented: **five meals a day** (including two liters of milk), prescribed rest periods, regulated outdoor time, and temperature monitoring
- The **Waldsanatorium** ("Forest Sanatorium Professor Jessen") opened on **July 4, 1911** — this was the sanatorium where **Katia Mann** (Thomas Mann's wife) was treated in 1912
- The **Schatzalp Sanatorium**, built between **1898 and 1900** by architects Otto Pfleghard and Max Haefeli in the **Art Nouveau style**, was the most luxurious — with innovations including underfloor heating, heated toilet seats, food elevators, and telephone service
- It opened on **December 21, 1900** and was considered the most advanced healing center in the region
- A **funicular railway** to the Schatzalp was built in **1899** — the first in Switzerland used for winter transport
- The sanatorium era wound down by the **mid-1950s** as effective antibiotics (particularly streptomycin) made altitude therapy obsolete
- The Schatzalp became a hotel in **1953**; the Waldsanatorium closed in **1957**
- Today, Davos maintains its medical tradition through the **Swiss Institute for Allergy and Asthma Research** and the **AO Research Institute**

## 6. Thomas Mann and "The Magic Mountain"

- In **March 1912**, Thomas Mann's wife **Katia** was diagnosed with a lung condition and sent to the **Waldsanatorium** (Forest Sanatorium Professor Jessen) in Davos, where she stayed from **March 12 to September 25, 1912**
- In **May/June 1912**, Thomas Mann visited his wife for three weeks — experiencing firsthand the peculiar world of the sanatorium: the daily rituals, the international patients, the rarefied mountain atmosphere
- Ironically, when Katia's original X-rays were later re-examined, **no signs of tuberculosis were found** — the novel arose partly from a misdiagnosis
- The visit inspired his novel **"Der Zauberberg" (The Magic Mountain)**, which he began writing in **1913**
- Work was interrupted by World War I; the novel was finally published in **November 1924**
- The story follows **Hans Castorp**, a young German engineer who visits his cousin at the fictional **Berghof sanatorium** in Davos for what should be three weeks — but stays for **seven years**
- The **Schatzalp** served as the primary model for the Berghof sanatorium, with its Art Nouveau architecture, terraces for rest cures, and international clientele
- Mann received the **Nobel Prize for Literature** in **1929** (primarily for "Buddenbrooks," but "The Magic Mountain" was cited as well)
- Today, a **Thomas-Mann-Weg** (Thomas Mann Path) leads from the site of the former Waldsanatorium to the Schatzalp, retracing the literary landscape
- In **2025**, Davos celebrated the **150th anniversary** of Thomas Mann's birth with a year of special events

## 7. Robert Louis Stevenson — Treasure Island in the Mountains

- The Scottish writer **Robert Louis Stevenson** (1850–1894) spent two winters in Davos seeking relief from his chronic lung disease
- **First winter (1880–1881)**: Stevenson, his wife Fanny, and stepson Lloyd arrived on **November 4, 1880** and stayed until mid-April 1881
- **Second winter (1881–1882)**: They returned on **October 18, 1881**, staying at the **chalet Villa am Stein** near Hotel Buol, and departed **April 20, 1882**
- Despite disliking the confined atmosphere of the sanatorium town, Stevenson was extraordinarily productive: he finished **"Treasure Island"** (published 1883), corrected proofs of **"Familiar Studies of Men and Books"**, and worked on **"The Silverado Squatters"**
- Stevenson found Davos stifling and depressing, yet the mountain air improved his health enough to continue his literary career

## 8. Ernst Ludwig Kirchner — Expressionism in the Alps

- **Ernst Ludwig Kirchner** (1880–1938) was a German Expressionist painter and co-founder of the artists' group **Die Brücke** ("The Bridge") in **1905** in Dresden
- He arrived in Davos in **1917** as a physical and psychological wreck — addicted to alcohol and morphine, suffering blackouts and paralysis after his breakdown during World War I
- **1917**: First stayed at a mountain hut on the **Stafelalp** above Davos, focusing on prints and drawings during recovery
- **September 1918**: Moved to **"In den Lärchen"** in Frauenkirch near Davos, where he rented rooms and decorated them with hand-carved furniture
- **1921**: His partner **Erna Schilling** moved permanently to Davos to join him
- **1923**: Moved to his final home, the **Haus auf dem Wildboden** in Frauenkirch
- Kirchner spent the last **20 years of his life** in the Davos region, producing an extraordinary body of work: vibrant **Alpine landscapes**, portraits of local farmers, tapestry-like panoramic mountain scenes
- From the mid-1920s, his style evolved toward more **abstract forms**, influenced by the overwhelming mountain environment
- In **1937**, the Nazis declared his work **"entartete Kunst" (degenerate art)** — over **600 of his works** were confiscated, sold, or destroyed
- Devastated by the persecution and suffering from long depression, Kirchner took his own life on **June 15, 1938** in Davos; he is buried at the **Waldfriedhof** (forest cemetery)

## 9. The Kirchner Museum Davos

- The museum was founded in **1982**, initially housed in the old post office building in Davos Platz
- The Swiss art dealer **Eberhard W. Kornfeld**, who had acquired Kirchner's final house on Wildboden in the 1960s, was instrumental in establishing the collection
- On **September 4, 1992**, a purpose-built museum opened — designed by the then-unknown architects **Annette Gigon** and **Mike Guyer** (it was their very first building and launched their careers)
- The architecture features four double-height glass volumes with white walls and oak parquet floors — designed not to compete with Kirchner's art but to serve it
- The collection is the **largest in the world** dedicated to Kirchner: approximately **40 paintings**, 7 sculptures, over **700 drawings and watercolors**, more than **300 prints**, **160 sketchbooks** (containing over 9,000 drawings), and more than **1,500 photographic negatives**
- The majority of the works date from his **Davos period (1918–1938)**, reflecting his constant engagement with the mountain world

## 10. Skiing Pioneer — Parsenn and the Cradle of Ski Tourism

- In **March 1893**, the **Branger brothers** (Tobias and Johannes) first traversed from Davos to Arosa over the Maienfelder Furka Pass on skis — a 14-mile journey
- In **March 1894**, the creator of Sherlock Holmes, **Arthur Conan Doyle**, recruited Tobias Branger as instructor, learned to ski, and repeated the crossing; he described the adventure in an essay called **"An Alpine Pass on 'Ski'"** published in the **Strand Magazine** in December 1894
- Doyle's article ignited enormous British interest in Alpine skiing and is credited with helping popularize ski tourism worldwide
- Local wagon maker **Peter Ettinger** and his sons began manufacturing skis; in **1895** they founded Switzerland's first ski manufacturing company (still operating today as Ettinger Sport)
- The **Parsenn funicular** opened in **1931** — making the Davos Klosters Bergbahn the **oldest ski lift operator in the world**
- A **J-bar lift** was installed in **1934**, upgraded to a **T-bar** by **1935** — pioneering mechanized surface lift technology
- The **Parsenn Derby**, first held in **1924**, is the **oldest ski race in Switzerland** — the first race from Parsennfurka to Küblis was won in 22 minutes 27 seconds
- Today, the Parsenn area offers over **100 km of pistes** and features one of the longest descents in Europe: the **12 km run from Weissfluhjoch (2,844 m) to Küblis (810 m)**

## 11. The Spengler Cup — World's Oldest Ice Hockey Tournament

- The **Spengler Cup** was founded at Christmas **1923** by **Dr. Carl Spengler**, son of the pioneering physician Alexander Spengler and himself a Davos doctor
- Carl Spengler's vision: to **"give the once-hostile nations an opportunity to rediscover mutual understanding and trust through fair and friendly competition"** — reconciliation through sport in the aftermath of World War I
- The first tournament in **1923** was won by **Oxford University Ice Hockey Club** (composed of Canadians studying at Oxford); other participants included Berliner SC, Vienna EV, and HC Davos
- **HC Davos**, founded in **1921** by dentist Dr. Paul Müller, is the tournament's permanent host and most successful club, with **17 titles** (as of 2025)
- Team Canada has won **16 titles**, making them the second most successful participant
- The tournament is played annually between **December 26 and 31** at the Eisstadion Davos (now the zondacrypto-Arena), nicknamed the **"Eispalast"** (Ice Palace) for its spectacular wooden roof
- The Spengler Cup has run continuously since 1923 except during parts of World War II (1939–1940, 1949) and COVID-19 (2020–2021)
- It is recognized as the **world's oldest invitational club ice hockey tournament**

## 12. The Rhaetian Railway and the World Economic Forum

### The Railway That Opened the Valley
- In **1888**, Dutch hotel owner **Willem Jan Holsboer** — Spengler's partner in developing Davos — founded the **Schmalspurbahn Landquart–Davos AG**, the precursor of the Rhaetian Railway
- The inaugural train reached **Klosters** on **September 29, 1889**; the full line to **Davos** opened in **1890**
- The railway transformed Davos from a remote mountain village accessible only by horse-drawn carriage into a connected resort town
- In **1895**, Holsboer renamed the company the **Rhätische Bahn** (Rhaetian Railway / RhB) as the network expanded across Graubünden
- In **2008**, the RhB's **Albula and Bernina lines** were inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** — the Albula line (67 km, 42 tunnels, 144 viaducts) and the Bernina line (61 km, 13 tunnels, 52 viaducts) are masterpieces of railway engineering

### The World Economic Forum
- In **January 1971**, German engineer and professor **Klaus Schwab** founded the **European Management Forum**, hosting 450 participants from 31 countries in Davos
- The choice of Davos was deliberate: a neutral Swiss mountain town with a long tradition of international gatherings and excellent conference infrastructure inherited from the sanatorium era
- In **1987**, the organization was renamed the **World Economic Forum** (WEF) to reflect its broadened global scope
- The annual meeting, held each January, now brings together some **3,000 participants** — heads of state, business leaders, economists, and celebrities — for five days of discussions on global issues
- The WEF has made "Davos" a global byword for elite international dialogue; the term **"Davos culture"** has been coined by globalization scholars
- Schwab retired from his roles at the Forum in **2025**

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The Walser child who followed the mountain path** — A young Walser girl migrates with her family from the Valais over high Alpine passes to the Landwasser valley around 1280; she discovers the freedom of the new community and helps negotiate the first rights with the Baron of Vaz
2. **The magic of the mountain air** — A sick child arrives at a Davos sanatorium in 1900, is frightened of the strict routines; befriends a marmot on the Schatzalp terrace who teaches her that the mountain air carries healing secrets
3. **Seven years on the Magic Mountain** — Inspired by Thomas Mann: a child visits a cousin in a mountain hotel and discovers that time moves differently at 1,560 meters — each floor of the Schatzalp reveals a different era of Davos history
4. **Kirchner's colors come alive** — A child discovers one of Kirchner's vibrant Alpine paintings in the museum; stepping into the canvas, they explore 1920s Davos with the artist, learning to see the mountains in bold new colors
5. **The Spengler Cup ghost match** — On December 26, the old Eisstadion comes alive with the spirits of hockey players from 1923; a child must help Oxford and Berlin play the first match again to keep the tradition of sporting peace alive
6. **Conan Doyle and the ski mystery** — Arthur Conan Doyle arrives in Davos in 1894 and recruits a local child and Tobias Branger to help him cross the Maienfelder Furka Pass on skis — but a Strand Magazine mystery must be solved along the way
7. **Stevenson's Treasure Island map** — A child staying in Davos finds an old map hidden in the walls of the Villa am Stein; following its clues through the snowy Landwasser valley, they discover it's Stevenson's original sketch for Treasure Island
8. **The train that opened the mountains** — In 1890, a child rides the very first train from Landquart to Davos; at each tunnel and viaduct, the Rhaetian Railway reveals a different Alpine secret, until they arrive at the highest town in Europe
