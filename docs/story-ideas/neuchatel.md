## 1. Founding — The New Castle (Novum Castellum)

- In **1011**, King **Rudolf III of Burgundy** donated a *novum castellum* ("new castle") on a hill overlooking the lake to his wife, **Ermengarde** — this Latin name gave the city its identity: *Novum Castellum* → Neuchâtel ("new castle" in French)
- The castle was likely the centre of a newly created royal court managing the western estates of the Burgundian kings
- When Rudolf III died without heirs in **1032**, Emperor **Conrad II** incorporated Neuchâtel into the Holy Roman Empire
- In **1034**, Conrad entrusted the territory to **Ulrich of Fenis**, creating the **County of Neuchâtel** (Comté de Neuchâtel) under the House of Neuchâtel
- The domain was officially dubbed a city in **1214**
- The **Château de Neuchâtel** combines **Romanesque, Gothic, and Renaissance** styles reflecting centuries of transformation — a Romanesque south wing from the late 12th century is the **oldest continuously used civil building in Switzerland**
- Today the castle is the seat of the **cantonal government** (Conseil d'État) and is a Swiss heritage site of national significance

## 2. The Collégiale (Collegiate Church) — Romanesque Jewel and the Cenotaph of the Counts

- Construction began in **1190** under Count **Ulrich II** and his wife **Berthe**, completed at the end of the 13th century
- The church blends two styles: a **Romanesque** base with **Gothic** upper parts and cloister
- Together with the castle it forms a single architectural ensemble crowning the hill above the old town
- **The Monument of the Counts** (Cénotaphe des Comtes): dated **1372**, this sculptural group consists of **15 polychrome statues** depicting Count **Louis de Neuchâtel** surrounded by his three successive wives, children, and family members — a masterpiece of 14th-century Gothic statuary and one of the finest medieval funerary monuments in Switzerland
- **The Reformation (1530)**: On **23 October 1530**, soldiers returning from a military campaign destroyed altars, paintings, and statues inside the church. On **4 November 1530**, the burghers voted by narrow majority to abolish the Mass, formally adopting the Reformation
- **Guillaume Farel** (1489–1565), the fiery French-born reformer sent by Bern, drove the Reformation in Neuchâtel — remarkably, this happened **against the will** of the ruling dynasty, who remained Catholic for nearly 200 more years
- Farel returned to Neuchâtel in **1543** and made it his base until his death; he is buried in the chapel of the Collégiale
- The **1535 Olivétan Bible** — the first French-language Protestant Bible — was printed in Neuchâtel

## 3. The Jaquet-Droz Automata — 18th-Century Mechanical Marvels

- Built between **1768 and 1774** by three master watchmakers from the Canton of Neuchâtel: **Pierre Jaquet-Droz** (1721–1790), his son **Henri-Louis Jaquet-Droz** (1752–1791), and **Jean-Frédéric Leschot** (1746–1824)
- Pierre Jaquet-Droz was born in **La Chaux-de-Fonds** in 1721; he studied at the Universities of Basel and Neuchâtel before becoming a watchmaker whose clocks impressed the **King of Spain**

### The Three Automata
- **L'Écrivain (The Writer)**: A boy seated at a desk, controlled by a mechanism of **6,000 metal parts**. He writes with a real goose quill, dipping it in ink, shaking off excess, and following the text with his eyes. He can be **programmed** to write any text up to 40 characters — often considered a remote ancestor of the modern computer
- **Le Dessinateur (The Draughtsman)**: A child who draws **four different images** — a portrait of Louis XV, a royal couple (believed to be Marie Antoinette and Louis XVI), a dog with *"Mon toutou"* written beside it, and Cupid driving a chariot pulled by a butterfly. Controlled by **2,000 parts**; he periodically blows on his pencil to remove dust
- **La Musicienne (The Musician)**: A young woman who **actually plays** a custom-built organ by pressing the keys with her fingers — not a music box but a real performance. Controlled by **2,500 parts**; her chest rises and falls as if breathing, and her eyes follow her hands

### Legacy
- The automata were designed to **advertise Jaquet-Droz's watches** to European royalty and nobility
- After touring the courts of Europe, the three automata were **donated to the City of Neuchâtel in 1909**
- They are still fully functional and displayed at the **Musée d'Art et d'Histoire** (MahN), with public demonstrations on the **first Sunday of every month**

## 4. Lake Neuchâtel (Lac de Neuchâtel) — Switzerland's Largest Inland Lake

- Surface area: **218.3 km²** — the **largest lake located entirely within Switzerland** (Lake Geneva and Lake Constance are shared with France and Germany/Austria respectively)
- Length: **38.3 km**; maximum width: **8.2 km**; maximum depth: **152 metres**
- Surface elevation: **429 metres** above sea level
- Part of the **Pays des Trois-Lacs** (Three Lakes Region) together with the lakes of Biel/Bienne and Morat/Murten
- The lake lies in **Romandie** (French-speaking Switzerland), shared by the cantons of **Neuchâtel, Vaud, Fribourg, and Bern**
- The southern shore is home to the **Grande Cariçaie** — one of the largest lakeside marshlands in Western Europe, a nature reserve of national importance
- First written mention of the lake: **998 CE**
- The shores have been frequented since prehistory — the discovery of the **La Tène** site (see below) was made on these very banks

## 5. The Laténium — La Tène Culture and 50,000 Years of History

- The site of **La Tène**, discovered in **1857** on the northern shore of Lake Neuchâtel, gave its name to the **La Tène period** of Celtic civilisation — the great flowering of Celtic culture across Europe from roughly **450 BC to 50 BC**, stretching from Ireland to Turkey
- The **Laténium** (Parc et musée d'archéologie de Neuchâtel) opened in **2001** in Hauterive, on the lake shore near the original discovery site
- It is **Switzerland's largest archaeological museum**, housing over **525,000 objects** spanning 50,000 years of regional history, with approximately **3,000 on permanent display**
- The outdoor **archaeological park** features dolmens, glacial erratics, reconstructions of a La Tène house, a Gallo-Roman ship, and a Celtic bridge
- In **2003**, the Laténium received the **European Museum of the Year Award** from the Council of Europe — the first Swiss museum ever so honoured
- The Celts (Helvetii) were the dominant culture in Switzerland for roughly a millennium; Lake Neuchâtel was the epicentre of their presence

## 6. Watchmaking — The Cradle of Swiss Horology

- The Canton of Neuchâtel is historically one of the world's most important centres of watchmaking (*horlogerie*)
- **Daniel JeanRichard** (1665–1741), born in the hamlet of Les Bressels between La Chaux-de-Fonds and Le Locle, is considered the **founder of the regional watchmaking industry**. He created his first watch in **1681** and pioneered the *établissage* (division of labour) system, training farmers to produce specific watch components during winter months
- By **1712**, the Neuchâtel mountains were home to some **hundred watchmakers**; the craft grew from a winter sideline for farmers into a world-leading industry
- JeanRichard's influence ran through dynasties: **Courvoisier, Perrelet, Jaquet-Droz, and Robert** — all central to Swiss watchmaking
- **La Chaux-de-Fonds** and **Le Locle** became the twin capitals of Swiss horology, their entire urban layouts designed around the industry — wide streets, checkerboard grids, and workshops with **oversized windows** facing the sun to provide natural light for watchmakers
- In **2009**, La Chaux-de-Fonds and Le Locle were inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** for their unique watchmaking town planning
- Neuchâtel hosted the world's first **quartz wristwatch** development (Beta 1 and Beta 2) at the Centre Électronique Horloger (CEH)
- The **Musée International d'Horlogerie** (MIH) in La Chaux-de-Fonds is the **world's largest watchmaking museum**, with over 4,500 pieces

## 7. The Prussian Connection — A Canton Ruled by a King

- Upon the death of **Marie de Nemours** (last of the Orléans-Longueville dynasty) in **1707**, the Neuchâtelois had to choose a new sovereign from **15 competing claimants**
- They chose the **Protestant King Frederick I of Prussia** (House of Hohenzollern), preferring a distant Protestant monarch over nearby Catholic French princes who might have imposed their religion
- From **1708 to 1806** and again **1814 to 1857**, the King of Prussia ruled the Principality of Neuchâtel (*Principauté de Neuchâtel*) in personal union with the Kingdom of Prussia
- During the Napoleonic Wars (**1806–1814**), Napoleon deposed the Prussian king and gave Neuchâtel to his chief of staff, Marshal **Louis-Alexandre Berthier**
- After Napoleon's defeat, the **Congress of Vienna (1815)** confirmed Neuchâtel's unique **dual status**: both a Swiss canton and a Prussian principality — making it the **first and only monarchy** among the otherwise entirely republican Swiss cantons

## 8. The Republic — Revolution and the Neuchâtel Crisis (1848–1857)

- On **1 March 1848**, amid a wave of revolutions sweeping Europe, Neuchâtel republicans led by **Fritz Courvoisier** and **Ami Girard** marched with approximately **1,000 insurgents**, seized the castle, and proclaimed the **République et Canton de Neuchâtel**
- King **Frederick William IV of Prussia** initially accepted the loss (preoccupied with his own domestic revolutions) but never formally renounced his claim, having it endorsed at a London conference in **1852**
- **The Neuchâtel Crisis (1856–1857)**: On **2 September 1856**, royalist conspirators attempted a counter-coup with Prussian encouragement. Swiss federal troops crushed the rebellion within a day, imprisoning about **500 royalists**
- When Frederick William demanded their release, Switzerland refused unless he renounced sovereignty. The standoff nearly triggered war — Switzerland mobilised approximately **30,000 troops**
- International pressure (especially from France and Britain) prevented conflict
- On **26 May 1857**, Frederick William signed a treaty in Paris **renouncing his sovereignty** over Neuchâtel, keeping only the hollow title *"Prince de Neuchâtel"*
- Neuchâtel was the **last Swiss canton** to resolve its sovereignty status, finally becoming fully and exclusively Swiss

## 9. Val-de-Travers — Birthplace of Absinthe

- **Absinthe** (*la fée verte* / "the green fairy") originated in **Couvet** in the Val-de-Travers, a valley in the Canton of Neuchâtel in the Jura mountains
- The first commercial absinthe distillery was opened in **1797** by **Major Dubied**, who bought the recipe from the **Henriod sisters** of Couvet
- Absinthe production boomed in the 19th century — at its peak, some **40 distilleries** operated in Switzerland, many concentrated in Val-de-Travers
- **1910**: Switzerland officially **banned** the production and sale of absinthe, enshrining the prohibition in the federal constitution
- Despite the ban, the people of Val-de-Travers **never stopped distilling** — a clandestine tradition persisted for nearly a century, with local authorities largely looking the other way
- **2005**: Absinthe was **re-legalised** in Switzerland. Today around **20 microdistilleries** produce absinthe in Val-de-Travers, and the region proudly celebrates its heritage
- The valley hosts the **Maison de l'Absinthe** museum in Môtiers, tracing the spirit's colourful history

## 10. Geography — Lake, Mountains, and Vineyards

- Neuchâtel sits at roughly **430 metres** elevation on the northwestern shore of Lake Neuchâtel, between the **Jura mountains** and the lake
- The old town (*vieille ville*) climbs the hillside in warm **Hauterive yellow sandstone** — a distinctive local stone that gives the city its golden character
- Population: approximately **34,000** (city); the canton has about **176,000** inhabitants
- The canton spans three distinct zones: the **lakeside strip** with vineyards, intermediate valleys (*Val-de-Travers, Val-de-Ruz*), and **highland Jura mountains**
- **Vineyards**: **606 hectares** of vines stretch along 30 km of sunny lakeside slopes on Jurassic limestone soils; vine cultivation dates to Roman times, with the **Priory of Bevaix** (founded **998**) planting the first documented vineyard
- **Pinot Noir** dominates (55% of plantings), followed by **Chasselas** (25%)
- Neuchâtel is the birthplace of **Œil-de-Perdrix** ("partridge's eye") — a pale rosé made from Pinot Noir, first labelled in **1861** by winemaker **Louis Bovet** of Areuse; it is considered the original Swiss rosé

## 11. Famous People of Neuchâtel

- **Jean Piaget** (1896–1980): Born in Neuchâtel on **9 August 1896**, the son of Arthur Piaget, professor of medieval literature at the University of Neuchâtel. Studied zoology at the University of Neuchâtel (PhD **1918**), then became the **major figure in 20th-century developmental psychology**, defining the four stages of cognitive development in children. Published over 60 books. Buried in the Cimetière des Rois in Geneva
- **Louis Agassiz** (1807–1873): Born in Môtier (Canton Fribourg), **professor of natural history at the University of Neuchâtel from 1832 to 1846**. Built the famous *"Hôtel des Neuchâtelois"* — a research hut on the Aar Glacier — and in **1837** proposed the revolutionary **Ice Age theory**: that vast glaciers had once covered Europe. Published *Études sur les glaciers* in **1840**. Later emigrated to the United States and became a professor at Harvard
- **Pierre Jaquet-Droz** (1721–1790): Master watchmaker born in La Chaux-de-Fonds, creator of the famous automata (see above), pioneer of Swiss luxury watchmaking who sold clocks to the King of Spain and exported over 600 timepieces to China
- **Daniel JeanRichard** (1665–1741): Born in Les Bressels, considered the founder of the Neuchâtel watchmaking industry
- **Guillaume Farel** (1489–1565): French-born reformer who brought the Reformation to Neuchâtel (and later convinced Calvin to stay in Geneva)

## 12. The University of Neuchâtel

- The institution traces its origins to the **Académie de Neuchâtel**, created in **1838** by King **Frederick William IV of Prussia**, then Prince of Neuchâtel
- Its founding owed much to the scientific prestige of **Louis Agassiz**, who was making groundbreaking glaciological discoveries in the region at the time
- By **1866**: Faculties of Arts, Science, and Law; **1873**: Faculty of Theology added
- On **18 May 1909**, the Grand Council officially elevated the Académie to the **Université de Neuchâtel**
- Today it is a public research university with approximately 4,000 students, known for linguistics, microtechnology, and hydrogeology
- Jean Piaget earned his doctorate here in 1918; his father Arthur Piaget was a long-serving professor

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The automaton who wanted to write his own story** — The Writer automaton in the museum comes alive at night and begins writing a story about himself, but his 40-character limit means the child must help him finish it, piece by piece, across all the landmarks of Neuchâtel
2. **The King who lived far away** — A child discovers that the King of Prussia once ruled Neuchâtel from distant Berlin; they "visit" the king through a magical letter carried by a mechanical bird, showing him the lake, the mountains, and the people he has never met
3. **The green fairy of Val-de-Travers** — A mischievous fairy made of absinthe mist escapes from a clandestine still and leads a child on a moonlit chase through the Jura valleys, revealing the valley's secret distilling history
4. **The partridge's eye** — A child notices that the local rosé wine has the exact colour of a partridge's eye; the partridge (alive and talking) guides them through Neuchâtel's vineyards and tells the story of the Roman monks who first planted the vines
5. **The Celts of the lake** — A child finds a Celtic sword on the shore of Lake Neuchâtel and is transported to 200 BC, when the Helvetii built bridges and houses at La Tène; they must return the sword to the lake before the Laténium museum opens
6. **The glacier professor's hut** — Young Louis Agassiz invites a child to his "Hôtel des Neuchâtelois" on the Aar Glacier, where they discover that the entire land was once covered in ice — and a frozen mammoth has something to tell them
7. **The night the castle changed hands** — On 1 March 1848, a child living in the castle wakes to hear the revolutionaries climbing the hill; they must decide whether to help the old Prussian governor or the republicans carrying torches and Swiss flags
8. **The three mechanical children** — The Writer, the Draughtsman, and the Musician escape the museum and explore modern Neuchâtel, amazed by things they cannot understand (cars, phones, electric light) while a child helps them find their way back before the Sunday demonstration begins
