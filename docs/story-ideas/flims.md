## 1. The Flimser Bergsturz — The Largest Landslide in the Alps

- Approximately **10,000 years ago** (around **8000 BC**), an enormous mass of rock detached from the north wall of the Vorderrhein (Anterior Rhine) valley near Flims
- Between **9 and 12 cubic kilometres** of Jurassic and Cretaceous limestone crashed into the valley — the **largest known rockslide in the entire Alpine arc** and one of the largest on Earth
- The debris field covered an area of roughly **50 square kilometres**, burying the Rhine valley under rubble several hundred metres thick
- The catastrophe likely took **less than one hour** — transforming from a rockslide into a rapid rock avalanche (Sturzstrom)
- The landslide **dammed the Anterior Rhine**, creating a massive lake behind the debris barrier
- Over thousands of years, the river slowly cut through the rubble, carving out the **Ruinaulta** (Rhine Gorge) and leaving behind the towering white limestone walls visible today
- The debris also created several smaller lakes: **Caumasee**, **Crestasee**, **Prau Pulte**, and **Tuleritg** — all sitting in hollows formed within the porous rockslide material
- The Rhine has still not returned to its pre-landslide level — it continues to run through the debris field to this day
- The landslide occurred during the early **Holocene**, a period of increased mass movement frequency in the Alps, likely related to post-glacial climate change and the retreat of Ice Age glaciers

## 2. Lake Cauma (Caumasee) — The Emerald Jewel

- **Lag la Cauma** in Romansh means **"Lake of the Midday Rest"** — from "cauma" (siesta, afternoon rest)
- Surrounded by forest on three sides with a wooded island at its centre, the lake sits directly on the ancient Flims rockslide debris
- The lake has **no visible inlet or outlet** — it is fed entirely by **underground springs** that percolate through the porous karst-like aquifer within the landslide rubble, up to **80 metres thick**
- Water levels fluctuate by **4 to 5 metres** depending on underground flow throughout the year
- The water shimmers from **turquoise to emerald green** depending on light conditions, a result of its mineral-rich underground origin and the limestone substrate
- Water temperature reaches **17 to 24 degrees Celsius** during bathing season
- Access is via a free **Caumasee lift** that descends approximately **80 metres** to the lake shore, or by forest paths from Flims Waldhaus (accessible to strollers and wheelchairs)
- The bathing facility **Bogn La Cauma** operates seasonally, with pedal boats, stand-up paddleboards, and restaurants

## 3. Lake Cresta (Crestasee) — The Quiet Sister

- **Lag la Cresta** is a small lake shared by the municipalities of **Flims and Trin**, situated at **841 metres** above sea level
- Like Caumasee, it was formed within a hollow in the Flims rockslide debris and is fed by underground springs
- The strong underground inflow ensures the lake water is **entirely renewed every few days**, maintaining excellent water quality
- Water temperatures exceed **24 degrees Celsius** in the height of summer — warmer than its famous neighbour
- A shallow area separated from the deeper portion makes it ideal for **families with young children**
- Since **1892**, a guest house on the northern shore has served visitors — it still operates as a restaurant in its original state
- The lake remains less developed than Caumasee, with a more natural and secluded atmosphere

## 4. The Ruinaulta — Switzerland's Grand Canyon

- The **Ruinaulta** (Rhine Gorge) stretches for approximately **14 kilometres** between **Ilanz/Glion** and **Reichenau** in the canton of Graubünden
- The name comes from **Rhaeto-Romanic**: "Ruinaulta" means **"high heap of rubble"** — an apt description of its origin
- The gorge was carved by the Anterior Rhine cutting through the massive Flims rockslide debris over thousands of years, producing dramatic **white limestone cliffs** rising up to **400 metres** above the river
- It lies within the **Swiss Tectonic Arena Sardona**, inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2008** — a site recognised for its exceptional geological significance, showing 250-million-year-old Verrucano rock thrust over 35–50-million-year-old Flysch rock
- The **Rhaetian Railway** runs through the gorge, with stations at **Valendas-Sagogn** and **Versam-Safien**
- The gorge supports rare **orchid species** and provides breeding grounds for **endangered birds**
- Seven designated viewpoints offer different perspectives on the canyon: **Il Spir, Islabord, Zault, Crap Signina, Spitg, Alix**, and **Wackenau**
- The **Punt Ruinaulta** suspension bridge, completed in **2010**, spans **105 metres** (with a 75-metre free span) connecting the communities of **Trin and Bonaduz** — one of the longest suspension bridges in Graubünden
- River rafting and canoeing from Ilanz to Reichenau is described as one of the **most exciting water routes in Europe**

## 5. The Il Spir Viewing Platform at Conn

- **Il Spir** (Romansh for **"Eurasian swift"**) is a viewing platform at **Conn**, perched on the edge of the Ruinaulta gorge near Flims
- Designed by engineer **Corinna Menn** and opened in **2006**
- The platform's shape echoes the silhouette of the **common swift**, a bird frequently seen soaring along the southern cliffs of the gorge
- It consists of a **single pylon anchored by wire rope**, cantilevering out over the void
- Offers a **180-degree panoramic view** of the Rhine Gorge, with the river lying some **400 metres below**
- There is **no vehicle access or public transport** to the platform — visitors reach it by hiking through the Flims forest from **Flims Waldhaus** (approximately one hour), or via a privately rented carriage ride from Staderas
- A favourite comparison: while the Grand Canyon in Arizona took **40 million years** to form, the Rhine carved the Ruinaulta in just **10,000 years**

## 6. The Pinut Via Ferrata — Switzerland's Oldest

- The **Pinut via ferrata** (Klettersteig Pinut) is the **oldest surviving via ferrata in all of Switzerland**
- First mentioned in writing in **1739** — the route over the Flimserstein cliffs above Fidaz has been used for centuries
- Opened in its current secured form in **1907** and comprehensively renovated in **2007**
- The route leads over **three nearly vertical rock faces** connected by two terraces, using **27 ladders**, natural tunnels, and caves — all secured by a continuous steel cable
- Total elevation gain of approximately **1,010 metres** from Flims village to the summit
- The via ferrata section itself covers **1.7 kilometres** with **700 metres of climbing** (approximately 2 hours 20 minutes)
- Children from **age 12** may attempt the route; equipment rental is available in Flims from **CHF 30**
- Guided tours are offered weekly during summer for **CHF 150** (CHF 130 with guest card)

## 7. From Spa Town to Ski Resort — Flims Tourism History

- Flims (Romansh: **Flem**) is first mentioned in written records in **765 AD** as "Fleme"
- The area has been inhabited since the **Bronze Age** — the ruins of the medieval **Burg Belmont** (10th–11th century, built for the Freiherr von Belmont) stand on the heights above Fidaz
- Modern tourism began when Flims developed as a **summer spa town** in the 19th century, favoured for its wind-sheltered, sun-drenched location
- In **1869**, Peter-Jakob Bener-Caviezel and Paul Lorenz from Chur founded the **"Waldhaus-Flims cure and lake-bathing establishment"**, centred on the healing waters of the Caumasee
- The **Hotel Waldhaus** opened in **1877** with 120 beds, charging guests **6 francs** for board and lodging
- By **1893**, **Queen Regent Emma of the Netherlands** visited with her daughter **Queen Wilhelmina** — establishing Flims as a destination of international prestige
- By **1904**, the Waldhaus complex offered over **437 guest beds** backed by **1.1 million Swiss francs** of share capital
- Notable firsts at the Waldhaus: **Switzerland's first hotel swimming pool** (1894), the first telephone in Flims (1901), and later Switzerland's first hotel **indoor tennis centre** (1977)
- **Flims-Waldhaus** was the original centre of tourism; Flims Dorf (the village) developed separately

## 8. Flims Laax Falera — The White Arena

- The three formerly separate resorts of **Flims, Laax, and Falera** were merged into the **Weisse Arena** (White Arena) ski area — one of the **largest linked ski areas in Graubünden**
- The combined area offers approximately **224 kilometres** of pistes, reaching from the valley at around **1,100 metres** up to the **Vorab Glacier at 3,018 metres**
- **LAAX** became a pioneer of **freestyle snowboarding** culture: when snowboarders first began riding Alpine slopes in the **mid-1980s**, LAAX was among the earliest adopters
- LAAX boasts **Europe's largest halfpipe** and a total of over **90 obstacles** spread across four snow parks
- The **LAAX Open** is a flagship **FIS Snowboard World Cup** halfpipe event, established around 2015 and celebrated its **10th anniversary in 2025** — drawing up to **10,000 spectators** to the finals at **Crap Sogn Gion**
- The **Freestyle Academy**, founded in **2003**, trains newcomers in freestyle skiing and snowboarding — the first indoor freestyle training centre of its kind
- Champions like **Chloe Kim** and **Scotty James** have each won **five LAAX Open titles**, cementing the event's legendary status in the snowboard world

## 9. Romansh Language and Surselva Culture

- Flims lies in the **Surselva** region — the name derives from Romansh **"sur selva"** meaning **"above the forest"**, referring to the Uaul Grond forest in the Flims rockslide zone
- **Romansh** is Switzerland's **fourth national language**, a Rhaeto-Romance language descended from the Vulgar Latin spoken after the Roman conquest of Rhaetia in **15 BC**
- The local dialect is **Sursilvan**, the most widely spoken Romansh variety — in the 2000 census, **17,897 people** (54.8% of the Surselva region) named Romansh as a habitually spoken language
- In Flims itself, German is now the majority language (**80%** as of 2000), with Romansh at just **6.7%** — a dramatic shift reflecting broader Germanisation trends in Graubünden
- Romansh developed as a **written language from the 16th century**, boosted by Reformation-era Bible translations
- Romansh place names pervade the landscape: **Lag la Cauma** (Lake of Midday Rest), **Ruinaulta** (High Heap of Rubble), **Il Spir** (The Swift), **Crap Sogn Gion** (Rock of St. John)
- The municipality consists of **Flims Dorf** (the village), **Flims Waldhaus** (the hotel quarter), and the hamlets of **Fidaz** and **Scheia**

## 10. Das Gelbe Haus — Architecture and the Olgiati Legacy

- **Das Gelbe Haus** (The Yellow House) is an art museum and exhibition space in Flims, housed in a building originally constructed in **1870** and formerly used as a greengrocer's shop
- The house stood empty for 20 years before Swiss architect **Rudolf Olgiati** (1910–1995) donated his cultural collection to the municipality of Flims shortly before his death in **1995**, on the condition that the building be renovated
- His son, internationally renowned architect **Valerio Olgiati** (born 1958 in Chur, raised in Flims), undertook the transformation in **1997–1999** — removing all interior walls to create open exhibition spaces
- Despite being repainted **white**, the old nickname "Gelbe Haus" (Yellow House) stuck — making it a famous architectural paradox
- The building serves as a venue for **Alpine and international architecture, design, and folk arts** exhibitions
- Valerio Olgiati, who studied at **ETH Zurich** and worked in Los Angeles before returning to Flims in 1996, established his architectural practice in Flims in **2008** and is recognised internationally for his minimalist, conceptual approach
- Rudolf Olgiati had worked as an architect in Flims from **1944**, dedicating decades to collecting and preserving Graubünden cultural heritage

## 11. Geography and Setting

- Flims sits at an elevation of approximately **1,081 metres** (3,547 feet) in the **Imboden District** of the canton of Graubünden
- The municipality covers **50.51 square kilometres** with a population of approximately **2,900** (as of 2024)
- It occupies a wide, sun-drenched terrace above the Rhine Gorge — the open, sheltered position gives it a notably mild microclimate for its altitude
- The landscape is entirely shaped by the Flims rockslide: the undulating terrain of hummocky debris, the hidden forest lakes, and the dramatic gorge below are all products of the catastrophe 10,000 years ago
- The **Vorab** (3,028 m) is the highest point accessible from the ski area, with a small glacier
- The **Rhaetian Railway** (connecting Chur to the Oberalp Pass) runs through the valley below, though Flims itself is not on the rail line — access is by **PostBus** from Chur (approximately 30 minutes)

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The mountain that fell asleep** — The Flimserstein tells its own story: 10,000 years ago it tumbled into the valley, and the child discovers that the forests, lakes, and gorge all grew from that one enormous event
2. **The secret springs of Caumasee** — A child follows the turquoise water underground through the rockslide debris, discovering the hidden springs that feed the lake with no river in or out
3. **Rafting through the Grand Canyon** — A nervous child's first rafting trip through the Ruinaulta; the white cliffs tell stories of different geological ages as the raft passes beneath them
4. **The swift of Il Spir** — A Eurasian swift nesting on the viewing platform takes a child on a flight through the Rhine Gorge, 400 metres above the river, showing all seven viewpoints
5. **Twenty-seven ladders to the sky** — On the Pinut via ferrata, each of the 27 ladders reveals a different era of Flims history, from Bronze Age settlers to the first tourists in 1877
6. **The house that changed colour** — The Yellow House was yellow, then white, but its name never changed; a child discovers that the architect's father hid something in the walls before the renovation
7. **The queen who came for a swim** — Queen Wilhelmina of the Netherlands visits Flims in 1893; a local child must help prepare the Waldhaus hotel and the Caumasee bathing establishment for the royal guest
8. **The last word in Romansh** — In a village where fewer people speak Romansh each year, a child discovers that the old place names (Lag la Cauma, Ruinaulta, Crap Sogn Gion) hold magical powers that only work when spoken in the ancient language
