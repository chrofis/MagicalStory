## 1. Geography — A Remote Valley in Graubunden

- Vals lies at **1,252 meters** above sea level in the **Valser Valley** (Valsertal), a roughly **16-kilometer-long** side valley branching south from the **Val Lumnezia** in the canton of Graubunden (Grisons)
- The village has a population of approximately **1,000 residents** and belongs to the **Surselva Region**
- Vals is a **German-speaking enclave** surrounded by the **Romansh-speaking** Val Lumnezia — a linguistic island created by Walser migration
- The valley is enclosed by peaks rising above **3,000 meters**, including the **Guferhorn (3,379 m)** to the south
- Until the construction of the road to **Ilanz** in **1879–1880**, all goods transport ran southward over the **Valserberg** pass to Hinterrhein — the valley had no direct northern road connection
- The village remains **unconnected to any railway**; access is by PostBus (Postauto) from Ilanz
- The hamlet of **Leis**, at **1,526 m**, sits above the main village and was where architect Peter Zumthor lived for nearly two decades
- On **1 January 2015**, the former municipality of **St. Martin** merged into Vals

## 2. The Walser — Settlers from Valais

- The **Walser** (from "Walliser") are descendants of Alemannic settlers who migrated from the upper Rhone valley (Valais/Wallis) into the high Alpine valleys between roughly **1150 and 1450**
- **Permanent settlement** of the Vals valley floor began in the **11th and 12th centuries** with **Romansh-speaking** settlers from the front Lugnez
- Around **1300**, **German-speaking Walser** from Upper Valais moved into the valley. They soon became the majority population
- The Walser received the so-called **Walserrecht** (colonist right): personal freedom, the right to form their own judicial communities, and hereditary land rights — in exchange for moderate interest payments and military service
- Vals was held as a fief for the **Bishop of Chur** by the **von Tersnaus** and **von Mont** families
- In **1457**, the expansion of the Walser was restricted: they were **forbidden from marrying or buying land** from the Romansh-speaking locals
- The Walser brought their distinctive **Blockbau** (log construction) building tradition — wooden houses built with interlocking horizontal logs, triangular roofs, and elevated granaries (Speicher) resting on mushroom-shaped stone stilts to keep out rodents
- The **Walser dialect** spoken in Vals preserves archaisms from **Old High German** that were lost in other German dialects — a living linguistic fossil, kept alive by centuries of Alpine isolation
- Approximately **10,000 people** across the Alps still speak variants of Walser German today

## 3. The Parish Church of Sts. Peter and Paul

- The church in Vals was **first documented in 1451**, originally dedicated to **St. Peter** alone
- A **reconstruction** took place around **1500**, during which a notable **Gothic winged altar** was created — it survives today in the Mother of God chapel
- Between **1640 and 1643**, a **Baroque nave** was built at a right angle to the old church under Father **Sebastian Ruttimann**. The Gothic choir was preserved as an eastern side chapel, though its vault ribs were removed
- The church was **reconsecrated on 6 September 1643** and rededicated to **Saints Peter and Paul**
- In **1711**, a chapel dedicated to **St. Anthony of Padua** was added to the west side
- Following the **Ilanzer Articles of 1524–1526**, which allowed individual municipalities to choose their religious affiliation, Vals chose to remain **Catholic** — as did most Walser communities in the region

## 4. Disasters — Floods, Avalanches, and the Threat of Emigration

- A **chronicle of avalanches** (die Lawinechronik) has been kept in Vals since **1598**, recording avalanches, floods, and rockslides
- **1868**: Catastrophic **floods** inundated the entire valley floor and destroyed parts of the settlement. The damage was so severe that the entire village seriously considered **mass emigration to America**
- **20 January 1951**: During the infamous **"Winter of Terror"** — Switzerland's most catastrophic avalanche season of the 20th century — a **300-meter-wide avalanche** struck Vals, burying **34 people** and destroying **23 buildings**. **19 people died**, including **14 children**
- The 1951 disaster led the municipality to invest **CHF 21.5 million** over subsequent decades in **reforestation** and protective structures (avalanche barriers, deflection dams)
- Across all of Switzerland that winter, **98 people** were killed by avalanches, approximately **1,500 buildings** were destroyed, and the disaster led directly to the founding of modern avalanche warning and hazard mapping systems by the **SLF** (Swiss Institute for Snow and Avalanche Research)

## 5. The Thermal Springs — From Kurhaus to World Fame

- The hot **mineral spring** in Vals (the **St. Petersquelle**) was **first mentioned by name in 1622**, though local knowledge of the warm waters likely predates this
- The spring was formally **developed in 1891**, and in the **summer of 1893** the **Kurhaus Therme** opened with **60 beds** and a bathhouse
- In **1960**, German investor **Kurt Vorlop** purchased and modernized the facility. It was shortly afterward sold to **Donald M. Hess**
- In **May 1970**, a new modern spa center opened, featuring thermal pools and **345 apartments** — transforming Vals into a tourist destination
- In the early 1990s, the municipality sought to revitalize the aging baths and commissioned **Peter Zumthor** — then a relatively unknown architect based in nearby Haldenstein — to design a new thermal bath
- Zumthor's **Therme Vals** opened on **14 December 1996** and rapidly became one of the most celebrated buildings of the late 20th century
- The natural spring water emerges at **30 degrees Celsius** from the St. Petersquelle at an elevation of **1,252 m**

## 6. Peter Zumthor and the Therme Vals — Architecture as Sensory Experience

- **Peter Zumthor** was born on **26 April 1943** in Basel, the son of cabinet maker **Oscar Zumthor**. He trained as a cabinet maker (1958–1962), then studied at the **Kunstgewerbeschule** in Basel and at **Pratt Institute** in New York
- From **1967**, he worked as a conservationist architect for the **Canton of Graubunden's Department for Monument Preservation**. In **1979**, he established his own practice in **Haldenstein**, near Chur, where he still works with a deliberately small team
- In **2009**, Zumthor was awarded the **Pritzker Architecture Prize** — architecture's highest honor — in Buenos Aires. The jury called him "a master architect admired by his colleagues around the world for work that is focused, uncompromising and exceptionally determined"
- The Therme Vals was built between **1993 and 1996**, conceived as a structure growing from the mountain itself — a cave-like building **half-buried in the hillside** with a **grass-covered roof** that merges with the surrounding meadow
- The building consists of **15 rectangular stone blocks**, each standing **5 meters high**, arranged in a grid pattern. The roofs of the blocks do not touch — **6-to-8-centimeter gaps** between them are covered with glass, allowing natural light to filter through as thin luminous lines
- **60,000 stone slabs** (62,000 linear feet) of **Valser quartzite**, quarried from the valley itself, clad every surface. The slabs come in three consistent thicknesses — **31 mm, 47 mm, and 63 mm** — and the total of the three always equals 15 cm, allowing for visual variety within a rigorous system
- The building extends **34 meters** into the hillside, with a gross floor area of **5,600 square meters**
- Each chamber offers a different sensory experience at a different temperature: the **indoor pool (32 degrees C)**, the **outdoor pool (36 degrees C in winter, 30 degrees C in summer)**, the **Fire Bath (42 degrees C)**, the **Ice Bath (14 degrees C)**, the **Flower Bath (33 degrees C)**, the **Sound Bath (35 degrees C)**, and the **Drinking Stone** with natural ferrous spring water at **29.8 degrees C**
- Zumthor's design philosophy: "Architecture today needs to reflect on the tasks and possibilities which are inherently its own. In a society that celebrates the inessential, architecture can put up a resistance"

## 7. Valser Quartzite — The Stone of Vals

- **Valser Quarzit** (Vals quartzite) is a **light grey and greenish fine-grained gneiss** with discernible linear patterning and occasional feldspar "eyes" — formed from granite that was metamorphosed during the Alpine orogeny under extreme heat and pressure
- It belongs to the **lowest Pennine nappes** in Mittelbunden, composed of strata of mica slate, gneiss, quartzite, and dolomites — rocks originally deposited in sea basins far south of where the Alps now stand
- The stone has exceptional physical properties: **compressive strength of approximately 217 N/mm squared**, **density of 2,698 kg per cubic meter**, and outstanding resistance to abrasion, freeze-thaw cycles, and heavy foot traffic
- **Truffer AG**, founded in **1983**, is the primary quarrying company, operating at the **Liemschboda** quarry in Vals. They extract approximately **7,000 cubic meters** of quartzite per year
- **60 percent** of all Vals quartzite mined is **exported**, reaching over **50 countries** on all continents
- Notable international projects include: Zumthor's Therme Vals (the building that made the stone world-famous), the **Rocksresort in Laax** (10,000 square meters of layered masonry), and the **US Embassy in Oslo, Norway** (4,000 square meters)
- The stone has been used for centuries in local Vals buildings — the village's traditional houses, walls, and roofs all feature the local quartzite alongside the Walser wooden Blockbau construction

## 8. Valser Mineral Water — From Alpine Spring to Market Leader

- Vals has **two commercial mineral springs**: the **St. Petersquelle** (at 1,252 m) and the **St. Paulsquelle** (at 1,815 m, among Switzerland's highest commercial springs)
- The St. Petersquelle was **first documented in 1622**; Romans may have already known of the springs in antiquity
- In **1960**, entrepreneurs **Hess and Schrauder** redeveloped the St. Petersquelle, launching the modern Valser brand. Bottling began at the **Haus Adula** with a home delivery service in **1961**
- The water takes **25 to 100 years** to travel from mountain rain and snow through the rock to emerge as crystal-clear mineral water
- In **1968**, the first major bottling facility was built at the current **Boda** location in Vals
- Key brand milestones: first TV commercial (**1970**), gastronomy launch (**1983**), Valser Naturelle still water (**1984**), designer glass bottles by **Luigi Colani** (**1990**), PET bottles designed by **Mario Botta** (**2001**)
- In **2002**, **Coca-Cola** acquired **Valser Mineralquellen AG** from the Hess Group. Valser became Switzerland's **market-leading mineral water brand**
- In **2010**, the **St. Paulsquelle** was tapped for **Valser Silence**, the still water product
- The brand introduced **100% recycled rPET bottles** in **2019** and installed solar panels at the Vals facility

## 9. The Zervreilasee — A Dam in the High Valley

- The **Zervreilasee** is a reservoir located southwest of Vals at an elevation of **1,862 meters**
- The **Zervreila Dam** is a **concrete arch dam**, built between **1953 and 1957**, standing **151 meters** high with a crest length of **504 meters** — impounding the **Valserrhein** river
- The dam contains **650,000 cubic meters of concrete** and holds back **100.5 million cubic meters** of water
- The reservoir has a surface area of **1.61 square kilometers** and a maximum depth of **140 meters**
- The village of **Zervreila** was submerged when the reservoir filled
- Construction of the dam between 1951 and 1958 provided a critical **economic stimulus** for the valley — bringing jobs, infrastructure, and concession fees/taxes that transformed the local economy at a time when Vals was still reeling from the 1951 avalanche disaster
- The surrounding landscape features dramatic high-Alpine scenery, with the **Guferhorn (3,379 m)** visible from the dam

## 10. The 2012 Sale and the Architecture Controversy

- For its first 16 years, the Therme Vals was **owned by the municipality** — a social project serving the community and drawing visitors to the valley
- In **2012**, the commune voted to sell the baths and hotel to local investor **Remo Stoffel** for **CHF 7.8 million**, with a pledge to invest **CHF 50 million** in renovations. Zumthor reportedly made a similar offer but was refused
- Stoffel transformed the spa into the luxury **7132 Hotel**, restricting bath access primarily to hotel guests. Zumthor stated: "The bath is a landmark so nothing will happen to the bath, but this social project is dead"
- Zumthor claimed tourism to the area **fell by around 60 percent** after the ownership change
- In **2015**, LA-based **Morphosis** (led by Pritzker laureate **Thom Mayne**) unveiled plans for a **381-meter-tall hotel tower** next to the Therme — which would have been among the tallest buildings in Europe, in a village of 1,000 people. The design was called "a gigantic mirror-clad middle finger" by The Guardian
- The competition jury later **distanced itself** from the appointment through the Swiss Society of Engineers and Architects. The tower was never built
- Meanwhile, four world-renowned architects designed rooms for the **7132 House of Architects**: **Tadao Ando** (18 rooms inspired by Japanese teahouses), **Kengo Kuma** (23 oak-clad rooms), **Peter Zumthor** (10 rooms with dark Stucco lustro plasterwork), and **Thom Mayne** (22 rooms in timber and local quartzite)
- The Therme Vals building itself was **heritage-listed** shortly after its 1996 opening, protecting it from alteration

## 11. Alpine Farming and Traditional Life

- For centuries, the economy of Vals was based on **subsistence agriculture and cattle breeding** in the harsh high-Alpine conditions
- The Walser settlers practiced **transhumance** (Alpwirtschaft) — the seasonal movement of livestock between valley floor in winter and high mountain pastures (Alpen) in summer, a tradition dating to at least the **12th century** in Switzerland
- The famous **Valser mountain porters** (Saumtreiber) carried heavy loads over the Valserberg to the south, especially in winter when pack animals could not manage the snow-covered passes. Livestock was driven to markets in **Bellinzona** and **Lugano**
- The road to Ilanz built in **1879–1880** ended centuries of dependence on the mountain passes, fundamentally changing the valley's economic orientation from south to north
- The ski resort **Vals-Dachberg** opened in **1975** and expanded in **1996**, becoming one of the three highest ski resorts in Graubunden
- Today, Vals has a **two-Michelin-star restaurant** (Restaurant 7132 Silver), a dramatic contrast to its centuries of subsistence farming
- **Villa Vals** (2009), designed by **SeARCH and Christian Muller Architects**, is an underground vacation home built into the hillside with an elliptical facade — featured on the Netflix series "The World's Most Extraordinary Homes"

## 12. Isolation, Preservation, and Identity

- Vals owes much of its cultural distinctiveness to its **geographic isolation** — a dead-end valley with no rail connection and no through road
- This isolation preserved the **Walser dialect**, the **Blockbau architecture**, the **Catholic faith** (while surrounding Romansh valleys had more mixed religious histories), and a strong communal identity
- The arrival of Zumthor's Therme in 1996 created a tension that defines modern Vals: **world-class architecture and international tourism** overlaid on one of Graubunden's most traditional, isolated communities
- The village's **postal code 7132** became the brand name for the luxury hotel complex — a symbol of how a four-digit number came to represent the collision of alpine tradition and global ambition
- The Valser quartzite, the mineral water, and the thermal springs all emerge from the same geology — the village's identity is literally **built from its own mountain**

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The stone that remembers** — A child touches the quartzite wall of the Therme and hears the mountain's memories: the ancient sea where the stone formed, the Walser builders, the quarry workers, and finally Peter Zumthor laying the first slab
2. **The mountain porters of Vals** — A child joins the legendary Valser Saumtreiber on a winter journey over the Valserberg pass, carrying salt and cheese through snowstorms to the markets of Bellinzona
3. **The warm spring's secret** — A child follows a trickle of warm water from the Therme's Drinking Stone back through the mountain, discovering an underground world where the water has journeyed for 100 years before emerging at 30 degrees
4. **The village under the lake** — At the Zervreilasee, the water level drops in autumn and a child glimpses the drowned village of Zervreila below — and hears the stories of the families who once lived there
5. **The avalanche chronicle** — A child finds the ancient Lawinechronik (kept since 1598) in the church and travels through its pages: witnessing the great flood of 1868, the terrible winter of 1951, and the reforestation that saved the valley
6. **The architect and the mountain** — Peter Zumthor cannot find the right design for the baths. A child from Vals shows him how the quartzite layers in the mountainside already contain the answer — the building is already inside the stone
7. **The Walser who walked from Valais** — A Walser family leaves the Rhone valley around 1300, crosses the high passes, and discovers the remote Vals valley. The child must help them build the first Blockbau house before winter arrives
8. **The water that became famous** — A raindrop falls on the mountain above Vals and begins a 100-year journey through rock, becoming mineral water. It emerges at the St. Petersquelle and must choose: become a bottle of Valser water seen around the world, or flow into the ancient Therme baths
