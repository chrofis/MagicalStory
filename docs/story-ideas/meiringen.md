## 1. Origins and the Hasli Valley — A Self-Governing Alpine Republic

- **First documented mention**: Meiringen appears as **"Magiringin"** in a **1234** charter by King Heinrich VII, gifting the church to the Lazarite order in Seedorf, Uri
- Meiringen was the political capital of the **Oberhasli** (Hasli valley), an imperial **reichsfrei** (free from feudal lords) bailiwick — de facto self-governing from early medieval settlement until **1798**
- **Origin legend**: According to chronicle tradition (first recorded in Ericus Olai's *Chronica regni Gothorum*, c. 1470), the Hasli people descended from **6,000 Swedes and Frisians** exiled by famine, led by chieftains named **Restius** and **Hastus**. They supposedly settled in the alpine valleys because the landscape reminded them of their homeland
- **1275**: Hasli formed a strategic **alliance with the city of Bern**
- **1311**: King Heinrich VII gave Hasli to the House of Weissenburg
- **1334**: After an unsuccessful **revolt**, Hasli passed to Bern as a subject territory in name — but Bern was careful to preserve most of Hasli's earlier privileges and appointed native Hasli men as administrators and judges
- The local **Hasli dialect** belongs to the **Highest Alemannic** variety of Swiss German, one of the most archaic dialect groups in Switzerland
- Population today: approximately **4,900 residents**, the economic centre of the Hasli valley

## 2. Geography — Gateway to the Great Alpine Passes

- Meiringen sits at **595 m** elevation in the **Bernese Oberland**, at the eastern end of Lake Brienz, where the Aare river descends from the high Alps
- The town is uniquely positioned at the **junction of five Alpine passes**, making it a vital crossroads for centuries of trans-Alpine trade:
  - **Grimsel Pass** (2,164 m) — south to the Valais and onward to Italy. A trade document signed **12 August 1397** between Bern and Valais regulated the route from Lucerne to Milan: trail upkeep, tolls, and the running of the Hospiz. Roman-era use is suggested
  - **Susten Pass** (2,224 m) — east to Uri and Central Switzerland. Documented as a mule-train route from the **17th century**; used strategically by the French in **1798–1799** during the Napoleonic wars
  - **Brünig Pass** (1,008 m) — northwest to Obwalden and Lucerne; the lowest and most-used pass, connecting the Bernese Oberland to Central Switzerland
  - **Grosse Scheidegg** (1,962 m) — southwest to Grindelwald and the Eiger/Jungfrau region
  - **Joch Pass** (2,207 m) — northeast to Engelberg
- For centuries, **Säumer** (muleteers) drove pack animals over these passes carrying cheese, leather, and iron goods northward, and wine, rice, and Murano glass southward

## 3. The Great Fires and Rebuilding

- **1879**: A devastating fire destroyed much of the village of Meiringen
- **1891**: Only twelve years later, a **second catastrophic fire** struck, destroying most of what remained — including the English Church
- Following both fires, Meiringen was **rebuilt with tourism in mind**, transforming from one of the poorest areas in Switzerland into an emerging Alpine resort destination
- The English Church was rebuilt on its old foundations after 1891 and later became the home of the **Sherlock Holmes Museum**

## 4. The Church of St. Michael — Over 1,000 Years of History

- The **first village church** was built in the **9th or 10th century** and later destroyed in a flood
- The replacement **Church of St. Michael** (first mentioned **1234**) was built approximately **5 metres above** the old church to protect against future flooding
- The current structure dates from the **15th century**, renovated in **1683–84**
- During renovations in **1915–16**, excavators discovered the **remains of three predecessor churches** buried five metres below the current floor level — archaeological finds of **national importance** documenting over 1,000 years of continuous worship

## 5. The Restiturm — Knights of the Alpine Passes

- The **Restiturm** (Castle Ruin Resti) was built starting in **1250** by **Peter von Resti**, with the tower completed around 1300
- The castle was the seat of the **Knights of Resti**, vassals of the Habsburgs
- Its strategic hilltop position overlooking the Haslital allowed it to **control and protect** the valley and the trade routes across all five Alpine passes
- Abandoned in the **16th century** and fell into ruin; restored in **1914** and again in **2004** with information boards and a viewing terrace

## 6. Sherlock Holmes and the Reichenbach Falls

### Conan Doyle's Visit (1893)
- In **1893**, Sir Arthur Conan Doyle and his wife toured Switzerland. Conan Doyle stayed at the **Park Hotel du Sauvage** in Meiringen (renamed "the Englischer Hof" in his fiction)
- Upon seeing the **Reichenbach Falls** — a thundering **250-metre** waterfall in multiple cascades — he decided this was the perfect stage for Holmes's death
- He wrote in his diary (which still exists): he wanted to **kill Sherlock Holmes** at the Reichenbach Falls

### "The Final Problem" (December 1893)
- Published in **The Strand Magazine** (UK) and **McClure's** (US) in December 1893
- In the story, Holmes and his arch-nemesis **Professor Moriarty** struggle at the edge of the falls on **4 May 1891** (fictional date), both apparently plunging to their deaths
- **Public outrage** was immediate and intense: readers cancelled magazine subscriptions, wrote furious letters, and pressured Conan Doyle to bring Holmes back
- Conan Doyle finally relented: in **"The Adventure of the Empty House"** (1903), Holmes reveals he survived using **baritsu** (a martial art) to throw Moriarty into the abyss, then went into hiding

### The Plaque and Memorial
- A plaque at the falls reads (in English, German, and French): **"At this fearful place, Sherlock Holmes vanquished Professor Moriarty, on 4 May 1891"**
- Located on a ledge on the opposite side of the falls from the funicular — few visitors actually reach it

### The Sherlock Holmes Statue
- In **1988**, sculptor **John Doubleday** created a life-size **bronze statue** of Holmes, unveiled in the presence of **Dame Jean Conan Doyle** (the author's daughter)
- The statue stands at **Conan Doyle Place** in the village centre, depicting Holmes in his deerstalker cap and cape moments before his confrontation with Moriarty
- Embedded in the statue are **60 sculpted mystery clues**, each referencing a different Sherlock Holmes story

### The Sherlock Holmes Museum
- Opened in **1991** (the centenary of Holmes's fictional death) in the basement of the **English Church**
- Houses the **world's only authentic reproduction** of Holmes's parlour at **221B Baker Street**, along with original Strand Magazine issues, pipes, magnifying glasses, and crime-scene exhibits
- Renovated and expanded in **May 2019**

## 7. The Reichenbach Falls and the Funicular

- The Reichenbach Falls comprise a series of cascades totalling **250 metres** of drop, with the upper fall measuring approximately **110–120 metres**
- The falls are fed by the Reichenbach stream descending from the Rosenlaui valley
- The **Reichenbachfall-Bahn** (funicular railway) was built in **1899** to plans by **Franz Josef Bucher** from Obwalden; engineer **Elias Flotron** of Meiringen secured the federal concession
- Technical details: **714 metres** long, climbs **244 metres** of elevation, journey time approximately **7 minutes**, two carriages of **24 passengers** each
- **Rebuilt in 1999** to the original design, preserving its nostalgic Belle Epoque character
- From **1912 to 1956**, a tramway (Meiringen–Reichenbach–Aareschlucht) connected the funicular's base station to the village centre

## 8. The Aareschlucht (Aare Gorge)

### Geology
- The gorge cuts through the **Kirchet**, a limestone ridge formed during the **Cretaceous Period** (130–60 million years ago) as marine sediment in the Alpine Tethys Ocean
- During Alpine mountain formation (~15 million years ago), these layers were pushed upward; the Kirchet limestone is **autochthonous** (underwent minimal folding), making it harder and more resistant to erosion
- The current gorge was carved **10,000 years ago** by torrential meltwater at the end of the last Ice Age
- Research by **Franz Müller** identified **five earlier gorges** (in addition to today's), completely or partially filled with glacial moraine — a new gorge formed in every interglacial period

### Dimensions and Access
- **1.4 kilometres** long, bordered by sheer cliffs up to **200 metres** high
- Width varies from **30 metres** at its widest to barely **1 metre** at its narrowest
- Walking paths were first permitted in **1887**; the gorge has been **open to the public since 1889**

### The Tatzelwurm Legend
- The Aare Gorge is the legendary home of the **Tatzelwurm**, an Alpine cryptid described as a "half dragon" with a snake-like body and clawed forelegs
- According to folklore, a **chicken lays a black egg in water**, which sunlight incubates into a Tatzelwurm; when it crawls through sand, the sand turns to glass
- In **1935**, Russian photographer **Balkin** published an image in the **Berliner Illustrirte Zeitung** claiming to show the creature; the newspaper offered **1,000 Reichsmarks** for its capture. The photograph is now considered a hoax
- The Tatzelwurm remains the **official mascot** of the Aare Gorge; a wooden sculpture greets visitors at the entrance, and local shops sell **chocolate Tatzelwurm** candies with marzipan tongues

## 9. The Meringue — A Dessert Named After Meiringen?

- According to documents once held by the **Museum of Culinary Art in Frankfurt am Main** (destroyed during World War II), the meringue was first created by a confectioner named **Gasparini** working in Meiringen **around 1600**
- He named his creation — whipped egg whites baked with sugar — after the town: **"Meiring"** or **"Meiringe"**
- As the dessert spread across Europe, the name evolved:
  - Austria and southern Germany: **"Meirinken"** / **"Merinken"**
  - Northern Germany: **"Spanischer Wind"** (Spanish Wind)
  - France: **"Meringue"** (Frenchified spelling)
  - England: reportedly called **"kiss"** by Queen Elizabeth I
- King **Stanislaus Leszczynski** (1677–1766) tasted meringues at his residence in Nancy and sent the recipe to his daughter **Maria**, wife of King Louis XV — spreading the meringue to the French royal court at Versailles
- **The debate**: The origin claim is supported by Bertelsmann and Brockhaus publishers and the Swiss Hoteliers' Association, but the destruction of the Frankfurt documents in WWII means definitive proof no longer exists
- In **1985**, Meiringen achieved the record for the **world's longest meringue**
- Today, Meiringen bakeries (especially Frutal) still produce traditional **Merängge** served with thick double cream

## 10. The Rosenlaui Valley and Glacier

- The **Rosenlaui Glacier** is a 5-km-long glacier in the municipality of Meiringen, feeding the Weissenbach stream that carved the spectacular **Rosenlaui Glacier Gorge** (Gletscherschlucht)
- The gorge lies within the **Swiss Alps Jungfrau-Aletsch UNESCO World Heritage Site**
- The **Hotel Rosenlaui**, first built in **1771** (rebuilt in **1862** after a fire, with a Belle Epoque wing added in **1905**), was one of the earliest Alpine tourist hotels — nearly 250 years of continuous hospitality
- The hotel attracted **painters, scientists, and Grand Tour travellers** from across Europe throughout the 19th century, drawn by the dramatic glacier landscape
- The area is accessible via the scenic **PostBus route** from Meiringen over the Grosse Scheidegg to Grindelwald

## 11. The Gauli Glacier Rescue (1946) — Birth of Swiss Air Rescue

- On **18 November 1946**, a US military **C-53 Skytrooper** aircraft crash-landed on the Gauli Glacier at **3,350 metres** elevation, carrying 12 people (including two high-ranking US officers, four women, and an 11-year-old girl)
- All survived, but several were injured; the crew sent emergency radio signals from the glacier
- On **23 November**, two Swiss soldiers on skis reached the crash site after a **13-hour ascent** from Innertkirchen (near Meiringen)
- Swiss pilots then landed **Fieseler Storch** light aircraft fitted with skis directly on the glacier to evacuate the injured — one of the first successful mountain air rescues in history
- The rescue was coordinated from **Meiringen Air Base** and became an **international media sensation**
- The event is considered the **birth of Swiss air rescue**; it directly led to the founding of **Rega** (Swiss Air-Rescue Guard) in **1952**

## 12. Meiringen Air Base — Jets Inside a Mountain

- **Meiringen Air Base** (Unterbach) began operations on **1 December 1941** during World War II
- It is one of only **three main operating bases** of the Swiss Air Force
- Famous for its **underground aircraft caverns** — tunnels carved into the mountain rock in the 1940s to hide and re-arm fighter jets, originally designed for WWII-era fighters
- Meiringen is the **only Swiss airfield** that still uses aircraft caverns in daily operations — F/A-18 Hornets taxi directly out of the mountainside
- Home to **Fliegerstaffel 11** (F/A-18 Hornet, professional pilots) and militia **Squadron 8 "Destructors"** (F-5E Tiger)

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The detective of the falls** — A child finds a magnifying glass at the base of the Reichenbach Falls and must solve 60 clues hidden on the Holmes statue to unlock a mystery before sunset
2. **The Tatzelwurm's egg** — Deep in the Aare Gorge, a child discovers a black egg in the water; must decide whether to protect the hatching creature or reveal it to the world
3. **The meringue that conquered Europe** — Time-travel tale: a child apprentice helps Gasparini perfect his egg-white creation in 1600 Meiringen, then follows the recipe's journey to the court of Versailles
4. **The ghost of the gorge** — A child walks through the Aareschlucht and at its narrowest point (1 metre wide) slips back 10,000 years to witness the glacier meltwater carving the gorge for the first time
5. **The muleteers' last journey** — A child joins the final Säumer caravan over the Grimsel Pass, guiding pack mules through snow, meeting traders from Italy carrying Murano glass and rice
6. **The rescue on the glacier** — 1946: a child in Meiringen watches a plane disappear into the clouds above the Gauli Glacier and joins the ski patrol racing to save the survivors
7. **Holmes and Moriarty's second chance** — The bronze statue comes alive at midnight; Holmes asks a child to help him solve one final case across Meiringen's landmarks before dawn returns him to bronze
8. **The church beneath the church** — A child discovers a hidden staircase in St. Michael's Church leading five metres down through 1,000 years of buried churches, each level a different century of Hasli valley life
