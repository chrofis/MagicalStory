## 1. Geography — The Upper Prättigau

- Klosters lies in the **upper Prättigau valley** in the canton of **Graubünden**, eastern Switzerland, at an elevation of approximately **1,191 meters** (3,907 ft)
- The municipality of **Klosters-Serneus** spans **219.8 km²** of Alpine terrain — from the valley floor at **830 m** (the Landquart river border) to the **Verstanclahorn** at **3,298 m**, the highest point on its territory
- The **Landquart river** flows northwestward through the valley, fed by tributaries from the surrounding mountains, eventually joining the Alpine Rhine at the town of Landquart
- The municipality consists of eight fractions: **Klosters Platz**, **Klosters Dorf**, **Selfranga**, **Aeuja**, **Monbiel**, **Serneus**, **Mezzaselva**, and **Saas**
- On **1 January 2016**, the former municipality of **Saas im Prättigau** merged into Klosters-Serneus; in **2021**, the municipality shortened its official name to simply **Klosters**
- Population: approximately **4,473** residents (2023)
- The village is roughly **10 km** north of Davos, connected by road and rail through the Prättigau valley

## 2. The Monastery — How Klosters Got Its Name

- Between **1208 and 1222**, **Premonstratensian monks** from the monastery of **Churwalden** migrated to the upper Prättigau and established a filial provostry dedicated to **St. Jakob** (St. James)
- This was a **Rodungskloster** — a "clearing monastery" — founded to develop agricultural land from the heavily forested valley floor
- The **Barons of Vaz** (Freiherren von Vaz) endowed and supported the monastery's development
- The first documented mention of the settlement appears in **1222** as **"ecclesiam sancti Iacobi"** — the church of St. James — making this the official founding date
- The village name **Klosters** derives from the Latin **"claustrum"** (cloister), referring directly to this monastic foundation
- In **1525**, Klosters adopted the **Protestant Reformation**, leading to the dissolution of the Premonstratensian monastery and a shift from ecclesiastical to communal governance
- Only the **Romanesque church tower** survives from the original medieval monastery; the current church building (the **Reformierte Kirche am Platz**) dates from **1493** and is listed as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**
- The church features stained glass windows by the Graubünden artist **Augusto Giacometti** (1877–1947), including his depiction of **"Jacob's Dream"**, and a **baroque house organ from 1686**
- In **2022**, Klosters celebrated its **800th anniversary** with a year-long program of events, including new commemorative bells hoisted into the church tower by schoolchildren

## 3. The Walser Settlers — Mountain Farmers from the Valais

- The **Walser people** (Walliser) were German-speaking mountain farmers from the **Upper Valais** (upper Rhone valley) who migrated outward across the Alps between approximately **1150 and 1450**
- Factors driving the migration: **overpopulation** in the Rhone valley, the end of the **Medieval Warm Period** (c. 800–1300), agricultural pressure, and feudal rivalries over land
- From the late **13th century**, the **Barons of Vaz** invited Walser colonists to settle in the Prättigau, granting them the **Walserrecht** — feudal charters conferring land in exchange for moderate taxes and military service
- The Walsers received extraordinary privileges: the right to their own **judicial bodies**, full **freedom of individual movement**, and exemption from personal taxes — privileges that persisted until **1805**
- In the Prättigau and Davos region, the **pure wooden house** made of round or squared timber in the **Strickbau** (log-cabin) technique became the dominant building form
- The **scattered single-farm settlement** pattern is characteristic of Walser colonization — isolated farmsteads rather than clustered villages
- **Wooden granaries built on stilts** protected food supplies from moisture and mice; beautiful historic examples survive in **Klosters Monbiel** and **Schlappin**
- Approximately **10,000 people** still speak Walser dialects today across Switzerland, Austria, Italy, and Liechtenstein

## 4. The Prättigau — Valley of Conflict and Freedom

- The **Prättigau** (from Romansh **"Prau Tigau"** — meadow valley) is the main valley of the **Landquart river**, stretching from the Rhine valley at Landquart up to the Davos/Klosters region
- In **1436**, the **League of the Ten Jurisdictions** (Zehngerichtebund) was founded in Davos — a political alliance of ten courts in the region, including the Prättigau, that formed one of the **Three Leagues** (Drei Bünde) governing Graubünden
- In **1622**, during the turmoil of the Thirty Years' War, the **Prättigau uprising** erupted: valley residents revolted against Habsburg Austrian occupation
- In April 1622, insurgents **drove out the occupation troops** and liberated the valley; the euphoric fighters even attacked the neighboring Austrian Montafon valley
- In September 1622, the Habsburg commander **Baldiron** returned with **10,000 men** and reconquered the Prättigau in a devastating campaign — **numerous villages were reduced to rubble and ashes**
- The Treaty of Lindau (September 30, 1622) forced Graubünden to cede the Prättigau temporarily to Austria
- In **1803**, the Three Leagues were incorporated into the Swiss Confederation as the canton of **Graubünden**

## 5. The Silvretta — Glaciers and High Peaks Above Klosters

- The **Silvretta mountain group** rises to the south and east of Klosters, straddling the Swiss-Austrian border — part of the **Rhaetian Alps**
- The range contains **329 named peaks**; over **50 summits** exceed 3,000 m; the highest is **Piz Linard** (3,410 m), first climbed on **August 1, 1835** by geologist **Oswald Heer** with guide Johann Madutz
- The Silvretta holds approximately **85 glaciers**; the largest, the **Silvrettagletscher**, covers 2.7 km² — earning the range its nickname **"Blue Silvretta"**
- The **Verstanclahorn** (3,298 m), within the Klosters municipal boundary, is the highest point of the municipality and the fourth-highest peak of the Silvretta
- The **Vereina Tunnel** (19,042 m), opened on **November 19, 1999**, connects the Landquart–Davos line with the Engadin — making it the **longest metre-gauge railway tunnel in the world** and giving Klosters a year-round, avalanche-safe rail link to the Lower Engadin
- Construction began in **1991** and was completed nearly six months ahead of schedule at a cost of **CHF 670 million**

## 6. Skiing — Parsenn, Gotschna, and Madrisa

- The legendary **Parsenn** descent was first discovered in **1895**, attracting sports pioneers to the region
- The **Parsenn funicular** from Davos opened in **1931** — making the Davos Klosters Bergbahn the **oldest ski lift operator in the world**
- The **Parsenn Derby**, first held in **1924**, running from Parsennfurka to Küblis through the Klosters vicinity, is the **oldest ski race in Switzerland**
- The **Gotschnabahn** cable car, providing direct access from Klosters Platz to the **Gotschnagrat** (2,281 m) and the Parsenn ski area, has operated in two sections since **1950**
- The **Madrisa** ski area, on the opposite side of the valley above the hamlet of Saas, reaches **2,611 m** — known as a family mountain with south-facing slopes and a cross-border ski touring circuit into Austria's Montafon
- Together, the Davos Klosters ski region offers over **300 km of pistes** across six mountains, with one of Europe's longest descents: the **12 km run from Weissfluhjoch (2,844 m) to Küblis (810 m)**

## 7. King Charles III — The Royal Connection

- **King Charles III** first visited Klosters in **1978**, accompanied by Lady Sarah Spencer (Princess Diana's elder sister), and has returned virtually every winter since — making it his favorite ski resort for over **45 years**
- Charles stayed at the **Hotel Walserhof** and later used **Chalet Eugenia** as his winter base; the village's traditional Swiss charm and the discretion of local residents kept him coming back
- **Princes William and Harry** learned to ski on the slopes of Klosters
- On **March 10, 1988**, tragedy struck: while skiing off-piste on the **Gotschnawang** slope below Gotschnagrat, an avalanche released at approximately **2:50 p.m.**, dragging two members of the royal party some **450 meters** downhill
- **Major Hugh Lindsay**, aged 34, a former equerry to Queen Elizabeth II, was killed; **Patti Palmer-Tomkinson** suffered serious leg injuries
- The Swiss Institute for Snow and Avalanche Research had issued a warning of **"considerable risk of slab avalanches"** that day; the group had been skiing off-piste in fresh deep snow
- Mountain guide **Bruno Sprecher** immediately alerted authorities and performed emergency rescue; Prince Charles helped dig victims free with his bare hands
- The Grisons public prosecutor closed the case on **June 27, 1988**, determining no individual bore criminal responsibility
- Despite the tragedy, Charles never abandoned his love for Klosters — a cabin on the **Gotschnabahn cable car** was named **"Prince of Wales"** in his honour (later updated to bear the King's name), a tribute that was briefly removed for a sponsor but restored after local protests

## 8. Hollywood on the Rocks — The Chesa Grischuna Era

- The **Chesa Grischuna**, a hotel and restaurant in Klosters, was established in **1938** by **Hans Guler**, descendant of mountain guides, and designed by Zurich architect **Hermann Schneider**
- Wall paintings were created by **Alois Carigiet**, the celebrated Graubünden illustrator (later famous for the children's book character **Schellenursli**)
- During World War II, **American escaped prisoners of war** interned in neutral Switzerland were housed at the Chesa Grischuna; after the war, they returned with families and Hollywood connections
- The hotel earned the nickname **"Hollywood on the Rocks"** and its guest book reads like a celebrity register: **Greta Garbo**, **Gene Kelly**, **Deborah Kerr**, **Rex Harrison**, **Julie Andrews** (1958), **David Niven** (1959), **Peter Sellers**, **Audrey Hepburn**
- Writers **Irwin Shaw**, **Peter Viertel**, and **John Irving** also frequented Klosters; Shaw became known as the **"King of Klosters"** after settling there in the post-war years
- **Greta Garbo** made Klosters her beloved second home for decades — drawn by her friend **Salka Viertel**, who relocated to Klosters in **1953**, and Salka's son **Peter Viertel** and his wife Deborah Kerr, who lived there from **1960**
- The reclusive star took daily walks along the Landquart river wearing her trademark hat, and the privacy she enjoyed became a selling point for the village itself
- **Winston Churchill** also visited and reportedly left a painting inscribed: **"My only regret is having to leave this wonderful place"**

## 9. Ernst Ludwig Kirchner — Expressionism in the Mountains

- **Ernst Ludwig Kirchner** (1880–1938), co-founder of the Expressionist group **Die Brücke** in Dresden (1905), arrived in the Davos region in **May 1917** as a broken man — addicted to alcohol and morphine, suffering paralysis after his wartime breakdown
- He first lived on the **Stafelalp** (1,894 m) above Frauenkirch in a mountain hut (the **Rüeschhütte**), producing important works including **"Rising Moon in the Stafelalp"** and the painting **"Stafelalp"** (1917–1918)
- In **September 1918**, he moved to **"In den Lärchen"** in Frauenkirch, and in **1923** to his final home, the **Haus auf dem Wildboden**
- Although based primarily in the Davos area, Kirchner's territory encompassed the entire Davos-Klosters region — the **Stafelalp** sits above the route between Davos and Klosters, and his panoramic Alpine landscapes captured the Prättigau world from multiple vantage points
- The **Kirchner Museum Davos**, opened in a purpose-built building in **1992** (designed by architects **Annette Gigon** and **Mike Guyer**), holds the world's largest collection of his work: approximately **40 paintings**, over **700 drawings**, more than **300 prints**, and **160 sketchbooks**
- Kirchner spent the last **20 years of his life** in the region, but the Nazi regime declared his work **"entartete Kunst" (degenerate art)** in **1937**, confiscating over **600 works**; he took his own life on **June 15, 1938**

## 10. The Nutli Hüschi — A Window into Walser Life

- The **Nutli Hüschi** is a historic Walser house built in **1565** by **Christian Nutli** in the traditional **Strickbau** (interlocking-log) technique
- In the Prättigau dialect, **"Hüschi"** means a small house
- The house was inhabited until the **mid-19th century**, then converted into a **village museum** displaying everyday objects from before 1850: furniture, textiles, and household items
- An attached old Prättigau barn houses agricultural equipment, a traditional carpenter's workshop, and a mountaineering parlor
- The museum is part of the **Klosterser Museums-Trilogie** — three complementary museums telling the story of Klosters
- The building offers visitors a tangible connection to the Walser way of life: the low ceilings, small windows, and dark timber interiors that characterized mountain dwelling in the Prättigau for centuries

## 11. Robert Louis Stevenson — Through the Prättigau to Davos

- The Scottish writer **Robert Louis Stevenson** (1850–1894) arrived by rail at **Landquart** on **November 4, 1880**, with his wife **Fanny**, stepson **Lloyd**, and their dog **Woggs**
- From Landquart, they took a roughly **eight-hour horse-drawn carriage ride** up the **Valley of Prättigau** — passing through Klosters — to reach Davos, where Stevenson had been sent for his chronic lung disease
- During two productive winters in Davos (**1880–1881** and **1881–1882**), Stevenson finished **"Treasure Island"** (1883), corrected proofs of **"Familiar Studies of Men and Books"**, and worked on **"The Silverado Squatters"**
- The Prättigau valley — with its dramatic gorges, wooden Walser houses, and the Landquart rushing below — was the gateway through which Stevenson entered the mountain world that would sustain his health and his writing

## 12. The Railway — Opening the Valley

- In **February 1888**, Dutch hotel owner **Willem Jan Holsboer** founded the **Schmalspurbahn Landquart–Davos AG** to connect the remote Prättigau valley to the Swiss rail network
- On **September 29, 1889**, the inaugural train reached the festively decorated **Klosters station** — the first section of the line (Landquart to Klosters) opened to the public ten days later (delayed by a landslide)
- The full line to **Davos** opened on **21 July 1890**
- In **1895**, the company was renamed the **Rhätische Bahn** (Rhaetian Railway / RhB) as the network expanded across Graubünden
- The **Vereina Tunnel** (1999) later gave Klosters an avalanche-proof rail link to the **Lower Engadin**, transforming the village into a year-round transport hub
- Today the Klosters Platz station sits at the junction of the Prättigau line and the Vereina route — a crossroads of Alpine rail travel

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The monks who cleared the forest** — A child follows Premonstratensian monks from Churwalden over mountain passes to a densely forested valley in 1222; together they build the first church of St. Jakob, and the child discovers why the village that grows around it will be called "Klosters"

2. **The Walser girl and the wooden house** — A young Walser girl migrates with her family from the Valais over high Alpine passes to the Prättigau around 1300; she helps build their new home in the Strickbau log technique and learns the freedoms of the Walserrecht

3. **The avalanche and the prince** — On a snowy March day in 1988, a child skiing on the Gotschnagrat witnesses the terrifying avalanche and helps mountain guide Bruno Sprecher with the rescue; a story about courage, the power of mountains, and why the slopes demand respect

4. **Greta Garbo's secret walks** — A child in 1960s Klosters keeps spotting a mysterious woman in a wide-brimmed hat walking alone along the Landquart river; following her trail leads to the Chesa Grischuna and the discovery that the world's most famous recluse found peace in this quiet village

5. **Kirchner's colors in the snow** — A child finds a hidden sketchbook on the Stafelalp and steps into Kirchner's world of bold Alpine colors; together they paint the Prättigau mountains in expressionist hues, learning to see the everyday landscape with fresh eyes

6. **The first train to Klosters** — In September 1889, a child rides the very first train up the Prättigau valley from Landquart; at each bridge and gorge, the Rhaetian Railway reveals a different Alpine secret, until they arrive at the festively decorated Klosters station

7. **The uprising in the valley** — In 1622, a child in the Prättigau watches as neighbors rise up against Austrian occupation; the story follows the euphoria of liberation and the devastating return of Baldiron's troops, teaching that freedom has a price

8. **Treasure Island in the Prättigau** — A child discovers an old map hidden in a Walser granary in Monbiel; following its clues through snowy mountain paths, they retrace the route Stevenson's carriage took through the Prättigau in 1880, and the map turns out to be a forgotten sketch for Treasure Island
