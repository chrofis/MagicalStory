## 1. Founding & Early History — Reichenau's Lakeside Outpost

- The area around Steckborn was already inhabited in **Roman times** (2nd century AD), with remains of walls and fortifications found nearby
- The first documented mention of Steckborn dates to the **mid-9th century (around 840)**, in connection with the **Monastery of Reichenau** — the powerful Benedictine abbey on the island in the Untersee
- The settlement was likely gifted to Reichenau Abbey around this time, placing it under monastic administration for centuries
- In **1282**, the abbey repurchased Steckborn and its surrounding possessions, consolidating direct control over the town
- Reichenau's abbots served as Steckborn's lords, administering justice and overseeing fortifications — the town was essentially a **lakeside outpost** protecting the abbey's mainland interests
- Steckborn was already a **fortified settlement with walls and gates** before it received formal market privileges, serving as a defensive stronghold for Reichenau's possessions on the south shore of the Untersee

## 2. Geography — Between the Seerücken and the Untersee

- Steckborn sits on the **southwestern shore of the Untersee** (Lower Lake), the smaller western arm of **Lake Constance** (Bodensee)
- The town faces north across the water toward the **Höri peninsula** (Germany) and lies roughly opposite **Reichenau Island**, its medieval overlord's seat
- Behind the town rises the **Seerücken**, a glacial ridge running east-west through Thurgau at roughly **700 meters** elevation, offering panoramic views over the Untersee to the Alps
- The municipality covers **8.76 km²**: about 46.5% forest, 36.1% farmland, and 17.4% built-up area
- The mild lake climate supports **wine-growing** — Steckborn is one of several Untersee villages producing fruity **Müller-Thurgau** wines, smooth Pinot Gris, and other varietals on slopes facing the lake
- Population: approximately **3,840 residents** (2020), making it a small but historically significant town
- Connected by the **Lake Line railway** (Seelinie) running along the Untersee shore between Kreuzlingen and Stein am Rhein

## 3. The Turmhof — Steckborn's Medieval Landmark

- The **Turmhof** (tower house) is Steckborn's most iconic building, a fortified residential tower standing at the heart of the old town near the lakeshore
- Built around **1282** by Reichenau **Abbot Albrecht von Ramstein** (confirmed through dendrochronological dating), though it was long attributed to his successor Abbot Diethelm von Castell
- Originally served as the **seat and residence of the Reichenau abbots** when visiting their mainland possessions — an exempt administrative center with its own jurisdiction
- A **coat-of-arms tower** was added in **1602**, with a heraldic stone dated **1613** and a **baroque roof structure** added in **1615**
- The building passed to the **city of Steckborn in 1639**, served as a **school from 1856 to 1864**, and has housed a **local history museum since 1937**
- The **Museum im Turmhof** today spans three floors covering **6,000 years of history** — from Neolithic pile dwellers to Romans, Alemanni, and modern times
- Special focal points include **stove building** (Kachelöfen), **bobbin lace-making** (Klöppelei), and an extensive **Bernina sewing machine collection**
- A furnished **smithy** and **woodturning workshop** demonstrate historic Steckborn trades
- The Turmhof enjoys **federal heritage protection** as a building of national significance

## 4. The 1313 Market Charter — Birth of a Medieval Town

- In the winter of **1312/1313**, Reichenau **Abbot Diethelm von Castell** traveled to Emperor **Henry VII's** military camp near Florence to petition for market privileges for Steckborn
- On **January 26, 1313**, the emperor issued a charter granting Steckborn the right to hold a **weekly market every Thursday**
- The original charter document is preserved in the **cantonal archive in Frauenfeld**
- Merchants traveling to Steckborn's market received **imperial protection** for themselves and their goods, with a penalty of **10 marks of gold** for anyone hindering the town's market privileges
- The charter gave Steckborn the three defining features of a medieval city: **weekly market, city rights, and fortification walls**
- The town's planned medieval layout forms an **isosceles triangle** with the parish church at the apex and the lakefront as the base
- Steckborn celebrated the **650th anniversary** of its market rights in **1963** (with historical reenactments) and the **700th jubilee in 2013**
- Note: Steckborn technically received **market rights** (Marktrecht), not a formal city charter (Stadtrecht) — a distinction local historians are careful to maintain

## 5. Feldbach Monastery — Cistercian Nuns on the Lake

- **Kloster Feldbach** was a **Cistercian nunnery** founded on a small peninsula just east of Steckborn's old town
- In **1252**, nobleman **Kuno von Feldbach** assigned his castle with fields and vineyard to a sister association from Constance
- In **1253**, **Abbess Sophia** moved with **20 nuns** to Feldbach, where they adopted the strict Cistercian rule
- The abbesses proved clever administrators, building up a **closed court circle** (eigener Gerichtsbezirk) that gave them judicial authority over several surrounding places
- An **early Gothic monastery church** was likely built around **1327**, when the convent received an indulgence
- The monastery survived the **Reformation** — in **1549**, Afra Schmid from the Cistercian Abbey of Magdenau was appointed abbess, leading a revival
- Abbess **Ursula Ott** built the confessor's house in **1613**, still standing today as the "old monastery"
- The church was renovated in a **baroque style in 1764**
- The monastery was **abolished in 1848** by resolution of the Thurgau Grand Council during the wave of Swiss secularization
- In **1853**, the remaining nuns (5 choir women and 4 lay sisters) moved to **Gwiggen** in Vorarlberg
- In **1849**, the municipality of Steckborn purchased the property; in **1895**, a fire destroyed the main building and church
- Today the site is the **See & Park Hotel Feldbach**, with 36 rooms in historic buildings surrounded by an idyllic lakeside park

## 6. Prehistoric Lake Dwellings — UNESCO World Heritage

- The shallow shores of the Untersee around Steckborn were home to **Neolithic and Bronze Age pile-dwelling settlements** (Pfahlbauten), built on wooden platforms raised above the water on stilts
- These settlements date from roughly **5000 to 500 BC** and are among the **111 sites** inscribed as the UNESCO World Heritage Site **"Prehistoric Pile Dwellings around the Alps"** (designated **June 27, 2011**)
- The unique **oxygen-poor lake-bed conditions** preserved organic materials that would normally decay: food remains, wooden tools, textiles, weapons, jewelry, and agricultural equipment — thousands of years old
- Discoveries from Lake Constance pile-dwelling sites include some of the **oldest wooden wheels in the world** (around 3,400 BC) and the **oldest textiles in Europe** (around 3,000 BC)
- Archaeological finds from Steckborn's lakeside settlements are displayed in the **Museum im Turmhof**, forming the opening chapter of its 6,000-year narrative
- The pile dwellings provide remarkable insight into complete **prehistoric villages** and ancient **trade routes** for flint, gold, amber, and pottery across the Alpine region

## 7. The Fires — Destruction and Resilience

- Steckborn's history is marked by devastating fires that reshaped its old town
- The **great fire of 1668** (Stadtbrand) destroyed large parts of the medieval town center, forcing significant rebuilding efforts in the late 17th century
- Much of the town's current architectural character — the mix of **half-timbered houses** (Fachwerkhäuser) and solid stone construction — dates from the reconstruction period after this catastrophe
- In **1895**, a fire destroyed the main building and church of **Feldbach Monastery**, though the southern structures survived — Karl Friedrich Gegauf's sewing machine prototype was famously the only thing rescued from the workshop housed there
- Most recently, on **December 21, 2015**, a **lithium-polymer battery** ignited a devastating fire that engulfed a row of historic houses along the Seestrasse in the old town
- **Six buildings** were completely or partially destroyed, **30 residents** lost their homes, and property damage totaled **six million Swiss francs**
- An architecture competition for the reconstruction was won in 2017 by the Zurich firm **Pater** with their project **"L'aquilino"**, which divides the rebuilding into five distinguishable houses responding to their historical context
- The 2015 fire and its careful reconstruction demonstrated Steckborn's enduring commitment to preserving its **medieval townscape** — the entire village is listed in the **Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites**

## 8. Bobbin Lace-Making — A Lost Textile Tradition

- For roughly **100 years**, Steckborn was a center of **bobbin lace-making** (Klöppelei), a delicate textile craft using multiple threads wound onto spindle-shaped bobbins, crossed in pairs and twisted together
- Lace traders operated from the **Haus zur Glocke**, a mercerie (notions shop) in the old town, bringing raw materials and patterns called **"Klöppelbriefe"** to home workers who needed supplementary income
- The finished lace was sold onward, with Steckborn's lace-makers collaborating with the **hat industry in Wohlen (AG)** for fine trimmings
- The craft eventually declined with industrialization, but its legacy is preserved in the **Turmhof museum's** lace-making collection
- Contemporary art exhibitions at the **Haus zur Glocke** continue to engage with Steckborn's Klöppel heritage, creating a dialogue between traditional craft and modern artistic expression

## 9. Bernina Sewing Machines — From Workshop to World Brand

- In **1890**, **Karl Friedrich Gegauf** (1860–1926) moved to Steckborn and opened an embroidery and mechanical workshop for the manufacture of his invention: a **monogram embroidery machine**
- On **February 27, 1892**, the Patent Office granted him patent No. 4670 for the world's first **hemstitch sewing machine**, capable of **100 stitches per minute** — triggering a textile revolution
- **1893** is considered the official founding year of what would become **BERNINA International**; Gegauf set up production in the old **Feldbach Convent** buildings
- The term **"Gegaufe"** became synonymous with the mechanical production of hemstitching
- By **1900**, the Steckborn workshop employed **70–80 people**
- After Gegauf's death in **1926**, his sons **Fritz** (1893–1980) and **Gustav Gegauf** developed the first household sewing machine, naming it **"Bernina"** (after the Bernina Pass) — it entered production by the end of **1932**
- **1938**: Introduction of the first Bernina zigzag machine
- **1945**: Launch of the world's first **portable zigzag sewing machine** with a free arm
- By mid-**1963**, one million Bernina zigzag machines had been manufactured in Steckborn
- Today, Bernina still maintains its **headquarters and main factory in Steckborn**, alongside a production facility in Thailand — a remarkable example of a global brand rooted in a small Swiss town
- The **Bernina Creative Center** at the Steckborn headquarters offers workshops in sewing, embroidery, textile art, and quilting

## 10. Notable People

- **Karl Friedrich Gegauf** (1860–1926): inventor and entrepreneur, founded what became BERNINA International in Steckborn
- **Fritz Gegauf** (1893–1980): son of Karl Friedrich, developed the first Bernina household sewing machine and built the company into a global brand
- **Susie Wolff** (born 1982): retired professional racing driver, the first woman in 22 years to take part in a Formula 1 race weekend (2014); resident of Steckborn

## 11. Key Landmarks & Heritage

- **Turmhof**: 1282 fortified tower house, now the town museum (federal heritage protection)
- **Wacht- und Pulverturm** (Watch and Powder Tower): medieval defensive tower on the northwestern Seepromenade, first mentioned in **1451**, with a foundation of large glacial boulders; restored in **1907**
- **Reformierte Kirche** (Reformed Church) on Kirchgasse: the parish church at the apex of the medieval town plan, a protected heritage building
- **Haus zur Glocke**: historic mercerie linked to the lace-making tradition, now an art exhibition space
- **Schloss Glarisegg**: a castle estate on the edge of Steckborn, housing a private school and an **intentional community** since 2003, with permaculture gardens and lakeside grounds; listed in the Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites
- **See & Park Hotel Feldbach**: the former Cistercian nunnery, now an atmospheric lakeside hotel
- **Seepromenade**: the scenic lakefront promenade connecting the old town's landmarks, with views across the Untersee to Germany's Höri peninsula
- **Seebadi Steckborn**: the popular lakeside swimming area (Strandbad) on the Untersee

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The abbot's long journey** — Abbot Diethelm travels from the Untersee all the way to the emperor's camp near Florence in winter 1312, carrying Steckborn's hopes for market rights in a leather satchel; a child from the town secretly follows
2. **The pile-dweller's secret** — A child swimming in the Untersee discovers an ancient wooden post sticking out of the lake bed; touching it transports them 5,000 years back to a Bronze Age stilt village, where they must help build a new platform before the autumn floods
3. **The lace-maker's pattern** — A mysterious Klöppelbrief (lace pattern) found in the attic of the Haus zur Glocke turns out to be a map of the old town, with each knot marking a hidden treasure from before the great fire of 1668
4. **Gegauf's last machine** — On the night of the 1895 Feldbach fire, young Karl Friedrich Gegauf must rescue his only hemstitch prototype from the burning convent; the machine seems to guide him through the smoke with its rhythmic clicking
5. **The tower's six thousand years** — A child visiting the Turmhof museum at night finds that each floor comes alive: the ground floor fills with lake water and pile dwellers, the first floor with Roman soldiers, the top floor with medieval knights — and they must find their way back to the present before the museum opens
6. **The watchman of the Pulverturm** — The last watchman of the medieval Watch and Powder Tower refuses to leave his post even as the town modernizes around him; a child befriends him and learns what he has been guarding all these centuries
7. **Nuns on the lake** — Abbess Sophia and her 20 Cistercian sisters arrive at windswept Feldbach in 1253 to find only a ruined castle; a child from the village helps them build their new home, discovering that even the strictest rules cannot silence laughter
8. **The sewing machine that stitched the world together** — A magical Bernina sewing machine in the Steckborn factory can stitch any two places together; a child accidentally sews Steckborn to Tokyo and must find the right thread to bring everyone home
