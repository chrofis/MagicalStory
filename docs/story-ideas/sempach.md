## 1. Geography — A Small Town on a Beautiful Lake

- Sempach lies at about **510 meters** above sea level on the **eastern shore of the Sempachersee** (Lake Sempach), in the district of **Sursee** in the canton of **Lucerne**
- The municipality covers **8.9 km2**, with a population of roughly **4,380** (2024)
- The **Sempachersee** has a surface area of **14.5 km2**, a maximum depth of **87 meters**, and sits at **504 m** elevation. It was formed by the retreating **Reuss Glacier** at the end of the Wurm Ice Age, about 12,000 years ago
- The main inflow is the **Grosse Aa** from the south; the lake drains via the **Suhre** at its northwestern end near Oberkirch, which eventually flows into the Aare
- A small **50-meter island** called **Gamma Insel** sits in the lake east of Sursee — the lake's only island
- The landscape is gentle **Swiss Mittelland** — rolling hills, farmland, and lake shores, with distant views of the Alps on clear days
- The lake and its reed beds are an important **habitat for waterbirds**, which is one reason the Swiss Ornithological Institute chose Sempach as its home
- In **1806**, the lake level was artificially lowered by about **1.7 meters** to reduce flooding and gain farmland. This severed Sempach's direct connection to the shore — the town had originally stood right on the water. Today, expansive green parkland fills the space between town and lake

## 2. Medieval Founding — A Habsburg Town

- Sempach was founded by the **Habsburgs in the 13th century** to control the lakeshore and the former **Gotthard route** — one of the most important north-south trade routes through the Alps
- The town was laid out with an **almost triangular ground plan**, enclosed by **stone walls** with towers and gates
- Key medieval structures survive: the **Luzernertor** (Lucerne Gate), the **Hexenturm** (Witches' Tower, built in the 13th century at the southeast corner of the walls), and sections of the original **town wall**
- The **Rathaus** (Town Hall) was built in **1475** in late-Gothic style. It contained everything a medieval public building needed: a council chamber, an archive room, and a market hall opening onto the main street. A **half-timbered facade** was added in the 17th century, and an upper floor with bay window in the 19th century
- The town is listed in the **ISOS** (Federal Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites) as a site of **national importance**

## 3. The Battle of Sempach (9 July 1386) — Turning Point of Swiss History

- By the 1380s, the city of **Lucerne** was aggressively expanding into Habsburg territory. In January **1386**, Lucerne incorporated the town of Sempach into its **Burgrecht** (civic alliance) — a direct provocation to Habsburg sovereignty
- **Duke Leopold III of Austria** assembled an army of roughly **4,000 men**, including heavily armored knights, nobles, and professional soldiers, to punish the Swiss and reassert Habsburg authority
- The Swiss Confederates fielded about **1,500 infantry** from Lucerne, Uri, Schwyz, and Unterwalden — mostly farmers and townspeople armed with halberds and pikes
- The armies met on **9 July 1386** at **Meiersholz**, near the village of Hildisrieden, northwest of Sempach
- The terrain — narrow, sloping fields — was unsuitable for mounted combat. The Austrian knights **dismounted** and formed a dense wall of pikes. According to chroniclers, they had to **cut off the long tips of their poulaines** (fashionable pointed shoes) because the elongated toes hindered movement on foot
- The Swiss attacked repeatedly but could not break through the Austrian pike wall, suffering mounting casualties
- According to legend, the breakthrough came through the sacrifice of **Arnold von Winkelried** (see section 4), though the exact circumstances remain debated
- The battle demonstrated that Swiss infantry could **defeat heavily armored chivalric elites in the open field**, whether mounted or on foot — a military revolution that echoed across Europe
- The battle was a **decisive Swiss victory**. Duke Leopold III was **killed in combat** along with **676 Austrian dead**, including a margrave, 3 counts, 5 barons, 7 bannerets, and 63 Austrian and Tyrolean knights. The Swiss lost **120 to 200 men**, more than half of them Lucernese
- Leopold's body was brought to **Konigsfelden Abbey** (Aargau), the traditional Habsburg burial church. His remains were later transferred to **St. Blasien** in the Black Forest and ultimately to **St. Paul im Lavanttal** (Carinthia), where they rest today

## 4. Arnold von Winkelried — The Legendary Hero

- According to legend, when the Swiss could not break the Austrian pike wall, a man from **Nidwalden** (Unterwalden) named **Arnold von Winkelried** cried out: *"I will open a passage into the line; protect, dear countrymen and confederates, my wife and children!"*
- He then **threw himself onto the Austrian pikes**, gathering as many lance points into his body as he could, dragging them down. His sacrifice opened a breach through which the Swiss halberdiers poured, overwhelming the Austrians in close combat
- The name Winkelried **first appears in 16th-century sources**. The chronicle of **Aegidius Tschudi** (1536) refers to "a man of Unterwalden, of the Winkelried family"; his final edition (1564) names him "Arnold von Winckelriet, a brave knight." Earlier accounts describe the heroic act but leave the hero **nameless**
- He is listed at the head of the **Nidwalden (Stans) fallen** on the battle's casualty roll as "Herr Arnold von Winckelriet, Ritter"
- His historicity remains **debated**: 19th-century historians accepted it as fact; 20th-century scholars deconstructed it as legend; current scholarship considers it **plausible but unproven**
- The concept of **"Winkelriedism"** — an individual sacrificing themselves idealistically for the common good — entered the German language as a philosophical concept derived from the legend
- A grand **Winkelried Monument** in Carrara marble, carved by sculptor **Ferdinand Schloth**, was inaugurated in **Stans** on 3 September **1865** with the entire Federal Council in attendance — it served as an act of **federal reconciliation** after the Sonderbund War of 1847
- On the Sempach battlefield itself, the **Winkelriedstein** monument (1864) marks the legendary site of his sacrifice
- The English Romantic poet **James Montgomery** immortalized the legend in his poem *"Arnold von Winkelried"*, with the famous line: *"Make way for liberty!"*

## 5. The Schlachtkapelle (Battle Chapel)

- According to tradition, a memorial to Duke Leopold and the fallen Austrian nobles was erected **one year after the battle** at the spot where the duke was killed
- The actual chapel was built in **1472/73** and has undergone multiple renovations: **1551-1554**, **1638-1641**, **1747**, **1885-1886**, and **1985-1986**
- Originally built by the **Habsburgs** to remember their dead, the chapel became a Swiss place of worship after Sempach came under Lucerne's control in **1415**
- In the mid-16th century, a prominent **battle fresco** was painted on the interior walls. During the 1638/41 renovation, Lucerne painter **Hans Ulrich Wagmann** and his circle significantly restored and updated the fresco
- An **ossuary** (bone house) was built in **1594** to preserve bones recovered from the battlefield
- The chapel sits along the **Sempach-Hildisrieden road** on the actual battlefield, surrounded by three stone crosses. Together with the Winkelriedstein and a guesthouse, it forms a solemn memorial landscape

## 6. The Sempacherbrief (1393) — Europe's Early Laws of War

- After the Swiss victories at Sempach (1386) and **Nafels** (1388, where Glarus defeated the Habsburgs), the **eight cantons** plus Solothurn signed the **Sempacherbrief** on **10 July 1393**
- It was the **first treaty uniting all eight members** of the Old Swiss Confederacy
- Key provisions: no canton could **unilaterally start a war** without the consent of the others; members agreed to **preserve peace** in their territories, assist each other militarily, and submit to **arbitration** in disputes
- The Sempacherbrief established the **first rules for the conduct of troops in war** — an early precursor to international humanitarian law
- It laid the groundwork for the **Tagsatzung** (federal diet), which developed in the 15th century as the Confederation's governing assembly
- The document represents a critical step from a loose alliance of convenience to a **structured confederacy** with shared obligations

## 7. The Luzernertor (Lucerne Gate) and Town Fortifications

- The **Luzernertor** is Sempach's most recognizable landmark — a medieval gate tower with a striking **pointed spire** that dominates the southern view of the old town
- The original stone gatehouse was built in the **13th century**, standing about **7.5 meters high** with internal stairs and a sloped roof
- In the first half of the **17th century**, the roof was replaced with the current **pyramidal tower design**, and a **town clock** was installed
- In **1911**, painter **Seraphin Weingartner** added a fresco to the gate's facade
- The gate was originally reached by a **bridge over the town moat** — the southern entrance to the walled town
- Nearby, the **Hexenturm** (Witches' Tower) at the southeast corner of the walls is another surviving element of the medieval defenses. Its name evokes the darker chapters of early modern history, though its exact connection to witch trials is undocumented

## 8. Churches — From Romanesque to Neoclassical

### Pfarrkirche St. Martin, Kirchbuhl
- The older of Sempach's two churches, standing on a terrace southeast of the old town on the site of a **Roman estate**
- First documented in **1275** in a tithe register of the Diocese of Konstanz, but archaeological evidence dates human presence and a church on this site to the **early 11th century**
- The **Romanesque-Gothic** building was probably constructed in the **12th century** on Roman foundations
- The interior walls are **completely covered with frescoes** dating from around **1300**, plus three Gothic altars
- When the newer St. Stefan took over as parish church in 1832, St. Martin was spared from Baroque or neoclassical modernization — a 20th-century restoration focused on recovering the original medieval state

### Pfarrkirche St. Stefan
- The old city church was declared beyond repair in **1803**; construction of a replacement was delayed by rejected proposals, the builder's death, location disputes, and the famine years following the **1816 "Year Without a Summer"**
- The new church was finally consecrated on **27 August 1831** in **neoclassical style**, designed by the brothers **Handli** and **Jost Kopp**
- The church stands on a large square at the top of the town's main street — the civic and spiritual heart of Sempach
- A large **national monument** commemorating the Battle of Sempach was erected in front of the church in **1886**, on the 500th anniversary
- The old church was demolished in **1832** to make way for the new building

## 9. The Vogelwarte Sempach (Swiss Ornithological Institute)

- Founded on **6 April 1924** by **Alfred Schifferli Sr.**, a local accountant and passionate amateur ornithologist who had been observing birds in the region since 1902
- It began as a modest **bird ringing station** with an observation tower near Sempach, dedicated to researching bird migration through the Alpine region
- In **1954**, the institute was converted into an **independent foundation** under Swiss law
- It has grown from a **one-man volunteer operation** into an internationally recognized research center with over **130 employees** and more than **2,000 volunteers**
- The institute monitors populations of all bird species in Switzerland, researches lifestyles of endangered species, tracks bird migration, and rings over **110,000 birds annually** from more than 170 species
- Its library is one of the most extensive collections of **ornithological literature** in Europe
- In **2015**, a striking new **visitor center** opened on the lakeshore, designed by architecture firm **:mlzd** — two polygonal cubes of **rammed earth** connected by a glazed foyer, making it **Switzerland's first three-story rammed-earth building** (Minergie P-Eco certified). It features interactive exhibits, a bird cinema, a mechanical theatre about birdsong, and aviaries on the lake shore
- The Vogelwarte is unique in being a **private foundation** — in most other countries, national ornithological institutes are state-run

## 10. The Sempacher Gedenkfeier (Battle Commemoration)

- After the victory on 9 July 1386, Lucerne's **Great Council declared the day a public holiday**
- Since the late 15th century, an **annual commemoration** has been held at the Schlachtkapelle, making it one of Switzerland's oldest continuous civic traditions
- The Canton of Lucerne organizes the ceremony, which now takes place on a **designated Sunday** near the anniversary (not always July 9 itself), alternating biennially
- The day begins at **8:45 AM** with the **Morgenbrot** (morning bread): women in medieval dress distribute roughly **1,100 bags** containing bread, cheese, butter, and jam — echoing the tradition that the Confederates ate their morning meal in the Meiersholz forest before the battle
- A ceremonial **procession** of Sempach warriors, flag bearers, and musicians marches through the town, followed by an **ecumenical service** in St. Stefan church
- A **keynote speech** is given by a historian or public figure; Federal Councillors have attended on numerous occasions
- Each year, a different canton is invited as **guest canton**, reinforcing the ceremony's role as a symbol of Swiss unity

## 11. The Rathausmuseum — History in the Town Hall

- The 1475 Rathaus was extensively restored and reopened in **2014** as the **Rathausmuseum**
- Across **four floors**, the multimedia exhibition covers the political, economic, social, and religious life of Sempach from the **late Middle Ages to the present**
- The **attic floor** is dedicated to the Battle of Sempach and the culture of remembrance: the current state of historical knowledge about the battle, the myth of Arnold von Winkelried, and the evolution of the annual Gedenkfeier
- The **late-Gothic council chamber** on the first floor was used for council meetings, court sessions, banquets, and festivities
- The museum provides a compact but thorough portrait of how a small Swiss town experienced 600 years of history

## 12. Notable Historical Connections

- **1415**: Sempach came under the control of **Lucerne** — nearly 30 years after the battle — as Lucerne gradually expanded its territory through the establishment of bailiwicks
- **1389**: A **seven-year peace** was signed in Vienna after the twin Swiss victories at Sempach and Nafels, leaving the Confederation in undisputed possession of all territory gained in the war
- **1388, Battle of Nafels**: Two years after Sempach, the men of **Glarus** (reinforced by Schwyz) defeated a Habsburg army at the northern entrance to their valley, cementing the Confederation's independence
- **1940**: During World War II, Sempach served as an **internment location** for French soldiers who had crossed into Switzerland
- The town's connection to the **Gotthard route** — the great medieval trade and pilgrimage road from northern Europe to Italy — gave this small settlement outsized strategic importance long before the famous battle
- The battles of **Morgarten** (1315), **Sempach** (1386), and **Nafels** (1388) form the three great military foundations of Swiss independence — all victories of peasant infantry over Habsburg feudal armies
- Sempach station lies on the main **Lucerne-Olten railway line**, about 2 miles north of the old town, connecting this small historic community to the Swiss rail network

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The boy who opened the way** — Retelling of the Winkelried legend from a child's perspective: a young page watches the hero's sacrifice and must carry the news to his family in Stans
2. **The duke's lost shoe** — A comedic tale about the Austrian knights cutting off their ridiculous poulaines before the battle; a child finds one of the discarded shoe tips and follows it back through the story of what happened that day
3. **Morning bread at the battlefield** — A child attending the Gedenkfeier bites into a piece of bread and is transported to 1386, sharing breakfast with the Swiss soldiers in the Meiersholz forest before the battle
4. **The birds who remember** — A child at the Vogelwarte discovers that the oldest stork knows the story of the battle, passed down through generations of birds who witnessed it from above the lake
5. **The chapel that changed sides** — The Schlachtkapelle was built by the Habsburgs to mourn their dead, then became Swiss. A child meets the ghost of a young Austrian page and a young Swiss soldier, and must help them make peace
6. **The gate at the end of the world** — The Luzernertor opens to a different century each time the clock strikes midnight. A child steps through and must find their way back through medieval Sempach
7. **Sempachersee's secret island** — The tiny Gamma Insel in the lake holds a treasure map. A child paddles out and discovers clues that lead through the old town, the Hexenturm, and the Kirchbuhl church with its 700-year-old frescoes
8. **Alfred and the curlew** — Based on the true story of Vogelwarte founder Alfred Schifferli and his hand-reared Eurasian curlew: a child in 1924 helps the eccentric birdwatcher build his first observation tower and learns to listen to birdsong
