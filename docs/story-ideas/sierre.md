## 1. Geography — The Town of a Hundred Hills

- Sierre (German: **Siders**) sits at **528 metres** elevation in the middle **Rhône valley** (vallée du Rhône), on the right bank of the river, in the canton of **Valais/Wallis**
- Area: **19.2 km²**; population approximately **18,000**
- The Romans called it **"the town of a hundred hills"** (la ville aux cent collines) — the distinctive hilly terrain of the valley floor derives from a massive **postglacial rock avalanche** that collapsed from the fractured slopes to the north, leaving a landscape of scattered mounds and knolls
- On these hills, medieval **castles and fortified settlements** grew up: the Gerunden, **Vieux-Sierre** (Old Sierre), and Plantzette hills each had a castle serving as the bishop's seat and refuge for the population
- The surrounding slopes are covered in **terraced vineyards** climbing the south-facing hillsides, forming one of the most striking wine landscapes in Switzerland
- The Rhône valley here is sheltered between the **Bernese Alps** to the north and the **Valais Alps** to the south, creating a dry inner-Alpine climate with remarkably little rainfall

## 2. Cité du Soleil — The Sunniest City in Switzerland

- Sierre is nicknamed the **Cité du Soleil** (City of the Sun / Sonnenstadt), the sunniest town in Switzerland
- The city enjoys approximately **2,170 hours of sunshine** per year — roughly **300 sunny days** annually
- The exceptional sunshine results from the unique geography: the Rhône valley sits in an inner-Alpine rain shadow, with the surrounding mountain ranges releasing most precipitation on their outer slopes, leaving Sierre's valley dry and sun-drenched
- The municipal **coat of arms** (blason) is: **"Gules, a Sun in Splendour Or"** — a golden sun with a human face and alternating straight and wavy rays on a red field, depicted as early as the **Schalbetter map** printed by Sebastian Münster in **1545**
- The sun imagery permeates the town: street names, festivals, and the local identity are shaped by this solar reputation

## 3. Early History — From Sidrium to Siders

- First documented mention: around **800 AD** as **Sidrium**; a 12th-century document claims the settlement was founded as early as **515 AD**, when King **Sigismund of Burgundy** gave the estate at Sierre to the **Abbey of Saint-Maurice** as a fief
- Later recorded as **Sirro** (1179) and **Syder** (1393)
- In **999**, King **Rudolph III of Burgundy** granted all county rights to the **Bishop of Sion** (évêque de Sion), who became prince-bishop — making the bishop the temporal ruler of the entire Valais, including Sierre
- By the 11th century, the fief of Sierre was directly owned by the Bishop of Sion, and the **vidomne** (vidame) — a lay officer administering church property and dispensing justice — governed on the bishop's behalf
- The region was inhabited long before written records: traces of **Ligurians, Celts, and Romans** have been found throughout the area

## 4. Medieval Castles and the Wars of the 14th Century

- Medieval Sierre was defined by its hilltop castles: the **Château de Vieux-Sierre** (episcopal castle, cited 1299), the castle on the **Plantzette** hill, and the fortress on the **Gerunden** hill
- In the mid-**14th century**, a bitter conflict erupted between the **noble families loyal to the Bishop** and the **Zenden** (districts) of the Upper Valais allied with the **Counts of Savoy**
- The castles were **razed** during these wars; the demolished fortifications and villages were abandoned, and most residents settled further north in **Plan-Sierre**, the flat area that became the modern town centre
- The only castle to survive: the **Tour de Goubing** (Goubing Tower), southeast of Sierre, belonging to the lords of Granges — cited as early as **1297** and later passing through the De Chevron, De Platéa, and De Courten families
- The episcopal castle at Vieux-Sierre was destroyed again in **1417** during the **Rarogne War** (Guerre de Rarogne); rebuilt briefly, it stood in ruins again by the late 1500s
- **Château des Vidomnes** — Built around **1490** by **Pertemand de Chevron**, it is a striking quadrangular tower on three floors with **four corner turrets** topped by pepperpot roofs (toits en poivrière). Its distinctive **ochre-orange** colour makes it unique among Swiss castles. The vidomnat passed to the **De Montheÿs** and then the **De Courten** families. Today privately inhabited and not open to the public, it stands at **Rue du Bourg 24**

## 5. Rainer Maria Rilke and the Château de Muzot

- The Bohemian-Austrian poet **Rainer Maria Rilke** (1875–1926), one of the most significant poets of the 20th century, spent his final five years in the Sierre region
- In **1921**, Rilke arrived in Sierre with his companion and discovered an advertisement for a small medieval château available to rent: the **Château de Muzot** (also Maison de Muzot), a **13th-century fortified manor house** near the village of **Veyras**, on the hillside above Sierre
- Swiss merchant and arts patron **Werner Reinhart** purchased the château and invited Rilke to live there **rent-free** — providing the poet with the solitude and peacefulness he desperately needed
- The château lacked gas and electricity; Rilke lived and worked by candlelight in this austere medieval tower

### The Creative Storm of February 1922
- After nearly a decade of creative paralysis — worsened by **World War I** and conscription — Rilke experienced an extraordinary burst of inspiration at Muzot
- Between **2 and 5 February 1922**: he wrote nearly all 26 sonnets of **Part I** of the *Sonnets to Orpheus* (Les Sonnets à Orphée)
- Between **7 and 15 February**: he completed the **Duino Elegies** (Duineser Elegien), ten long philosophical poems he had begun in **1912** at **Duino Castle** on the Adriatic, as a guest of Princess Marie von Thurn und Taxis
- Between **16 and 22 February**: he completed **Part II** of the *Sonnets to Orpheus*
- Both masterworks were published in **1923** and are considered the pinnacle of Rilke's art

### French Poetry in Valais
- Living in French-speaking Valais, Rilke began writing directly in French — a remarkable achievement for a German-language poet
- He composed **Les Quatrains Valaisans** (The Valaisian Quatrains, 1924), a cycle addressed to the landscape of Valais, dedicated to his Sierre friend **Jeanne de Sépibus-de Preux**
- Other French cycles written at Muzot: **Vergers** (Orchards), **Les Roses**, and **Les Fenêtres** (The Windows) — all published 1926
- His Parisian friends **André Gide**, **Edmond Jaloux**, and **Romain Rolland** received the French poems warmly

### Death and the Legend of the Rose
- Rilke had been ill since **1923**, suffering from fatigue, mouth ulcers, and weight loss — later diagnosed as **leukaemia**
- A famous legend: while gathering roses for a visitor, the Egyptian beauty **Nimet Eloui Bey**, Rilke pricked his hand on a thorn; the wound refused to heal, his arm swelled, and so he died. In reality, his compromised immune system from leukaemia made him vulnerable to even minor infections
- He died on **29 December 1926** at the **Val-Mont sanatorium** above Montreux
- Buried **2 January 1927** in the churchyard of **Raron** (Rarogne), a village in the Valais he had chosen himself, at the foot of the medieval **Burgkirche** (castle church) on its dramatic cliff
- His self-composed epitaph: **"Rose, oh reiner Widerspruch, Lust, Niemandes Schlaf zu sein unter soviel Lidern"** (Rose, oh pure contradiction, delight of being no one's sleep under so many lids)

### The Fondation Rilke
- Established **1 October 1986** in Sierre; the museum occupies the **Maison de Courten**, an elegant patrician house at Rue du Bourg 30, built in **1769** by **Jean-Antoine-Adrien de Courten**
- Holdings include over **25,000 objects**: manuscripts, letters, photographs, and personal items
- A new permanent exhibition opened in **March 2026** for the **centenary of Rilke's death**

## 6. Wine Capital of Switzerland — The Château de Villa

- Sierre calls itself the **capitale du vin suisse** (capital of Swiss wine) — it sits at the heart of the **Valais wine region**, the largest wine-producing canton in Switzerland
- The surrounding vineyards produce celebrated Valais varieties: **Fendant** (Chasselas), **Johannisberg** (Sylvaner), **Petite Arvine**, **Cornalin**, **Humagne Rouge**, and **Heida** (Païen)
- **Château de Villa** — a **17th-century patrician residence** in the old town, now houses a wine complex: a **wine bar**, a **speciality restaurant**, and the Sierre branch of the **Musée valaisan de la Vigne et du Vin** (Valais Wine and Vine Museum)
- The museum occupies **two sites**: temporary exhibitions at the Château de Villa in Sierre, and a permanent exhibition in the wine village of **Salgesch** (Salquenen), connected by a **six-kilometre wine trail** (sentier viticole) through the vineyards
- Over **650 wines** from Valais producers are available for tasting at the Château de Villa
- **VINEA** — founded in **1994**, this is one of Switzerland's premier wine events. The annual **Salon VINEA** brings together wine producers from across Switzerland's six wine-growing regions. The **30th edition** is planned for **November 2026** in Sierre

## 7. The Bisses — Medieval Irrigation Channels

- The **bisses** (German: Suonen) are historic irrigation channels unique to Valais, built between the **13th and 20th centuries** to divert meltwater from mountain rivers to pastures, orchards, and vineyards
- Construction was perilous: channels were carved into cliffsides hundreds of metres high using only picks, shovels, timber, and rope — some builders lost their lives
- Communities formed **consortages** (associations of water users) governed by statutes regulating construction, maintenance, and water distribution — a remarkable example of medieval communal self-governance
- At their peak in the early 1900s, over **200 bisses** totalling approximately **1,800 kilometres** operated across Valais; a 2018 survey found **188** still extant
- The **Bisse de Clavau**, built in **1453** by the Chapter of the Cathedral of Sion, runs approximately **8 kilometres** from the Lienne river (at 702 m) to the Sionne (at 560 m), irrigating vineyards of Ayent, Grimisuat, and Sion. It features **enormous drystone walls**, corbelled structures, overhanging wooden-beam sections, and a short tunnel — showcasing nearly every construction technique used for bisses in Valais
- Today, many bisses have been converted into **popular hiking trails** (sentiers des bisses), combining cultural heritage with scenic walking through vine-covered hillsides

## 8. The French-German Language Border

- Sierre is the **last French-speaking town** in the Rhône valley before the language shifts to German
- The **Raspille** stream (Raspille/Raspillibach) between Sierre and **Salgesch** marks the official **French-German language border** (frontière linguistique), dividing the **Bas-Valais** (Lower Valais, French-speaking) from the **Haut-Valais** (Upper Valais, German-speaking/Walliserdeutsch)
- The **Pfynwald / Bois de Finges** (Forêt de Finges), a **10 km² nature reserve** of national importance between Sierre and Leuk, historically formed a natural barrier that reinforced the linguistic separation — the dense forest had a fearsome reputation for harbouring **robbers and highwaymen**, discouraging passage between the two halves of the canton
- Remarkably, until the **end of the 19th century**, Sierre was predominantly **German-speaking**, and even Sion had a large German-speaking population — the language border has **shifted westward** over the centuries
- Rural populations historically spoke **Franco-Provençal patois** rather than standard French — this dialect is now steadily disappearing
- Rilke himself was drawn to this linguistic borderland, frequently walking through the Pfyn-Finges forest, which he compared to music, painting, and sculpture

## 9. The Funiculaire Sierre–Montana–Crans

- The funicular company was established on **29 September 1908**; operations began on **28 December 1911** with a track gauge of 1,000 mm
- Originally built in **two segments** meeting at **Saint-Maurice-de-Laques** (1,074 m), because the 4.2 km cable was too long for a single section in 1911 — passengers transferred between the two funiculars. The journey took **one hour**, replacing a **four-hour mule ride** up to the resort
- Since **1997**, the line has operated as a **single section** of approximately **4.2 km** — making it the **longest funicular in Europe**
- Route: from the lower station near **Sierre railway station** (540 m) to the **Crans-Montana plateau** (1,471 m) — a height gain of **927 metres**
- After a nine-month renovation, the funicular was **fully renewed** and reopened on **11 December 2022**, now completing the journey in **13 minutes** at a speed of 8 m/s
- Operated by **CIE-SMC** (Compagnie du Chemin de Fer et d'Autobus Sierre-Montana-Crans SA)
- The funicular opened the way for Crans-Montana to develop into one of Switzerland's premier ski and golf resorts

## 10. Lac de Géronde and the Monastery Hill

- **Lac de Géronde** — a small lake (0.055 km²) at 523 m elevation, less than a **12-minute walk** from Sierre station, surrounded by hills and vineyards
- The lake was once a **branch of the wild Rhône** river before the river was channelled
- The **Géronde hill** above the lake is one of Sierre's most historically layered sites — archaeological vestiges testify to **ancient human presence** dating back thousands of years, making it one of the earliest inhabited sites in the Valais
- The **Monastery of Géronde** (Couvent de Géronde) on the hill has housed a remarkable succession of religious orders: **Augustinian Canons** (before 1233–1331), **Carthusians** (1331–c. 1355), **Carmelites** (1425–1644), **Jesuits** (1651–1660), **Trappists** (1803–1805 and 1831–1834), **Dominicans** (1870–1873), and an institute for deaf-mutes
- Since **2 May 1935**, the monastery has been home to **Bernardine Cistercian nuns** (Bernardines), who cultivate **1.3 hectares of vineyards** — their wine production generates approximately two-thirds of the convent's revenue
- In **1980**, a fire during roofing work destroyed the 14th-century roof; the sisters were temporarily housed in military barracks during restoration
- Today the lake offers a **public bathing beach** (Bains de Géronde) with a swimming pool, waterslide, and a 1.8 km walking trail around the lake — a cherished local swimming spot in summer

## 11. The Napoleonic Period and the Battle of Finges

- In **1798**, French revolutionary troops invaded Switzerland, ending the old regime
- The Upper Valais mounted fierce resistance; Sierre was occupied by **French and Vaudois troops** in **1799** and became the **French military headquarters** in the region
- The decisive engagement was the **Battle of Finges** (Bataille du Bois de Finges / Pfynwaldschlacht), fought on **27–28 May 1799** in the Pfynwald forest east of Sierre
- Upper Valais forces led by **Ferdinand Venetz** made a final stand in the forest but were defeated by the French, ending organised resistance in the Valais
- The battle was one of the bloodiest episodes of the French invasion of Switzerland, fought in the same forest that had long served as the natural boundary between the two halves of the canton

## 12. Notable Historical Facts

- **515**: Estate of Sierre given by King Sigismund of Burgundy to the Abbey of Saint-Maurice — one of the earliest documented references to the area
- **999**: Bishop of Sion becomes prince-bishop and temporal ruler of the Valais
- **c. 1900**: Sierre transitions from an agricultural centre into a town of **industry and services**, while maintaining its wine-growing identity
- **1911**: Opening of the Sierre–Montana–Crans funicular, transforming access to the mountains above
- **1921–1926**: Rilke's years at the Château de Muzot — the most celebrated literary chapter in the town's history
- **1986**: Founding of the **Fondation Rilke** in Sierre
- **1994**: First edition of **VINEA**, establishing Sierre's role as a national wine event centre
- **2022**: Reopening of the fully modernised funicular — longest in Europe

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The poet and the rose garden** — A child discovers Rilke's ghost tending roses at the Château de Muzot; each rose whispers a line of poetry, and the child must gather the petals to complete the lost final sonnet before dawn
2. **The sun that stayed** — Sierre's golden sun from the coat of arms comes alive and refuses to set; a child must journey across the vine-covered hills to convince the sun that the vineyards need the night too, visiting each castle ruin along the way
3. **The water that climbs mountains** — A child follows a drop of water along the Bisse de Clavau, meeting the medieval builders (consortiers) who carved the channel into the cliff face in 1453, learning about courage and community
4. **The language bridge** — At the Raspille stream, a child discovers they can speak both French and German; they must cross the Pfynwald forest — once feared for its bandits — to deliver an urgent message between the two halves of the valley
5. **The nuns who make wine** — A child visits the Bernardine sisters at Géronde and helps harvest grapes from their ancient vineyard, discovering the monastery's eight centuries of different inhabitants — from Carthusian monks to nuns who make altar bread
6. **The funicular to the stars** — The Sierre–Montana funicular doesn't stop at Crans-Montana but keeps climbing into the clouds; a child rides it through 1911, when mules carried travellers, and emerges in the snowy peaks where the resort was born
7. **The hundred hills** — A child discovers that each of Sierre's hills holds a sleeping castle; to wake the town's forgotten history, they must climb all the hills before the sun sets, finding one hidden story on each mound left by the ancient landslide
8. **Rilke's February** — A child finds an unfinished manuscript at the Fondation Rilke museum; stepping into the pages, they enter the Château de Muzot in February 1922, where Rilke is writing feverishly by candlelight, and must help him find the final words of the Duino Elegies before the inspiration fades
