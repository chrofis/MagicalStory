## 1. Geography — The Upper Engadine Lake Plateau

- St. Moritz (Romansh: **San Murezzan**) sits at **1,856 m** above sea level in the **Upper Engadine** (Oberengadin), a high Alpine valley in the canton of **Graubünden** (Grisons)
- The municipality covers about **29 km²** with a population of roughly **5,000** inhabitants
- The town is divided into four areas: **St. Moritz-Dorf** (the village centre), **St. Moritz-Bad** (the spa quarter), **Suvretta**, and **Champfèr**
- The Upper Engadine is a rare **flat, glaciated plateau** at nearly 1,800 m, containing a chain of lakes running southwest to northeast: **Lej da Segl** (Lake Sils, the largest natural Alpine lake above 1,000 m at 4.1 km²), **Lej da Silvaplauna** (Lake Silvaplana), **Lej da Champfèr**, and **Lej da San Murezzan** (Lake St. Moritz)
- St. Moritz lies on the southern slopes of the **Albula Alps** below **Piz Nair** (3,057 m), overlooking the lake plateau
- The valley was historically **Romansh-speaking** — part of the ancient Rhaeto-Romanic culture. By 2000, only 4.7% of St. Moritz's residents still spoke Romansh, with German dominant (58.8%) and Italian second (21.8%)

## 2. The Healing Springs — 3,500 Years of Sacred Water

- The **Mauritius mineral spring** (named after the town's patron saint) has been used since the **late Bronze Age**, around **1411 BC** — dated by dendrochronology and radiocarbon analysis of wooden headworks found at the spring site
- **Archaeological finds** at the spring include Bronze Age **votive offerings, swords, and needles**, indicating the Celts venerated the water as sacred
- The spring's wooden capture system — two hollowed **larch trunks** surrounded by plank-and-log construction — is considered the **oldest preserved prehistoric wooden structure in Europe**, confirmed by the Archaeological Service of Graubünden in 1998
- The water is exceptionally rich in **iron** and dissolved **carbon dioxide (CO₂)**, giving it a sparkling, slightly metallic taste. The carbonic acid penetrates the skin when bathing, stimulating circulation; drinking it was credited with aiding digestion
- In **1535**, the physician **Paracelsus** visited St. Moritz and praised the springs as among the **finest healing waters in Europe**, establishing the town's medical reputation
- In the Middle Ages, **Pope Leo X** chronicled the springs' beneficial properties
- Health pilgrims arrived in growing numbers after the first **guesthouse** was built beside the springs in **1832**; by 1859, some 450 guests could be accommodated
- The name "St. Moritz" derives from **Saint Maurice** (Latin: Mauritius), the 3rd-century Egyptian commander of the Roman **Theban Legion**, martyred in Switzerland for refusing to persecute Christians. The town's church is dedicated to him
- Today, the **Forum Paracelsus** (restored 2014) houses the spring and a museum, and visitors can still taste the healing water at a drinking fountain

## 3. The Birth of Winter Tourism — Badrutt's Famous Bet (1864)

- Before 1864, St. Moritz was exclusively a **summer spa destination** — guests came for the healing waters, then left before the harsh Alpine winter
- **Johannes Badrutt** (1819–1889) was a pioneering hotelier who in **1856** acquired the small Pension Faller and transformed it into the **Engadiner Kulm** (today's Kulm Hotel St. Moritz), the town's first proper hotel
- In the autumn of **1864**, Badrutt made a legendary wager with four of his **British summer guests**: he invited them to return in December, promising that on clear winter days they could enjoy sunshine on the hotel terrace without a jacket. If they were disappointed, he would **reimburse all their travel and accommodation costs**
- The Englishmen accepted — and **stayed until Easter**, captivated by the brilliant Alpine winter sunshine and the sparkling snow
- When they returned to England, they spread the word, and a **new travel trend** was born: the Alpine winter holiday
- St. Moritz became **Europe's first winter resort**, transforming from a quiet spa village into an international playground for the wealthy
- Badrutt was also a technological pioneer: after visiting the **1878 Paris World Exhibition** and seeing the Jablochkoff arc lamp, he built a **hydroelectric plant** and installed electric lights in the Kulm Hotel's dining room on **18 July 1879** — making St. Moritz one of the **first places in Switzerland with electric light**. By 1880, only 160 such lamps existed worldwide; eight were at the Kulm

## 4. Luxury Hotels — Palace, Kulm, and Grand Hotel des Bains

- The **Kulm Hotel** (founded 1856 by Johannes Badrutt) remains one of the most storied Alpine hotels. It hosted events during the **1928 and 1948 Winter Olympics**, and its golf course (opened 1896) was the **highest in the Alps**
- In **1889**, the Kulm introduced **Switzerland's first public telephone network** with 24 local connections
- **Badrutt's Palace Hotel** was opened in **1896** by **Caspar Badrutt** (Johannes's son), who hired the Zurich architects **Chiodera & Tschudi** to design the grand building. It became the symbol of St. Moritz luxury, hosting guests like **Marlene Dietrich, Audrey Hepburn, Alfred Hitchcock**, and **Gunter Sachs**
- The **Grand Hotel des Bains** (today Kempinski) stands near the Mauritius mineral spring in St. Moritz-Bad, continuing the spa tradition
- These grand hotels shaped the image of St. Moritz as a destination for royalty, film stars, and the international elite — a reputation maintained for over 150 years

## 5. The Cresta Run — The Legendary Ice Track (Since 1885)

- In the winter of **1884/85**, the first Cresta Run was built near the hamlet of **Cresta** in the municipality of Celerina, financed by **Johannes and Caspar Badrutt** and the Outdoor Amusement Committee of the Kulm Hotel
- A 22-year-old Swiss geometrician named **Peter Bonorand** designed the course using mathematical calculations to plot a route from St. Moritz to Celerina
- The run was completed in **January 1885** after nearly nine weeks of construction
- The first **"Grand National Toboggan Race"** took place on **16 January 1885**, with a winner named Mr. Austin reaching average speeds above 40 km/h
- The **St. Moritz Tobogganing Club (SMTC)** was formally established in **1887** by British winter sports enthusiasts
- Length: approximately **1,212 metres** with a drop of **157 metres** (514 feet)
- At the first three Grand Nationals (1885–1887), competitors raced **seated**. By 1892, the head-first "skeleton" style on lightweight "America" sleds dominated — giving birth to the sport of **skeleton racing**
- The most infamous corner is **Shuttlecock** — riders who are ejected there automatically become members of the exclusive **Shuttlecock Club** and may wear the Shuttlecock tie
- The Cresta Run season begins just before Christmas and runs for about ten weeks, with riding every day except Christmas Day
- The track is **rebuilt entirely from natural ice** each winter — no artificial refrigeration

## 6. Two Winter Olympics — 1928 and 1948

### The 1928 Winter Olympics
- Held in **February 1928**, with athletes from **25 nations** competing in six sports
- The **Olympia Bob Run** (St. Moritz–Celerina), inaugurated in **1904**, hosted the bobsled events — it is the **oldest bobsled run still in use** and the world's **last remaining natural ice track**
- **Skeleton** debuted as an Olympic sport on the **Cresta Run** — the only time it appeared at the Games until 2002
- The **weather** caused chaos: temperatures reached 25°C, melting the ice. The 10,000 m speed skating event had to be cancelled mid-race, and bobsled was shortened to two runs
- **Billy Fiske**, the American bobsled pilot, was just **16 years old** when his team won gold — one of the youngest Winter Olympic gold medallists ever

### The 1948 Winter Olympics
- Held **30 January – 8 February 1948**, the first Winter Games after World War II (the 1936 Games having been the last)
- St. Moritz was chosen again partly because Switzerland had been **neutral during the war** and its infrastructure was intact
- The Cresta Run again hosted skeleton; the Olympia Bob Run hosted bobsled
- These Games were the **first Winter Olympics to be broadcast on television**

## 7. The Frozen Lake — White Turf, Snow Polo, and Cricket on Ice

- **Lake St. Moritz** freezes solid each winter with ice **40–80 cm thick**, transforming into one of the world's most spectacular natural sporting arenas

### White Turf (since 1907)
- Founded in **1907**, White Turf is an international horse racing event held on the frozen lake over three Sundays in February
- The inaugural events featured **Skikjöring** — a daring sport where skiers are towed by riderless thoroughbreds at high speed across the ice
- **Trotting races** were added soon after, and **flat races** from **1911**
- White Turf is the **only horse race in the world held on a frozen lake**, attracting thoroughbreds and jockeys from across Europe

### Snow Polo (since 1985)
- Polo first came to St. Moritz in **1899**, introduced by stationed **English soldiers** who created the first polo field
- The **St. Moritz Polo Club** was founded in **1959** by local residents
- In **1985**, the **world's first polo tournament on snow** was held on the frozen lake — an invention that launched a global trend (now played in Aspen, Cortina, Kitzbühel, and elsewhere)
- Hotelier **Reto Gaudenzi** was instrumental: he formed the first Swiss polo team in **1978**, and by 1983 Switzerland played its first international match
- The Snow Polo World Cup St. Moritz remains the most prestigious snow polo event worldwide

### Cricket on Ice (since 1989)
- In **1988**, Anthony Bouchier (working for the St. Moritz Tourist Board), Simon Doggart, and John Sellars tested the concept of cricket on the frozen lake
- The first official match — **Eton vs. Harrow** with 25 players — took place in **1989**
- The **St. Moritz Cricket Club** was formalised in **1990** with a three-way tournament
- The annual Cricket on Ice event has become one of Switzerland's most distinctive sporting traditions

## 8. The Sun Logo — 322 Days of Sunshine

- St. Moritz enjoys an average of **322 sunny days per year**, thanks to its high altitude and the Engadine's dry, continental climate — locals call it the "sunny high valley"
- The famous **St. Moritz sun logo** was created in **1932** by Zurich graphic designer **Walter Herdeg** (1908–1995), who worked almost exclusively for the St. Moritz Tourist Board from 1932 to 1938
- Herdeg was a pioneer of **photo-graphic poster design**, combining photographic, graphic, and typographic elements in innovative ways — working alongside Herbert Matter as one of Switzerland's great photo-poster designers
- He later co-founded **Graphis**, the influential trilingual design magazine (first published 1944)
- The sun trademark is considered the **oldest continuously used tourism logo in the world** still in the trademark register
- St. Moritz's slogan: **"Top of the World"**

## 9. The Segantini Museum — Alpine Art at Its Finest

- **Giovanni Segantini** (15 January 1858 – 28 September 1899) was an Italian-born painter who became one of the most famous artists in late-19th-century Europe
- Born in the Austrian town of **Arco**, orphaned young, he studied at the **Accademia di Brera** in Milan
- In **1894**, he settled in the **Engadine**, captivated by its luminous Alpine light. He developed a distinctive **Divisionist** painting technique — applying small strokes of pure colour to capture the brilliance of mountain landscapes
- His final masterwork was the **Alpine Triptych**: "La Vita – La Natura – La Morte" (Life – Nature – Death), conceived for the **1900 Paris World Exhibition**
- In September 1899, while painting the final panel high on the **Schafberg** above Pontresina, he fell gravely ill (likely peritonitis) and died on **28 September 1899** in a small mountain hut now called the **Segantinihütte**
- The **Segantini Museum** opened in **1908**, designed by Engadine architect **Nicolaus Hartmann** (1880–1956). Its striking **domed rotunda** is based on the pavilion Segantini had planned for his Paris panorama
- The museum houses **37 paintings** and **26 drawings** — the world's most comprehensive collection of his work. The two side panels of the triptych were acquired in 1911 through public subscription; the centre panel was later reunited from a private Parisian collection

## 10. The Bernina Express — UNESCO World Heritage Railway

- The **Rhaetian Railway's Albula and Bernina lines** were inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** on **7 July 2008** under the name "Rhaetian Railway in the Albula / Bernina Landscapes"
- The Bernina line reaches its highest point at **Ospizio Bernina** (2,253 m) — the **highest railway crossing in the Alps** with year-round traffic, without the use of rack-and-pinion
- The route from **St. Moritz to Tirano** (Italy) crosses **196 bridges and viaducts**, passes through **55 tunnels**, and descends from glaciers to palm trees
- Iconic engineering highlights include the **Landwasser Viaduct** (65 m high, curving directly into a cliff-face tunnel), **helical tunnels** on the Albula line, and the **Brusio Circular Viaduct** — a 360° spiral bridge
- The **Albula line** (opened 1904) runs 67 km from Thusis to St. Moritz with 42 tunnels and 144 viaducts
- The railway is celebrated for its engineering genius — built in the early 1900s through extreme Alpine terrain without destroying the landscape, it is considered a masterpiece of civil engineering harmonised with nature

## 11. The Engadin Museum and Romansh Heritage

- The **Museum Engiadinais** (Engadine Museum) was built in **1906** on the initiative of collector **Riet Campell**, designed by architect **Nicolaus Hartmann** in traditional Engadine style
- The exterior features characteristic **sgraffito decorations** (ornamental plasterwork scratched to reveal contrasting layers), **Romansh inscriptions**, and the traditional **Erker** (oriel window)
- Inside, **21 exhibition rooms** display historical interiors spanning five centuries: panelled rooms, a grand kitchen, a bedroom, and a stateroom from the Valtellina — recreating the domestic culture of the Engadine
- The museum includes a permanent exhibition on the **Romansh language**, one of Switzerland's four national languages (recognised in a federal referendum in **1938**). Romansh belongs to the **Rhaeto-Romanic** language family and was once the dominant language of the Engadine
- Nearby in Samedan, the **Chesa Planta** museum preserves further Romansh literary heritage
- The Romansh name for the Engadine is **Engiadina** — derived from the river **Inn** (En in Romansh), which rises near the Maloja Pass and flows northeast through the valley

## 12. The Corviglia Ski Area

- **Corviglia** (2,486 m) is St. Moritz's local mountain, accessible by the **St. Moritz–Corviglia funicular** via Chantarella
- An aerial tramway continues to **Piz Nair** (3,057 m), the highest point of the ski area
- The resort offers **155 km of slopes** between 1,720 m and 3,057 m elevation — a vertical drop of **1,337 m** — making it among the 10 largest ski areas in Switzerland
- Together with the **Marguns** area above neighbouring Celerina, it forms the largest skiing area in the Engadine
- The ski course opened in **1934** and has hosted a record **five FIS Alpine World Ski Championships** (1934, 1948, 1974, 2003, 2017) as well as Alpine events during the **1948 Winter Olympics**

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The hotelier's winter bet** — Retelling of Badrutt's 1864 wager from a child's perspective: a young kitchen helper at the Kulm must convince sceptical British guests that winter is magical by showing them the sparkling frozen lake, the sunshine terrace, and the first toboggan ride
2. **The first ride down the Cresta** — January 1885: a child watches Peter Bonorand measure the ice track with string and stakes, then sneaks a ride on a wooden toboggan during the first Grand National race
3. **The painter on the mountain** — A child shepherding goats on the Schafberg meets Giovanni Segantini painting his Alpine Triptych in September 1899, and must race down to Pontresina for help when the artist falls ill
4. **The light that came from Paris** — Johannes Badrutt returns from the 1878 World Exhibition with a strange glass lamp; a child helps wire the hotel's dining room and witnesses the magical moment when electric light first glows in St. Moritz
5. **Horses on the frozen lake** — A nervous young horse arrives for its first White Turf race; a child groom must calm it on the strange frozen surface of Lake St. Moritz while skijöring riders zoom past
6. **The Bronze Age spring** — A child falls through time at the Forum Paracelsus drinking fountain and lands among Bronze Age Celts offering swords and needles to the sacred mineral spring — and must find the way back through 3,500 years of visitors (Romans, Paracelsus, Victorian spa guests)
7. **The train across the sky** — A child's first Bernina Express journey from St. Moritz to Italy: the train climbs over glaciers at Ospizio Bernina, crosses the soaring Landwasser Viaduct, spirals around the Brusio loop, and descends to palm trees — each landmark revealing a piece of railway-building history
8. **The house that remembers Romansh** — In the Engadine Museum at night, the sgraffito figures on the walls begin to speak in Romansh, telling a child stories of the old valley before the tourists came — of farmers, hunters, and the river Inn that gave the valley its name
