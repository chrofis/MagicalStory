## 1. Ancient Origins — From Neolithic Shores to Roman Muralto

- The earliest inhabitants settled along the shores of **Lago Maggiore** (Lake Maggiore) during the **Neolithic period**, drawn by fertile land and the temperate climate of the Verbano basin
- **Celtic and Ligurian** artifacts — tools, pottery, burial sites — indicate a well-established community during the Bronze and Iron Ages, trading between the Alpine valleys and the **Pianura Padana** (Po Valley)
- The Romans thoroughly colonised the area: a large **necropolis** discovered in **1936** near Muralto, along with the ruins of a villa with thermal baths and a commercial forum, confirmed **Muralto** as the most important Roman settlement in the upper Verbano
- Locarno is first mentioned by name as **"Leocarni"** in a document from **807 AD**
- In **866**, a royal palace (palatium regale) in Locarno is recorded, indicating the town's importance under Carolingian rule
- By the **10th century**, noble families — the **Orelli, Magoria, and Muralto** — controlled the area, fortifying the town with towers and castles

## 2. Castello Visconteo — The Visconti Fortress

- The oldest surviving parts of the **Castello Visconteo** (Visconti Castle) date to the **late 12th or early 13th century**, though a noble castle stood on the site earlier
- In **1164**, Emperor **Federico Barbarossa** (Frederick Barbarossa) granted Locarno market rights and first mentioned the **Capitanei di Locarno**, the noble families who administered the town
- Around **1240**, Locarno became a stronghold of the **Guelph** faction (pro-papal), possibly prompting the castle's expansion
- In **1260**, pro-imperial **Ghibellines** attacked and destroyed the castle; it was rebuilt shortly after
- In **1342**, **Luchino and Giovanni Visconti** of Milan besieged and captured the castle from both the landward and lake sides, beginning Milanese rule
- Under the **Rusca family**, appointed as podesta in **1439**, the castle was transformed into an ornate late-medieval administrative residence
- In **1507**, a triangular bastion called the **Rivellino** was added to the north side — historian Marino Vigano presented evidence in **2006** that it may have been designed by **Leonardo da Vinci**, though no surviving documents confirm this
- The Swiss Confederates occupied Locarno in **1503** and gained the castle after the **Battle of Novara in 1513**; the **Treaty of Freiburg (1516)** gave them full control
- Today the castle houses the **Museo Civico e Archeologico** (Municipal and Archaeological Museum), a Swiss heritage site of national significance

## 3. The Reformation Exile of 1555

- In the 1530s, **Giovanni Beccaria**, a priest and schoolmaster at the convent of San Francesco, began spreading Protestant ideas in Locarno
- A Reformed community grew steadily; in **1549**, the Catholic cantons demanded a theological disputation, after which Beccaria was arrested and exiled
- **Taddeo Duno** became the leading figure of the remaining Protestants
- Out of a population of roughly **4,000**, some **211 people** (71 men, 54 women, 86 children) declared themselves Protestant
- After intense pressure, the number halved. On **3 March 1555**, approximately **100 Locarnesi** departed for Zurich, arriving in mid-May
- The reformer **Heinrich Bullinger** welcomed them and arranged for Italian-language worship at Zurich's **Peterskirche** (St. Peter's Church)
- The exiled families — including the **Duno, Muralto, and Orelli** — eventually flourished in Zurich; the Orelli family later founded the prominent **Orell Fussli** publishing house

## 4. Madonna del Sasso — The Pilgrimage Church Above the Lake

- On the evening of **15 August 1480**, the Franciscan friar **Fra Bartolomeo Piatti d'Ivrea** reportedly saw a vision of the **Madonna** (Virgin Mary) on a rocky outcrop above Locarno, the night before the **Festa dell'Assunzione** (Feast of the Assumption)
- In **1487**, Fra Bartolomeo founded the **Santuario della Madonna del Sasso** (Sanctuary of the Madonna of the Rock) at the site, in the municipality of **Orselina**
- The church was enlarged in **1616** and is today a **Sacro Monte** (Sacred Mount) complex, including the Church of the Annunciation, a Via Crucis with stations in niches, a chapel of the Pieta, and a scene of the Last Supper in terracotta
- Notable artworks inside: the **Fuga in Egitto** (Flight into Egypt) altarpiece by **Bramantino** and the **Trasporto di Cristo al Sepolcro** (Entombment of Christ) by **Antonio Ciseri**
- The **Funicolare Locarno-Madonna del Sasso** opened on **1 March 1906**, built after a concession first granted in **1897**. In its first year, it carried over **116,000 passengers** (and 611 dogs)
- The sanctuary remains the principal pilgrimage destination of Italian-speaking Switzerland, with panoramic views over Locarno and the lake

## 5. Piazza Grande — The Heart of Locarno

- The **Piazza Grande** emerged thanks to the gradual retreat of the lakefront, which in ancient times reached the buildings directly — the piazza was laid out as the water receded
- In **1825**, the square was paved with stone from the nearby **Fiume Maggia** (Maggia River)
- Measuring approximately **190 meters long and 60 meters wide**, it is one of the most spacious squares in Switzerland
- The piazza is framed by distinctive **Lombard-style arcades** (portici) that extend over **700 metres** between the square and the railway station
- Each Thursday, the square hosts the traditional **mercato** (open-air market)
- In summer, the piazza transforms into one of **Europe's largest open-air cinemas** for the Locarno Film Festival, seating up to **8,000 spectators** before a giant screen

## 6. The Locarno Film Festival — Cinema Under the Stars

- Founded in **August 1946**, the **Festival del film Locarno** is one of the world's oldest film festivals, alongside Cannes and Venice
- The inaugural edition ran from **23 August to 2 September 1946**, initiated by the tourist office **Pro Locarno** and film industry professionals
- The first prize went to **Rene Clair's** *And Then There Were None* — Clair remains the only director to win the top prize twice (1946 and 1947)
- The award was originally called **Miglior Film** (Best Film), then **Gran Premio**, then **Vela d'Oro** (Golden Sail); it became the **Pardo d'Oro** (Golden Leopard) in **1968**
- In **1971**, the festival introduced its most iconic feature: open-air screenings on the **Piazza Grande**, creating one of the world's most atmospheric cinema experiences
- The festival was conceived in the aftermath of World War II as a platform for the rebirth of **liberta di espressione artistica** (freedom of artistic expression), championing bold and avant-garde filmmaking
- Today the festival screens approximately **250 films** across multiple sections each August, drawing audiences and filmmakers from around the world

## 7. The Pact of Locarno (1925) — A City of Peace

- From **5 to 16 October 1925**, delegates from seven nations gathered in Locarno to negotiate a set of post-World War I peace agreements
- The primary venue was the **Palazzo del Pretorio**; delegates also used the Hotel du Parc, Hotel Esplanade, and Grand Hotel Locarno
- Locarno was chosen for the **neutralita** (neutrality) of Switzerland and its proximity to Italy, facilitating Mussolini's participation
- The main agreement, the **Patto Renano** (Rhineland Pact), was a treaty of mutual guarantee signed by **Germany, France, Belgium, Great Britain, and Italy**
- Key signatories: German Foreign Minister **Gustav Stresemann**, French Foreign Minister **Aristide Briand**, and British Foreign Secretary **Austen Chamberlain**
- The treaties were initialled in Locarno on **16 October 1925** and formally signed in London on **1 December 1925**
- The agreements ushered in the **"Spirit of Locarno"** — a period of optimism and diplomatic detente; Germany was admitted to the **League of Nations** in **1926**
- Stresemann and Briand received the **Nobel Peace Prize in 1926** for their efforts; Chamberlain received his in **1925**
- The pact was effectively destroyed on **7 March 1936** when Nazi troops entered the demilitarised Rhineland
- Today, the **Locarno Citta della Pace** (City of Peace) initiative preserves the memory of this diplomatic legacy

## 8. Lago Maggiore and the Isole di Brissago

- **Lago Maggiore** (Lake Maggiore) sits at **193 metres above sea level**, making the area around its delta the **lowest point in Switzerland**
- The lake straddles the Swiss-Italian border; Locarno sits on its northern shore, where the **Maggia** and **Verzasca** rivers flow in
- The **Isole di Brissago** (Brissago Islands) — two small islands in the lake — are home to Switzerland's only island botanical garden
- In **1885**, the Russian-born Baroness **Antoinette de Saint-Leger** purchased the islands and created a subtropical garden, hosting painters like **Giovanni Segantini** and the composer **Ruggero Leoncavallo**
- In **1927**, Hamburg department-store magnate **Max Emden** bought the islands, demolished the existing house, and built a **palazzo** designed by Berlin architect **Alfred Breslauer**, with 24 rooms and a 33-metre Roman-style pool
- In **1949**, the **Canton of Ticino** acquired 50% of the property; the rest was split among the municipalities of Ascona, Brissago, and Ronco sopra Ascona
- The **Parco Botanico delle Isole di Brissago** opened to the public on **2 April 1950**, displaying approximately **2,000 plant species** from Mediterranean and subtropical regions of all five continents

## 9. Cardada-Cimetta — Between Switzerland's Extremes

- From Orselina (reached via the Madonna del Sasso funicular), a cable car rises to **Cardada** at **1,340 m** and a chairlift continues to **Cimetta** at **1,670 m**
- The modern cable car was inaugurated in **June 2000**, designed by Ticinese architect **Mario Botta** — its distinctive raindrop-shaped cabins are made of light steel and glass
- The landscape promontory at Cardada and the **Osservatorio Geologico** (Geological Observatory) at Cimetta were designed by landscape architect **Paolo Burgi**
- From the summit, one can see both the **lowest point in Switzerland** — the Lago Maggiore delta at **193 m** — and the **highest point** — **Dufourspitze** at **4,634 m**
- The geological observatory explains the collision of the **European and African continental plates**, whose boundary runs through this region

## 10. The Centovalli Railway — A Hundred Valleys

- The **Ferrovia Vigezzina-Centovalli** connects Locarno to **Domodossola, Italy**, a **52-kilometre** metre-gauge line crossing **83 bridges** and passing through **31 tunnels**
- The name **"Centovalli"** (hundred valleys) refers to the countless side valleys visible along the route
- Until the early 20th century, the only connection between Locarno and Domodossola was a bridle path; the first road opened in **1907**
- The railway was championed by mayor of Locarno **Francesco Balli** (1852-1924), teacher **Andrea Testore** (1855-1936), and engineer **Giacomo Sutter** (1873-1939)
- Construction was delayed by the collapse of a French bank (**1913**) and World War I; Switzerland and Italy signed a bilateral operating treaty in **1918**
- The line opened on **25 November 1923** with an electric railcar built by **Carminati & Toselli** of Milan
- During World War II, the railway served refugees fleeing the Italian conflict; a catastrophic storm in **August 1978** severely damaged the Italian section, requiring reconstruction completed in **1980**
- A **2.59-kilometre tunnel** opened in **1990**, bringing the Locarno terminus underground to ease urban congestion
- Today the journey takes approximately **2 hours** and is one of Switzerland's most celebrated scenic rail experiences

## 11. Monte Verita — The Hill of Truth

- In the **autumn of 1900**, five idealists — **Henri Oedenkoven** (a Belgian industrialist's son), pianist **Ida Hofmann**, and the brothers **Karl and Gustav (Gusto) Graser** along with **Lotte Hattemer** — purchased land on Monte Monescia above **Ascona** and renamed it **Monte Verita** (Mount Truth)
- They founded a **cooperative colony** based on vegetarianism, nudism, theosophy, sun worship, and the **emancipazione della donna** (emancipation of women)
- The anarchist physician **Raphael Friedeberg** arrived in **1904**, drawing a wave of anarchists, communists, and social reformers
- Over the following decades, an extraordinary roster of visitors passed through: writers **Hermann Hesse** and **D.H. Lawrence**, dancer **Isadora Duncan**, choreographer **Rudolf von Laban**, artist **Hans Arp**, Dadaist **Hugo Ball**, and poet **Erich Muhsam**, who called Ascona **"la Repubblica dei senza patria"** (the Republic of the Homeless)
- After Oedenkoven departed for Brazil in **1920**, the German banker and art collector **Baron Eduard von der Heydt** purchased the property and commissioned modernist architecture, filling the villa **Casa Anatta** with his collection of African, Indian, and Chinese art
- Since **1981**, Casa Anatta has served as the **Museo di Monte Verita** (History Museum); today the complex operates as a hotel, conference centre, and cultural park

## 12. Climate, Camellias, and the Collegiata

### The Warmest Corner of Switzerland
- Locarno enjoys over **2,300 hours of sunshine** per year, earning it the title of the **warmest city in Switzerland**
- The sheltered position south of the Alps and the warming effect of Lago Maggiore produce a **Mediterranean microclimate** where **palms, lemon trees, oleanders, and bougainvillea** flourish
- The **Parco delle Camelie** (Camellia Park), inaugurated in **2005** on the occasion of the International Camellia Society World Congress, covers **10,000 m²** along the lakeshore with approximately **850 catalogued varieties** of camellias — recognised as a **Garden of Excellence** by the International Camellia Society

### Collegiata di San Vittore
- The **Collegiata di San Vittore** in neighbouring **Muralto** is one of the finest Romanesque churches in Ticino
- An early Christian basilica stood on the site as early as the **5th-6th centuries**, built over the ruins of a Roman villa; the current church dates to **1090-1100**
- The **22 carved capitals** of the crypt columns — decorated with geometric motifs, human heads, and fantastic animals — form an extraordinary collection of Romanesque sculpture
- The church served as the **parish church of Locarno** until **1818**

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The vision on the rock** — A child climbing to Madonna del Sasso on a hot August evening meets the ghost of Fra Bartolomeo, who needs help finding the exact spot where the Madonna appeared in 1480 before the midnight bells ring
2. **The leopard of Piazza Grande** — During the film festival, the golden leopard statue on the Pardo d'Oro trophy winks at a child; together they step into the giant screen and must escape through scenes from famous films projected since 1946
3. **The peace treaty's lost page** — A child exploring the Palazzo del Pretorio discovers a hidden room where the 1925 delegates' ghosts are still arguing; a missing page of the treaty must be found across Locarno's hotels before the Spirit of Locarno fades forever
4. **The Baroness's island garden** — On the Brissago Islands, a child finds Baroness Antoinette de Saint-Leger's secret diary; each pressed flower in its pages opens a portal to the plant's homeland — from the Amazon to Japan — and the child must return before the last ferry departs
5. **The hundred valleys express** — A child boards the Centovalli train and discovers that each of the hundred valleys holds a sleeping giant; when the 1923 locomotive whistle sounds, one giant wakes and the child must lull it back to sleep before the train reaches Domodossola
6. **The exiles of 1555** — A child in Locarno's old town finds a hidden letter from a Protestant child who was forced to leave; following the letter's clues across the city and over the Alps, the child retraces the exile route to Zurich and delivers the message 470 years late
7. **The summit between two worlds** — On Cimetta, a child steps onto the geological observatory and falls through the crack between the European and African tectonic plates into an underground world where the mountains are still being born
8. **The vegetarians of Monte Verita** — A child visiting Ascona stumbles into 1900 and joins the barefoot colony on the Hill of Truth; to return home, the child must convince the eccentric idealists to let go of a magical sundial that has stopped time on their mountain
