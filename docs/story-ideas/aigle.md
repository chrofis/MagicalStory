## 1. Name and Origins — The Town of the Eagle

- **Aigle** means **"eagle"** (l'aigle) in French, from the Latin **aquila**. The Romans called the settlement variously **Ala** (wing), **Alena** (little wing), and **Aquilegia** — all eagle-related names
- The first medieval mention of the town appears in **1150** under the name **Alium**; a second mention in **1153** gives the name as **Aleo**
- The town's **coat of arms** (armoiries) features **two eagles counterchanged** on a field divided horizontally in black and gold — a **canting arms** (armes parlantes), where the heraldic design directly illustrates the town's name. The eagles appear on seals dating to the **15th century**
- Aigle sits at **415 m** elevation on the eastern edge of the **Rhône valley** (vallée du Rhône), at the foot of the **Chablais Alps** (Alpes chablaisiens), where the **Grande Eau** river — flowing down from Les Diablerets — joins the Rhône
- Population: approximately **11,750** (2024). Aigle is the capital of the **district d'Aigle** in the canton of **Vaud**

## 2. Savoyard Rule — From the House of Savoy to Market Town

- In **1076**, Holy Roman Emperor **Henry IV** granted the territory of Aigle to the **House of Savoy** (Maison de Savoie)
- The **Barons of Aigle** (Seigneurs d'Aigle, the knights of Allio) are first documented in **1179**, when they controlled a fortification along the roads over the **Col du Pillon** and **Col des Mosses** — critical Alpine passes linking the Rhône valley to the Bernese Oberland and the Pays-d'Enhaut
- Before **1200**, the Barons became vassals of the Counts of Savoy. In **1232**, Count **Thomas I of Savoy** granted Aigle as a fief (fief) to the brothers Jacob and Peter of Saillon in exchange for their ancestral castle in Valais
- In **1231**, Aigle was elevated to a **market town** (bourg de marché) by Thomas I of Savoy
- In **1314**, **Amadeus V of Savoy** raised Aigle to a **free town** (ville franche) — granting the inhabitants civic rights and commercial privileges

## 3. The Bernese Conquest (1475–1798) — Three Centuries of German-Speaking Rule

- In August **1475**, during the **Burgundian Wars** (Guerres de Bourgogne), Bernese troops, aided by mountain people from the Haute Gruyère, conquered the castle of Aigle. The Savoyard lord **Jean de Compey** fled and was killed shortly afterward in Vevey
- From **1476**, Aigle was integrated into the Canton of Bern — becoming the **first French-speaking territory** (premier territoire francophone) to join the Swiss Confederation
- Bern sent **governors** (baillis) to Aigle for **six-year terms** (mandats de six ans). The governors resided in the rebuilt Château d'Aigle as their official seat. Over the 322 years of Bernese rule, dozens of Bernese bailiffs administered the district
- The **Reformation** (Réforme) arrived in **1528** under Bernese decree — the churches became Reformed (réformées), and the ancient priory of Saint-Maurice was suppressed
- In **1798**, inspired by the French Revolution, the Vaudois population revolted against Bernese rule. On 24 January 1798, the **Lemanic Republic** (République lémanique) was proclaimed. Aigle became part of the new **Canton de Léman** under the Helvetic Republic, which in **1803** became the **Canton de Vaud**

## 4. Château d'Aigle — The Castle Among the Vines

### Construction and Savoyard Period
- Around **1200**, two towers stood on the Blonaire hill: the older one (late 12th century) belonged to the **knights of Allio** (the lords of Aigle); a second was built by the Count of Savoy and transferred to the Saillon family around **1232**
- The castle controlled the ancient transalpine road to the **Great St. Bernard Pass** (Col du Grand-Saint-Bernard) — a major route since Roman times
- Between the late 13th and early 14th centuries, modifications reduced the castle's defensive character in favor of **residential comfort**

### Bernese Rebuilding (1479–1584)
- After conquering Aigle in 1475, the Bernese **rebuilt the castle almost entirely** between **1479 and 1510** — reinforcing it with **circular towers** (tours circulaires) adapted for artillery and adding a large **square residential tower** (tour carrée) for prestige
- Interior features included Gothic doors and stone **cross windows** (fenêtres à croisée)
- In **1584**, a new wing was constructed housing the governor's private apartments. The second floor features an audience chamber (salle d'audience) with a **ceiling decorated with Moorish motifs** (motifs mauresques) painted by **Andreas Stoss**
- Between **1587–1589**, the old Savoy tower was converted into a **tithe house** (maison de la dîme) — a barn for collecting agricultural taxes
- Two **Bernese bear escutcheons** (écussons à l'ours bernois) adorned the governor's coat of arms above the entrance — these were hammered out during the Revolution of 1798

### Modern Era
- After the Vaudois Revolution, the castle was sold to the **Commune d'Aigle** in **1804**
- It served as **district prisons** (prisons de district) in the circular towers until **1972**, and also housed a hospital, poorhouse, and district court (1825–1964)
- Listed as a **historical monument** (monument historique) in **1900**
- After extensive restoration in the 1970s, the castle was transformed into a cultural venue

## 5. Musée de la Vigne, du Vin et de l'Étiquette — The Wine Museum

- The museum was founded in **1971** by the **Confrérie du Guillon** (Guillon Brotherhood), a wine brotherhood established in **1954** dedicated to promoting Vaudois wines
- The Confrérie's collection originated from displays at Switzerland's **1964 national exhibition** (Exposition nationale)
- The museum moved into the Château d'Aigle in **1967** and opened to the public in **1975**
- Since **2010**, it has been fully reimagined as an interactive, educational institution
- The collection includes over **10,000 objects** (objets) related to viticulture and winemaking — historical tools, cooperage equipment, glassware, drinking vessels, fine art
- The **wine label collection** (collection d'étiquettes) exceeds **400,000 labels** from around the world — one of the largest such collections in existence
- Interactive laboratories allow visitors to exercise their senses: recognizing **aromas** (arômes), tasting wines, and learning about vineyard flora and fauna

## 6. The Chablais Wine Region — Vineyards at the Foot of the Alps

- The **Chablais AOC** (Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée) encompasses nearly **600 hectares** of vineyard on the right bank of the Rhône, divided among five communes: **Aigle, Yvorne, Ollon, Bex, and Villeneuve**
- **Aigle's vineyards** cover approximately **135 hectares** on glacial moraine soils (sols morainiques glaciaires)
- The dominant grape is **Chasselas** — known locally as **Fendant** — producing crisp, elegant white wines with mineral character
- **Aigle Les Murailles** ("The Walls") is one of Switzerland's most famous wines, produced by **Maison Henri Badoux** (founded **1908** in Aigle). The wine takes its name from the distinctive **stone terrace walls** (murs de vigne) that surround the vineyard plots
- These ancient walls create a unique **microclimate** (microclimat): they absorb heat during the day and release it at night, extending the growing season and protecting vines from cold
- The **foehn** (le foehn) — a warm, dry Alpine wind — blows down the Rhône valley and is a critical factor in Aigle's viticulture: it reduces disease pressure, accelerates ripening, and gives the wines their distinctive mineral crispness
- The **Sentier des Vignes** (Vineyard Path) between Aigle and Ollon winds through the terraced vineyards with views of the castle rising from among the vines — one of the most photographed scenes in Swiss wine country

## 7. The UCI and the World Cycling Centre — Capital of World Cycling

- The **Union Cycliste Internationale** (UCI) was founded on **14 April 1900** in Paris by the cycling federations of Belgium, the United States, France, Italy, and Switzerland
- After a reorganization in **1992**, the UCI relocated its headquarters to **Aigle** — chosen for its proximity to the **International Olympic Committee** (CIO) in nearby Lausanne
- The **Centre Mondial du Cyclisme** (World Cycling Centre, CMC) was inaugurated in **2002** to celebrate the UCI's 100th anniversary
- Facilities include a **200-meter indoor velodrome** (vélodrome couvert), a **BMX racing track** (piste de BMX), training laboratories, and administrative offices
- The UCI headquarters sit at **Allée Ferdi Kübler 12**, named after **Ferdinand "Ferdi" Kübler** (1919–2016), the first Swiss cyclist to win the **Tour de France** (in **1950**) and **World Road Race Champion** in **1951**
- The CMC serves as a **development center** for young cyclists from around the world, particularly from nations where cycling infrastructure is limited — a training pipeline that has launched numerous Olympic and professional careers
- Aigle's location at the gateway to the Alps makes it ideal for road cycling training, with legendary climbs to the **Col des Mosses** (1,445 m), **Col du Pillon** (1,546 m), and the mountain roads to **Leysin** and **Les Diablerets** all departing from town

## 8. The Salt Connection — Bex and the Route du Sel

- Just **7 km** south of Aigle lies **Bex**, home to **Switzerland's oldest active salt mine** (mine de sel), where salt has been extracted continuously since **1554**
- Legend has it that a young shepherd named **Jean du Bouillet** discovered the salt source when he noticed his goats always drinking from the same spot in the river — he tasted the water and found it salty
- In **1685**, the mines came under **Bernese state control** (contrôle bernois), making salt a government monopoly. The strategic importance of salt — essential for food preservation — gave Aigle's region immense economic value
- The **Coulat Gallery** (Galerie du Coulat), excavated between **1684 and 1691**, required seven years of hand-chiseling. Today the mine comprises over **50 km of tunnels** and produces **30,000 tonnes of salt per year**
- Before the Bex mines, Switzerland depended on imported salt from **Franche-Comté** in eastern France. The historic **Via Salina** (Route du Sel / salt road) ran from the Royal Saltworks at **Arc-et-Senans** through the Jura mountains to Bern — one of Switzerland's twelve recognized cultural routes
- The Bernese word for this monopoly control was **"Salzregal"** — the salt privilege. Control of the Bex salt mines was one of the key economic reasons Bern was so determined to hold the Aigle district

## 9. Les Diablerets — The Legend of the Devil's Peaks

- The **Diablerets massif** (3,210 m) rises dramatically to Aigle's southeast. The name literally means **"abode of devils"** (demeure des diables) — from the French **diable** (devil)
- According to medieval legend, **demons** arrived in the mountains and transformed the once-beautiful meadow of **Tsanfleuron** (Champ de Fleurs / "Field of Flowers") into an icy wasteland
- The devils amused themselves by playing **ninepins** (quilles) with enormous boulders, trying to knock over a massive rock formation called the **Quille du Diable** (Devil's Skittle) near the peak of **Scex Rouge**
- Local shepherds, terrified by the falling rocks, **abandoned the high pastures** — attributing the natural rockfalls to demonic activity
- A separate legend tells of a **shepherdess** who discovered a cave near Scex Rouge decorated with **rubies and amethysts**, inhabited by a mountain spirit. The spirit allowed her to take three rubies in exchange for secrecy — since then, no one has found the cave again
- The legends reflect real geological catastrophes: on **23 September 1714**, a massive rockslide buried the **Derborence valley**, killing shepherds and destroying pastures. Scientists estimate the collapse involved approximately **50 million cubic meters** of rock
- A second, even larger slide struck in **1749**, but the valley had been largely abandoned after the first disaster, so fewer lives were lost
- The rockslides created **Lac de Derborence** — one of **Europe's youngest natural mountain lakes** — by damming the mountain stream. The valley is now a protected nature reserve

## 10. The Railways — Mountain Trains from the Rhône to the Peaks

### Aigle–Leysin Railway (Chemin de fer Aigle–Leysin, AL)
- Opened **5 May 1900** — the earliest narrow-gauge line in the Chablais region
- A **rack railway** (chemin de fer à crémaillère) using the **Abt system**, with a gauge of **1,000 mm**
- The line is **6.5 km** long and climbs **1,047 meters** from Aigle (415 m) to the Grand Hôtel at Leysin — with a maximum gradient of **23%** (1 in 4.3)
- The railway was built to serve **Leysin's sanatoriums**: from **1894**, the mountain village became a world-renowned center for **tuberculosis treatment** through heliotherapy (héliothérapie — sun exposure therapy), pioneered by Dr. **Auguste Rollier** from 1903. By **1930**, Leysin had nearly 3,000 tuberculosis patients

### Aigle–Sépey–Diablerets Railway (Chemin de fer Aigle–Sépey–Diablerets, ASD)
- Opened in two stages: Aigle to Le Sépey on **22 December 1913**, and Le Sépey to Les Diablerets on **7 July 1914**
- Electrified from the start at **1,500 V DC**

### Transports Publics du Chablais (TPC)
- In **1999–2000**, the four independent Chablais railway companies — **AL, ASD, AOMC** (Aigle–Ollon–Monthey–Champéry), and **BVB** (Bex–Villars–Bretaye) — merged to form the **Transports Publics du Chablais** (TPC), headquartered in Aigle
- Together, these narrow-gauge mountain railways form a unique network radiating from Aigle into the surrounding Alps — all departing from stops near the main SBB station

## 11. The Medieval Old Town — Le Cloître and the Church

- The old town quarter of **Le Cloître** (The Cloister) takes its name from a **priory** (prieuré) established by the monks of the **Abbey of Saint-Maurice d'Agaune** — one of the oldest continuously inhabited monasteries in the West, founded in **515**
- The **Église du Cloître** (Cloister Church), dedicated to **Saint-Maurice**, was originally a **Romanesque** building from the **12th century**. Archaeological excavations in **1899** revealed an older rectangular nave and semicircular apse beneath the current structure
- In the late **15th century**, likely under Bernese orders after **1482**, the nave was rebuilt with **Gothic vaults** (voûtes gothiques) and flanked with irregular side chapels featuring transverse barrel vaults
- The church became a **Reformed temple** (temple réformé) in **1528** when Bern imposed the Reformation on its French-speaking territories
- The neighborhood at the foot of the castle — with its narrow streets, old stone houses, and the church — still preserves the atmosphere of a medieval market town

## 12. Gateway to the Alps — Geography and Strategic Position

- Aigle commands a **strategic crossroads** in the upper Rhône valley: the town sits where the **Grande Eau** valley (leading to Les Diablerets via the Col du Pillon) meets the broad Rhône plain
- In Roman times, Aigle lay on the road from the **Great St. Bernard Pass** via **Viviscus** (Vevey) to **Aventicum** (Avenches), the Roman capital of Helvetia
- The **Col des Mosses** (1,445 m) connects Aigle northward to **Château-d'Oex** and the **Pays-d'Enhaut** in the valley of the Sarine
- The **Col du Pillon** (1,546 m) leads to **Gstaad** and the **Bernese Oberland** — historically a critical trade and military route between French- and German-speaking Switzerland
- This position made Aigle a natural **toll station** and **military chokepoint** — controlling who could pass between the Rhône valley and the inner Alps. The Barons of Aigle, the Counts of Savoy, and later the Bernese all fortified the town precisely because of this strategic geography
- Today, Aigle remains a transportation hub: the main **SBB railway line** (Lausanne–Brig) passes through, and the narrow-gauge mountain railways fan out to Leysin, Les Diablerets, Villars, and Champéry

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The eagles of the castle** — Two eagle chicks living on the castle towers witness centuries of history: Savoyard knights, Bernese governors, the 1798 Revolution — told through the eyes of Aigle's heraldic birds
2. **The devil's ninepins** — A child hears thundering rockfalls and climbs to Tsanfleuron, where playful (but clumsy) devils are bowling with boulders. The child teaches them a gentler game to save the valley
3. **The wine label that came alive** — In the castle museum's collection of 400,000 wine labels, one label peels off at night. The child follows the painted vineyard scene into the terraced slopes, meeting a talking lizard who lives in the ancient stone walls
4. **The little train that climbed too high** — The Aigle–Leysin rack railway chugs past its station and keeps climbing into the clouds, carrying a sleeping child to a mountaintop where Dr. Rollier's sun-healed patients tell stories of courage
5. **The shepherd and the salt spring** — Retelling of the legend of Jean du Bouillet: a child and their goat discover a salty spring near Bex, leading them deep underground through 50 km of tunnels where salt crystals glow like lanterns
6. **The Bernese bear and the Vaudois eagle** — When the carved bear escutcheons were hammered off the castle in 1798, one small stone bear escapes and befriends an eagle. Together they learn that freedom means more than winning
7. **The foehn wind's secret** — A warm Alpine wind whispers through the vineyard walls at night, ripening the grapes. A child follows the wind uphill to discover it is the breath of a sleeping mountain giant who has guarded the vines since Roman times
8. **The world's fastest wheels** — A child sneaks into the UCI velodrome at night and discovers the ghosts of champion cyclists from 125 years of racing history, each riding their era's bicycle — from penny-farthings to carbon fiber
