## 1. Geography — Where Switzerland Meets

- Olten lies on the **Aare river** at the southern foot of the **Jura mountains**, in the canton of **Solothurn**
- Elevation: **396 meters** above sea level; area: **11.49 km²**; population: approximately **19,000** (2024)
- The town sits in a valley formed by three Jura foothills: the **Born**, the **Engelberg**, and the **Säli** — earning it the nickname **"Dreitannenstadt"** (City of Three Fir Trees), reflected in its coat of arms showing three green fir trees on a hill
- Olten occupies a uniquely **central position** in Switzerland: Zurich, Basel, Bern, and Lucerne are all reachable within **30 minutes by train**, making it perhaps the most equidistant point from all major Swiss cities
- This centrality gave Olten its reputation as **"the place where Switzerland meets"** — national organizations, political parties, trade unions, and associations have traditionally chosen Olten for their assemblies because no delegation has to travel further than any other

## 2. Ancient Origins — From Prehistoric Caves to Roman Vicus

- Archaeological finds near Olten date back to the **Magdalenian period** (approximately 16,000–14,000 years ago), making the area one of the oldest known human habitation sites in the Swiss Mittelland
- A **Roman vicus** (small civilian settlement) existed at the site, its importance tied to the **Aare river crossing** — a natural bottleneck where roads converged
- The Roman settlement was probably destroyed in the **late 3rd century AD** during the period of Germanic incursions
- The prehistoric and Roman hilltop settlement of **Dickenbännli** is listed as a Swiss heritage site of **national significance**
- First documented mention of Olten: **1201**, recorded as **"Oltun"** — the name possibly derives from a Gaulish compound with the suffix *dunum* meaning "fort," suggesting the site's ancient function as a fortified river crossing

## 3. Medieval Olten — Frohburg Counts, Fires, and Fortifications

- In the **12th and 13th centuries**, Olten was controlled by the **Counts of Frohburg**, who fortified the town around its strategic bridge
- **Count Friedrich IV von Froburg** built the original defensive tower that would become the Stadtturm
- **1295**: First documentary mention of a **wooden bridge** over the Aare — the ancestor of today's Alte Holzbrücke
- **1377**: Olten passed to the **Kyburg** family; **1384**: to the **Habsburgs**
- **1395**: Duke Leopold granted Olten a **weekly market** as reward for loyalty shown during the **Battle of Sempach** (1386)
- **1407**: The city of **Basel** took administrative control and invested in infrastructure
- **1411** and **1422**: Two devastating **fires** destroyed much of the town; Basel, unwilling to rebuild again, **sold Olten to Solothurn** in **1426**
- Throughout the medieval period, Olten remained a small fortified bridgehead of roughly **500 inhabitants** (estimated for 1600), with blacksmiths, taverns, and the essential bridge
- **1653**: Olten **lost its city rights** as punishment for supporting rebels in the **Swiss Peasant War** — reflecting a longstanding tradition of resistance to authority

## 4. The Stadtturm (City Tower) — Olten's Landmark

- Originally built in the **13th century** as part of the town's fortifications by the Frohburg counts
- Rebuilt by **Konrad Gibelin** of Solothurn in **1521** as a bell tower for the adjacent **St. Martin's Church**
- Between **1628 and 1676**, the tower was raised by one story and given a **Baroque renovation**, reaching a height of approximately **42 meters**
- The tower houses **five bells**, the oldest dating to **1402**
- Its **clock face** dates to **1577** and measures nearly **four meters in diameter** — one of the largest and oldest working public clocks in Switzerland
- Over the centuries, the tower served multiple functions: **watchtower, prison, council chamber**, and even **fire station**
- In **1844**, the adjacent St. Martin's Church was demolished due to structural concerns, but the Stadtturm was preserved — standing alone as a historical beacon in the heart of the old town
- Today, guided tours allow visitors to climb to the top for panoramic views over the old town, the Aare, and the Jura foothills

## 5. The Alte Holzbrücke (Old Wooden Bridge)

- First mentioned in **1295**, making it one of the oldest documented bridge sites in Switzerland
- The bridge spans approximately **89 meters** across the Aare, connecting the old town with the **Born district** on the opposite bank
- Constructed primarily from **spruce wood** in a covered timber truss design
- Over the centuries, the bridge has been **repeatedly destroyed** by floods, ice drifts, and fire, each time rebuilt
- During the French invasion, **Blasius Baltenschwiler** erected a temporary bridge in **1797**; the **current structure** dates to **1803**
- A severe fire in **1844** caused significant damage; afterward, **water pipes were installed within the bridge** as a fire protection measure
- The bridge is a **pedestrian and cyclist crossing**, illuminated at night, and one of the last historic covered wooden bridges still in daily use in Switzerland
- It stands as a symbol of Olten's identity — the town that exists because of its bridge

## 6. The Hauenstein Tunnel — Switzerland's First Railway Tunnel

- The **Jura mountains** presented the greatest obstacle for connecting Basel (and northern Europe) to the Swiss interior; the **Hauenstein Pass** (Unterer Hauenstein) was the ancient route through this barrier
- In **1853**, the **Schweizerische Centralbahn (SCB)** — the Swiss Central Railway, established in Basel — began construction of the first true railway tunnel in Switzerland
- The **Hauenstein Summit Tunnel**: **2,495 meters** long, bored between **Läufelfingen** (Canton Basel-Landschaft) and **Trimbach** (Canton Solothurn)
- English contractor **Thomas Brassey** drove the tunnel from **five points** simultaneously — both ends and three perpendicular shafts — to accelerate the work
- **Tragedy**: In **May 1857**, a shaft fire caused a devastating cave-in, killing **52 workers and 11 rescuers** — one of the worst construction disasters in 19th-century Switzerland
- The tunnel officially opened on **1 May 1858**, a year behind schedule due to repeated flooding
- Federal Councillors **Wilhelm Matthias Naeff** and **Giovanni Battista Pioda** attended the opening
- A second, longer tunnel — the **Hauenstein Base Tunnel** (**8,134 meters**) — was built between **1912 and 1916**, opening on **8 January 1916** without ceremony due to World War I. It remains one of the busiest railway tunnels in Switzerland

## 7. Railway Crossroads — Olten as Switzerland's Rail Hub

- When the Schweizerische Centralbahn planned its network in the 1850s, **Olten was designated as the junction** where the lines to Basel, Zurich, Lucerne, Bern, and Biel would intersect
- Olten served as the **provisional "Kilometre Zero"** for surveying the SCB network — a memorial stone on **track 12** of the station commemorates this
- In **1855**, the SCB built its **main railway workshop** in Olten; under master machinist **Niklaus Riggenbach** (from 1856), the workshop grew into a full locomotive and bridge manufacturing works
- The railway transformed Olten from a sleepy town of about 1,000 people into an **industrial center** — reaching **7,000 by 1900** and peaking at **21,000 in 1970**
- Today, Olten station handles approximately **1,100 trains** and **80,000 passengers per day** — busier than even Geneva, and one of the **10 busiest stations** in Switzerland, despite the town having only about 19,000 inhabitants
- The **Bahnhofbuffet Olten** (railway station restaurant), opened in **1856**, became legendary as the place where Switzerland's organizations met. In spring **1893**, three of Europe's most powerful rulers — **Emperor Franz Joseph I of Austria**, **Queen Victoria of England**, and **Emperor Wilhelm II of Germany** — all stopped at Olten during their Swiss journeys

## 8. The Oltener Aktionskomitee (1918) — Organizing the General Strike

- By late 1917, wartime hardship had pushed Swiss workers to the breaking point: food shortages, inflation, and no social safety net
- On **4 February 1918**, the **Oltener Aktionskomitee** (Olten Action Committee) was founded in Olten — chosen precisely because of its central railway connections
- Chairman: **Robert Grimm**, the charismatic Social Democratic leader
- Founding members included **Rosa Bloch-Bollag**, **Konrad Ilg** (metalworkers' president), and representatives from trade unions, the Social Democratic Party, and the labor press
- The committee formulated **nine demands**: re-election of the National Council by proportional representation, **women's suffrage**, the **48-hour work week**, old-age and disability insurance, food supply security, a wealth tax, army reform, a state export monopoly, and an end to compulsory civilian service
- On **7 November 1918**, the committee called a protest strike in response to military occupation of Zurich; by **10 November**, this escalated into a **nationwide general strike** (Landesstreik)
- Approximately **250,000 workers** participated across Switzerland — the largest labor action in Swiss history
- On **13 November**, the Federal Council issued an ultimatum; fearing armed conflict, the committee voted to end the strike on **14 November**
- **Aftermath**: Grimm, Friedrich Schneider, and Fritz Platten received **six-month prison sentences** for incitement
- **Legacy**: Though the strike was called off, two key demands were quickly achieved — the **48-hour work week** (1919) and **proportional representation** for National Council elections. Women's suffrage took until **1971**, but the Landesstreik fundamentally reshaped Swiss labor relations and social policy

## 9. The Gruppe Olten — Switzerland's Literary Rebellion

- In **1971**, a group of prominent Swiss writers **seceded from the Schweizerischer Schriftstellerverein (SSV)** — the Swiss Writers' Union — which they considered unprogressive and too close to the establishment
- They founded the **Gruppe Olten**, meeting at the **Bahnhofbuffet Olten** — choosing the station restaurant as a deliberate symbol: neutral ground, accessible to all, no pretension
- Founding members included some of the greatest names in 20th-century German-language literature: **Max Frisch**, **Friedrich Dürrenmatt**, **Peter Bichsel**, **Adolf Muschg**, and **Otto F. Walter**
- The group championed **political engagement** in literature, freedom of expression, and progressive causes
- At its peak, it comprised **22 established writers** and served as a counterweight to the conservative literary mainstream
- The Gruppe Olten disbanded in **2002**, the same year the SSV itself dissolved — both merging into new structures
- The **Schriftstellerweg** (Writers' Trail) in Olten today features over **70 listening stations** with six audio tours narrated by Swiss authors including **Franz Hohler**, **Alex Capus**, and **Pedro Lenz**

## 10. The Medieval Old Town

- Olten's Altstadt has a distinctive **onion-shaped layout** clustered around the Marktgasse and narrow side alleys
- The old town contains approximately **30 buildings and monuments** of historical significance
- Key landmarks beyond the Stadtturm and bridge:
  - **Hexenturm** (Witch Tower): Dating to approximately **1420**, a four-story stone tower that served as a prison for accused witches and later as storage — one of the oldest structures in Olten
  - **Spittelschür**: Built in **1415**, originally a hospital ("Spittel") that later became a granary ("Schür") — a well-preserved timber-framed building with high gables and exposed beams
  - **Capuchin Monastery**: Founded **1646** at the Klosterplatz
  - **St. Martin's Church**: The current church replaced the medieval one demolished in 1844; the original was rebuilt in **1461** after the devastating fire of 1422
- The old town is now a **car-free** promenade along the Marktgasse, with cafes, shops, and cultural venues

## 11. Notable People Connected to Olten

- **Martin Disteli** (1802–1844): Born in Olten, Switzerland's foremost **political caricaturist** of the pre-1848 era. His satirical drawings bridged French and German graphical traditions. The **Kunstmuseum Olten** (opened 1905, originally called the Martin-Disteli-Museum until 1964) houses a major collection of his work
- **Bernhard Hammer** (1822–1907): Born in Olten, member of the **Swiss Federal Council** (1875–1890) and **President of the Confederation** twice (1879 and 1889)
- **Werner Munzinger** (1832–1875): Born in Olten, explorer and administrator in the **Horn of Africa**. Served as French consul at Massawa, explored Eritrea and Sudan. Killed in **1875** leading an Egyptian expedition near Aussa (Ethiopia)
- **Paul Hermann Müller** (1899–1965): Born in Olten, chemist at J.R. Geigy who discovered the insecticidal properties of **DDT** in 1939. Awarded the **1948 Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine** for enabling the control of malaria, typhus, and other vector-borne diseases
- **Lilian Uchtenhagen** (1928–2016): Politician and economist, the first woman nominated for the Swiss Federal Council (1983)
- **Pedro Lenz** (born 1965): Writer known for his Bernese dialect prose, lives and works in Olten
- **Alex Capus** (born 1961): French-Swiss novelist who writes in German, longtime Olten resident and owner of a bar in town. His novel *Munzinger Pascha* (1997) tells the story of Olten's own Werner Munzinger

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The bridge that wouldn't give up** — Told from the perspective of the old wooden bridge itself: surviving floods, fires, and wars across 700 years, watching Olten grow from a tiny bridgehead into a railway city
2. **Kilometre Zero** — A child discovers the memorial stone on track 12 and is transported back to 1856, when the first trains arrived and transformed a quiet town forever. They ride the first locomotive through the dark Hauenstein tunnel
3. **The night the writers met** — At the Bahnhofbuffet, the ghosts of Frisch and Dürrenmatt are still arguing about literature. A child overhears them and must settle their debate before the last train departs
4. **The Stadtturm clock strikes thirteen** — The 1577 clock face malfunctions at midnight; each chime beyond twelve reveals a different century of Olten's history — Roman soldiers, Frohburg knights, striking workers, famous writers
5. **250,000 voices** — November 1918: a child in Olten watches Robert Grimm and the committee debate through the night. Should they call the strike? The child sees history being made in a railway station restaurant
6. **The explorer from the Dreitannenstadt** — Young Werner Munzinger leaves Olten for Africa, carrying a fir cone from the Born forest as a lucky charm. A story of adventure, curiosity, and homesickness
7. **The three fir trees** — Origin legend: why does Olten's coat of arms show three fir trees? A child plants three seedlings on three hills and discovers each tree can whisper the story of the town it watches over
8. **Where Switzerland meets** — A modern-day treasure hunt: clues hidden at six railway platforms lead a child across the station, the bridge, the Hexenturm, and the Stadtturm to discover why this small town became the heart of a nation
