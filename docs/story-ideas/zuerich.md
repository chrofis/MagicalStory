## 1. Celtic Origins — The Oppidum on the Lindenhof

- Recent archaeological research has pushed Zurich's origins back further than previously thought: it was **not the Roman military around 15 BC**, but rather **Celtic settlers around 80 BC** who founded the settlement
- These inhabitants belonged to the **Helvetii**, the Celtic tribe whose territory covered the entire Swiss lowlands
- Traces of this Celtic settlement have been found only on the **left bank of the Limmat** — on and around the **Lindenhof**, a moraine hill rising about **20 meters** above the Limmat and the lake
- A crucial find: **tapping plates** (casting molds for coin blanks) — identifying the settlement as a **coin-minting center** and therefore a central administrative place for the surrounding region
- In November 2007, excavations uncovered well plates used by Celts to melt small amounts of metal into blanks for coinage
- The strategic hilltop position and the lake crossing point made the Lindenhof a natural stronghold

## 2. Roman Period — Turicum

- Roman military arrived at the Celtic settlement from around **40 BC**, controlling the strategic lake crossing
- The settlement became known as **Turicum** — a Roman vicus (small town) serving as a **customs station and river port**
- The earliest written record of the name: a **2nd-century tombstone** found in 1747 on the Lindenhof, inscribed "STA(tio) TUR(i)CEN(sis)" — confirming Turicum's role as a customs post
- In the **2nd century AD**: an enlarged public bathhouse with **hypocaust heating** (underfloor heating) stood southeast of the Lindenhof — its remains are visible today in the **Thermengasse**
- The Roman fortress on the Lindenhof controlled river traffic and overland routes
- The Roman name "Turicum" eventually evolved into "Zürich"

## 3. The Legend of Felix and Regula — Zurich's Patron Saints

- Felix and Regula (and their servant Exuperantius) were members of the **Theban Legion** — a Roman legion from Upper Egypt composed entirely of Christians
- Under Emperor **Maximian** (286–305 AD), the legion was ordered to participate in persecutions of Christians. They refused and were massacred at **Agaunum** (today Saint-Maurice in Valais)
- Felix, Regula, and Exuperantius escaped and fled north to **Turicum** (Zurich)
- They were captured near the **Lindenhof** — the site of the Roman fortress, where Governor Decius resided
- They were **tortured and beheaded** at the location where the **Wasserkirche** (Water Church) now stands, on the small island in the Limmat
- **The miracle**: After their beheading, the three saints **picked up their own severed heads**, walked **40 paces uphill**, and lay down at the spot where they wished to be buried — the site where the **Grossmünster** was later built
- The oldest written record of this legend dates from the **8th century**
- Felix and Regula became **Zurich's patron saints**; their images appear on the city's coat of arms (depicted carrying their heads)

## 4. The Legend of Charlemagne and the Snake (Karl der Grosse und die Schlange)

- According to the chronicle of **Heinrich Brennwald** (written 1508–1516):
- Emperor Karl der Grosse lived at the Felix and Regula church. He erected a **column with a bell** in the forecourt — anyone could ring it at noon to file complaints and seek justice
- One day, a **giant snake** rang the bell. Curious, the emperor followed the snake to its nest by the lake
- There he found a **fat toad** sitting on the snake's eggs, having stolen the nest
- Karl judged in the snake's favor: the toad was **condemned to death by fire**
- Days later, the grateful snake crawled into the emperor's hall, opened a **golden goblet**, and dropped a **precious gemstone** inside before bowing and departing
- The delighted emperor had a church built at the location of the nest — the people called it **"Wasserkilch"** (Water Church → Wasserkirche)
- The legend elegantly explains the founding of both the Grossmünster (graves of the saints) and the Wasserkirche (the snake's nest site)

## 5. The Legend of Charlemagne and the Stag

- A separate legend: Kaiser Karl was hunting in Germany when he spotted a magnificent **white stag**
- He pursued it across all of Europe until the stag led him to Zurich
- At the spot where Felix and Regula were buried, Karl's **horse suddenly knelt down** — honoring the saints' burial site
- Karl recognized the divine sign and ordered the construction of the **Grossmünster** above the graves
- This legend was used historically to explain why the Grossmünster bore Charlemagne's patronage, even though the historical Charlemagne's direct connection to Zurich is debatable
- Historical reality: the pilgrimage site on the Münsterhügel (cathedral hill) developed into a **men's monastery**, confirmed by Emperor **Karl III ("the Fat")** — Charlemagne's great-grandson — around **870** as a collegiate foundation. The confusion between the two Karls likely gave rise to the legend

## 6. The Grossmünster

- Built above the **graves of Felix and Regula** on the Münsterhügel
- The current **Romanesque building** was begun around **1100** and completed around **1220**
- The twin towers with their distinctive flat tops are Zurich's most recognizable landmark
- Features characteristics of **German Romanesque architecture**
- A seated figure of **Charlemagne** holding a sword once adorned the south tower (now a copy; original in the crypt)
- In **1519**, the city council elected **Huldrych Zwingli** as "Leutpriester" (parish priest) at the Grossmünster — launching the **Reformation** in Zurich. Zwingli preached against mercenary service, saint worship, indulgence trafficking, and the mass

## 7. The Fraumünster

- Founded **July 21, 853** by King **Ludwig der Deutsche** (Louis the German), who granted his daughter **Hildegard** a royal convent in the "settlement of Zurich"
- Hildegard became the first abbess; after her early death, her younger sister **Bertha** succeeded her
- The convent followed **Benedictine rules** and was richly endowed
- Ludwig gave it the entire **royal court of Zurich**, the **Albis forest**, the **land of Uri**, and later the royal court of Cham
- In **1045**, King Henry III granted the convent the rights to **hold markets, collect tolls, and mint coins** — effectively making the **abbess the ruler of the city**
- For centuries, the Fraumünster abbess held **more political and economic power** than any other institution in Zurich — one of the most powerful ecclesiastical positions held by women in medieval Switzerland
- The abbey's political power gradually waned as the guilds and city council gained strength
- Today famous for the **5 stained-glass windows by Marc Chagall** (installed 1970) and the **rose window by Augusto Giacometti** (1945) in the transept

## 8. The Wasserkirche (Water Church)

- Located on what was originally a **small island in the Limmat** (now connected to the shore)
- According to legend: the site where **Felix and Regula were beheaded** and where **Charlemagne's snake** had its nest
- Also the location where a statue of **Zwingli** stands
- The present Late Gothic building dates from **1479–1484**
- After the Reformation, it served as a library and was secularized; returned to church use in the 19th century

## 9. The Lindenhof

- A **moraine hill** 20 meters above the Limmat, the **oldest settled area** of Zurich
- Roman fortress site (Celtic before that)
- The **Roman governor** reportedly resided here — and in the Felix & Regula legend, this is where they were captured and tortured
- Legend says **treasure is buried** under the linden trees
- Today: a peaceful park with panoramic views over the old town, the Limmat, and the university — one of Zurich's most beloved gathering spots

## 10. The Guilds (Zünfte) and Political History

- In **1336**, Rudolf Brun led a revolution overthrowing the old aristocratic council and establishing the **Zunftordnung** (guild constitution)
- **13 guilds** were established, each representing a different trade — they shared political power with the Constaffel (society of the nobility and merchants)
- The guilds controlled prices, wages, working conditions, and had their own halls, coats of arms, and traditions
- They lost their formal **political power in the 19th century** but continue as **private associations** — 26 guilds exist today
- The guilds are central to the **Sechseläuten** tradition

## 11. Sechseläuten and the Böögg

- Zurich's **spring festival**, held on the third Monday of April
- **Medieval origins**: City ordinances strictly regulated the length of the workday. In winter, workers labored as long as there was daylight. In summer (starting the Monday after the equinox), work legally ended when **church bells rang at 6 pm** — the "six o'clock ringing" (Sechseläuten)
- The first day of summer hours was celebrated in the guild halls — the origin of the festival
- **The Böögg**: Originally a masked carnival figure who frightened children and caused mischief. In **1892**, the guilds took responsibility for the ritual burning. Since then, the Böögg has represented **winter itself** — hence the snowman shape
- **1902**: The combination of the guild parade and the official Böögg burning was established
- **The ritual**: After an elaborate parade of all guilds through the city, a **Böögg (snowman effigy stuffed with firecrackers)** is placed atop a pyre at **Sechseläutenplatz** and lit at exactly 6 pm
- **The prediction**: The faster the Böögg's **head explodes**, the better the coming summer will be. This is taken semi-seriously and reported in national media every year
- The fastest Böögg explosion: **5 minutes 7 seconds** (2003). The slowest: **26 minutes 28 seconds** (some years have infamously long burns leading to gloomy summer predictions)

## 12. Baker Wackerbold (1280)

- A medieval tale from Zurich's guild history
- Baker Wackerbold was caught **baking underweight bread** — charging full price for loaves that were too light
- As punishment, he was placed in the **"Schnelli"** — a cage that was publicly **dunked into the Limmat** three times
- The story illustrates medieval guild justice and consumer protection — guilds enforced strict quality standards

## 13. Famous People Connected to Zurich

- **Huldrych Zwingli** (1484–1531): Reformer, priest at Grossmünster from 1519, launched the Swiss Reformation
- **Gottfried Keller** (1819–1890): Born in Zurich, writer and poet, author of "Kleider machen Leute" (Clothes Make the Man), also served as First State Secretary of Canton Zurich
- **James Joyce** (1882–1941): Irish writer, lived in exile in Zurich, died there 1941, buried at Fluntern Cemetery. Wrote "Ulysses" and "Finnegans Wake"
- **Thomas Mann** (1875–1955): Nobel Prize-winning author (Buddenbrooks, The Magic Mountain), died in Zurich
- **Lenin** (Vladimir Ilyich Ulyanov): Lived in exile in Zurich during WWI (about a year), returned to Russia in 1917 when the February Revolution broke out — left from Zurich Hauptbahnhof
- **Marc Chagall** (1887–1985): Created the 5 famous stained-glass windows in the Fraumünster (1970)
- **Augusto Giacometti** (1877–1947): Created the rose window in the Fraumünster transept (1945)
- **Albert Einstein**: Studied at ETH Zurich (1896–1900), returned to teach there (1912–1914)
- **Richard Wagner**: Lived in Zurich 1849–1858 during his exile from Germany
- **Johanna Spyri** (1827–1901): Author of "Heidi," lived and worked in Zurich

## 14. Geography

- **Lake Zurich**: 40 km long glacial lake, southern boundary of the city
- **Limmat**: The river flowing from the lake through the old town, dividing it into left-bank and right-bank neighborhoods
- **Uetliberg**: Zurich's "house mountain" (871m), offering a 360° panorama of the city, lake, and Alps. Accessible by train (S10 line)
- **Zürichberg**: The eastern hill district, residential, with forest and the Dolder recreation area
