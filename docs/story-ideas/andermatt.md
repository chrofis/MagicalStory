## 1. Geography — The Crossroads of the Alps

- Andermatt lies at **1,437 meters** above sea level in the **Ursern valley** (Urseren), a high Alpine valley in the canton of **Uri**, on the headwaters of the river **Reuss**
- The village sits at the intersection of **four major Alpine passes**: the **Gotthard** (2,106 m, south to Ticino), the **Oberalp** (2,044 m, east to Graubünden), the **Furka** (2,429 m, west to Valais), and the **Susten** (2,224 m, northwest to the Bernese Oberland)
- This unique position makes Andermatt the **natural crossroads of Switzerland** — where north-south and east-west routes have met since ancient times
- The Ursern valley runs southwest to northeast, encompassing three villages: **Realp**, **Hospental**, and **Andermatt** — surrounded entirely by mountains
- Four rivers originate near Andermatt: the **Reuss** (flowing north to Lake Lucerne), the **Ticino** (south to Italy), the **Rhine** (east, with its source at Lake Toma near the Oberalp), and the **Rhône** (west, from the glacier on the Furka side)
- On the Oberalp Pass stands the **only lighthouse in the Alps** — a replica symbolising the nearby source of the Rhine; the original stands at the Rhine's mouth near Rotterdam

## 2. The Ursern Valley — A Free Alpine Community

- Archaeological finds dating back to **4000 BC** indicate the Ursern valley was already populated in the **Neolithic period**
- The valley was colonised by **Walser** settlers (Alemannic-speaking mountain farmers) prior to the **12th century**, whose distinctive dialect survives in the region
- From around **800 AD**, the valley belonged to the **Benedictine Disentis Abbey**, which held spiritual and temporal authority over the Ursern
- The parish of Andermatt was first documented in **1203**
- In **1332**, settlers clashed with troops of Disentis Abbey at the Oberalp Pass, defeating the abbey's forces
- Urseren became **reichsfrei** (a free imperial territory) in **1382** and joined the **Old Swiss Confederacy** in **1410**, allying with Uri for defence while retaining its own privileges as an independent community
- The valley's autonomy and self-governance are a source of deep local pride — the **Talmuseum Ursern** (Ursern Valley Museum) preserves this heritage in the "Suworow House," a patrician building from **1786**

## 3. The Gotthard Pass — Switzerland's Most Important Alpine Crossing

- The **Gotthard Pass** (2,106 m / 6,909 ft) connects **Andermatt** in Uri with **Airolo** in Ticino — the gateway between northern and southern Europe
- Although the Romans knew of the pass, it was **not a major trans-Alpine route until the early 13th century**, when the Schöllenen Gorge was finally bridged
- The pass is named after **Saint Godehard of Hildesheim** (960–1038), a Bavarian-born Benedictine bishop canonised in **1131**; the dukes of Bavaria built a **hospice** at the summit in his honour, first documented in the **14th century**
- For medieval traders, the Gotthard was the **shortest route** between the markets of Flanders and Lombardy — mule caravans carried cloth, salt, wine, and spices
- The transit trade transformed the communities along the route and was a driving force behind the formation of the **Swiss Confederacy** — the original cantons of Uri, Schwyz, and Unterwalden controlled access to the pass
- Today three tunnels pierce the massif, each the **world's longest** at its time of construction: the **Gotthard Rail Tunnel** (1882), the **Gotthard Road Tunnel** (1980), and the **Gotthard Base Tunnel** (2016)

## 4. The Devil's Bridge and the Schöllenen Gorge

- The **Schöllenen Gorge** (Schöllenenschlucht) is a narrow, sheer-walled granite gorge carved by the Reuss between **Göschenen** and **Andermatt** — for centuries the impassable barrier blocking access to the Gotthard
- Around **1220–1230**, the first **wooden bridge** and a daring boardwalk of beams wedged into the cliff face were built across the gorge, opening the Gotthard route and transforming European trade
- The legendary **Teufelsbrücke** (Devil's Bridge) is the gorge's most famous feature; the name "Teiffels Brucken" was first recorded in **1587**
- **The legend** (documented by the naturalist Johann Jakob Scheuchzer in **1716**): The people of Uri, unable to build a bridge across the raging Reuss, recruited the **Devil** himself. He demanded the **soul of the first being to cross**. On the advice of a clever alderman, they drove a **goat** across instead. Enraged at being tricked, the Devil fetched an enormous boulder to smash the bridge, but encountered a holy woman who marked it with a **cross**, forcing him to drop it and flee
- The **Teufelsstein** (Devil's Stone), a granite boulder of about **2,200 tonnes** and 12 metres high, sat near Göschenen for centuries. In **1970**, it was scheduled for demolition for the N2 motorway, but a public campaign saved it — in **1971** it was moved 127 metres at a cost of 250,000 francs
- In **1595**, the original wooden bridge was replaced by a **stone arch bridge** — the "old Devil's Bridge" whose picturesque ruin is still visible today
- In **1950**, the Uri painter **Heinrich Danioth** (1896–1953) painted a striking expressionist **mural of the Devil with a billy goat** in fierce red on the rock face above the road bridge — controversial in conservative 1950s Uri, it is now a beloved landmark

## 5. Suvorov's Alpine Crossing (1799)

- In September **1799**, during the War of the Second Coalition, Russian Field Marshal **Alexander Vasilyevich Suvorov** led **21,000 troops** on a daring march from northern Italy across the Alps to confront the French under General **André Masséna**
- Suvorov chose the **Gotthard Pass** — the shortest but most dangerous route — arriving on **21 September 1799**
- French forces under General **Lecourbe** (8,500 men) held the pass; after two repulsed assaults, General **Bagration** outflanked the French, forcing their retreat
- On **25 September**, the French blew up the **Devil's Bridge** to halt the Russian advance. Suvorov's officers improvised a repair using **wooden beams lashed together with their own neckerchiefs**, and the Russians stormed across under heavy fire — with terrible losses
- That same day, Suvorov used the **Suworow House** in Andermatt (now the Ursern Valley Museum) as his **headquarters**
- The campaign ended in strategic failure: before Suvorov could link up with his allies, Masséna defeated the Russian corps at the **Second Battle of Zurich** on 25 September
- After a gruelling **300 km march** over 21 days — scaling passes at Gotthard, Kinzig, Pragel, and Panix — only **15,000 of the original 21,000** soldiers reached safety; 5,000 were badly wounded
- Masséna later confessed he would **"exchange all his victories for Suvorov's passage of the Alps"**
- In **1898**, Russia erected a **memorial cross** carved into the rock face near the Devil's Bridge — it stands to this day and is easily accessible from Andermatt
- The famous painting **"Suvorov Crossing the Alps in 1799"** by **Vasily Surikov** (1899) immortalised the campaign in Russian art

## 6. The Gotthard Railway Tunnel (1882) — Engineering Marvel, Human Tragedy

- Construction began in September **1872** at both portals — **Göschenen** (north) and **Airolo** (south) — under the direction of the Genevan engineer **Louis Favre**
- The tunnel was **15 km** long — the **world's longest** at the time — running roughly 1,000 metres beneath the summit of the pass
- The project marked the **first large-scale use of dynamite** in tunnel construction
- Working conditions were brutal: temperatures underground reached **40°C**, and a hookworm epidemic (**Ancylostoma duodenale**) ravaged the mostly **Italian migrant workers** from Piedmont
- In **1875**, desperate workers went on strike over the inhuman conditions; the **Swiss Army** was called in, killing **4 strikers** and wounding 13
- Around **199 workers** died during construction from dynamite accidents, rockfalls, water inrushes, and disease
- Louis Favre himself never saw the tunnel completed — he died of a **heart attack** inside the tunnel at Göschenen on **19 July 1879**
- **Breakthrough** came on **29 February 1880**, with remarkable precision for the era
- The tunnel opened for traffic on **1 January 1882**; in its first full year (1883), some **250,000 passengers** and **300,000 tonnes** of freight passed through
- A **memorial** to the fallen workers was erected near Airolo station in **1932**

## 7. The Gotthard Base Tunnel (2016) — World Record Under the Alps

- The **Gotthard Base Tunnel** (GBT), with a route length of **57.09 km** (35.5 miles), is the **world's longest railway tunnel** and the deepest traffic tunnel, reaching **2,300 metres** below the mountain surface
- The concept was first proposed in **1947**; Swiss voters approved the project by referendum in **1992**; formal construction began on **4 November 1999**
- Two single-track tubes connect **Erstfeld** (Uri) with **Bodio** (Ticino), passing beneath **Sedrun** (Graubünden)
- East tube breakthrough: **October 2010**; west tube: **March 2011** — with a horizontal deviation of just **8 cm** and vertical deviation of **1 cm**
- **Nine workers** died during the 17 years of construction
- Final cost: **CHF 12.2 billion** (approximately USD 12 billion)
- The tunnel was ceremonially opened on **1 June 2016**, with regular service beginning in **December 2016**
- Passenger trains travel at **200 km/h**, crossing from Erstfeld to Bodio in about **20 minutes** — reducing trans-Alpine journey times by approximately **40 minutes**
- The GBT is the centrepiece of the Swiss **NEAT** (Neue Eisenbahn-Alpentransversale / New Railway Link through the Alps) programme

## 8. Military Significance — The Fortress and the National Redoubt

- Since **1885**, Andermatt has been a **garrison town** of the Swiss Federal Army, which built extensive infrastructure around the Gotthard
- In the **1880s**, Switzerland began constructing **fortress complexes** at three strategic Alpine crossing points: **St. Maurice**, **St. Gotthard**, and **Sargans** — defending the routes between Germany and Italy
- During World War II, General **Henri Guisan** expanded this into the **National Redoubt** (Schweizer Reduit / Réduit national): if invaded, the Swiss Army would withdraw into the Alps, abandoning the lowland cities but making the Alpine passes **impregnable** — denying the Axis powers the vital north-south transit routes
- The Gotthard fortress was one of the **largest underground defence installations** in Switzerland — hidden behind inconspicuous grey doors in the rock face, the caverns contained cannons, howitzers, dormitories, kitchens, field hospitals, bakeries, and ammunition magazines — accommodating **100 to 600 soldiers**
- Abandoned fortifications — including **Fort Stöckli** and the **Artillery Works Gütsch** — still overlook Andermatt as relics of this era
- The fortress remained a **top-secret military facility** until **2001**, when it was declassified
- Today, the **Sasso San Gottardo** museum at the summit of the pass occupies the former fortress caverns — the **largest fortress complex open to the public** in Switzerland; visitors ride an underground funicular ("Metro del Sasso") to explore the gun rooms, troop quarters, and command facilities
- The museum also displays **1.5 tonnes of giant quartz crystals** discovered in the massif — a geological "find of the century"

## 9. The Glacier Express — The Slowest Express Train in the World

- The **Glacier Express** connects **Zermatt** and **St. Moritz** via Andermatt — a **291 km** journey through the heart of the Swiss Alps lasting approximately **8 hours**, earning it the title of the **"slowest express train in the world"**
- The first Glacier Express ran in **June 1930**, operated jointly by the BVZ (Brig-Visp-Zermatt-Bahn), the FOB (Furka-Oberalp-Bahn), and the RhB (Rhätische Bahn) — initially in **summer only**, since the line over the Oberalp Pass could not be kept open in winter
- The opening of the **Furka Base Tunnel** on **26 June 1982** made **year-round service** possible for the first time
- The route crosses **291 bridges** and passes through **91 tunnels**, reaching its highest point at the **Oberalp Pass (2,033 m)** — near Andermatt
- In **2003**, the BVZ and FOB merged to form the **Matterhorn Gotthard Bahn** (MGB)
- Since **2017**, the Glacier Express is operated by **Glacier Express AG**, headquartered in Andermatt, jointly owned by MGB and RhB

## 10. The Furka Pass — Glaciers, Goldfinger, and Steam

- The **Furka Pass** (2,429 m) connects Andermatt (via Realp) with Gletsch in the **Valais** — one of the highest and most spectacular pass roads in Switzerland
- The pass road was built for **military reasons** and opened in **1867** — at the time the longest pass road in Switzerland
- Near the summit lies the **Rhône Glacier** (Rhonegletscher), the source of the **river Rhône** — once a major tourist attraction with an ice grotto; the glacier has dramatically retreated due to climate change
- The legendary **Hotel Belvédère**, built in **1882** by Josef Seiler on a hairpin bend directly overlooking the glacier, became one of the most photographed buildings in the Alps; it has been **closed since 2015**
- In **1964**, the Furka Pass achieved cinematic fame as a filming location for **"Goldfinger"**, the third James Bond film — the scene where Tilly Masterson attempts to shoot Goldfinger was filmed against the backdrop of the Rhône Glacier; a curve is now marked as **"James Bond Strasse"**
- The **Furka Cogwheel Steam Railway** (Dampfbahn Furka-Bergstrecke / DFB) is a heritage railway running **18 km** between Realp and Oberwald on the original Glacier Express route over the mountain — with century-old locomotives and 1930s carriages painstakingly restored by **volunteers**; the line was progressively reopened from **1992** and completed in **2010**
- The steam railway climbs to **2,160 m** at the Furka station — offering views of the glacier and surrounding peaks that the modern tunnel bypasses entirely

## 11. The Andermatt Swiss Alps Resort — Renaissance of a Village

- For most of the 20th century, Andermatt was shaped by the **Swiss military** — the garrison was the village's primary employer and reason for existence
- In **2005**, the Swiss Army announced the **partial closure** of the Andermatt garrison, freeing large areas of land and threatening the village's economic future
- Egyptian entrepreneur **Samih Sawiris**, head of the Orascom Development group, proposed transforming Andermatt into a **luxury year-round Alpine destination** — his first major project outside the Middle East
- In **2007**, the local population approved the development with a **96% affirmative vote**
- The company **Andermatt Swiss Alps AG** was formally established in **2008**; construction began in **2009**
- The flagship **The Chedi Andermatt** hotel opened in **December 2013** — a five-star property blending Alpine and Asian design
- By 2025, the project had invested approximately **CHF 1.8 billion**, creating over **500 direct jobs** and making ASA one of the **largest employers** in the canton of Uri
- The development includes **six hotels**, **42 residential buildings** with over **700 apartments** in the new **Andermatt Reuss** quarter, an **18-hole golf course**, and a concert hall
- The **SkiArena Andermatt-Sedrun** — expanded with modern lifts connecting Andermatt to Sedrun over the Oberalp — is now the **largest ski resort in central Switzerland**
- The transformation has reversed Andermatt's population decline: from approximately **1,351 residents** in 2005 to about **1,578 in 2024**

## 12. Notable Figures and Cultural Heritage

- **Bernhard Russi** (born 1948 in Andermatt): Olympic downhill skiing champion (**1972 Sapporo gold medal**), legendary Swiss ski racer, and later a renowned course designer for Olympic and World Cup downhill courses worldwide — the local hero, honoured with a permanent exhibition in the Ursern Valley Museum
- **Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** crossed the Gotthard Pass **three times** (1775, 1779, 1797), collecting minerals and drawing inspiration — the Sasso San Gottardo museum houses Switzerland's **first permanent Goethe exhibition**
- The Ursern valley preserves a distinctive **Walser-Alemannic dialect** and traditional Alpine farming culture — summer **Alpaufzug** (cattle drives to high pastures) continue to this day
- The valley's churches feature notable Baroque interiors; Andermatt's **Pfarrkirche St. Peter und Paul** contains significant religious art

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The Devil's deal at the bridge** — Retelling of the Teufelsbrücke legend from the goat's perspective: a brave little goat volunteers to cross first and outwits the Devil, saving the village
2. **Suvorov's lost drummer boy** — A young Russian drummer boy gets separated during the storming of the Devil's Bridge in 1799 and is helped by a local Urseren child; they navigate the gorge together
3. **The tunnel builders** — A child in 1878 follows their Italian father into the Gotthard tunnel, discovering the dangerous world of the dynamite workers; a story about courage and the human cost of progress
4. **The crossroads map** — A magical old map in the Ursern Valley Museum shows four glowing paths (one for each pass); the child must travel each pass to collect a token before the mountain spirit seals the routes for winter
5. **The glacier's memory** — Near the Rhône Glacier on the Furka Pass, a child finds an old photograph from 1900 showing the glacier reaching the road; they are transported back in time and must find their way home as the ice retreats around them
6. **The slowest express in the world** — A child boards the Glacier Express in Andermatt and discovers each tunnel is a portal to a different era of Swiss Alpine history — from medieval mule drivers to the 2016 tunnel breakthrough
7. **The fortress in the mountain** — A child on a school trip to the Sasso San Gottardo museum gets lost in the underground fortress corridors and must solve riddles left by soldiers from different eras to find the way out — discovering the giant crystal cavern at the end
8. **Bernhard Russi and the mountain wind** — Young Bernhard can hear the mountain wind speaking to him, showing him the fastest line down the slope; a story about a local boy's gift becoming Olympic gold
