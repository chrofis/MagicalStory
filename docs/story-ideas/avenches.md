## 1. Aventicum — Capital of Roman Helvetia (Capitale de l'Helvétie romaine)

- **Aventicum** was the largest city and capital of **Roman Helvetia** (Civitas Helvetiorum), named after the Celtic spring goddess **Aventia** — a deity of water and healing
- The settlement was established in the late **1st century BC** or early **1st century AD** as the administrative centre of the recently conquered Helvetii territory
- Under **Augustus**, the city was incorporated into the province of **Gallia Belgica**
- The Roman historian **Tacitus** designated Aventicum **"Caput gentis"** — capital of the Helvetian people
- Emperor **Vespasian** elevated Aventicum to the rank of **colonia** around **73–74 AD**, granting it exceptional civic status. The full title: **Colonia Pia Flavia Constans Emerita Helvetiorum Foederata**
- Vespasian had a personal connection: his father, **Titus Flavius Sabinus**, had worked as a banker in Aventicum and died there in **69 AD**
- At its peak in the **2nd century AD**, the city housed nearly **20,000 inhabitants** — a population Avenches has never matched since
- Aventicum featured a **forum**, **thermal baths** (Thermes du Forum, built c. 77 AD), a **theatre**, an **amphitheatre**, multiple **temples**, and a network of **six aqueducts** — the longest stretching 17 km

## 2. Geography — The Broye Plain (Plaine de la Broye)

- Avenches sits at approximately **480 metres** above sea level on a gentle hill in the wide **Broye plain** (Plaine de la Broye), in the north of **Canton Vaud** near the border with Canton Fribourg
- Located between **Bern** (30 km east) and **Lausanne** (55 km southwest), midway along the Swiss Plateau
- **Lake Murten** (Lac de Morat / Murtensee) lies just 5 km to the northwest; **Lake Neuchatel** is to the southwest
- The landscape of gentle rolling hills, vineyards, fields, and nature reserves between the two lakes makes the region ideal for walking and cycling
- The modern town has a population of approximately **4,500** — a striking contrast to the 20,000 who lived within the Roman walls
- Avenches is a member of **"Les Plus Beaux Villages de Suisse"** (The Most Beautiful Villages in Switzerland)

## 3. The Roman City Walls (L'enceinte romaine)

- Aventicum was encircled by a monumental wall measuring **5.5 km** in length, incorporating **73 towers** and **4 gates**
- The wall foundations were approximately **3 metres** wide, with walls rising to about **5 metres** in height, topped by a crenellated parapet
- A surrounding ditch (fossé), roughly **3.8 metres** wide and **1.6 metres** deep, encircled the fortifications
- In marshy areas, **45,000 oak posts** were driven into the ground to support the foundations
- The enclosed area was approximately **230 hectares** — far larger than the medieval or modern town
- The walls were primarily a display of **civic status** rather than serious military defences — Aventicum enjoyed the security of the Pax Romana
- The **Eastern Gate** (Porte de l'Est) was a monumental structure measuring **28 by 26 metres**, with a circular courtyard, two carriageways, and pedestrian passages flanked by polygonal towers
- The **Tornallaz tower**, north of the Eastern Gate, is the best-preserved of the 73 towers — later repurposed as a medieval watchtower and restored in the 20th century

## 4. The Roman Amphitheatre (L'amphitheatre romain)

- Built in **two stages** in the 2nd century AD: the first phase around **130 AD** (capacity approximately **9,000**), expanded around **165 AD** to **16,000 spectators** in **31 rows**
- Original dimensions: **99 by 86 metres**; after expansion: **105 by 92 metres**
- Stairs, walls, and axial entrances were made of **stone**; the seating tiers were **wood**
- Spectators of all social classes attended gladiatorial combats (munera), exotic animal hunts (venationes), and public spectacles
- **Ceased to be used** in the 4th century; subsequently served as a **stone quarry** for centuries
- In **1074**, Bishop **Burkhard von Oltigen** of Lausanne built a fortified tower on the eastern entrance — this 12th-century **Tour de l'Amphitheatre** now houses the Roman Museum
- Today the amphitheatre is celebrated for its **splendid acoustics** and hosts major annual events:
  - **Rock Oz'Arenes** (August) — a rock festival founded in the 1990s, "the smallest of the big festivals," featuring international stars
  - **Avenches Tattoo** (September) — an international military music festival
  - **Opera festival** — classical opera performances during summer months

## 5. The Cigognier Sanctuary (Le sanctuaire du Cigognier)

- The **Cigognier** (from French **cigogne** = stork) was the most important temple complex in Aventicum, likely the chief sanctuary of the **Imperial cult** for the entire Helvetian civitas
- Construction began in **98 AD**, the first year of Emperor **Trajan's** reign, dated by dendrochronological analysis of oak foundation posts
- The complex was monumental: courtyard measuring **106.80 by 76.65 metres**, temple podium **41 by 26 metres**, inner chamber (cella) **18.70 by 24.05 metres** and approximately **20.60 metres** tall
- A processional staircase over **11 metres** wide led to the temple; the foundations rested on **45,000 oak posts**
- The design was inspired by Rome's **Templum Pacis** (Temple of Peace), with decorative friezes featuring **griffins, marine creatures, and eagle capitals**
- The sanctuary was likely dedicated to both the **Imperial cult** and the Romano-Celtic war god **Mars Caturix**
- Today, a single preserved column — a pier with two semi-columns, **9.96 metres** high, made of Jura limestone — marks the temple's location
- The name "Cigognier" dates from at least **1642**, when a **stork's nest** was documented atop the column. The nest was removed during restoration in **1978**, but the name endures

## 6. The Golden Bust (Le buste en or)

- On **19 April 1939**, unemployed workers from Lausanne — participating in an excavation programme organised by the association **Pro Aventico** — discovered a **golden bust** buried in a drain beneath the Cigognier sanctuary
- The bust measures **33.5 cm** high and weighs **1.59 kg** of **24-carat gold**
- It is one of only **six known golden busts** from the Roman Imperial period and one of the most important archaeological finds in Switzerland
- At a 1939 congress in Berlin, archaeologists identified the figure as **Emperor Marcus Aurelius** (reigned 161–180 AD), the philosopher-emperor
- The identification has been debated: archaeologist **Jean-Charles Balty** proposed it depicts Emperor **Julian**; Professor **Michel Fuchs** of the University of Lausanne recently argued it may represent **Tetricus I** (271–274 AD), the last emperor of the Gallic Empire
- The original is kept for security at the **Banque cantonale vaudoise** in Lausanne; a replica is on permanent display at the Roman Museum in Avenches
- In 2023, the bust was exhibited at the **J. Paul Getty Museum** in Los Angeles — titled "The Gold Emperor from Aventicum"

## 7. The Roman Museum (Musee romain d'Avenches)

- **Opened in 1838**, making it one of the oldest archaeological museums in Switzerland
- Housed in the **12th-century fortified tower** (Tour de l'Amphitheatre) built by the Bishop of Lausanne on the eastern entrance of the amphitheatre
- In the cellars, the original Roman **exterior wall** of the amphitheatre is still clearly visible
- The collection is devoted entirely to **Aventicum**: mosaics, friezes, inscriptions, everyday objects — all found in situ during archaeological excavations
- Notable exhibits include the golden bust replica, a mysterious **dodecahedron** (a hollow bronze twelve-sided object whose purpose remains unknown), and ceramics illustrating daily life
- The association **Pro Aventico**, founded in **1884–1885**, has driven systematic excavation and preservation ever since; its scholarly bulletin (Bulletin de l'Association Pro Aventico) has been published since **1887**
- **Free admission**; open Tuesday–Sunday with seasonal hours

## 8. The Medieval Town and the Chateau d'Avenches

- After the **Alemanni incursions** of approximately **275 AD**, Aventicum was heavily damaged and never recovered
- The city remained the seat of a **bishopric** until **585 AD**, when Bishop **Marius of Avenches** (Marius Aventicensis, 532–596) — a chronicler, goldsmith, and scholar venerated as a saint — transferred the episcopal see to **Lausanne**
- During the Middle Ages, the population moved **up the hill** for defensive purposes, abandoning the Roman city in the plain. The medieval town was far smaller, built within and atop the ancient ruins
- The **Chateau d'Avenches** was built in the **13th century** by the **bishops of Lausanne**, who served as the town's suzerains (seigneurs)
- The castle's emblematic **keep** (donjon) dates from this period
- Renovated in the **15th century** and again during the **Bernese period** (1536–1798), when Bern conquered Vaud and imposed the **Reformation**. The Bernese architects preserved the medieval keep but added a remarkable **Renaissance facade** — one of the finest examples in Switzerland
- After Vaud gained independence in **1803**, the castle became community property, serving variously as offices, a prison, and a library
- Today it houses an **art gallery** and a **theatre**, and is listed as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**

## 9. The Swiss National Stud Farm (Haras national suisse)

- In **1898**, the Swiss Confederation decided to establish a new stallion depot at Avenches, replacing an insufficient facility in Thun
- The **Haras national** was formally established in **1899**; the first horses arrived in **1901**
- Until **1927**, it housed only stallions and male foals. That year, the acquisition of ten **Franches-Montagnes** broodmares transformed it into a full stud farm (Haras federal)
- The Franches-Montagnes (Freiberger) is the **only typically Swiss horse breed** — the stud farm's primary mission is its promotion and preservation
- Architectural expansions followed: a mare facility (**1939**), a veterinary clinic (**1959**), and a reproduction centre later in the 20th century
- In **1997**, part of the stud farm was privatised, creating the **Institut equestre national Avenches** (IENA) — a multidisciplinary equestrian centre for training, competitions, and events
- Avenches is known as the **"capitale du cheval"** (capital of the horse) in Switzerland, hosting major equestrian competitions year-round
- The stud farm grounds are also the main nesting site for **white storks** (see below)

## 10. The Storks of Avenches (Les cigognes d'Avenches)

- Avenches is the **only nesting site for white storks** (cigognes blanches) in French-speaking Switzerland, ideally situated on their migratory route
- The connection between storks and the town is ancient: the **Cigognier column** had a documented stork nest as early as **1642**
- Today, nearly **30 pairs** of storks return each year to raise their young, primarily at the **Swiss National Stud Farm**, where nests are built on specially constructed platforms, rooftops, and trees
- After a month's incubation, chicks hatch at the end of **April** and take their first flight two months later in **June**
- The storks are easily observable at the stud farm and in surrounding fields, their characteristic **beak-clapping** (claquement de bec) echoing across the town
- The stork population has been growing steadily year by year, becoming an emblem of modern Avenches just as the Roman column that bears their name remains an emblem of the ancient city

## 11. The Decline and Rediscovery of Aventicum

- The **3rd-century crisis** of the Roman Empire struck Aventicum hard: around **275 AD**, Alemanni raiders pillaged and partially destroyed the city
- Unlike nearby Augusta Raurica (Augst), Aventicum never recovered — the shifting Roman frontier left it exposed and economically marginalised
- By the **4th century**, the amphitheatre had ceased functioning; the population dwindled
- The transfer of the bishopric to Lausanne in **585 AD** marked the symbolic end of Aventicum's importance
- For centuries, the Roman ruins were quarried for building stone — medieval and early modern Avenches was literally built from the bones of the ancient city
- In **1710**, scholar **Marquard Wild** was the first to argue convincingly that Aventicum had been the capital of Helvetia
- The founding of **Pro Aventico** (1884–1885) launched systematic excavation, and the site has been under continuous archaeological investigation ever since
- The **Aventicum archaeological park** allows visitors to walk a **3 km trail** (approximately 1.5 hours) through the open-air ruins: amphitheatre, theatre, Cigognier sanctuary, thermal baths, Eastern Gate, and Tornallaz tower — all freely accessible

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The golden emperor in the drain** — A child exploring the Cigognier ruins discovers a glinting object in an ancient pipe; flashback to 275 AD when a temple guardian hid the golden bust from Alemanni raiders, trusting the goddess Aventia to protect it for centuries
2. **The amphitheatre that refused to sleep** — A Roman gladiator's ghost still haunts the arena; a child attending a Rock Oz'Arenes concert must help him understand that the cheering crowd proves his arena was never forgotten
3. **The last stork of the Cigognier** — In 1978, when the stork nest is removed from the ancient column for restoration, one stubborn stork refuses to leave; a child must convince her to nest at the stud farm instead, founding the colony that thrives today
4. **Twenty thousand voices** — A child touches the Tornallaz tower wall and hears whispers; each of the 73 towers along the Roman wall tells one fragment of a story, and the child must walk the full 5.5 km circuit to piece together the tale of Aventicum's last day
5. **The bishop who carried a city** — Bishop Marius, goldsmith and scholar, must decide whether to abandon declining Aventicum for Lausanne in 585 AD; a child travels back to help him pack the most precious things — and learns what makes a city live on
6. **The horse that dreamed of chariots** — A young Franches-Montagnes foal at the Haras national discovers Roman chariot fittings buried beneath its stable; dreaming each night of racing in the amphitheatre, it must learn that its own kind of greatness lies in the modern arena
7. **Vespasian's promise** — Young Vespasian visits his father's grave in Aventicum and vows to make the small Helvetian town a true Roman city; a child witnesses the transformation as walls, temples, and an amphitheatre rise from the Broye plain
8. **The dodecahedron's secret** — The mysterious twelve-sided bronze object in the museum starts glowing at midnight; a child must solve its puzzle — rotating each face to reveal a different scene from Aventicum's 2,000-year history — before dawn breaks
