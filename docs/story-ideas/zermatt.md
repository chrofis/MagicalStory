## 1. Geography — The Mattertal and the Crown of Peaks

- Zermatt lies at the upper end of the **Mattertal** (Matter Valley) at an elevation of **1,620 meters**, at the foot of Switzerland's highest peaks
- The Mattertal is a lateral branch of the grand **Rhone Valley**, running roughly north to south in the canton of **Valais**
- The village is surrounded by an extraordinary concentration of high peaks: **38 summits over 4,000 meters** can be reached from Zermatt — the greatest concentration of four-thousanders anywhere in the Alps
- Among these peaks: the **Matterhorn** (4,478 m), **Dufourspitze** (4,634 m, highest point in Switzerland), **Dom** (4,545 m, highest peak entirely within Swiss borders), **Weisshorn**, **Zinalrothorn**, **Dent Blanche**, **Breithorn**, **Strahlhorn**, **Rimpfischhorn**, and **Alphubel**
- From the viewing platform at the **Matterhorn Glacier Paradise** (3,883 m), visitors can see across 38 four-thousanders, 14 glaciers, and three countries (Switzerland, Italy, France)

## 2. Walser Settlement and Early History

- The earliest human traces in the upper Mattertal date back thousands of years: a **Neolithic stone axe** (3000–5000 BC) found locally is displayed in the Matterhorn Museum
- The area was settled by **Walser** people — Alemannic German-speaking migrants from the upper Rhone Valley who expanded into high Alpine valleys from roughly **1150 to 1450**
- The Walser were specialists in **high-altitude subsistence farming**, making them ideal settlers for remote mountain territory
- Zermatt was first documented around **1280** under the Latin name **"Pratobornum"** (meadow of the springs)
- The German name **"zer Matt"** (at the meadow) appeared in documents in **1495** and **1544**; the current spelling "Zermatt" was standardized in the **19th century**
- Throughout the Middle Ages, Zermatt and all its residents were under the authority of the **Bishop of Sion**
- In **1618**, the community regained its freedom from the bishop's sovereignty
- In **1791**, three communes merged into a single municipality (June 14)
- Zermatt joined the **Swiss Confederation** in **1815** following Napoleon's fall
- More than **30 historic Walser buildings** survive in the area, including barns and granaries up to 500 years old, built of robust **larch wood** in the distinctive **Blockbau** technique (interlocking logs)

## 3. The Matterhorn — Switzerland's Most Iconic Mountain

- Height: **4,478 meters** (14,692 feet), straddling the border between Switzerland (Canton Valais) and Italy (Aosta Valley)
- The Matterhorn's near-perfect **pyramidal shape** — four steep triangular faces separated by sharp ridges — makes it one of the most recognizable mountains on Earth
- The **four faces**: North Face (Nordwand), East Face (Ostwand), South Face (Italian Face), and West Face
- The **four ridges**: Hörnli Ridge (northeast, the standard climbing route), Furggen Ridge (southeast), Lion Ridge (southwest, the Italian route), and Zmutt Ridge (northwest)
- **Geological formation**: The summit is composed of **gneiss**, a metamorphic rock originating from the **African tectonic plate**, resting atop younger European rock — the Matterhorn is literally a piece of Africa on top of Europe
- The distinctive pyramid was carved by **glacial erosion** over millions of years: four cirque glaciers retreated and advanced, sculpting the mountain into its present form
- In local **Walliserdeutsch** dialect, the mountain is called **"ds Hore"** (Zermatt dialect) or **"ds Horu"** (Upper Valais dialect), simply meaning "the peak"
- The French name is **Cervin** (Mont Cervin); the Italian name is **Cervino**

## 4. The First Ascent (1865) — Triumph and Tragedy

- The Matterhorn was the **last great Alpine peak** to be climbed, and its first ascent marked the end of the **Golden Age of Alpinism** (1854–1865)
- For years, English engraver **Edward Whymper** (born 1840, London) and Italian mountaineer **Jean-Antoine Carrel** (born 1829, Valtournenche) competed to be the first to reach the summit
- Between **1861 and 1865**, both men made several attempts together via the southwest ridge (Lion Ridge), but the partnership dissolved into rivalry — Carrel believed an Italian should be first
- In July 1865, Carrel secretly agreed to lead an Italian party up the Italian side, leaving Whymper stranded. Knowing the Italian team would take over a week, Whymper hastily assembled a team to attempt the **Hörnli Ridge** from the Swiss side

### The Team (July 14, 1865)
- **Edward Whymper** (English, 25 years old)
- **Michel Croz** (French guide from Chamonix)
- **Rev. Charles Hudson** (English clergyman and experienced climber)
- **Lord Francis Douglas** (English, 18 years old)
- **Douglas Hadow** (English, inexperienced, invited by Hudson)
- **Peter Taugwalder father** (Swiss guide from Zermatt)
- **Peter Taugwalder son** (Swiss, assistant guide)

### The Ascent and Descent
- The seven climbers reached the summit at approximately **1:40 pm** on July 14, 1865
- From the top, they could see Carrel's Italian party still **200 meters below** on the other side — they shouted and threw rocks to signal their triumph
- On the descent, disaster struck: **Douglas Hadow** slipped and dragged **Michel Croz**, **Charles Hudson**, and **Lord Francis Douglas** down the **North Face**, a fall of approximately **1,200 meters**
- The rope connecting the fallen four to Whymper and the Taugwalders **snapped** — it was later discovered they had mistakenly used the **oldest, weakest rope** in their pack
- Whymper and the two Taugwalders survived; the four others died
- Carrel's Italian team reached the summit **three days later** (July 17) via the Lion Ridge

### The Aftermath
- The Taugwalder guides were accused of **cutting the rope** to save themselves, but an inquiry found **no evidence** and they were acquitted
- The tragedy shocked Victorian society and sparked a national debate in England about the morality of mountaineering
- Whymper never climbed the Matterhorn again; he died on **September 16, 1911**, in Chamonix

## 5. The Birth of Alpine Tourism — Alexander Seiler

- Until the mid-19th century, Zermatt was a remote **pastoral village** of mountain farmers
- In **1839**, the first inn was constructed to accommodate growing visitor interest
- **Alexander Seiler** (born February 21, 1819), a trained soap and candle maker from the neighboring Goms valley, recognized the tourism potential of the Alps early on
- In **1855**, Seiler purchased a wooden chalet and named it **Hotel Monte Rosa**, converting it into a 35-bed hotel
- The Hotel Monte Rosa became the headquarters of pioneering mountaineers, including **Edward Whymper** himself
- Seiler expanded relentlessly: he rented the **Riffelberg** mountain guest house (then the highest-altitude guesthouse in Europe), bought the rival **Hotel Mont Cervin** in **1867**, and by **1890** controlled nearly every hotel in Zermatt — a total of **1,000 beds and 600 jobs**
- In **1891**, the railway line to **Visp** was inaugurated, and over **30,000 passengers** reached Zermatt that first summer — transforming the village forever

## 6. The Gornergrat Railway — Europe's Highest Open-Air Cog Railway

- Opened on **August 20, 1898**, making it the **first electric cogwheel railway in Switzerland** and the oldest functional electric rack railway in the world
- Climbs from Zermatt (1,620 m) to the Gornergrat summit station at **3,089 meters** in **33 minutes**
- The line is **9,339 meters** long, traversing an altitude difference of **1,469 meters**
- Built to **metre gauge** using the **Abt rack system**, powered by a rare **three-phase electric** system (two overhead conductors, track as the third)
- Construction took just **two years** (1896–1898), employing over **1,000 workers** simultaneously, most of them Italian
- Originally operated in summer only; year-round service extended to the summit in **1941**
- **6 stations** along the route, with the Gornergrat summit offering a panorama of **29 four-thousanders** including the Matterhorn, Monte Rosa, and the Gorner Glacier
- The Gornergrat was the highest railway in Europe until the opening of the **Jungfrau Railway** in **1912**; it remains the highest **open-air** cog railway on the continent

## 7. Monte Rosa and the Gorner Glacier

### Dufourspitze — Switzerland's Highest Point
- The **Dufourspitze** (4,634 m) is the highest peak of the Monte Rosa massif — and the **highest point in Switzerland**
- It is also the **second-highest mountain in the Alps** (after Mont Blanc) and the highest in the Pennine Alps
- The peak lies wholly on Swiss territory, though the Monte Rosa massif straddles the Swiss-Italian border
- First ascent: **August 1, 1855** (Swiss National Day), by a party of eight led by guides **Matthäus and Johannes Zumtaugwald**, including Charles Hudson (who would die on the Matterhorn ten years later)
- Named after General **Guillaume-Henri Dufour**, founder of the Swiss Federal Topographic Bureau

### Gorner Glacier
- The **Gorner Glacier** (Gornergletscher) is the **second-largest glacial system in the Alps** after the Aletsch Glacier, covering approximately **53 km²** (2007 measurement)
- Length: approximately **12.4 km** (2014), width 1 to 1.5 km
- Located on the west side of the Monte Rosa massif, visible from the Gornergrat
- The glacier is the source of the **Gornera** river, which flows through Zermatt itself
- A notable feature is the **Gornersee**, an ice-marginal lake that fills each year and drains in summer, sometimes as a dramatic glacial outburst flood
- Like almost all Alpine glaciers, the Gorner Glacier is **retreating rapidly** — losing approximately 30 meters per year, with a record loss of 290 meters in 2008

## 8. The Car-Free Village

- Zermatt is one of Switzerland's few completely **car-free** resort villages — no combustion-engine vehicles are permitted within the village limits
- Citizens voted to ban most cars in **1966**
- In the **early 1980s**, the village converted fully to electric vehicles, with strict regulations: vehicles may be no longer than **4 meters**, no wider than **1.4 meters**, no higher than **2 meters**, and limited to **20 km/h**
- The **Imboden family**, who operated a horse-drawn taxi company since the 1950s, pioneered the electric transition, founding the company **STIMBO** (from the initials of Stefan, Imboden, and Bruno) to manufacture custom electric vehicles adapted to mountain conditions
- STIMBO has produced approximately **350–400 vehicles** since 1996 at a rate of about 15 per year, priced between **CHF 60,000 and 140,000**
- Today, approximately **500 electric vehicles** serve the entire village — private ownership is prohibited; only businesses demonstrating necessity may operate them
- Visitors arrive by train: the **Matterhorn Gotthard Bahn** shuttle runs from the car park at **Täsch** (5 km north) to Zermatt in 12 minutes

## 9. The Glacier Express — The Slowest Express Train in the World

- Connects **Zermatt** to **St. Moritz** across **291 km** in approximately **8 hours**, earning its nickname as the "slowest express train in the world" (average speed: 39 km/h)
- The first Glacier Express departed on **June 25, 1930**; the name honors the **Rhone Glacier** near Gletsch on the Furka Pass
- The route crosses **91 tunnels** and **291 bridges**, including the spectacular **Landwasser Viaduct** (65 m high, 142 m long) — part of the UNESCO World Heritage **Albula/Bernina railway**
- Highest point: the **Oberalp Pass** at **2,033 meters**
- Until **1982**, the train operated only in summer because the Furka and Oberalp passes were snowed over; the opening of the **Furka Base Tunnel** (15.4 km) enabled year-round service
- Near Kalpetran, just outside Zermatt, the train passes through the **Kipfen Gorge** — one of the most scenic sections, with the train running alongside the wild River Vispa

## 10. The Matterhorn Museum — Zermatlantis

- Opened in **2006** on Zermatt's Kirchplatz, the museum is built almost entirely **underground** — the name "Zermatlantis" refers to the "sunken world" of old Zermatt
- Designed by the firm **Steiner Sarnen** (who also designed the Glasi Hergiswil and Hamburg Port Museum)
- The subterranean space recreates **original Walser houses and interiors**, tracing Zermatt's transformation from a mountain farming village to a world-famous resort
- The museum's most powerful artifact: the **original broken rope** from the first ascent of July 14, 1865, along with personal effects of the climbers who perished
- A **multivision room** screens excerpts from Luis Trenker's **1937/38 film** "Der Berg ruft" ("The Mountain Calls"), dramatizing the first ascent and the fatal fall
- An illuminated **relief model** of the Matterhorn shows all climbing routes to the summit
- Approximately **40,000 visitors** per year

## 11. The Toblerone Mountain — Commercial Fame

- The Matterhorn became one of the world's most commercially recognized mountains through the **Toblerone** chocolate bar
- **Theodor Tobler** and his cousin **Emil Baumann** created Toblerone in **Bern** in **1908** — the name combines "Tobler" with "torrone" (Italian for nougat)
- The chocolate's distinctive **triangular prism shape** became associated with the Matterhorn, though Tobler reportedly denied the mountain was the direct inspiration
- The Matterhorn image was added to the packaging in **1970**; the logo also contained a **hidden bear** within the mountain silhouette, honoring Bern's cantonal symbol
- In **March 2023**, Toblerone was forced to **remove the Matterhorn** from its packaging after owner Mondelez International moved part of production to **Slovakia** — violating Switzerland's **"Swissness Act"** (2017), which requires products using Swiss national symbols to be manufactured in Switzerland
- The Matterhorn was replaced with a generic mountain design; the packaging now reads "Established in Switzerland in 1908" instead of "Swiss chocolate"

## 12. Walliserdeutsch — The Local Dialect

- Zermatt's primary language is **Walliserdeutsch** (Walser German), one of the oldest Alemannic dialects, preserved by centuries of geographic isolation in the Valais valleys
- The dialect is so distinctive that it sometimes requires **subtitles on Swiss television** — even other Swiss German speakers struggle to understand it
- Key local terms: the Matterhorn is **"ds Hore"** in Zermatt or **"ds Horu"** in Upper Valais; these simply mean "the peak"
- Approximately **10,000 people** across the Alps still speak Walser dialect variants today
- The dialect features characteristic sound shifts, including shifting Germanic "s" to "sch" sounds and unique vowel patterns
- The Walser dialect is rich in proverbs and expressions reflecting their values of mountain resilience and community solidarity

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The race to the top** — Two rival mountain goats (one from the Swiss side, one from the Italian side) race up the Matterhorn, mirroring the Whymper-Carrel rivalry; they learn the mountain is big enough for both
2. **The rope that remembers** — A child visits the Matterhorn Museum and touches the broken rope; it whispers the story of the seven climbers, the summit joy, and the terrible fall
3. **The silent village** — A child arrives in Zermatt and notices something strange: no car noise. The village's electric taxis tell the story of how they replaced horses and why clean air matters
4. **The little cog train that climbed** — The Gornergrat Railway engine is nervous about its first trip to the top in 1898; a child conductor encourages it past the gorge, through the snow, to the 3,089-meter summit and its breathtaking panorama
5. **The glacier's memory** — The Gorner Glacier tells a child about the centuries it has seen — growing during the Little Ice Age, retreating now — and asks the child to remember what it looked like
6. **The peak that came from Africa** — A child learns the Matterhorn's summit rock traveled from Africa on a tectonic plate; the mountain tells its 100-million-year journey in a single magical night
7. **Alexander Seiler and the mountain guests** — A boy from the Goms valley sees strangers arriving in Zermatt with ropes and ice axes; he opens a small inn and discovers that sharing his mountains with the world changes everything
8. **The Horu speaks** — A child who speaks only standard German arrives in Zermatt and cannot understand anyone; the Matterhorn itself teaches her the old Walser words, one peak at a time
