## 1. Geography — The Saanenland Valley

- Gstaad lies at **1,050 meters** above sea level in the **Saanenland**, the westernmost valley of the **Bernese Oberland**, in the Canton of Bern
- Gstaad is not its own municipality — it belongs to the **municipality of Saanen** (population ~7,000), which also includes the villages of **Schönried, Saanenmöser, Turbach, Abländschen, and Lauenen**
- The Saanenland sits at a cultural and linguistic crossroads: it borders the **Canton of Vaud** (French-speaking) to the west, **Canton of Fribourg** to the north, **Canton of Valais** to the south, and the **Simmental** to the east
- The **Saane river** (which becomes **La Sarine** when it crosses into French-speaking territory) flows through the valley — a physical manifestation of Switzerland's language border
- The town core developed at the **fork in the trails** leading over alpine passes into the Valais and Vaud — making it a natural trading crossroads since medieval times
- Today the **GoldenPass Express** panoramic train runs through Gstaad on the Montreux–Interlaken route, connecting Lake Geneva to the Bernese Oberland via the historic **Montreux-Oberland Bernois (MOB)** railway line

## 2. Medieval History — From Gruyère to Bern

- During the Middle Ages, the Saanenland belonged to the **county of Gruyère**, itself a vassal of the **House of Savoy** from **1244** onward
- The earliest documented reference to the Saanen region dates to **1228** (in connection with the church of St. Mauritius)
- Gstaad's medieval role was as a **transport and trading hub**: by the 13th–14th centuries, it had an inn, a warehouse for trade goods, and oxen to help pull wagons over the alpine passes
- A small **Chapel of St. Nicholas** was built in Gstaad in **1402**, indicating the settlement's growing importance
- In **1555**, the last **Count of Gruyère** went bankrupt, and Bern acquired the entire Saanenland and the Obersimmental. Bern introduced the **Protestant Reformation** and installed Bernese bailiffs (Landvögte), though local self-governance in guilds and parishes was largely retained
- This confessional division persists in the region today: Protestant Saanen (Bernese) borders Catholic Fribourg territory

## 3. The Great Transformation — From Farming Village to Jet-Set Resort

- For centuries, the Saanenland was a **pastoral farming community**: cattle herding, seasonal alpine farming (Alpwirtschaft), forestry, and providing warehousing and draft oxen for wagon trains crossing the passes
- The **great fire of 1898** destroyed eight buildings in Gstaad's center, marking a turning point — the village was rebuilt with tourism in mind
- **1901–1905**: The **Montreux-Oberland Bernois (MOB) railway** was opened in stages; the line reached Gstaad on **20 December 1904** and Zweisimmen on **6 July 1905**. It was **Switzerland's first fully electric railway line** — the connection to the outside world that made tourism possible
- **1906**: The first **tourist associations** were founded in Gstaad and Saanen
- **1907/08**: Gstaad's **first winter sports season** commenced
- **1908**: Switzerland's first **purpose-built ski house** was constructed at Saanenmöser
- The **1920s** brought a golden era: the Kurpark, Kursaal, golf course, and tennis courts were built
- **1934**: The first **"funi"** (cable-pulled sled lift) opened on the Wispile — an innovative early ski lift system
- **1945–1946**: Switzerland's **first chair lift** was built in the Saanenland; visitors first rode it to the Wasserngrat in April 1946
- **1954**: The region's first **gondola lift** was installed on the Eggli
- In the **1960s**, *Time* magazine named Gstaad **"The Place"** — cementing its reputation as the world's most glamorous alpine village

## 4. The Gstaad Palace — Legendary Luxury Hotel (1913)

- In **1905**, **Robert Steffen** — a village schoolteacher with extraordinary vision — began purchasing plots of land on the hill above Gstaad for a grand hotel
- On **15 December 1911**, the public holding company **"Royal-Hôtel & Winter Palace Gstaad"** was founded in Lausanne
- Construction began in April 1912, designed by architects **Adrien van Dorsser** and **Charles-François Bonjour**, with a budget of **2.5 million Swiss francs** — an enormous sum for the time
- The Palace opened on **8 December 1913** with **250 beds in 165 rooms**, of which 70 had private bathrooms — along with central heating and an in-house telegrapher
- **1915**: The hotel's tennis courts hosted the first international tennis tournament, which evolved into the **Swiss Open Gstaad**
- **1938**: **Ernst and Silvia Scherz-Bezzola** assumed management of the hotel
- **1947**: **Ernst Scherz** acquired full ownership — the beginning of the Scherz family dynasty
- The Palace is today run by the **third generation**, under **Andrea Scherz** (General Manager since 2001), making it one of Europe's last **family-run grand hotels**
- In **1964**, the Palace ballroom hosted performances by **Marlene Dietrich** and **Louis Armstrong**
- **1971**: The legendary **GreenGo nightclub** opened in the Palace's basement — it became the after-dark epicenter of Gstaad's social scene

## 5. Celebrity Connections — The Jet-Set Village

- **Elizabeth Taylor** bought **Chalet Ariel** in **1962** with her fourth husband Eddie Fisher. She later spent winters there with **Richard Burton**, who declared: *"Gstaad is the most beautiful place in the world... we only feel at home in Gstaad"* (1972). Taylor was a regular at the Palace Hotel and at the **Hotel Olden**, where she appreciated that "film stars and farmers used to sit at the same table"
- **Julie Andrews** first came to Gstaad in **1968** with her husband, director **Blake Edwards**. They bought a chalet called **"Fleur de Lys"** in 1971. Edwards wrote the screenplay for *Victor/Victoria* (1982) in the chalet and shot *The Return of the Pink Panther* (1975) in Gstaad. Andrews described the village as **"the last paradise in a crazy world."** In **2014**, she was made an **honorary citizen of Saanen** — only the tenth person ever to receive this honor. She donated a bronze sculpture by Edwards, **"The Sitting Duck"**, to the village
- **Roger Moore** was a regular visitor; he once quipped: *"The locals are more interested in my car than they are in me"* (1983)
- **David Niven** spent winters in nearby Château-d'Oex and regularly skied the slopes around Gstaad
- **Yehudi Menuhin** first came in **1954** and owned the chalet **"Chankly Bore"** from 1960; he received **honorary citizenship from Saanen** in **1970**
- **Michael Jackson** reportedly stayed at Elizabeth Taylor's Chalet Ariel and once asked if he could buy the Palace Hotel
- **Roman Polanski** lived in a chalet called **"Milky Way"**
- Other famous residents or regular visitors: **Princess Grace of Monaco**, **Jackie Kennedy**, **Brigitte Bardot**, **Peter Ustinov**, **Peter Sellers**

## 6. The Menuhin Festival — Music in the Mountains (1957)

- In **1957**, the legendary violinist **Yehudi Menuhin** founded a summer music festival in Gstaad, at the suggestion of **Paul Valentin**, the local tourism director (Kurdirektor)
- Menuhin found his ideal concert venue in the **15th-century Mauritius Church (Mauritiuskirche)** in neighboring Saanen — its intimate acoustics and medieval atmosphere were perfectly suited to chamber music
- The first concerts were organized together with Menuhin's close friends: pianist and composer **Benjamin Britten**, tenor **Peter Pears**, and cellist **Maurice Gendron**
- Menuhin said: *"I feel at home in Gstaad. I started a small music festival there in the wonderful little church in Saanen"*
- The Menuhin family settled permanently in Gstaad, and in **1970**, Menuhin received **honorary citizenship** from the commune of Saanen
- In **1977**, the **International Menuhin Music Academy (IMMA)** relocated to Gstaad under the direction of **Alberto Lysy**
- Today the festival has grown to over **50 concerts across seven weeks** each summer, performed in the churches of Saanen, Zweisimmen, Lauenen, Gsteig, and Rougemont, as well as in a large Festival Tent in Gstaad
- The festival is built on Menuhin's humanistic legacy — his vision of **music as a unifying force** continues to shape its programming

## 7. The Saanen Church — Romanesque Jewel of the Oberland

- The **Church of St. Mauritius (Mauritiuskirche)** in Saanen is the oldest building in the region, first documented in **1228**, though certainly older — likely founded in the **10th or 11th century** by the Counts of Gruyère
- Originally built in the **Romanesque style**, the church was significantly expanded in **1444–1447** and received Late Gothic additions
- The interior contains remarkable **15th-century frescoes** (painted around **1470**) depicting scenes from the **Old Testament**, the **life of Mary**, and the **legend of St. Mauritius and the Theban Legion** — a Roman legion from Upper Egypt whose soldiers refused to persecute Christians and were martyred
- On the north side of the nave, a fresco of the **Last Judgment** survives
- The frescoes were covered over during the Reformation and only **rediscovered and restored in the 20th century**
- The church serves as the principal venue for the **Menuhin Festival**, its medieval acoustics lending an incomparable atmosphere to chamber music performances
- Both the church and the nearby **Alte Kastlanei** (old bailiff's house) are listed as **Swiss heritage sites of national significance**

## 8. Traditional Chalet Architecture — Building Codes That Preserve a Village

- Since the **1950s**, Gstaad and the Saanenland have enforced **strict building regulations** requiring all new construction to follow the traditional **wooden chalet style** — one of the strictest architectural codes in Switzerland
- Only **timber construction with wooden panelling** in the regional style is permitted; modern glass-and-steel designs are banned
- Traditional Bernese Oberland chalets are characterized by: **widely projecting roofs** (protecting against heavy snowfall), **heavy timber planks** (3–6 inches thick), **carved wooden balconies** with ornamental railings, upper stories projecting over lower ones on decorative brackets, and **flower-box-laden facades**
- These regulations are credited with preserving Gstaad's village character despite intense development pressure from international wealth — preventing the kind of architectural disruption seen in other alpine resorts
- The **car-free pedestrian promenade** in the village center (a 7-minute walk end to end) reinforces this atmosphere: cobbled streets lined with chalet-style boutiques, galleries, and cafes, all a few steps from the train station

## 9. The Saanen Goat — A Global Export from the Valley

- The **Saanen goat** takes its name directly from the **Saanental (Saanen Valley)** — bred here for several hundred years through selective dairy farming
- It is a **pure white** goat with short hair, one of the **largest dairy goat breeds** in the world
- The Saanen is recognized as the **most widely distributed dairy goat breed worldwide**, found in over **80 countries** — often called the **"Holstein of the dairy goat world"** for its exceptional milk production
- Average milk yield: **838 kg per lactation** (264 days) — the highest of any Swiss goat breed
- The breed was first exported internationally in the **19th century** and has since given rise to many local sub-breeds through crossbreeding
- The Saanen goat is valued for its **abundant milk production, hardiness, and calm temperament**

## 10. Glacier 3000 and the Peak Walk

- **Glacier 3000** is a mountain excursion destination accessible from the Col du Pillon pass, about 30 minutes from Gstaad, rising to **2,971 meters** above sea level
- The **Peak Walk by Tissot**, opened in **October 2014**, is a **107-meter-long, 80-centimeter-wide suspension bridge** connecting two mountain summits: the **View Point** peak and the main summit **Scex Rouge** (5 meters higher)
- It is the **only suspension bridge in the world connecting two mountain peaks**
- The bridge was built by the firm **Seiler AG** of Bönigen (steel and metal constructions)
- From the bridge, visitors can see **Mont Blanc, the Matterhorn, the Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau** — an unparalleled Alpine panorama
- Access to the bridge is **free of charge** for Glacier 3000 visitors and is open year-round, weather permitting

## 11. The Swiss Open — The Wimbledon of the Alps (1915)

- The first international tennis tournament in Gstaad was held in **1915** on the clay courts of the **Gstaad Palace Hotel** — making it the **eighth-oldest tennis tournament in the world**
- Originally called the **Swiss International Championships**, the tournament ran from 1897 to 1914 as a grass court event in other locations before moving to Gstaad
- **Roy Emerson**, the Australian Grand Slam champion, holds the record with **five titles** at Gstaad. He recalled: *"I could barely concentrate when I played here for the first time in the 1950s... the mountains, the train that ran around the court and whistled"*
- In **1973**, Emerson and **Ernst Andrea Scherz** (Palace Hotel) created the famous **Roy Emerson Tennis Weeks** — a popular program combining coaching with Palace luxury
- In **1991**, the **Roy Emerson Arena** was built in the village center, moving the tournament from the Palace courts to a dedicated venue
- Notable winners: **Rod Laver** (1962, during his 22-title season), **Ilie Nastase** (1973), **Heinz Günthardt** (1980, first Swiss winner since WWII), **Roger Federer** (2004), **Casper Ruud** (2021, 2022)
- At 1,050 meters, it is **Europe's highest ATP tournament** — earning it the nickname **"Wimbledon of the Alps"**
- The tournament celebrated its **100th edition** in 2015

## 12. The Alphorn and Mountain Traditions

- The **alphorn** (Alphorn) is one of Switzerland's most iconic instruments, deeply rooted in alpine cattle-herding culture. It was used to **call cows from pastures** to the barn at milking time and to **signal across valleys** between high pastures and valley-floor communities
- Alphorn sounds can carry for **several kilometers** across mountain terrain
- From the **17th to 19th centuries**, the alphorn was an essential tool of alpine life; as cheese production shifted from high pastures to village dairies after **1800**, its practical use declined — but it survived as a beloved cultural symbol and tourist attraction
- The Saanenland maintains strong traditions of **alpine transhumance** (seasonal movement of livestock to high pastures), the annual **Alpabzug** (ceremonial cattle descent in autumn with flower-decorated cows), and traditional **yodeling** (Naturjodel)
- The **Saanen cow** and the **Simmental cattle** breed are historically significant in the region's dairy farming heritage
- Traditional crafts like **Scherenschnitt** (paper-cutting art) and **decorative woodcarving** remain practiced in the valley

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The schoolteacher who dreamed a palace** — Robert Steffen, a village teacher in 1905, imagines a grand hotel on the hill; a child helps him convince skeptical farmers that visitors will come if the railway does
2. **The violin that echoed in the church** — A child discovers an old violin in the Mauritius Church attic; playing it summons the ghost of Yehudi Menuhin, who guides the child through the church's 800-year history via music
3. **The goat who conquered the world** — A proud Saanen goat from the valley learns that her relatives live in 80 countries; she sets off on an imaginary journey across the world, discovering how far the name "Saanen" has traveled
4. **The bridge between two peaks** — A child afraid of heights must cross the Peak Walk suspension bridge at Glacier 3000; at the midpoint, suspended between the Matterhorn and Mont Blanc, they discover courage and a spectacular secret
5. **The chalet that wouldn't change** — In a village where strict building codes keep every house in traditional wooden style, a rebellious chalet dreams of being made of glass and steel — until it discovers why its wooden walls and carved balconies are worth preserving
6. **The train that opened the mountains** — It is December 1904 and the first MOB electric train arrives in Gstaad; a farm child who has never left the valley takes the train to Montreux and sees Lake Geneva for the first time
7. **Stars and cowbells** — A child in 1960s Gstaad is baffled when famous film stars start appearing in the village; they must help Elizabeth Taylor find her lost cat in a comedy of mistaken identities between celebrities and farmers at the Hotel Olden
8. **The frescoes that woke up** — During a Menuhin Festival concert in the Saanen church, the 15th-century frescoes of St. Mauritius and the Theban Legion come alive, and a child must help the painted soldiers find their way back to their wall before the music ends
