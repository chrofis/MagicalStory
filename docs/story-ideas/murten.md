## 1. Founding — The Zähringen and the Lakeside Fortress

- The area around Murten has been settled for at least **6,000 years** — Neolithic and Bronze Age lake-dwellings (Pfahlbauten/cités lacustres) have been excavated on the shores of Lake Murten
- The town was founded by **Duke Berchtold IV of Zähringen** in the **1170s**, part of the same dynasty that founded **Bern (1191)**, **Fribourg (1157)**, and **Thun** — all sharing the characteristic Zähringen layout of a single broad main street with arcades
- Murten was first mentioned as a city (**civitas**) in **1238**
- The site was chosen on a **gentle hill (453 m above sea level)** on the southern shore of Lake Murten, at the edge of the **Grosses Moos** (Great Marsh) — a strategic position controlling traffic between the Swiss Plateau and the lake region
- In **1255**, Murten fell under the protection of **Count Peter II of Savoy**, who ordered the construction of the **castle** (Schloss Murten / Château de Morat) — the tall square **donjon** (keep) that still dominates the town skyline dates from this period
- The city changed hands repeatedly: the **Habsburgs** seized it as a royal estate; **Amadeus V of Savoy** bought it back in **1291**, lost it, then repurchased it in **1310 for 4,000 marks of silver**
- After the Battle of Murten in 1476, the town became a **gemeine Herrschaft** (joint territory) of **Bern and Fribourg from 1484**, who alternated the bailiff every five years for over **300 years**

## 2. Geography — Lake Murten and the Three Lakes Region

- **Lake Murten** (Murtensee / Lac de Morat) is the **smallest of the three lakes** in the **Drei-Seen-Land / Pays des Trois-Lacs** (Three Lakes Region), alongside Lake Neuchâtel and Lake Biel/Bienne
- Dimensions: **8.2 km long**, up to **2.8 km wide**, maximum depth **45 m**, surface area **22.8 km²**, volume approximately **0.55 km³**, elevation **429 m**
- Thanks to its shallow depth, the lake **warms quickly** and is one of the most popular swimming lakes in western Switzerland from spring through autumn
- Since the **Jura Water Correction** (Juragewässerkorrektion, 1868-1891), the lake drains through the **Broye Canal** (Canal de la Broye) into Lake Neuchâtel, which connects via the Thielle Canal to Lake Biel — creating a linked waterway system
- The surrounding landscape is the gentle, fertile **Swiss Mittelland** — rolling hills, vineyards on the slopes of **Mont Vully** across the lake, and the reclaimed marshland of the Grosses Moos, one of Switzerland's most important agricultural regions
- The **Tour du Lac de Morat** cycling route encircles the lake in approximately **28 km**

## 3. The Medieval Town Walls — Switzerland's Most Walkable Ramparts

- Murten's **13th-century ramparts** are among the **best-preserved medieval fortifications in Switzerland** and uniquely remain **fully walkable** by the public — the only such ring wall open for walking in the country
- City walls were erected in phases starting from **12th-century stone foundations**, with major expansion ordered by **King Conrad IV around 1250**
- The circuit originally included **12 fortress towers** spaced for overlapping fields of fire, and three main gates:
  - **Berntor** (Bern Gate / Porte de Berne): first mentioned in **1239**, destroyed and rebuilt multiple times, the current structure dates to the **second half of the 18th century** — the main eastern entrance to the old town
  - **Fribourgtor** (Fribourg Gate): facing south toward Fribourg
  - **Lakentor**: the third historical gate
- The **walkable rampart path** runs approximately **2 km** around the upper old town, offering panoramic views over the rooftops, Lake Murten, and the Jura foothills
- **No entrance fee** — the ramparts are open daily from sunrise to sunset
- Information panels along the wall explain the history of the fortifications and the Battle of Murten

## 4. The Battle of Murten (22 June 1476) — Turning Point of the Burgundian Wars

- The Battle of Murten was fought on **22 June 1476** between **Charles the Bold, Duke of Burgundy** and the **Swiss Confederate army** — it was one of the **most decisive battles in Swiss history**
- **Context**: The Burgundian Wars (1474-1477) erupted when Charles the Bold attempted to create a new kingdom stretching from the Low Countries to Italy. After his defeat at **Grandson** (March 1476), where the Swiss captured his legendary treasure, Charles marched on Murten to avenge his humiliation
- Charles arrived before Murten on **9 June 1476** with approximately **21,000-25,000 men** and laid siege to the town
- The garrison was commanded by **Adrian von Bubenberg** (c. 1424-1479), a Bernese knight, former Schultheiss (mayor) of Bern, and Knight of the Holy Sepulchre, who had been appointed commander in **April 1476** and brought **1,500 additional men** to reinforce the town
- Von Bubenberg, who had personally witnessed Burgundian military brutality during diplomatic missions, **strengthened the castle, walls, and ramparts** and stockpiled enormous supplies of **gunpowder, cannons, and weapons** before the siege began — his preparations proved critical
- The garrison **held out for 13 days** against bombardment from massive Burgundian cannons (which arrived on 17 June), repelling multiple assaults
- On **22 June** — the **Feast of the Ten Thousand Martyrs** — the Swiss relief army of approximately **24,000 men** launched a surprise attack on the Burgundian position at the **Grünhag** (a palisade/hedge fortification). Charles had not expected the Swiss to fight on a religious feast day
- The Burgundian army was caught unprepared, unable to don armor or man their artillery in time. The Swiss infantry destroyed the Burgundian force in a devastating rout
- **Approximately 10,000-12,000 Burgundians** were killed — cut down or **drowned in Lake Murten** trying to flee. Swiss casualties were light: only about **410 men**
- The victory, along with Charles the Bold's subsequent death at the **Battle of Nancy** (January 1477), ended the Burgundian threat and cemented the Swiss Confederation's reputation as a formidable military power in Europe

## 5. The Ossuary and the Memory of 1476

- After the battle, the fields west of Murten were strewn with approximately **10,000 fallen Burgundians** — the landowners buried them in pits on the spot
- In **1485**, the prior of the Katherine Chapel in Murten, **Peter von Erlach**, initiated the construction of an **ossuary** (Beinhaus zu Murten) at **Merlach**, west of the town, to house the exhumed bones and skulls — the structure was reportedly **filled to the ceiling**
- In **1506**, commemorative inscriptions were added to the exterior
- The ossuary stood for over **300 years** as a monument to Swiss victory — until **1798**, when soldiers of the **French 75th demi-brigade** (Burgundian-origin troops) demolished it, attempting first to burn the bones, then to blow up the building, seeking to erase the memory of their ancestors' defeat
- Today the **Museum Murten / Musée de Morat** (housed in the old city mill at Ryf 4) preserves the memory with a permanent exhibition spanning 6,000 years of local history, including a **multimedia show** dramatizing the Battle of Murten
- A monumental **panorama painting** of the Battle of Murten was created by **Louis Braun** and his team in **1893** — the museum is currently digitizing and conserving this work

## 6. The Murtenlauf — Switzerland's Oldest Road Race

- The **Murtenlauf** (Course Morat-Fribourg) commemorates the legend that after the 1476 victory, a **messenger ran from Murten to Fribourg** carrying a **linden branch** (Lindenast/branche de tilleul) to announce the Swiss triumph — and **collapsed dead from exhaustion** upon arrival
- According to tradition, onlookers planted the linden twig, which grew into the famous **Tilleul de Morat** (Linde von Murten) on the Place de l'Hôtel-de-Ville in Fribourg — the tree stood for over **500 years** until a drunk driver destroyed it in **1983**; a cutting was replanted in **1984**
- The first official race was held on **25 June 1933**, with just **14 participants** — winner Basel runner **Alexandre Zosso** finished in **1:01:00**
- The race is the **oldest road race in Switzerland** still held today, taking place on the **first Sunday of October**
- Distance: **17.17 km** (historically: 16.4 km from 1933-1976, 17.15 km from 1977-1995, 17.17 km from 1996 onward)
- The route runs from **Murten Hauptgasse (453 m)** to **Fribourg Place Georges Python (615 m)**, with a total elevation gain of **388 m**
- Today the race attracts around **10,000 participants** annually
- Records: men's record **50:28** by Abraham Kipyatich (2014); women's record **57:50.6** by Helen Bekele Tola (2019)

## 7. The Altstadt — Arcades, Churches, and the Bilingual Character

- Murten's **Altstadt** (Vieille Ville / old town) is a remarkably intact example of a **Zähringen-plan town**: a single broad main street — the **Hauptgasse** (Grande-Rue / Rue Principale) — lined with **arcaded walkways** (Lauben/arcades) sheltering shops and cafes, flanked by baroque townhouses with colorful facades
- Key landmarks:
  - **Deutsche Kirche** (German Church / Église allemande): a chapel dedicated to the Virgin Mary existed here by **1399**; the town adopted the **Reformation in 1530** under the influence of **Guillaume Farel** and Bernese pressure, transforming it into an evangelical-reformed church. The choir tower was rebuilt in **1681-1683** and the nave in **1710-1712** in the **Baroque style**, featuring a large stucco ceiling medallion with the coats of arms of Murten, Bern, and Fribourg
  - **Französische Kirche** (French Church / Église française): built in the **late-Gothic style in 1481** to replace an earlier chapel of St. Catherine; a memorial plaque above the entrance commemorates Guillaume Farel's role in the Reformation (1530)
  - In **1762**, the Marienkirche became the parish church for **German-speaking** inhabitants, and the Church of St. Catherine became the parish church for **French-speaking** inhabitants — a bilingual arrangement that persists to this day
  - **Schloss Murten** (Château de Morat): built from **1255** under Peter of Savoy, with the tall square donjon used by Adrian von Bubenberg as his watchtower during the 1476 siege; later served as residence for the alternating Bernese and Fribourg bailiffs, then as hospital, prison, and barracks — today it houses the **cantonal prefecture**
- The town's **bilingual character** is fundamental: Murten sits directly on the **Röstigraben** — the cultural and linguistic boundary between German-speaking and French-speaking Switzerland
- Population: approximately **9,500 inhabitants** (2024); roughly **83% German-speaking** and **15% French-speaking** — a German-speaking majority in the predominantly French-speaking Canton of Fribourg
- Murten was named **"Swiss Village of the Year 2024"** by the readers of Schweizer Illustrierte, L'illustré, and La Domenica, and recognized by the **UNWTO** (World Tourism Organization) as one of the **32 Best Tourism Villages** in the world

## 8. Aventicum — The Roman Capital Next Door

- Just **7 km south** of Murten lies **Avenches** (Aventicum), the **capital of Roman Helvetia** — the largest and most important Roman city in Switzerland
- Aventicum was probably founded in the **early 1st century AD** as the administrative capital of the conquered **Helvetii** territory
- Under **Emperor Vespasian** — who reportedly grew up in the area — Aventicum was elevated to a **colonia** in **72 AD**, triggering its golden age
- At its peak: approximately **20,000 inhabitants**, a city wall of **5.5-5.6 km** with **73 towers**, a forum, thermal baths, temples, and a theatre
- The **amphitheatre**, dating from the early 2nd century AD, seated up to **16,000 spectators** — today it hosts the annual **Avenches Opera Festival** and rock concerts
- The **Cigognier sanctuary** (a large Gallo-Roman temple), the **eastern gate**, and wall towers are among the best-preserved Roman structures north of the Alps
- **Bishop Marius of Aventicum** (c. 530-594) was the last prominent figure of the ancient city; he chronicled events of his era and is an important source for early medieval Swiss history
- The proximity of Roman Aventicum to medieval Murten creates a rare historical layering — 2,000 years of civilization within a short walk

## 9. The Murten Light Festival (Murten Licht-Festival / Festival des Lumières de Morat)

- The **Murten Licht-Festival** was first held in **January 2016**, launched by Murten Tourismus to bring winter visitors to the medieval town
- The festival runs for **12 evenings** each January, transforming the old town into a spectacular open-air gallery of light art
- Approximately **20 arteplages** (light installations) by national and international artists are distributed throughout the old town, on building facades, streets, towers, and the lakeshore
- Key installations include: **projections on the school building** near the Berntor, a **light and sound spectacle inside the Deutsche Kirche**, illuminated museum installations, and shows along the Hauptgasse
- In **2026**, the festival celebrated its **10th anniversary** (21 January - 1 February 2026) with expanded programming including "Le Grand Bleu" in an immersive version 2.0
- Tickets: daily ticket **CHF 8** (advance) / **CHF 12** (on-site); children under 16 **free**; Festival Pass **CHF 20**
- The festival has become a major regional draw, reachable in **30 minutes by train** from Bern, Fribourg, or Neuchâtel

## 10. The Nidelkuchen — Murten's Signature Pastry

- The **Nidelkuchen** (cream cake) is Murten's signature local specialty — "Nidel" means cream in Swiss German
- Created in **1931** by **Ernst Aebersold**, founder of the Aebersold bakery in Murten, as a lighter, creamier version of the traditional **Gâteau du Vully** from the nearby wine-growing village
- The distinctive technique: at least **five thin coats of cream** are applied to a light raised-pastry base — the first three are lightly acidulated and sweetened, added **during baking** to form a fine caramel layer; the final two coats of **Gruyère double cream** (crème double de la Gruyère) are added afterward
- The recipe has been passed down through **three generations** at the Aebersold bakery, which remains the most famous producer
- Available in sweet and savory versions (with bacon or tomatoes) at bakeries throughout Murten

## 11. The Burgundian Wars — Broader Historical Context

- The **Burgundian Wars (1474-1477)** pitted the expanding Duchy of Burgundy under **Charles the Bold** against the **Old Swiss Confederacy** and its allies (including Lorraine and the Habsburg emperor)
- Charles sought to forge a continuous kingdom from the Low Countries through Burgundy to the Alps — the Swiss Confederation stood in his path
- The three decisive battles:
  1. **Battle of Grandson** (2 March 1476): the Swiss routed Charles's army and captured his legendary **treasure, tapestries, and artillery** — but failed to pursue the fleeing Burgundians
  2. **Battle of Murten** (22 June 1476): the devastating defeat that destroyed Charles's military power (see Section 4)
  3. **Battle of Nancy** (5 January 1477): Charles the Bold was **killed**, his body found days later in a frozen ditch, his face half-eaten by wolves — ending the Burgundian dynasty
- The Swiss saying commemorating these battles: **"Bei Grandson das Gut, bei Murten den Mut, bei Nancy das Blut"** ("At Grandson the goods, at Murten the courage, at Nancy the blood")
- The victories established the Swiss Confederation's reputation as a major European military power and launched the era of **Swiss mercenary service** across the continent
- The Burgundian Netherlands eventually passed to the **House of Habsburg** through the marriage of Charles's daughter **Mary of Burgundy** to **Maximilian I** — a consequence that shaped European politics for centuries

## 12. Notable Facts and Events

- **1530**: Murten adopted the **Protestant Reformation** under the influence of **Guillaume Farel** and pressure from Bern — creating religious tension with Catholic Fribourg, its joint ruler
- **1798**: Napoleon's forces ended the Ancien Régime, and French soldiers destroyed the Burgundian ossuary at Merlach
- **1816**: The beautifully restored **tower clock** of the former city hall was constructed and is now designated an ancient monument
- **1848**: With the founding of the modern Swiss federal state, Murten became part of the new **Canton of Fribourg**
- **1 January 2016**: The former municipalities of **Courlevon, Jeuss (Jentes), Lurtigen (Lourtens), and Salvenach (Salvagny)** merged into the municipality of Murten
- **2022**: Murten recognized by the **UNWTO** as one of the **Best Tourism Villages** in the world
- **2024**: Named **"Swiss Village of the Year"** by readers of Schweizer Illustrierte
- **Adrian von Bubenberg** (c. 1424-1479) remains Murten's most celebrated historical figure — a Bernese Schultheiss, crusading knight (dubbed **Knight of the Holy Sepulchre** in Jerusalem in 1466), and military commander whose defense of Murten saved the Swiss Confederation. A monument to him stands in Bern's **Bubenbergplatz**

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The knight who wouldn't leave** — Adrian von Bubenberg prepares Murten for siege; a child helps him stockpile the last barrels of gunpowder, learning that courage means staying when everyone else wants to flee. The 13 days of siege unfold from the ramparts

2. **The runner with the linden branch** — A child joins the legendary messenger racing from Murten to Fribourg after the 1476 victory, carrying the linden twig that will one day become a 500-year-old tree. Each kilometer reveals a different scene of Swiss history

3. **Two names for everything** — A child discovers that in Murten, every street, every gate, and every church has two names — one German, one French. The Deutsche Kirche and the Französische Kirche argue over which language the bells should ring in, and the child must find the word that sounds the same in both

4. **The lights in the walls** — During the Murten Licht-Festival, a child touches a light projection on the Berntor and falls through the wall into 1476, where the only lights are campfires of the Burgundian siege army. To return, they must find the one light that shines in both centuries

5. **The bones that remembered** — A child visits the site of the destroyed ossuary at Merlach and finds a single Burgundian bone that tells the story of the battle from the other side — a young soldier from Flanders who did not want to fight the Swiss

6. **The lake that keeps secrets** — Lake Murten is the smallest of the Three Lakes, but it holds the most secrets: Burgundian armor on the bottom, Neolithic villages in the mud, and a Roman road leading to Aventicum. A child follows a fish through the Broye Canal connecting all three lakes

7. **The cream that takes five coats** — A baker's apprentice must learn to make the perfect Nidelkuchen with exactly five layers of cream, but each layer reveals a different era of Murten's history: Zähringen founding, Savoy castle-building, Burgundian siege, Reformation, and the present day

8. **The gate that saw it all** — The Berntor, first mentioned in 1239, has been destroyed and rebuilt many times. A child discovers it remembers every version of itself — each rebuild a different chapter, from medieval knights to Napoleonic soldiers to modern-day runners of the Murtenlauf streaming through on their way to Fribourg
