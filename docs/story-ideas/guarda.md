## 1. Geography — A Balcony Above the Inn

- Guarda sits at **1,653 meters** above sea level on a broad, sun-drenched terrace on the northern slopes of the **Lower Engadin** valley, in the canton of **Graubunden** (Grisons)
- The village perches more than **300 meters above the Inn river** (Romansh: **En**), which carves through the valley floor far below at around 1,360 m
- The municipality spans **3,154 hectares**, reaching up to **Piz Buin (3,312 m)** in the Silvretta range — one of the highest peaks in the region
- A small hamlet called **Giarsun** on the valley floor is officially part of Guarda
- The name "Guarda" derives from the Romansh **"Warda"**, meaning **"to guard"** or "lookout" — reflecting its elevated vantage point surveying the valley
- Since **2015**, Guarda has been part of the merged municipality of **Scuol** (together with Tarasp, Ardez, and Sent)
- Population: approximately **190 residents** — a tiny village, but one of Switzerland's most famous

## 2. Medieval Origins — First Mention in 1160

- Guarda is first documented in **1160** under the name **"Warda"**
- In **1494**, the village separated from the parish of **Ardez** to form its own parish, and the **Gothic village church** was built at that time
- In **1529**, the village adopted the **Reformation**, becoming Protestant — as did most of the Engadin
- Unlike many Lower Engadin villages, Guarda **never suffered a catastrophic fire** — this is the key reason its historic fabric survived intact while neighboring villages had to rebuild
- The houses that stand today preserve their **16th- and 17th-century** character, making Guarda a living time capsule of Engadin village life

## 3. Engadin Architecture — The Thick-Walled Mountain House

- The traditional **Engadin house** (Romansh: **chesa**) is a distinctive building type adapted to harsh alpine winters at high altitude
- Construction: massive-looking stone houses are actually **wooden houses at their core** — walls built from wooden beams were clad in stone after the wood had dried, creating a dual-layer structure
- The **thick walls stored heat**, while **small, deep-set windows** minimized energy loss — and **stables under the living quarters** used animal body heat to warm the house above, a perfect symbiosis of form and function
- The defining feature is the **Suler** (Romansh: **suler**): a **large arched entrance portal** wide enough for a hay cart, leading into a central passage through the house that served as storeroom, workroom, and summer dining room
- Off the Suler: the **kitchen**, storage rooms, and access to the stable; upstairs, the **Stuva sura** (great hall) and bedrooms
- Many houses feature **carved inscriptions** over the portal — blessings, family names, and construction dates — often in Romansh
- Guarda contains **over 60 historic houses** in this style, all protected as architectural heritage

## 4. Sgraffito — Scratching Art into Plaster

- **Sgraffito** (from the Italian verb **"sgraffiare"**, meaning "to scratch") is the distinctive decorative technique found on Engadin house facades
- The technique: a dark lime mortar layer is applied to the wall, then covered with a **light-colored lime coat**; while the outer layer is still damp (approximately **two hours** before it hardens), artisans **scratch ornamental designs** through it with styli and knives, revealing the dark layer beneath
- **Renaissance master builders from Italy** brought the technique to Graubunden in the **16th century**
- Sgraffito experienced its **heyday in the 17th and 18th centuries** — the period when most of Guarda's surviving decorations were created
- Popular motifs: **geometric patterns, wavy ribbons, rosettes, mythological figures** (mermaids, dragons), and heraldic symbols
- The decorations are found throughout the **Engadin, Bergell, Val Mustair**, and other parts of Graubunden, but Guarda is considered one of the finest concentrations
- A **20th-century revival** began in the early 1900s; today, courses and seminars teach the traditional technique to new practitioners

## 5. The Great Restoration — Iachen Ulrich Konz

- Between **1939 and 1945**, the Swiss architect **Iachen Ulrich Konz** (1899-1980) conducted a **major building restoration** of Guarda's historic houses
- Konz had studied architecture at **ETH Zurich** and Stuttgart; he ran practices in Davos (1926-1927) and Zuoz before settling in Guarda in **1939**
- His restoration preserved the village's authentic Engadin character at a time when many alpine villages were modernizing or deteriorating
- In **1939**, Konz married **Selina Chonz** (born Meyer) — who would go on to write the most famous Swiss children's book after Heidi
- The couple lived together in Guarda, and their son **Steivan Liun Konz** became a painter and draftsman
- Konz's meticulous restoration work laid the foundation for Guarda's **1975 Wakker Prize** — awarded by the **Swiss Heritage Society** (Schweizer Heimatschutz) to the municipality that best preserves and enhances its architectural heritage
- The Wakker Prize, first awarded in **1972** and funded by the bequest of Geneva businessman **Henri-Louis Wakker**, confirmed Guarda as one of the best-preserved villages in the Engadin

## 6. Schellen-Ursli — Switzerland's Most Beloved Picture Book

- **Schellen-Ursli** ("A Bell for Ursli") was published in **1945**, written by **Selina Chonz** (1910-2000) and illustrated by **Alois Carigiet** (1902-1985)
- Chonz was born in **Samedan** in the Upper Engadin and worked as a **kindergarten teacher**; she lived in Guarda at **House 47** (Chesa 47)
- Carigiet, born in **Trun** (Surselva), was a graphic designer, poster artist, and co-founder of the Zurich cabaret **"Cornichon"** (1934-1951); in 1939 he retreated to the mountains and shifted to painting
- From **1940 to 1945**, Carigiet visited Guarda and created the illustrations, using **Chesa 51** as the model for Ursli's house
- **The story**: Young Ursli is given the **smallest cowbell** for the Chalandamarz procession and is mocked by the other village boys. Determined, he treks alone through deep snow to his family's **Maiensass** (mountain hut) to retrieve a large cowbell. His parents and the village search for him through the night. In the morning, Ursli returns triumphantly and leads the bell procession with the **biggest bell of all**
- The book has been translated into **ten languages** and sold an estimated **1.7 million copies** worldwide — particularly successful in **Japan** (over 42,000 copies since 1973)
- Sequels by the same team: **Flurina und das Wildvoglein** (1952, featuring Ursli's sister) and **Der grosse Schnee** (1957)
- Carigiet later wrote and illustrated **Zottel, Zick und Zwerg** on his own, winning the **Swiss Youth Book Prize** in 1966
- In **1966**, Carigiet received the inaugural **Hans Christian Andersen Award** for illustration — the highest international distinction for children's book illustrators
- A **feature film** directed by **Xavier Koller** was released in **2015**, shot almost entirely in the Lower Engadin; it drew over **400,000 viewers** in its first three months

## 7. Chalandamarz — Driving Out Winter with Cowbells

- **Chalandamarz** (Romansh: "first day of March") is celebrated on **March 1st** across the Romansh-speaking Engadin — a spring festival with roots reaching back to **Roman times** and the old Roman calendar that began the year in March
- In Guarda and throughout the Lower Engadin, **schoolchildren** dress in traditional costumes: **blue Chalandamarz shirts, red scarves**, and pointed caps
- The children parade through the village **ringing cowbells** of different sizes — large bells (Plumpas), medium bells, and small goat bells — accompanied by the **cracking of whips** and traditional **Romansh songs**
- The purpose: to **drive away the evil spirits of winter** and wake the good spirits of spring
- Traditionally, the **oldest (or biggest) child** receives the **largest bell** and leads the procession — this is the custom at the heart of the Schellen-Ursli story
- After the procession, the children go door to door **collecting donations** of food and money, used for school trips
- The tradition is celebrated in the **Engadin, Val Mustair, Albula (Surmeir)**, and parts of Italian-speaking Graubunden (Poschiavo, Bregaglia, Mesolcina)
- Chalandamarz was already a living tradition long before the book — but **Schellen-Ursli made it famous** far beyond the Engadin

## 8. Romansh — Switzerland's Fourth Language

- Guarda is a **Romansh-speaking village**, part of the Lower Engadin where the dialect **Vallader** is spoken — one of five Romansh idioms (alongside Sursilvan, Sutsilvan, Surmiran, and Puter)
- Romansh descends from the **Latin vernacular** introduced during the Roman conquest of **Rhaetia in 15 BC**
- It became a **national language** of Switzerland in **1938** (approved by 91.6% of voters) and an **official language** in **1996**
- Total speakers: approximately **60,000** people, or about **0.5%** of the Swiss population (2021) — down from 1.1% in 1910
- In the Lower Engadin specifically, the **2000 census** found **79.2%** of residents named Romansh as a habitually spoken language (6,448 people)
- **Rumantsch Grischun**, a unified written standard, was created in **1982** by Professor **Heinrich Schmid** to serve administrative needs across all five dialects
- Each dialect has its own **400-year literary tradition**, and the diversity stems from the isolation of Graubunden's approximately **150 valleys**
- Common Romansh greetings: **"Allegra!"** (hello/welcome), **"Bun di"** (good day)

## 9. The Swiss National Park — Wilderness Next Door

- The **Swiss National Park** (Romansh: **Parc Naziunal Svizzer**), founded on **1 August 1914**, is the **oldest national park in the Alps** and in Central Europe
- Located near **Zernez**, just a short distance from Guarda in the Lower Engadin
- Two Basel academics drove its creation: botanist **Steivan Brunies** (1877-1953), who knew the Lower Engadin intimately, and anthropologist **Paul Sarasin** (1856-1929), who became the first chair of the Swiss League for the Protection of Nature in **1906**
- In **1909**, supporters leased the **Val Cluozza** from the municipality of Zernez; in **1913**, a parliamentary committee visited and endorsed the project; in **1914**, the park officially opened
- The park covers approximately **170 square kilometers** of strictly protected wilderness — **IUCN Category Ia** (the highest protection level) and a **UNESCO Biosphere Reserve**
- The motivation was alarm at environmental degradation: overexploited forests, collapsing wildlife populations (the **last lynx** was spotted in **1876**), and advancing industrialization
- Today, the park is home to **ibex, chamois, marmots, golden eagles**, and — since reintroduction efforts — **bearded vultures** (Bartgeier)

## 10. The Schellenursli Weg — A Story Come to Life

- The **Schellenursli Weg** (Schellen-Ursli Trail) is a **3.5 km circular hiking trail** in the forest above Guarda, reopened in its current form in **autumn 2016**
- The trail involves a **250-meter climb** and takes approximately **1.5 to 2 hours** for families; it is open **May through October**
- Approximately **20 interactive play stations** along the route correspond to pages from the book, with signboards in **German and English**
- Highlights include: a memory game about Ursli's daily life; the distribution of cowbells (where children discover Ursli received the smallest); a rope bridge; a snow field crossing; the **alpine hut** (with barbecue area and the large bell "Plumpa"); animal recognition stations; and the final **procession of the bells**
- In Guarda village itself, a small **Schellenursli Museum** displays traditional objects from the book — including the cowbells — open daily **10:00 to 17:00**
- The trail starts and ends in Guarda, passing through the hamlets of **Clus** and **Lajet** and the alpine meadow **Plan dal Nov**

## 11. Val Tuoi and the High Mountains

- From the eastern edge of Guarda, a path leads northwest into the **Val Tuoi**, a dramatic side valley reaching deep into the **Silvretta range**
- At the head of the valley, at **2,250 m**, stands the **Chamonna Tuoi** (Tuoi Hut), a **Swiss Alpine Club (SAC)** mountain hut — reachable in about **2.5 hours on foot** from Guarda
- The hut is one of the few SAC huts **open nearly year-round**, serving as a base for mountaineers, ski tourers, and families
- From Chamonna Tuoi, experienced mountaineers can summit **Piz Buin (3,312 m)** — offering panoramic views across the Lower Engadin, the Silvrettahorn (3,243 m), and the Dreilanderspitz (3,197 m) on the Austrian border
- The Val Tuoi hike passes through **Clus** and **Alp Suot**, with views of glaciers and the rugged Silvretta peaks

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The bell that chose Ursli** — Retelling from the perspective of the big cowbell waiting alone in the dark mountain hut, hoping someone brave enough will come to find it
2. **The sgraffito dragon** — A child in Guarda discovers that the mythological dragon scratched into a house facade comes alive at night and guards the village from winter storms
3. **Chalandamarz morning** — A shy child who has never dared to ring a bell joins the March 1st procession for the first time, discovering that even the smallest bell can drive away winter
4. **The house that remembered** — An old Engadin chesa with walls so thick they hold centuries of memories whispers stories from the Suler to a child who presses an ear to the stone
5. **Lost in Val Tuoi** — A child follows a marmot's whistle up into Val Tuoi and must find the way back to Guarda before darkness falls, guided by the sound of village bells echoing up from far below
6. **The architect's daughter** — Set during Konz's 1939-1945 restoration: a girl helps her father carefully scratch new sgraffito onto a freshly plastered wall, learning that beauty takes patience
7. **Allegra! The last Romansh word** — In a future where fewer and fewer people speak Romansh, a child in Guarda discovers a magical dictionary that brings forgotten words back to life as real things — "En" summons a river, "Chalandamarz" summons spring
8. **The park where nothing is allowed** — A child sneaks into the Swiss National Park expecting adventure but learns that the greatest magic is watching nature heal itself when humans simply step aside — and spots a bearded vulture soaring overhead for the first time
