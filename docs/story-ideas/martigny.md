## 1. Geography — At the Bend of the Rhône

- Martigny sits at **471 meters** elevation in the canton of Valais, at the dramatic point where the **Rhône river turns ninety degrees** from its southwestern course to flow northward toward Lac Léman (Lake Geneva)
- This makes it a natural **crossroads (carrefour)**: the gateway to the **Col du Grand-Saint-Bernard** (Great St. Bernard Pass) leading south to Italy, and the entrance to the upper Rhône valley leading east toward Sion and the Simplon
- The **Dranse** river flows from the southern Alps through the Val d'Entremont and joins the Rhône at Martigny, just after the Rhône's sharp bend — historically making the town vulnerable to flooding from both rivers
- Surrounded by the peaks of the **Grand Muveran**, **Luisin**, and **La Breya**, with steep vineyard-covered slopes rising directly above the town
- Population: approximately **23,500** (2020), making it the **second-largest town in Valais** after Sion
- French-speaking at **89%** — the town's official name is French, though the German name **Martinach** appears in historical documents
- The name **Martigny** derives from the medieval Latin **Martiniacum** (first recorded 1018), honoring **Saint Martin of Tours** as patron saint — replacing the far older Celtic and Roman names

## 2. Celtic Origins — The Veragri at Octodurus

- In the **1st century BC**, the site of present-day Martigny was a Celtic settlement called **Octodurus** (or **Octodurum**), the capital of the **Veragri** tribe
- The Veragri were a Gallic people controlling the upper Rhône valley and the approaches to the Alpine passes — they charged **tolls** on merchants crossing the mountains
- The settlement's strategic position at the junction of the Rhône and the Dranse, guarding the route over the **Grand-Saint-Bernard**, made it both commercially and militarily important
- In the winter of **57–56 BC**, Julius Caesar sent his legate **Servius Galba** with the **Twelfth Legion (Legio XII Fulminata)** to secure the pass for Roman merchants
- Galba established winter quarters at Octodurus, dividing the town between Romans and locals — a fatal error
- The **Battle of Octodurus (Bataille d'Octodure)**: the Veragri and neighboring **Seduni** attacked with a force Caesar claimed numbered **30,000 warriors**, surrounding the Roman camp from the surrounding heights
- After **six hours** of desperate defense, centurion **Publius Sextius Baculus** and tribune **Gaius Volusenus** urged a coordinated sortie from all gates — the gamble worked, routing the attackers
- Caesar claimed one-third of the 30,000 were killed, though modern historians consider the numbers inflated given the narrow valley
- Despite this tactical victory, Galba **withdrew** from the Alps — short on supplies and unable to hold the position through winter

## 3. Roman Forum Claudii Vallensium — Capital of the Valais

- The Romans completed their conquest of the region before **10 BC**, and under Emperor **Claudius** (reigned AD 41–54) the settlement was elevated to the status of a **forum** (market town) with **ius Latinum** (Latin rights)
- Renamed **Forum Claudii Vallensium** — literally "the Market of Claudius of the Valaisans" — it became the **capital of the Roman province of Vallis Poenina** (the Pennine Valley)
- Claudius developed the town as a strategic hub because the pass was the **most direct route to Britain** from northern Italy
- The Roman city was laid out on a **grid plan** with **insulae** (residential blocks) of approximately **90 x 70 meters**, straddling the Dranse
- Major public buildings included a **forum** (92 x 65 m) with a monumental entrance, a **basilica**, **public baths** with **hypocaust** underfloor heating, a **Gallo-Roman temple** (12 m per side), and an **aqueduct** system
- The town was burned in the **late 2nd century** and again in the **early 3rd century**, but was rebuilt both times and continued to function
- Decline set in after **AD 400** as traffic to Italy diminished, benefiting downstream **Acaunum** (Saint-Maurice) instead

## 4. The Roman Amphitheatre (Amphithéâtre romain)

- Built at the **beginning of the 2nd century**, probably during the reign of Emperor **Trajan** (98–117 AD)
- Dimensions: approximately **74 x 62 meters**, with a capacity of about **5,000 to 6,000 spectators**
- Used for gladiatorial combat and other spectacles (**jeux violents**) until the **end of the 4th century**
- In the **16th century**, the historian Gabriel Simeoni mistakenly identified the ruins as a Roman military camp — the true nature of the structure was only confirmed through modern archaeology
- Systematic excavation and restoration were carried out under the direction of archaeologist **François Wiblé**, culminating in a **1978 restoration** of the walls to their original condition
- It is the **only ancient Roman monument** whose remains are still visible above ground in Martigny — all other Roman structures lie beneath the modern town
- Today it hosts **open-air cinema**, theatrical performances, and the famous **Combat de Reines** (cow fights) — Hérens cattle dueling for dominance in the same arena where gladiators once fought

## 5. The Mithraeum (Mithréum) and Gallo-Roman Temples

- In **1993**, during construction of an apartment building, workers discovered a **Mithraeum** — a temple dedicated to **Mithra**, the Persian-origin sun god worshipped across the Roman Empire
- **Léonard Gianadda** intervened to ensure the sanctuary was fully excavated and **conserved in situ** (preserved in its original location)
- Dimensions: **8.95 m wide and 23.36 m long**, with a gabled roof reaching **6 meters** in height
- Dating from the reign of **Marcus Aurelius or Commodus** (161–192 AD), it remained in use until the early 5th century
- Excavations recovered an extraordinary collection: **three stone altars**, bronze figurines of the **Tauroctony** (the ritual bull-slaying), painted wall fragments, and **2,091 votive coins** — mostly from **378–402 AD**
- It is the **only Mithraeum open to the public in Switzerland** — and one of the most extensively documented in the entire Roman world
- A separate **Gallo-Roman temple** (temple indigène) of approximately **18 x 13 meters** was discovered in **1976** on the site where the **Fondation Pierre Gianadda** now stands

## 6. The Bishopric — Oldest Episcopal See in Switzerland

- An **episcopal see** (siège épiscopal) was established at Octodurus in the **4th century**, making it the **oldest bishopric** in what is now Switzerland
- The first historically attested bishop was **Theodorus** (Théodore/Théodule), who signed the acts of the **Council of Aquileia in 381** as "Theodorus Episcopus Octodorensis"
- Theodorus also attended a synod summoned by Pope Damasus in Rome between **368 and 373** — he died around **391**
- In **580** (some sources say 589), Bishop **Saint Héliodore** transferred the see from Octodurus to **Sion (Sedunum)**, abandoning the flood-prone low-lying site where the Dranse joins the Rhône
- The **Diocese of Sion** (Diocèse de Sion) thus traces its continuous history back to Martigny — making it one of the oldest in all of western Christendom
- The relics of Theodorus were transferred to Sion at the time of the move

## 7. The Château de la Bâtiaz (Tour de la Bâtiaz)

- A fortification on the rocky promontory above Martigny, the present **round tower (donjon circulaire)** was built between **1260 and 1268** by **Pierre II de Savoie** (Peter II of Savoy)
- The foundations of a castle on the site date to the **11th century** — earlier than the current tower
- The castle became a prized possession fought over between the **House of Savoy** (Maison de Savoie) and the **bishops of Sion** (évêques de Sion), changing hands multiple times as the two powers struggled for control of the lower Valais
- The episcopal **châtellenie** (castellany) of Martigny passed into Savoyard hands in **1384**
- In **1475**, the Seven Tithings (**Sept Dizains**) of the Upper Valais, allied with the bishop of Sion and the canton of Bern, seized the entire Lower Valais from Savoy
- In **1518**, during a civil war for control of the Valais, the castle was **besieged and burned** — it was never rebuilt and has remained in ruin since
- A **1980 restoration** stabilized the ruins and opened them to the public
- Today the tower offers a **panoramic view** over the Rhône valley, the vineyards, and the surrounding peaks — and houses a collection of **medieval torture instruments** and a seasonal medieval tavern called **À la Part des Anges**

## 8. The Great St. Bernard Pass (Col du Grand-Saint-Bernard)

- At **2,469 meters** (8,100 feet), one of the highest and most historic Alpine crossings, linking Martigny in the Rhône valley to **Aosta** (Augusta Praetoria) in Italy
- Used since at least the **Bronze Age**; the Romans built a military road across it by **AD 69**, portions of which — **3.7 meters wide**, cut into rock — are still visible
- Around **1050** (some sources say 962), **Saint Bernard de Menthon** (Bernard of Menthon), Archdeacon of Aosta, founded a **hospice (hospice)** at the summit to shelter pilgrims, merchants, and travelers from the extreme weather, avalanches, and bandits
- The hospice was placed under the care of **Augustinian canons** (Chanoines réguliers du Grand-Saint-Bernard) — it has operated continuously for nearly **1,000 years**, making it one of Europe's oldest functioning mountain refuges
- The pass was a key section of the **Via Francigena**, the great medieval pilgrimage route from Canterbury to Rome
- A **road** was built across the pass in **1823**, and in **1964** the **Grand-Saint-Bernard Tunnel** (5.6 km long) was completed, allowing year-round passage and reducing travel time by one hour

## 9. The St. Bernard Dogs (Chiens du Saint-Bernard) and the Fondation Barry

- Dogs were first introduced to the hospice between **1660 and 1670**, originally as **watchdogs**
- The earliest written record of a dog at the hospice dates from **1707** in the monastery archives
- By the early **18th century**, the monks began training the dogs for **mountain rescue** — the animals had an uncanny sense of direction in snowstorms and deep cold
- Over nearly **200 years**, the dogs helped rescue an estimated **2,000 people** — from lost pilgrims to Napoleon's soldiers
- The most famous: **Barry der Menschenretter** ("Barry the People-Rescuer"), who lived at the hospice from **1800 to 1812** and is credited with saving **over 40 lives**
- Barry retired to Bern and died in **1814**; his preserved body is displayed at the **Naturhistorisches Museum Bern** (Natural History Museum of Bern)
- The breed was officially named **Saint Bernard** in **1880**
- In January **2005**, the **Fondation Barry du Grand-Saint-Bernard** was established in Martigny, taking over the **world's oldest breeding program** for the Swiss national dog from the Augustinian canons
- Today the foundation cares for approximately **34 dogs** at **Barryland** in Martigny (year-round) and at the pass (summer), breeding about **20 pedigreed puppies** per year and conducting **animal-assisted therapy** programs

## 10. Napoleon's Crossing (1800)

- In May **1800**, First Consul **Napoleon Bonaparte** chose the Grand-Saint-Bernard as the route for his **Armée de Réserve** of approximately **40,000 men** to cross into Italy and surprise the Austrians
- Napoleon established his headquarters in **Martigny** from **May 17 to 20**, using the town as the staging base
- From **May 15 to 20**, the army crossed the pass single file — about **6,000 men per day**, struggling through heavy snow that many had deemed the route impassable
- At the summit, the hospice monks handed each passing soldier **two glasses of wine and a slice of rye bread with cheese** — the canons opened their doors to the entire army
- Napoleon himself reached the summit on **May 20**, received by the hospice prior **Père Berenfaller** — though contrary to Jacques-Louis David's famous painting series **"Bonaparte franchissant le Grand-Saint-Bernard"** (1801–1805), he crossed on a **mule**, not a rearing stallion
- David's painting, commissioned by the Spanish ambassador, inscribes the names of **Hannibal** and **Charlemagne** on the rocks alongside Napoleon's — the three great commanders who led armies over the Alps
- The army reached Piedmont by **May 25** and defeated the Austrians at the **Battle of Marengo** on **June 14, 1800**
- Moved by the death of General **Desaix** at Marengo, Napoleon had his friend's tomb built in the hospice chapel — Desaix was buried there in **1805**

## 11. The Fondation Pierre Gianadda — Art Among the Ruins

- In **1976**, engineer and art patron **Léonard Gianadda** began construction of an apartment building in Martigny — workers unearthed the remains of a **Gallo-Roman temple** dating to the 1st century AD
- That same year, Léonard's younger brother **Pierre Gianadda** was killed in an airplane accident
- Rather than destroy the temple, Léonard halted construction and built a **cultural center** over the ruins — dedicated to his brother's memory and inaugurated in **1978**
- The foundation, at **Rue du Forum 59**, houses: the **Musée gallo-romain** (Gallo-Roman Museum) displaying the temple and local archaeological finds; the **Musée de l'Automobile** with **50 classic cars** dating from 1897 to 1939; one of Europe's most beautiful **sculpture parks** (parc de sculptures); and rotating world-class art exhibitions
- Over **10 million visitors** have seen exhibitions featuring works by **Van Gogh, Picasso, Rodin, Giacometti, Chagall, Klee, Goya, Modigliani**, and many others — borrowed from the world's greatest museums and private collections
- Léonard Gianadda also installed **17 modern sculptures** on Martigny's roundabouts, donated stained-glass windows to local churches, and founded the **Musée et Chiens du Saint-Bernard** (Barryland)
- The foundation's endowment exceeds **70 million Swiss francs**
- In **2019**, Léonard Gianadda received the **European Heritage Award** (Europa Nostra) as a Heritage Champion for his lifetime of cultural philanthropy

## 12. Viticulture, Traditions, and Modern Martigny

- The steep slopes above Martigny, particularly along the Dranse, are covered with **terraced vineyards (vignobles en terrasses)** supported by **dry-stone walls (murs en pierres sèches)** reaching up to **20 meters** in height
- The **Clos de Cochetta**, perched on these dramatic terraces, is one of the most prestigious vineyard sites — accessible via a secret tunnel leading to a sun terrace
- In **1812**, the best Valais wine was already reported to come from Martigny's hillsides
- Key grape varieties: **Chasselas** (called **Fendant** in the Valais) and **Pinot Blanc** for whites; **Pinot Noir**, **Gamay**, and indigenous varieties like **Cornalin** and **Humagne Rouge** for reds
- The **Foire du Valais** (Valais Trade Fair), founded in **1959** as the "Comptoir de Martigny," is held annually in late September/early October — attracting over **200,000 visitors** and more than 400 exhibitors, making it the **most-visited fair in French-speaking Switzerland**
- The **Combat de Reines** (queen cow fights) are held during the fair in the **Roman amphitheatre** — **Hérens cattle** (vaches d'Hérens), a small, fierce, ancient Valais breed, naturally push and shove horn-to-horn to establish herd dominance. The winner earns the title **La Reine des Reines** (Queen of Queens) and a ceremonial bell
- The **Foire au Lard** (Bacon Fair), held the first Monday of December, traces its origins to **1801** when Martigny-Bourg was granted the right to hold two annual fairs. It celebrates the Alpine tradition of **le lard** — salted and dried pork, a protected **IGP** (Indication Géographique Protégée) product of the Valais
- The **Pont de la Bâtiaz** (Bâtiaz Bridge), a **covered wooden bridge** over the Dranse, was first documented in **1349** as a toll bridge. Destroyed by floods in 1635 and by the catastrophic **Giétroz glacier break** of **1818**, it was rebuilt in **1829** with **larch-wood arches** — it is the **last arched covered bridge in the Valais** still in use

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The legion that wouldn't retreat** — Winter 57 BC: a young Roman soldier in Galba's Twelfth Legion survives the Battle of Octodurus, befriended by a Veragri child who shows that the mountains belong to everyone
2. **Barry the brave** — The legendary St. Bernard rescue dog Barry senses a child lost in a blizzard on the Grand-Saint-Bernard; told from the dog's perspective as he digs through snow and lies on the child to keep them warm
3. **The temple under the house** — A child in 1976 Martigny watches as builders discover ancient Roman walls beneath a construction site; the ghost of a Roman priest of Mithra guides them through the underground sanctuary by torchlight
4. **Napoleon's mule** — The true story of Napoleon crossing the Alps, told from the perspective of the sturdy mule who actually carried him (not the rearing stallion in the painting), grumbling about the snow and the 40,000 soldiers eating all the cheese
5. **The queen of queens** — A young Valais farm girl raises a spirited Hérens calf; together they train for the Combat de Reines in the ancient Roman amphitheatre, where two thousand years of history echo in every clash of horns
6. **The castle between two masters** — A child living in the shadow of the Tour de la Bâtiaz in the 13th century watches as the castle changes hands between Savoy and the bishop of Sion, learning that true loyalty belongs to the land, not to lords
7. **The art and the ruins** — A child visiting the Fondation Pierre Gianadda discovers that the paintings come alive at night, and the Roman temple beneath the museum opens a portal to Forum Claudii Vallensium, where a young Roman gives them a tour of the ancient city
8. **The bishop who moved** — In 580 AD, after another devastating flood of the Dranse and the Rhône, young Bishop Héliodore must convince his flock to abandon Octodurus and follow him upriver to Sion — carrying the relics of Saint Théodore on a perilous journey through the valley
