## 1. The Oldest City in Switzerland — 5,000 Years of Settlement

- Chur is **Switzerland's oldest city**, with evidence of human habitation spanning over **5,000 years**
- **~11,000 BC**: Late Palaeolithic hunter-gatherers camped in the area — the earliest traces found during a 1998 car park excavation
- **~3900–3500 BC**: Permanent settlement during the **Pfyn culture** (Neolithic period), the earliest evidence of structured habitation at the site
- **~1300–800 BC**: Bronze-Age **Urnfield and Laugen-Melaun** settlements in the Welschdörfli district
- **5th–3rd century BC**: Iron-Age settlements, likely of **Raetian** origin
- The **Welschdörfli** district south of the Plessur River is the key archaeological zone — continuous settlement from the Neolithic period to the present day
- Excavations on the **Ackermann grounds** (Seilerbahnweg) revealed finds spanning millennia; the protective structures covering these Roman-era remains were designed in **1986** by the architect **Peter Zumthor**
- Important nuance: archaeologist **Thomas Reitmaier** (Director of the Archaeological Service of Canton Graubünden) notes that "a real continuous occupation can only be attested from the Roman times on" — earlier finds represent seasonal or intermittent settlement

## 2. Roman Curia Raetorum — Capital of a Province

- In **15 BC**, Emperor **Augustus** conquered the Alpine region and incorporated it into the Roman province of **Raetia**
- The settlement at Chur became known as **Curia Raetorum** — a strategic crossroads for north–south Alpine trade routes
- **~280 AD**: Chur's first written mention appears in the **Itinerarium Antonini**, a Roman road register
- Under Emperor **Diocletian** (late 3rd century), Curia Raetorum was elevated to **capital of Raetia Prima**, the newly divided western half of the province
- The Roman **vicus** (civilian settlement) in today's Welschdörfli included residential buildings, workshops, and a **thermal bath**
- Chur's strategic value lay in controlling the junction of routes leading to the **Julier, Splügen, Septimer, and San Bernardino** passes — the same passes that define the city to this day
- The Roman name **"Curia"** evolved over centuries into the German "Chur" and the Romansh "Cuira"

## 3. The Bishopric of Chur — One of the Oldest North of the Alps

- Chur became the seat of a **Christian bishopric** in the 4th or early 5th century — one of the oldest north of the Alps
- The first documented bishop is **Asinio** (also written Asinius), who attended the **Synod of Milan in 451/452**
- Local tradition ascribes the diocese's founding to **Saint Lucius**, a legendary British king turned missionary — though this is not confirmed by historical sources
- In **831**, the bishop's temporal domains were made directly dependent on the Empire, giving the bishop great secular power
- In **1170**, the Bishop of Chur became a **Prince of the Holy Roman Empire** (Reichsfürst), wielding both spiritual and political authority
- The **Bishop's Palace** (Bischöflicher Hof) sits on a fortified hilltop above the old town — an enclosed precinct containing the cathedral, the palace, and chapter residences
- The palace received its present **Baroque form** under Bishop Joseph Benedict von Rost (**1728–1754**); part of it now houses the **Domschatzmuseum** (Cathedral Treasury Museum)
- The bishopric has approximately **1,600 years** of continuous church history — one of the longest unbroken episcopal traditions in Switzerland

## 4. Cathedral of the Assumption (Kathedrale St. Mariä Himmelfahrt)

- The current late-**Romanesque** cathedral was built over a **120-year period** and consecrated on **19 June 1272** under Bishop **Henry of Montfort**
- Dedicated to the **Assumption of the Blessed Virgin Mary**
- Earlier churches stood on the same site: the first predecessor dates to the **5th century**, with an 8th-century structure called the **Tellokirche** (~750–760)
- The cathedral's Romanesque **sculptures, capitals, and column decorations** are among the best-preserved in Europe

### The Jakob Russ High Altar (1486–1492)
- Commissioned by Bishop **Ortlieb von Brandis** in **1486**
- Created by the sculptor **Jakob Russ** from **Ravensburg** (southern Germany), who moved his workshop to Chur for the project
- A magnificent **late-Gothic triptych** of gilded wood containing **152 carved figures**
- Considered one of the finest carved Gothic altars of Swiss late Gothic art
- The cathedral was **completely restored between 2001 and 2007**

## 5. The Altstadt — A Car-Free Medieval Old Town

- Chur's old town is **almost entirely car-free** — one of Switzerland's most intact historic centres
- The **great fire of 27 April 1464** destroyed most of the town; only the bishop's precinct and the **St. Luzi monastery** survived
- **German craftsmen** were brought in to rebuild the town, giving it its current **late-Gothic** character
- The winding cobblestone lanes feature **richly painted house facades** — some depicting former guilds, others restored to their original Gothic glory (e.g., the **Zschaler House** with its exquisite frescoes)
- Hidden courtyards, ornate doors, historic clocks, and fountains line the streets
- The **Rathaus** (Town Hall) was rebuilt after the 1464 fire on the site of a former hospital — its visibly sloping walls reveal its centuries of age
- The **Malteserturm** near the Obertor (Upper Gate) is part of the oldest city fortifications, damaged in the 1464 fire and rebuilt in the early 16th century
- **Red signs** mark a self-guided walking route through the old town's key historical sites
- The Saturday morning market (May–October) and Advent Christmas market continue centuries of trading tradition

## 6. The Reformation in Chur (1523–1527)

- In **1523**, the city council called **Johannes Comander** to preach at **St. Martin's Church**, the main parish church
- Comander preached in the reformatory sense, and in **1526** distributed the **Eucharist** in place of the Catholic Mass for the first time
- By **1527**, the Reformation was fully carried through in Chur — altars, jewellery, vestments, and banners were removed from St. Martin's (the high altar was kept until 1529)
- **St. Martin's Church** had been built in the **8th century** (consecrated 769), largely destroyed in the **1464 fire**, and rebuilt in **late-Gothic style by 1491** — the largest late-Gothic building in Graubünden
- The result was a unique religious division: the **bishop's hilltop precinct remained Catholic**, while the city below became **Protestant** — an isolated Catholic enclave within a Reformed city, comparable to St. Gallen's similar split

## 7. The Three Leagues (Drei Bünde) — Birthplace of Graubünden

- Three defensive alliances formed in the eastern Alps to resist **Habsburg** expansion and **episcopal** authority:
  1. **Gotteshausbund** (League of God's House): founded **29 January 1367**, centred on Chur
  2. **Grauer Bund** (Grey League): founded **1395**, in the Upper Rhine valley
  3. **Zehngerichtebund** (League of the Ten Jurisdictions): founded **1436** at Davos
- On **23 September 1524**, the three leagues formally merged into the **Free State of the Three Leagues** via the **Bundesbrief** — an oath to maintain "peace, protection and order"
- The **First Ilanz Article** (4 April 1524) was the first state law for all three leagues, weakening feudal lords and strengthening democratic institutions
- The **Second Ilanz Article** (1526) reinforced democratic structures further
- Governance was radically decentralized: autonomous **judicial communities** made decisions through the **Landsgemeinde** (citizens' assemblies); there was **no joint treasury, no common judiciary, and no central authority**
- The **Bundstag** (diet) rotated between three cities: **Chur, Ilanz, and Davos**
- The Free State existed until **1798**, when Napoleon's forces annexed it to the Helvetic Republic
- In **1803**, it became **Canton Graubünden** — an equal canton of the Swiss Confederation. Chur became its capital

## 8. Gateway to Graubünden — The Alpine Passes

- Chur's strategic importance has always derived from its position as the **gateway to the great Alpine passes**
- From the **Obertor** (Upper Gate), two historic roads diverge:
  - The **Untere Strasse** (Lower Road): via Thusis and the dramatic **Viamala gorge** to the **Splügen Pass** (2,113 m) and **San Bernardino Pass** (2,065 m)
  - The **Obere Strasse** (Upper Road): via Lenzerheide and Tiefencastel to the **Julier Pass** (2,284 m), **Albula Pass** (2,312 m), and **Septimer Pass** (2,310 m)
- These routes were critical since **Roman times** — Emperor Augustus used them for military and trade movements after conquering Raetia in 15 BC
- In the early 19th century, **Chur merchants** funded the modernization of these passes into commercial roads: the San Bernardino road was built **1818–1823**, the Splügen road completed **1822**, and the Julier/Maloja road expanded **1820–1828** at a cost of **1.24 million Francs**
- Today Chur remains the starting point for journeys into the Engadin, to St. Moritz, and across the Alps into Italy

## 9. The Rhaetian Railway — UNESCO World Heritage

- The **Rhaetian Railway** (Rhätische Bahn / RhB) replaced the mule-driven pass routes with engineering marvels of the early 20th century
- **Albula line**: opened **1904** (construction began September 1898), **67 km** long, featuring **42 tunnels** and **144 viaducts and bridges** between Thusis and St. Moritz
- **Bernina line**: inaugurated **1910**, **61 km** long, with **13 tunnels** and **52 viaducts and bridges**, crossing the Bernina Pass at **2,253 m** — the highest railway crossing in the Alps without a tunnel
- The **Landwasser Viaduct** (built 1901–1902) is the most iconic structure: **65 m high**, **136 m long**, six arches curving at a 100 m radius, leading directly into the Landwasser Tunnel — built without scaffolding using two cranes
- Inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2008** as "Rhaetian Railway in the Albula / Bernina Landscapes"
- The **Glacier Express** (Chur–Zermatt) and **Bernina Express** (Chur–Tirano, Italy) are among the world's most celebrated scenic train journeys
- Chur's **Hauptbahnhof** is the starting station — making the city the gateway to both routes

## 10. Heidi's Mountains — Johanna Spyri and Maienfeld

- **Johanna Spyri** (1827–1901) set her world-famous children's novel **"Heidi"** (1881) in the mountains above the town of **Maienfeld** — just **17 km north of Chur** in the Bündner Herrschaft
- Spyri spent several summers in the villages of **Maienfeld, Jenins, and near Chur**, drawing direct inspiration from the landscape
- The novel tells of orphaned Heidi, sent to live with her grandfather (the **Alpöhi**) in a mountain hut above Maienfeld, and her friendship with the goatherd **Peter**
- Today, **Heididorf** in Maienfeld is a popular museum village recreating scenes from the novel, and the entire region is marketed as **"Heidiland"**
- A Swiss woman named **Heidi Schwaller** (born near Chur) recalled meeting Spyri in the early 1900s, lending credence to the area's deep personal connection to the author

## 11. The Bündner Herrschaft — Wine Country at the Foot of the Alps

- The **Bündner Herrschaft** is Graubünden's most important wine-growing area, centred on the four villages of **Fläsch, Maienfeld, Jenins, and Malans** — all within 20 km of Chur
- The **warmest wine region in German-speaking Switzerland**, benefiting from the **Föhn** (warm Alpine wind), chalky soils, and mountain-sheltered microclimates
- **Pinot Noir** (Blauburgunder) dominates, accounting for roughly **70%** of vines; over **45 grape varieties** produce more than **50 distinct wines**
- **Schloss Salenegg** in Maienfeld has produced wine since **1068** — it is **Europe's oldest continuously operating winery**. Its massive wooden wine press (Torkelbaum) dates from **1656**
- The total vineyard area of Canton Graubünden is approximately **420 hectares**
- The dramatic temperature differences between warm days and cool Alpine nights are crucial for producing the intense, high-quality Pinot Noir the region is known for

## 12. Notable People and Geography

### Angelika Kauffmann (1741–1807)
- Born in Chur on **30 October 1741**, she became one of the most celebrated female painters of the 18th century
- A **Neoclassical** painter, she was one of only **two women** among the founding members of the **Royal Academy of Arts in London** in 1768 (alongside Mary Moser)
- After years in London and Rome, she died in 1807; the sculptor **Antonio Canova** arranged her funeral, and a bust was placed in the **Pantheon** beside Raphael's
- The **Bündner Kunstmuseum** in Chur holds a collection of her works

### Peter Zumthor (born 1943)
- While born in Basel, the Pritzker Prize-winning architect has worked from his studio in **Haldenstein** (near Chur) since the 1960s
- In **1968**, he became conservationist architect for Graubünden's Department for the Preservation of Monuments
- His **1986 shelter for the Roman ruins** in Chur's Welschdörfli is an early masterwork — delicate timber structures protecting ancient excavations

### Geography
- Chur sits at **594 m above sea level** on the right bank of the **Plessur River**, just above its confluence with the **Rhine**
- The city lies in the **Chur Rhine Valley** (Churer Rheintal), where the Alpine Rhine turns northward
- The **Calanda massif** rises to over **2,800 m** directly west of the city
- The city's own **Hausberg** is **Brambrüesch** (2,174 m), reachable by cable car from the old town
- City altitude ranges from **600 m** to **1,800 m**, making it an "alpine city" with ski slopes accessible from the centre
- Population: approximately **38,000** (city), making it the largest city in Canton Graubünden

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The oldest secret in Switzerland** — A child discovers a Neolithic clay pot in Welschdörfli that whispers stories of 5,000 years of inhabitants — each voice from a different era (Pfyn culture, Roman soldiers, medieval bishops)
2. **The road to Rome begins in Chur** — A Roman child living in Curia Raetorum must deliver an urgent message over the Julier Pass in winter; the mountain spirits test their courage at every turn
3. **The 152 carved figures** — At midnight in the cathedral, the 152 wooden figures of Jakob Russ's altarpiece step down from the triptych and re-enact the events of 1492 Chur; a child must help them return before dawn
4. **The fire that remade a city** — On the night of 27 April 1464, a child watches Chur burn — then travels forward in time to see German craftsmen rebuild the town, painting its famous facades
5. **Three leagues, one handshake** — A child from each of the three leagues (Gotteshausbund, Grauer Bund, Zehngerichtebund) must meet at the Bundstag in Chur to solve a dispute — learning about democracy along the way
6. **The little train that climbed** — A child rides the first Albula line train in 1904, crossing 144 bridges and through 42 tunnels, befriending the engineer who built the Landwasser Viaduct without scaffolding
7. **Heidi's real mountains** — A child visiting Maienfeld meets a mysterious old woman who might be Johanna Spyri herself, and together they walk the paths that inspired the world's most famous Swiss story
8. **Angelika and the king's portrait** — Young Angelika Kauffmann, growing up in Chur, discovers she can paint people's hidden feelings; when summoned to London, she must choose between fame and her beloved mountains
