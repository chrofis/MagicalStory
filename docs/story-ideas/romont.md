## 1. Geography — The Round Hill Between Fribourg and Lausanne

- **Romont** (from the Latin **Rotundus Mons**, "round hill") sits dramatically perched on an isolated hilltop at **780 m** above sea level in the upper **Glâne Valley** (vallée de la Glâne), on the southwestern Fribourg plateau
- The town is the capital of the **district de la Glâne** (Glâne district) in the **canton of Fribourg** (canton de Fribourg), close to the border with the Canton of Vaud
- Strategically located at the crossroads of routes between **Fribourg**, **Lausanne**, and **Bulle** — the town controlled a key corridor between Lake Geneva and central Switzerland
- The railway station sits at **707 m**, while the castle crowns the summit at **780 m** — visitors literally climb into the Middle Ages
- Area: **10.9 km²**, of which 67% agricultural land, 14% forest, 19% settled
- Population: approximately **5,400** (2020); primarily French-speaking (**83.8%**)
- First mentioned in **1177** as **"Rotundo Monte"**; the name "Romont" appears from **1244**
- The hilltop position offers panoramic views from the **Mont Blanc** massif to the **Jura** mountains

## 2. Founding — Pierre II de Savoie, the "Little Charlemagne"

- In **1239**, **Pierre II de Savoie** (Peter II of Savoy, c. 1203–1268), known as the **"Petit Charlemagne"** (Little Charlemagne), acquired the rights to the hill of Romont from **Anselme de Billens** — the territory had belonged to the Bishop of Lausanne
- In **1240**, Pierre II sent a **châtelain** (castellan) and began building the **castle** and founding a **new town** (burgum) beside the existing fortified settlement (castrum)
- The **Peace of Evian** (Paix d'Évian) of **1244** confirmed Savoy's rights to Romont, settling the dispute with the Bishop of Lausanne
- Pierre II was a prolific castle-builder across Savoy, Vaud, and even England — in **1243** he was granted land in London where he built the famous **Savoy Palace** on the Strand
- Romont was strategically founded to provide an alternative route to the Broye and Sarine corridors, advancing Savoy's territorial expansion toward the northeast against rival Habsburg, Bernese, and Fribourgeois powers
- The town received **town rights** (droits de ville) modeled on those of Moudon, probably between **1285 and 1293**, certainly no later than **1328**
- By the end of the **13th century**, the population exceeded **1,000**; before the Burgundian Wars (**1474**), it reached nearly **1,500** — making Romont one of the most important towns in the Pays de Vaud

## 3. The Château de Romont — A Savoy Fortress of Glass

- The **Grand Donjon** (great keep), built on the characteristic **Savoy square tower plan**, was completed before **1260**
- The well shaft is reputedly as deep as the donjon is tall — approximately **40 meters**
- A second tower, the **Petit Donjon** (now the **Tour à Boyer**), a round keep, was also likely built by Pierre II between **1250 and 1260**
- The castle served as the seat of the **châtellenie de Romont** (castellany of Romont) under Savoy rule from **1240 to 1536**
- After the Savoy era, Fribourg governors (baillis) took residence; in the **16th century**, they built a new **Fribourg wing** (aile fribourgeoise)
- The Grand Donjon partially collapsed in **1579** and was rebuilt between **1579 and 1591** by the government of Fribourg
- A **footbridge** (passerelle) was added in **2006**, connecting the Savoyard core with the Fribourg wing
- Today the castle houses the **Vitromusée Romont** and is listed as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance** (bien culturel d'importance nationale)

## 4. The Vitromusée — World Capital of Glass Art

- The **Musée du Vitrail** (Museum of Stained Glass) officially opened on **20 June 1981**, as part of celebrations marking the **500th anniversary** of the Canton of Fribourg joining the Swiss Confederation
- Renamed **Musée Suisse du Vitrail** (Swiss Museum of Stained Glass) in **January 1988**, then **Vitromusée Romont** in **2006**
- Houses over **14,000 works** spanning from Antiquity to the present day — stained glass windows, **peintures sous verre** (reverse glass paintings), glass objects, and tools of the glass arts
- The Savoyard wing displays the **stained glass collection**, from medieval panels to contemporary art
- The Fribourg wing holds the **world's largest collection of reverse glass painting** (peinture sous verre) — approximately **300 selected objects** from the Middle Ages to the 21st century, largely from the bequest of **Ruth and Frieder Ryser**
- The adjacent **Vitrocentre Romont** serves as an international research centre for glass art, with a database of over **100,000** entries (Vitrosearch)
- The museum hosts artist residencies, workshops, and demonstrations of glass-painting techniques
- Opening hours: April–October 10:00–17:00, November–March 11:00–17:00, Tuesday to Sunday; admission CHF 12

## 5. The Collégiale Notre-Dame de l'Assomption — Seven Centuries of Light

- The **Collégiale Notre-Dame de l'Assomption** (Collegiate Church of Our Lady of the Assumption) was first consecrated in the **13th century**
- Destroyed by fire in the **15th century**, it was immediately rebuilt in local **molasse** (sandstone) in the **Flamboyant Gothic** (gothique flamboyant) style
- The church is a living showcase of the **evolution of stained glass** from the medieval period to the modern era:
  - **1348**: The oldest stained glass windows still in situ — among the earliest surviving medieval glass in the region
  - **16th century**: Renaissance-era panels
  - **19th century**: Neo-Gothic restorations, including a Marian glass in the choir
  - **1938–1939**: **Alexandre Cingria** created the twelve apostles in the high nave windows and the baptismal chapel glass
  - **1968**: **Yoki** (Yoki Aebischer) created an abstract Marian cycle for the **Chapelle du Portail** (Gate Chapel)
  - **1981**: **Sergio de Castro** created windows depicting prophets in the northern side-aisles
- The **stalls** (stalles) in the choir — ornate wooden seats reserved for clergy — were commissioned in **1464** from the woodcarvers **Antoine and Claude de Peney**
- Together with the Vitromusée across the square, the church forms an unparalleled ensemble for studying the art of stained glass

## 6. The Town Walls and Towers — A Fortified Promenade

- Romont's medieval **remparts** (ramparts) stretch approximately **1,500 meters** (1.5 km) around the hilltop, forming a nearly complete defensive ring
- Built starting in the mid-**13th century** under Pierre II de Savoie, the walls originally featured a **covered wooden roof** (toit couvert), which disappeared during the **19th century**
- Three **gate-towers** (tours-portes) guarded the entrances to the town
- The **Tour à Boyer** — a high circular keep in the **Savoyard style**, walls **3.10 m thick** at the base and **2.60 m** at the top, rising approximately **38 meters** above ground — is the most impressive surviving tower
- Other towers along the perimeter include the **Tour du Sauvage**, the **Tour de Billens**, and the **Tour de Fribourg**
- The **promenade des remparts** (rampart walk) offers panoramic views from **Mont Blanc** to the **Jura** and is one of Switzerland's best-preserved medieval wall circuits
- The **Vitroparcours**, an educational trail with **8 interactive stations** about glass art, follows the ramparts — combining medieval fortifications with modern glass education
- The entire ensemble — castle, walls, towers, and church — is listed as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**

## 7. Les Pleureuses — The Weeping Women of Good Friday

- **Les Pleureuses** (the Weeping Women / the Mourners) is a solemn **Good Friday procession** (procession du Vendredi saint) dating back to the **15th century** (some sources cite the **14th century**)
- Romont is one of the **last places in Switzerland** to maintain this ancient Catholic procession
- After the reading of the **Passion** (Passion du Christ) at the Collégiale, a funeral cortege processes through the streets of the upper town
- A **cross-bearer** (porteur de croix), head covered in black cloth, leads the procession
- He is followed by approximately **20 women veiled in black** — the Pleureuses — who maintain **absolute silence** to preserve the sacred character of the ceremony
- On **scarlet cushions** (coussins écarlates), they carry the **instruments of the Passion** (instruments de la Passion): a **crown of thorns** (couronne d'épines), **nails** (clous), a **hammer** (marteau), **birch sticks**, a **whip** (fouet), and **St. Veronica's shroud** (voile de Véronique) — a white cloth printed with the face of Christ
- A young girl portrays the **Virgin Mary** (la Vierge Marie), walking behind the cross
- The cortege stops at the **fourteen Stations of the Cross** (quatorze stations du chemin de croix), praying and chanting, leading to Calvary and the death of Christ
- The procession draws visitors from across Switzerland and is recognized as an important piece of **living intangible cultural heritage**

## 8. Jacques de Savoie, Comte de Romont — A Count in the Burgundian Wars

- **Jacques de Savoie** (Jacques of Savoy, 12 November 1450 – 30 January 1486), seventh son of **Louis, Duc de Savoie**, received Romont and the Pays de Vaud as an **appanage** (fief) in **1460**
- He became a close ally of **Charles le Téméraire** (Charles the Bold), Duke of Burgundy, and in **1473** was appointed **Governor of Burgundy**, Grand Marshal, and supreme commander of a third of the Burgundian army
- During the **Burgundian Wars** (Guerres de Bourgogne, 1474–1477), Romont sided with Burgundy against the Swiss Confederation
- After the Confederation's victories at **Grandson** (2 March 1476) and **Morat/Murten** (22 June 1476), the Confederates **conquered and plundered Romont**
- At the Battle of Morat, Jacques's Savoyard division escaped around Lake Murten and retreated to Romont
- Savoy briefly regained control through the **Peace of Fribourg** (1476), but Jacques permanently lost his rights to the Pays de Vaud
- Jacques died in **1486**, ending Romont's direct association with a Savoy count of its own name

## 9. From Savoy to Fribourg — The Conquest of 1536

- For nearly **three centuries** (1240–1536), Romont was a Savoyard town, and the Counts (later Dukes) of Savoy styled themselves **"Seigneurs de Moudon et Romont"** (Lords of Moudon and Romont)
- In **1536**, Protestant **Bern** conquered the entire **Pays de Vaud** from Savoy
- Romont chose to seek the protection of **Catholic Fribourg** rather than submit to Protestant Bern — a decision that preserved the town's **Catholic faith** and shaped its identity to this day
- From 1536, Fribourg governors (baillis) administered Romont and built the new wing of the castle
- Between **1843 and 1865**, a series of **five fires** destroyed much of the Gothic town centre, which was rebuilt with more modern structures — though the ramparts, towers, castle, and church survived
- Romont remains part of the **canton of Fribourg** and the capital of the **district de la Glâne**, while the nearby town of Moudon — once its twin under Savoy — became Protestant and joined the Canton of Vaud

## 10. L'Abbaye de la Fille-Dieu — A Living Medieval Monastery

- In **1265**, three women — **Juliette, Pernette, and Cécile de Villa** — founded a small house of prayer near Romont
- In **1268**, the Bishop of Lausanne, **Jean de Cossonay**, authorized them to erect a monastery and named it **La Fille-Dieu** (the Daughter of God)
- The first church was consecrated in **1346**; in **1350**, Pope **Clement VI** elevated it to the status of an **abbey**
- The abbey has been **continuously occupied** by nuns for over **750 years** — one of the longest-running monastic communities in Switzerland
- In **1906**, the community joined the **Cistercian Order of the Strict Observance** (Trappists / Ordre cistercien de la stricte observance)
- Between **1995 and 1996**, British artist **Brian Clarke** designed **15 stained glass windows** for the restored abbey church — resolutely contemporary and non-figurative, they were described as "the expression of a life of hope in a convent inhabited continuously since the thirteenth century"
- In **2009**, a hailstorm destroyed Clarke's **oculus window**; he returned to recreate it, calling the destruction "a sign of God" that let him perfect his earlier design
- The abbey is listed as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance** and is a stop on the **Sentier du Vitrail** (Stained Glass Trail)

## 11. The Sentier du Vitrail — A Trail of Light Through the Countryside

- The **Sentier du Vitrail** (Stained Glass Trail) is a **15.7 km loop hike** through the rolling green hills of the Glâne district, starting and ending at the Vitromusée in Romont
- Duration: approximately **4 hours**, rated as easy; also accessible by bicycle or car
- Key stops along the trail:
  - **Chapelle Sainte-Anne** — a small roadside chapel (after about 30 minutes)
  - **Église paroissiale de Mézières** — the parish church of Mézières, with **34 abstract stained glass windows by Yoki**
  - **Chapelle de La Neirigue** — built in **1641**
  - **Chapelle Notre-Dame de l'Épine** in Berlens — a pilgrimage chapel attracting the faithful since the Middle Ages
  - **Abbaye de la Fille-Dieu** — the Cistercian monastery with Brian Clarke's modern windows
- The trail passes through fields, forests, farmhouses with flower displays, and quiet villages — connecting centuries of glass art from medieval to contemporary in one walk
- Romont has branded itself **"Pays du verre et du vitrail"** (Land of Glass and Stained Glass), a unique identity among Swiss towns

## 12. The Market Tradition and Cultural Life

- Romont holds a **weekly market** (marché hebdomadaire) every **Tuesday** at the **Place Saint-Jacques** in the medieval old town, from **7:00 to 11:00**
- The tradition of markets and fairs goes back to the founding era: Pierre II de Savoie deliberately laid out **wide streets** (rues larges) to accommodate markets and great fairs — a feature that defines the town's layout to this day
- The **Bicubic** is Romont's modern cultural centre, hosting exhibitions of painting, photography, graphic arts, and sculpture, as well as concerts and performances
- The **Vitrofestival Romont** celebrates glass art with international artists and events
- The **"20 Heures Musique Romont"** is an annual music festival held each September

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The weeping women's secret** — A child joins the silent Good Friday procession and discovers that the scarlet cushions hold not just the instruments of the Passion, but a hidden story whispered only once a year through the medieval streets
2. **The castle of coloured light** — A child wanders into the Vitromusée at closing time; the stained glass windows come alive, and each panel from a different century tells the story of the person who made it — from a medieval monk to a modern artist
3. **The Little Charlemagne's round hill** — Pierre II de Savoie, the great castle-builder, arrives at the wild hilltop in 1240 with a dream; a child from the village helps him choose the perfect spot for the donjon by finding water for the 40-meter well
4. **The tower that watches** — The Tour à Boyer, 38 meters tall with walls thicker than a room, has stood guard for 770 years; one night, its ancient stones begin to hum, and a child discovers each tower along the ramparts has a different voice
5. **Seven centuries of glass** — A child touches the oldest stained glass window (1348) in the Collégiale and is pulled through time, meeting each artist in turn — the medieval glazier, Alexandre Cingria painting apostles in 1938, Sergio de Castro creating prophets in 1981
6. **The trail of light** — On the Sentier du Vitrail, a child follows a beam of coloured light from chapel to chapel across the green hills, guided by a mysterious glass butterfly that leads them to the secret of the Fille-Dieu nunnery
7. **The count who lost everything** — Jacques de Savoie, proud Count of Romont and commander of Burgundian armies, retreats to his castle after the Battle of Morat; a child must help him decide whether to fight on or save the town from destruction
8. **The market on the wide street** — On a Tuesday morning, a child at the Place Saint-Jacques market discovers that one market stall has been there since 1285, selling something no one else can see — and the seller knows the secret of why the streets are so wide
