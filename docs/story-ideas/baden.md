## 1. Roman Period — Aquae Helveticae

- The thermal springs were first used shortly after **14 AD**, when legionaries from the nearby Roman camp **Vindonissa** (today Windisch, 5 km away) began bathing in the hot springs along the Limmat river
- The Romans called the settlement **Aquae Helveticae** — literally "Waters of the Helvetii"
- Archaeological finds from 2006–2022 in the Bäderquartier reveal an extraordinarily wealthy small-town settlement from the 1st to late 3rd century AD
- The Roman thermal baths rank among the **most significant bath complexes north of the Alps** in terms of size and quality
- Outside the bath district: luxurious villas, workshops, and commercial buildings — evidence of a prosperous spa economy
- Peak prosperity in the **2nd and 3rd centuries AD**
- The settlement was largely abandoned during the **4th century** as Roman power waned
- The thermal springs emerge on both sides of the Limmat at the **"Limmatknie"** (Limmat bend) — the sharp westward turn the river makes in the Klus gorge — at a temperature of **47°C**
- After the Romans, the Alemanni settled the area

## 2. The Burg Stein (Castle Stein)

- Built **before the year 1000** on a 445m hill overlooking the Altstadt
- Together with the Landvogteischloss on the opposite bank, the castle secured the narrow **Klus gorge** between Schlossberg and Lägern — a strategic chokepoint
- Passed through several noble families: **Nellenburger, Lenzburger**, and from the 13th century the **Habsburgs**
- Under Habsburg rule, the castle served as the **administrative center of the Vorlande** (Habsburg territories west of the Arlberg)
- The **Landvogt** (governor) resided here
- Crucially: the castle housed the **Habsburg archive** — the famous **Habsburger Urbar** (a detailed record of all Habsburg possessions and rights), plus numerous legal documents and charters
- **1415: The Eidgenossen conquer Baden.** With encouragement from King Sigismund (who had placed Duke Friedrich IV under imperial ban), the Swiss confederates conquered the Aargau and Baden. They **plundered and destroyed the Stein** after fierce fighting
- What enraged the Habsburgs even more than the physical destruction was the **theft of their archive** — the loss of centuries of legal records documenting their territorial claims
- **1657–1670**: The city of Baden rebuilt the fortress as part of the city fortifications
- **1712: Destroyed again** during the Second Villmergen War (Zweiter Villmergerkrieg) when Reformed cantons defeated the Catholics. The rubble was reused to build the **Reformierte Kirche** (Reformed Church)
- Today: only the **Burgkapelle Sankt-Nikolaus** (chapel) survives intact. The ruins offer panoramic views and are a popular hiking destination

## 3. The Tagsatzung (Federal Diet) — Baden's Golden Age

- After the conquest of 1415, Baden became a **jointly administered subject territory** (Gemeine Herrschaft) of the Swiss cantons
- **From 1421**, the Confederation chose Baden as the regular meeting place of the **Tagsatzung** — the annual multi-week assembly of all 8 (later 13) Swiss cantons, comparable to today's Council of States (Ständerat)
- Why Baden? It was **neutral ground** (no single canton owned it), centrally located, had excellent inns, and the thermal baths offered relaxation between political sessions
- The **Tagsatzung hall** in the second floor of the Rathaus (town hall) was the most important gathering place of Swiss governance for **nearly 300 years**
- Foreign envoys attended — Baden was a hub of **international diplomacy**
- The bath season (Easter to September) offered the perfect cover for negotiations: politics mixed with leisure in a spa setting
- **1526**: The first major **public religious disputation** (Disputation) between Catholics and Reformed took place in Baden. Baden's citizens decided in favor of the **old (Catholic) faith**
- **1712: Catastrophe year.** The Catholics lost the Second Villmergen War. The castle was destroyed. The Catholic cantons refused to ever meet again at the site of their defeat. The Tagsatzung was relocated to **Frauenfeld** — ending almost 300 years of Baden's special status

## 4. The Altstadt (Old Town)

- **1297**: Baden formally received **city rights** under Habsburg rule
- The Habsburgs recognized Baden's strategic importance at the Limmat narrows and expanded the town to **block Zurich's expansion**
- The medieval Altstadt features picturesque lanes with **16th–18th century burgher houses** in late Gothic and Baroque styles
- The **Stadtturm** (city tower, built 1441) is classified as a **cultural property of national significance**
- **Löwenbrunnen** (Lion Fountain): placed in front of the Stadtturm in 1822, carved by stonemason Hieronymus Moser of Würenlos. Current lion figure from 1918 by sculptor Hans Trudel of Baden
- **Sebastianskapelle**: built 1509 as a charnel house (Beinhaus), Baroque interior decoration added in the 17th century
- The historic **Marktplatz** (market square) is the heart of the old town, surrounded by colorful burgher houses

## 5. The Holzbrücke (Wooden Bridge)

- A **covered wooden bridge** crossing the Limmat at the narrowest point of the Klus between Schlossberg and Lägern
- **39 meters long**, connecting the left-bank Altstadt to the Landvogteischloss on the right bank
- First mention of a bridge at this location: **1242**
- At least **5 predecessor bridges** at the same spot since the Middle Ages
- The current bridge dates from **1810**
- At the western end: a life-sized **sandstone statue of St. John of Nepomuk** (bridge saint), installed 1707 by an unknown Zurich sculptor — the **only large Baroque sculpture in Baden**
- The bridge and castle together form one of the most photographed motifs in the Aargau

## 6. The Landvogteischloss (Bailiff's Castle)

- The most historically significant building outside the Altstadt and Bäderquartier
- Sits on the right bank of the Limmat, forming the **eastern bridgehead** of the Holzbrücke
- Counterpart to the Ruine Stein — together they secured the Klus narrows
- Served as the **residence of the Landvogt** (bailiff/governor) of the Grafschaft Baden until **1798**
- Today houses the **Historisches Museum Baden** and the city archive
- Notable governor: **Aegidius Tschudi** (served 1533–1535 and 1549–1551), who was also a famous Swiss chronicler

## 7. The Thermal Baths — 2000 Years of Bathing

- **47°C thermal springs** — among the hottest in Switzerland
- Used continuously for over **2000 years**: Romans → Middle Ages → Modern era
- From the Middle Ages to the early modern period, Baden was the **most important spa town in German-speaking Europe**
- Bathhouses and hotels competed for guests; numerous **prominent visitors** throughout history, including the writer **Hermann Hesse**
- **1847: The Spanisch-Brötli-Bahn** — the first railway line entirely on Swiss soil — connected Zürich to Baden. Built precisely because Zurich's wealthy wanted fast access to the spa. The line fueled Baden's golden age as a fashionable resort
- For common people, visits were driven by medical need. For the nobility, baths had a distinctly **representative character** — a chance for political networking and socializing
- **2021: Fortyseven** — Baden's new thermal spa complex opened after years of construction, reviving the ancient bathing tradition in modern form

## 8. Industrial History — Brown, Boveri & Cie / ABB

- **October 2, 1891**: Charles Eugene Lancelot Brown (born 1863 in Winterthur, son of a British engineer — brilliant designer) and Walter Boveri (born 1865 in Bamberg, Germany — cool-headed project manager) founded **Brown, Boveri & Cie (BBC)** in Baden
- BBC produced: AC and DC motors, generators, steam turbines, transformers
- **February 1892**: First employees began work in the rapidly built factory. That same year, the first machine group of the **ROR power station** was started — supplying electricity to both the town and the factory
- BBC transformed Baden from a sleepy spa town into an **industrial powerhouse**
- **1987**: ASEA (Sweden) and BBC merged to form **ABB (ASEA Brown Boveri)** — today a global leader in power and automation technology
- Baden remains ABB's historical home and an internationally significant center of electrical engineering
- The **Villa Boveri** (built 1900, 125th anniversary in 2025) is a landmark of Baden's industrial heritage

## 9. The Teufelskeller (Devil's Cellar)

- A prehistoric **landslide area** near Baden, formed roughly **100,000 years ago** when a glacier retreated and the steep eastern slope between Baregg and Chrüzliberg lost its support
- A gravel layer (up to **50 meters thick**) gradually slid on soft molasse sediments, breaking into individual blocks and rock towers
- Bizarre **Nagelfluh (conglomerate) rock formations** — some towers rise **20 meters** above the forest floor
- Contains several caves; the **Teufelskeller cave** at 43.7 meters is among the longest in Canton Aargau
- Since 1998: designated as a **natural forest reserve** (Naturwaldreservat)
- **The Sage (Legend)**: The name derives from the legend of the **Chrüzliberg** (Kreuzliberg), a castle site at 514m above the Teufelskeller. The king's daughter of the castle would visit a nearby hill inhabited by spirits. During terrible storms, the spirits devastated the hill. When the princess descended to see the damage, she was **turned to stone by the spirits**. She was eventually rescued by a miraculous image from a nearby monastery and led back to the castle

## 10. The Teufelsbrücke Legend (Devil's Bridge)

- Local legend tells of the **devil offering to build a bridge** over the wild Limmat
- In exchange, he demanded the **soul of the first person to cross**
- The townspeople outwitted him by **sending a goat across first**
- The furious devil, bound by his own bargain, had to accept the goat's soul
- Note: This is the **Baden version** of the famous Swiss Teufelsbrücke legend (the more famous one is at the Schöllenen Gorge on the Gotthard route, but Baden has its own local variant)

## 11. The Limmatklus (Limmat Gorge)

- The Limmat flows through a **200-meter-wide gorge (Klus)** next to the Altstadt
- This gorge formed as the river continuously cut into a rising hill chain during geological folding
- The narrow passage gave Baden its **strategic military importance** — whoever controlled the gorge controlled north-south traffic
- The geography shaped the entire city: castle on one side, Landvogteischloss on the other, bridge in between

## Story Ideas (brief — can be generated on the fly)

1. **Roman spa adventure** — Child discovers underground Roman baths beneath the modern thermal spa, meets a Roman child from Aquae Helveticae
2. **The Tagsatzung of animals** — Animals of the Swiss cantons hold their own parliament in the old Tagsatzung hall, child mediates disputes
3. **The ghost knight of Ruine Stein** — Child releases a Habsburg page trapped since 1415, shows him peaceful modern Baden
4. **The Devil's Bridge trick** — Lighthearted retelling of the Teufelsbrücke legend with child and clever goat outsmarting the devil
5. **The inventor's notebook** — Child finds Brown/Boveri's sketches and builds a magical machine from Baden's landmarks
6. **The enchanted princess of Teufelskeller** — Based on the actual Chrüzliberg legend: child solves riddles to free a petrified princess in the cave system
7. **The Spanisch-Brötli-Bahn race** — Child rides Switzerland's first train from Zurich to Baden in 1847, competing against a horse rider
8. **The bridge saint's secret** — The St. Nepomuk statue on the Holzbrücke whispers a riddle each night that leads to a hidden treasure in the Altstadt
