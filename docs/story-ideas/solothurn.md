## 1. Geography — The Aare and the Jura

- Solothurn lies on the **banks of the Aare** in northwestern Switzerland, at the foot of the **Jura Mountains**, south of Basel
- The city's position at the **junction of river and mountain routes** has been strategically important since Celtic times — **seven railway lines** branch off from Solothurn today, reflecting this enduring role as a transport hub
- The old town sits on the **south bank of the Aare**, with the river forming a natural boundary to the north
- The city's fortifications combined medieval walls with **17th-century bastions** built according to the principles of the French military engineer **Vauban** — some wall sections and the star-fort bastions survive today
- The **Baseltor** (Basel Gate, **1504**) and **Bieltor** (Biel Gate, 13th century) are the main surviving city gates
- Population: approximately **17,000** (German-speaking, Roman Catholic majority)

## 2. Roman Origins — Salodurum, the Water Gate

- Around **20 AD**, the Romans founded a settlement called **Salodurum** at a strategic crossing of the Aare river, on the route between **Aventicum** (Avenches) and **Vindonissa** (Windisch)
- The name derives from the Celtic word meaning **"Water Gate"** — the Celts had already used this river crossing before the Romans arrived
- First written mention: **219 AD**, on the so-called **Eponastein**, inscribed "vico salod[uro]"
- The early wooden settlement was destroyed by fire in the late 1st century and rebuilt in **stone and timber framing**
- Around **330 AD**, the Romans converted Salodurum into a **castrum** (military fortress): **152 meters along the Aare by 117 meters**, with walls **3.2 meters thick**
- Remnants of the Roman fort walls are still visible in the old town today — making Solothurn, alongside Trier and Cologne, one of the **oldest settlements north of the Alps**

## 3. Saints Ursus and Victor — The Theban Martyrs

- Around **303 AD**, soldiers **Ursus and Victor** of the **Theban Legion** — a Roman legion from Upper Egypt composed entirely of Christians — were martyred at Salodurum
- The Theban Legion had been summoned by Emperor **Maximian** to suppress revolts in Gaul. When ordered to persecute fellow Christians, the entire legion refused and was massacred at **Agaunum** (today Saint-Maurice in Valais)
- Ursus and Victor escaped the initial massacre and fled north to Salodurum, where they were captured by the Roman governor **Hirtacus**
- They were **tortured and beheaded** — becoming the city's **patron saints**
- By **400 AD**, a pilgrimage church stood over the grave of St. Ursus, maintained by canons, eventually becoming the site of today's **St. Ursen Cathedral**
- Ursus and Victor are commemorated on **30 September** in the Roman Martyrology
- Crucially for Solothurn's identity: they were said to belong to the **11th** Theban Legion — the earliest link between the city and its magic number

## 4. Medieval Rise — Free Imperial City

- The **Zähringen** dukes controlled the town from **1127** until the dynasty died out in **1218**, when Solothurn became a **free imperial city** under direct rule of the Holy Roman Emperor
- **1038**: Emperor **Conrad II** held court in Solothurn and crowned his son **Henry III** as King of Burgundy — a moment of great prestige for the young city
- **1252**: The guilds that formed the town council elected **11 members** — the first documented civic connection to the number 11
- **1295**: Solothurn formed an alliance with **Bern** — an alliance that would endure for centuries and shape both cities' fates
- **1382**: The Habsburgs attacked Solothurn but were repulsed — the city defended its independence
- **1481**: Solothurn joined the **Swiss Confederation** as the **11th canton**, cementing its bond with the number 11

## 5. The Number 11 — Solothurn's Sacred Obsession

- The attachment to the number 11 is officially recognized as a **"living tradition" of Switzerland** (Lebendige Traditionen)
- The city features **11 churches and chapels**, **11 fountains**, and **11 towers**
- Between **1344 and 1532**, the canton was divided into **11 protectorates** (Vogteien)
- The city historically had **11 trade guilds**
- The **Zeitglockenturm** (Clock Tower) has an **11-hour dial** — conspicuously lacking a 12
- The **Öufi-Bier** (dialect for "11-Beer") is brewed by the local brewery, founded on **11.11.2000**; "Öufi" means 11 in the Solothurn dialect
- There is **11-i Schokolade** (11-Chocolate), **Öufi Yoga**, and an **Öufi Boat** on the Aare
- The Solothurn tourist office lists **11 must-see attractions** and **11 most important events**
- Even the kneelers in St. Ursen Cathedral are arranged in **rows of 11**

## 6. St. Ursen Cathedral (Kathedrale St. Urs und Viktor)

- The present cathedral was built **1762–1773** — exactly **11 years** — designed by the Ticino architect **Gaetano Matteo Pisoni** from Ascona
- It replaced earlier churches on the same site going back to the **5th century** pilgrimage church over St. Ursus's grave
- The cathedral became the **seat of the Bishop of Basel** in **1828** (after the bishopric relocated from Basel following the Reformation)
- **Architectural style**: The facade and tower are **late Baroque**, while the interior is **early Neoclassical** with elegant, restrained forms — built of pale **Solothurn Jura limestone**
- The monumental outdoor staircase has **3 sets of 11 steps = 33 steps**
- The bell tower is **66 meters** tall (6 x 11) and houses **11 bells**, cast by the Kaiser foundry in Solothurn (1764–1768)
- Inside: **11 altars**, all visible simultaneously from a single spot — the **11th black floor stone** in the nave
- The organ pipes are arranged in numbers **divisible by 11**
- The cathedral is considered the most significant **early Neoclassical sacred building** in Switzerland

## 7. The French Ambassador's City (1530–1792)

- From **1530 to 1792**, Solothurn served as the permanent **seat of the French ambassador** to the Swiss Confederation — making it effectively the diplomatic capital of Switzerland
- The ambassador resided in the **Ambassadorenhof**, still standing today
- France maintained this embassy to secure Swiss **mercenary soldiers** — the famous Swiss Guards — and to keep political influence over the Confederation
- The French presence brought enormous wealth, courtly culture, and architectural taste to the small city
- In **1717**, a major fire destroyed parts of the ambassador's residence; it was rebuilt in **1725** by the Vorarlberg architect **Franz Beer**
- The last French ambassador, **Charles-Olivier de Saint-Georges, Marquis de Vérac**, departed in **1792** when the French Revolution ended the monarchy
- This 262-year French presence explains why Solothurn is called the **"Ambassador's City"** (Ambassadorenstadt) and why its architecture blends **Italian grandeur, French elegance, and German practicality**

## 8. Baroque Architecture — "The Most Beautiful Baroque City in Switzerland"

- Solothurn's old town, built largely between **1530 and 1792** under French diplomatic influence, is widely regarded as **Switzerland's finest Baroque ensemble**
- Key Baroque and early modern buildings:
  - **Jesuitenkirche** (Jesuit Church, **1680–1689**): Designed by Jesuit architect **Heinrich Mayer** following Vorarlberg master-builder principles; features stunning **Italian-style stuccowork** with botanical and figurative motifs; the high altar (1704) frames a painting of the Assumption by **Franz Carl Stauder**
  - **Schloss Waldegg** (Waldegg Castle, **1682–1686**): Built as a summer residence for **Johann Viktor von Besenval**, Schultheiss of Solothurn; one of the first **French-style Baroque gardens** in Switzerland
  - **Palais Besenval** (**1703–1706**): Town palace of the Besenval family, an elegant Baroque townhouse
  - **Altes Zeughaus** (Old Arsenal, **early 1600s**): Houses one of Europe's most impressive armor collections — over **300 suits of armor** from the 15th to 17th centuries; the only Swiss arsenal that maintained its original character as a weapons storage facility
- Solothurn remained **Catholic** throughout the Reformation — as a Counter-Reformation stronghold, the **Jesuits** founded a college in the city in **1646**, and the **Capuchins** also established a monastery
- The Catholic identity, combined with French wealth, produced the extraordinary concentration of Baroque sacred and civic architecture

## 9. The Zeitglockenturm (Clock Tower)

- The **oldest building in Solothurn**, built in the early **13th century** (circa **1250**) as part of the castle fortifications
- First documented as a "clock tower" in **1406**
- The remarkable **astronomical clock** was built by **Lorenz Liechti** and **Joachim Habrecht** in **1545** — it displays the day, month, and year
- Every hour, three mechanical figures perform beneath a canopy: a **knight**, a **king**, and a **skeleton** (Death) — classic medieval memento mori symbolism
- A **bell ringer** figure strikes the tower bell with a large hammer
- The clock dial famously shows only **11 hours** instead of 12 — playing the **Solothurner Lied** (the city's unofficial anthem) on 11 chimes
- From the tower, the view stretches eastward along the Hauptgasse to the **Jesuit Church** and the facade of **St. Ursen Cathedral**

## 10. The Verenaschlucht (Verena Gorge) and the Hermitage

- A forested limestone gorge just **north of Solothurn**, one of the city's most beloved walking destinations
- Named after **St. Verena of Zurzach** (born circa **260 AD** in Thebes, Egypt), a Christian holy woman who accompanied the **Theban Legion** to Switzerland
- After the legion's massacre at Agaunum, Verena traveled across the Alps and settled as a **hermit** in a cave in this gorge near Solothurn, devoting herself to prayer and charitable works among the local **Alemanni** tribes
- The **Einsiedelei** (hermitage) dates to the **15th century** and includes a hermit's dwelling and **two chapels** (St. Verena Chapel and St. Martin Chapel)
- Remarkably, following a **centuries-old tradition**, the hermitage is still maintained by a **real hermit** — one of the last active hermitages in Switzerland
- Beyond the hermitage, a trail leads to **Kreuzen** along one of the **oldest Ways of the Cross (Kreuzweg) north of the Alps**
- The gorge trail continues upward and eventually connects to the path to the **Weissenstein**

## 11. The Weissenstein — Solothurn's House Mountain

- Height: **1,395 meters** — the easternmost summit above 1,300 meters in the **Jura Mountains**
- Solothurn's **Hausberg** (house mountain), offering panoramic views from **Säntis to Mont Blanc** on clear days
- Accessible by **gondola lift** from Oberdorf (opened in **2014**, replacing an older chairlift that operated from **1950 to 2009**)
- The **Kurhaus Weissenstein** (spa hotel) has been welcoming guests since the **19th century**
- The Weissenstein is a favorite for rising above the **Mittelland fog** — when the lowlands are shrouded in mist, sunshine awaits on the ridge
- A railway tunnel from **Oberdorf to Gänsbrunnen** (on the Solothurn–Moutier line) was opened in **1908**, passing beneath the mountain

## 12. Notable People and Cultural Legacy

- **Tadeusz Kosciuszko** (1746–1817): The Polish-American military hero — who fought in the **American Revolution** and led the **1794 Polish Uprising** against Russia and Prussia — spent his final years in exile in Solothurn, dying there on **15 October 1817**. A **Kosciuszko Museum** at his last residence preserves his memory
- **Frank Buchser** (1828–1890): Born near Solothurn, a pioneering Swiss painter who traveled to America during the **Civil War era** and painted notable figures; approximately 80 of his paintings are in the **Kunstmuseum Solothurn**
- **Urs Graf** (circa 1485–1528): Renaissance goldsmith, painter, printmaker, and mercenary from Solothurn
- The **Solothurner Filmtage** (Solothurn Film Festival): Founded in **1966** as a conference called "Swiss film today" and formalized in **1967**; held annually in **January**, it is **Switzerland's most important festival for Swiss film productions**, drawing over **60,000 visitors** each year. The "Swiss Panorama" section showcases current films from all four linguistic regions
- The **Kunstmuseum Solothurn** (opened **1902**) holds significant collections of old masters, Swiss landscapes, and contemporary art
- The **Museum Altes Zeughaus** has been a museum since **1907**, when a new arsenal was built and the historic building became redundant

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The city that counts to 11** — A child discovers Solothurn's clock only goes to 11 and must solve 11 riddles hidden in 11 fountains, churches, and towers before the 11th bell rings
2. **The hermit of the Verenaschlucht** — St. Verena appears in the gorge and guides a lost child through the forest to the hermitage, teaching kindness to strangers along the way
3. **The 11th step** — A child climbing the cathedral staircase finds that the 11th step of each section opens a portal to a different era: Roman Salodurum, the medieval guilds, the Baroque ambassador's court
4. **The ambassador's secret letter** — In 1780, a child in the Ambassadorenhof discovers a coded letter from the French ambassador and must deliver it across the old town before midnight — passing every Baroque landmark
5. **The skeleton, the knight, and the king** — The three clock tower figures come alive at the 11th hour and take a child on a tour through Solothurn's history, each showing their own century
6. **Kosciuszko's last journey** — The old Polish hero befriends a Solothurn child and tells stories of the American Revolution and the fight for freedom, seen through the child's wonder
7. **The 11th bell** — One of the cathedral's 11 bells goes missing; a child must search through 11 Solothurn landmarks to find it before the next Sunday service
8. **Above the fog** — A child climbs the Weissenstein on a grey, foggy day and discovers a magical world of sunshine above the clouds, where the entire Alpine chain tells stories of Swiss history
