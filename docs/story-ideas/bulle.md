## 1. Geography — Gateway to the Fribourg Pre-Alps

- Bulle sits at an elevation of **770 meters** on a hill above the left bank of the **Trême** river, at the foot of the **Fribourg Pre-Alps** (Préalpes fribourgeoises)
- It is the **capital of the Gruyère district** (district de la Gruyère) and the **second-largest town** in the canton of Fribourg
- The town lies at a natural **crossroads** connecting the routes to Fribourg, Vevey, Romont, the Vallée de la Jogne, and the Pays-d'Enhaut — this strategic position as a trading hub shaped its entire history
- The **Sarine** (German: Saane) river flows through the broader region; the **Lac de la Gruyère** (Lake of Gruyère), an artificial reservoir formed by the **Rossens Dam** (completed **1948**, 83 m high), lies between Bulle and Fribourg with a surface area of nearly **10 km²**
- The dominant peak visible from Bulle is **Le Moléson** (2,002 m), the emblematic mountain of the Gruyère region — accessible by funicular from Moléson-sur-Gruyères to **Plan-Francey** (1,520 m) and then by aerial cableway to the summit, where a **360-degree panorama** reveals the Bernese Alps, Lake Geneva, Mont Blanc, and the Jura
- Population: approximately **1,000** inhabitants in 1722, **4,000** in 1912, and over **23,000** today (after the **2006 merger** with the neighboring commune of La Tour-de-Trême)

## 2. Early History — Under the Bishop of Lausanne

- The earliest written mention dates to **855**, when the settlement appears as **Butulum** in documents; later forms include **Bollo** (1200) and **Bullo**
- Bulle probably came under the secular authority of the **Bishop of Lausanne** (évêque de Lausanne) as early as the **6th century**, and together with Avenches and Curtilles formed the original temporal territory of the bishopric
- A church dedicated to **Saint Eusebius** was built in Bulle in the **6th or 7th century** by the Bishop of Lausanne — the oldest known reference to a church here dates to **850**
- Starting at the end of the **12th century**, individual freedoms and privileges were conferred on the citizens of Bulle; the townspeople enjoyed rights comparable to those of the citizens of Lausanne
- The bishop's feudal administration included a **lord** (châtelain) resident at the castle who maintained a court of law and collected taxes, and a **mayor** (maire) who handled day-to-day governance

## 3. The Château de Bulle — The Bishop's Fortress

- Construction of the **Château de Bulle** (Baillival Castle) began in **1291** under Bishop **Guillaume de Champvent** (c. 1239–1301), who was elected Bishop of Lausanne in **1273** and pursued an aggressive policy of defending the church's territorial rights against Savoy and the Counts of Gruyère
- The castle took **forty years** to complete, finished in **1331**
- The **donjon** (main keep) rises **33 meters** (108 feet) — the most imposing feature of the structure
- The castle follows a **carré savoyard** (Savoyard square) plan: a central courtyard surrounded by buildings, with the keep at the southwest corner and three smaller **échauguettes** (pepper-pot turrets) at the other corners
- Its purpose was defensive: to protect Bulle against the rival claims of the **Counts of Gruyère** (vassals of Savoy), the **Counts of Savoy** based at Châtel-Saint-Denis, and **Louis de Savoie** at Romont
- In **1536**, as the Bernese conquered the Vaud and the Reformation swept through, Bishop **Sébastien de Montfalcon** fled Lausanne and placed himself and Bulle under the protection of Catholic Fribourg — but protection became domination, and from **1537** a Fribourg-appointed **bailli** (bailiff) governed from the castle
- From **1763 to 1768**, the castle was remodeled as the bailiff's residence; in **1854**, prisons were installed in the south wing
- Today the castle houses the **préfecture** (district prefecture) — it has retained its administrative vocation for over **seven centuries**

## 4. The Place du Marché — A Thousand-Year Market Tradition

- Bulle's **Place du Marché** (market square) lies between the Château and the Church of Saint-Pierre-aux-Liens — it has hosted weekly markets for over **a thousand years**, making it one of the longest continuous market traditions in Switzerland
- The town's position at the meeting point of several valleys and trading routes made it a natural **cattle and cheese trading hub** from the 12th century onward
- By the **1880s**, the October cattle fairs at Bulle attracted **3,000 to 4,000 head** of cattle — an enormous gathering that underscored the town's role as the commercial heart of the dairy region
- Today, every **Thursday** up to **150 traders** set up on the market square; in July and August the **Marché Folklorique** (folk market) welcomes up to **250 exhibitors** with regional cheese, honey, pastries, handicrafts, Swiss folk music, and choral performances
- The market tradition survived even the catastrophic fire of 1805 and was central to the town's economic recovery

## 5. The Great Fire of 1805 — Destruction and Rebirth

- On **2 April 1805**, an enormous fire (le grand incendie) swept through Bulle and **destroyed almost the entire town in a few hours** — more than a thousand inhabitants were ruined and most public buildings were lost
- It remains the **greatest fire in the history of the canton of Fribourg**
- An outpouring of solidarity followed from across Switzerland: the cantons of **Zurich, Vaud, Bern, Neuchâtel, Valais, and Geneva** contributed to the relief effort, along with Swiss residents of Paris
- The **reconstruction lasted half a century** and transformed Bulle's appearance: the new town featured **wide, straight streets** (rues larges) and a **new central square**, with new neighborhoods built along the main roads to give the town a distinctive **star shape** (forme étoilée)
- The architecture of the rebuilt town is characterized by **unified neoclassical facades** — a functional, elegant style that gives Bulle a remarkably coherent appearance unlike most medieval Swiss towns
- The **granary** was rebuilt first; the **town hall** (Hôtel de Ville) was completed in **1808**; the **church** was consecrated in **1816**
- A direct consequence of the Bulle fire was the creation of the **cantonal building fire insurance** (assurance immobilière cantonale) — the disaster prompted Fribourg to institute systematic fire protection

## 6. The Church of Saint-Pierre-aux-Liens (1816)

- The oldest references to a church in Bulle date to **850**, though the original construction likely dates to the **6th century**
- The church was rebuilt or converted several times, notably in **1750–1751**
- The fire of **1805** left only a few walls standing; reconstruction began almost immediately
- The **freestone** for the new bell tower was quarried at **Grandvillard** during the winter of 1806; construction of the tower began in September **1808** and lasted two years; **fifteen lotteries** were organized to raise funds
- The rebuilt church, in **neo-baroque style** with **neoclassical stucco** interiors, was consecrated on **22 September 1816**
- The **great organ** is the work of **Aloys Mooser** of Fribourg (the same builder who created the celebrated organ in Fribourg's Cathédrale Saint-Nicolas); installed around **1814** with **28 stops on two manuals and pedals**
- Mooser added an innovative **Echo keyboard** (clavier d'écho) whose pipes speak from the **narthex**, creating a striking effect of spatial distance — this arrangement was widely imitated, notably by the Callinet brothers in Alsace
- The organ was thoroughly **restored by Hans Füglister in 1976**, undoing modifications made in 1932 and 1946–48
- Two **cannons** purchased from the State of Fribourg were melted down to cast the church's **six bells**

## 7. Pierre-Nicolas Chenaux — The Gruérien Rebel

- **Pierre-Nicolas Chenaux** (1740–1781), born in nearby **La Tour-de-Trême**, led the most significant popular revolt in the history of the canton of Fribourg
- Chenaux opposed the **patrician oligarchy** of Fribourg, which had revoked religious festivals, abolished the convent of **La Valsainte**, and planned new taxes; he promised his followers debt forgiveness and land reform
- On **29 April 1781**, Chenaux assembled a force of some **2,500 men** at the **Auberge de l'Épée Couronnée** (Inn of the Crowned Sword, today the Hôtel du Cheval Blanc) in Bulle and marched on Fribourg
- The city of Fribourg called on **Bern** for military aid; Bernese cavalry and artillery routed the insurgents
- Chenaux was captured and, on **5 May 1781**, executed by beheading; his body was dismembered under the **Lex Carolina**
- His **skull** is preserved in an urn at the **Musée gruérien** in Bulle
- On **24 September 1933**, a bronze statue by sculptor **Carl Angst** was erected in the center of Bulle, facing the castle — Chenaux is honored as a martyr of democratic ideals, a precursor of the liberal movements that would transform Switzerland in the 19th century

## 8. Gruyère Cheese and the Dairy Tradition

- The production of cheese in the Gruyère region can be traced to **1115** — medieval chronicles praise the expertise of the inhabitants who turned Alpine milk into full-fat cheese exported to **France and Italy**
- The name "Gruyère" became officially recognized in the **17th century**, coinciding with a boom in exports and the first measures to protect the cheese's origin through marking
- The term entered the **Académie française** dictionary in **1762**
- Gruyère received **AOC** (Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée) status in **2001** and **AOP** (Appellation d'Origine Protégée) at the European level in **2011**
- The cows that produce milk for Gruyère AOP are fed exclusively on **natural forage** — fresh grass in summer, hay in winter — with no silage or additives permitted
- The cheese is matured in **caves d'affinage** (maturing cellars) for between **5 and 18 months**
- Bulle, as the capital of the Gruyère district, has been the **commercial center** of this cheese trade for centuries — the cattle fairs and Thursday markets were the primary venues for buying and selling the region's dairy products
- The nearby **Maison du Gruyère** cheese dairy at Pringy (between Bulle and Gruyères) offers demonstrations of traditional cheesemaking

## 9. The Musée Gruérien — Museum of Gruyère Culture

- Founded in **1917** thanks to the generosity of writer and journalist **Victor Tissot**, who left his estate to the town of Bulle with the express wish that it fund a museum and library
- The museum took up residence in **1923** in the **Hôtel Moderne**; it moved to a purpose-built building in **1978**, extended in **2001**, and renovated in **2012**
- The collection comprises over **25,000 objects**: prints, drawings, paintings, sculpture, textiles, traditional tools, woodwork, ceramics, furniture, and photographs
- The **1,000 m² permanent exhibition** (titled "La Gruyère — itinéraires et empreintes") presents the history, art, culture, and traditions of the region through authentic artifacts, models, reconstructions, and audiovisual material
- Star exhibits include **poya paintings** (montée à l'alpage), decorated **cowbells** (sonnailles/cloches), carved **cream spoons** (cuillères à crème), embroidered leather collars, butter molds, and traditional costumes
- The skull of **Pierre-Nicolas Chenaux** is preserved in the museum
- The museum closed for major refurbishment in **2025** and is due to reopen at the end of **2027**

## 10. Poya Paintings — Art of the Alpine Cattle Drive

- In the dialect of Fribourg, the word **poya** describes the ritual **montée à l'alpage** — the seasonal driving of cattle up to the high mountain pastures (inalpe) at the start of summer; the return in autumn is called the **désalpe**
- Since the **1960s**, the term has been applied specifically to the **paintings** depicting this tradition, though poyas first appeared on farmhouse facades in the **early 19th century**
- **Sylvestre Pidoux** (1800–1871), born in **Vuadens** near Bulle, is considered the **first poya painter**; his compositions from around **1850** became the enduring template for the genre
- A poya shows a **procession of cattle** winding up a mountain trail, accompanied by herders in tall hats, other farm animals, and elements of Alpine life — originally, each painting served as an **inventory** of the farmer's herd and equipment, displayed on the front wall as a sign of prosperity
- Later notable artists include **Emile Bussard**, **Henri Ecoffey**, **Simon Pasquier**, and **Joseph Dupasquier** (active 1906–1976)
- Approximately **800 poyas** have been counted in the Gruyère region, with about **fifteen active painters** still creating them today
- The **Désalpe de Charmey**, held annually in a village near Bulle, is one of the most celebrated events in the region: over **200 decorated cows** parade through the streets with flower headdresses and clanging bells, accompanied by traditional music and a vibrant market

## 11. Bénichon (Kilbi) — The Harvest Thanksgiving of Fribourg

- The **Bénichon** (from Latin *benedictio*, "blessing"; German: **Kilbi**) is a centuries-old harvest festival unique to the canton of Fribourg — for many Fribourgeois, it is **more important than Christmas**
- Originally a **religious celebration** marking the anniversary of the local church's consecration, it evolved into a folk festival celebrating the **end of the harvest** and the **return of cattle from the Alpine pastures**
- In the lowlands (including Bulle), the Bénichon falls on the **second Sunday of September**; in the mountain villages, the **second Sunday of October**
- The traditional **menu de Bénichon** is a legendary multi-course feast lasting **six hours or more**:
  - **Cuchaule**: a golden saffron brioche bread — the "queen of the Bénichon"
  - **Moutarde de Bénichon**: a sweet-spicy spread made with mustard powder, white wine, vin cuit (thick pear syrup), cinnamon, and star anise — not a conventional mustard at all
  - **Bouillon** and **pot-au-feu** with boiled beef
  - **Ragoût d'agneau** (lamb stew) with raisins, smoked ham, sausages, and beans
  - **Poires à Botzi**: small round pears (a Fribourg AOP variety) poached in spiced wine syrup with cinnamon and star anise
  - **Bricelets**: thin, hand-rolled wafer cookies cooked on decorated iron plates
  - **Meringues** with **double cream** (crème de la Gruyère)
- According to tradition: you eat and drink for six hours straight — and if you can still stand, you **dance**

## 12. Gruyères — The Castle Town Nearby

- Just **5 km** south of Bulle, the medieval hilltop town of **Gruyères** (with a final "s") is one of the most visited sites in Switzerland
- The **Château de Gruyères**, built between **1270 and 1282** in the **carré savoyard** (Savoyard square) plan, was the seat of the **Counts of Gruyère** — **nineteen counts** ruled from here between the **11th and 16th centuries**
- The last count, **Michel de Gruyère**, faced lifelong financial difficulties and was declared **bankrupt in 1554**; his creditors, the cantons of **Fribourg and Bern**, divided his earldom
- From **1555 to 1798**, Fribourg installed bailiffs at Gruyères castle; in **1848**, the prefecture moved to **Bulle**, and the castle was auctioned
- In **1849**, the **Bovy brothers** from Geneva purchased the castle and established an **artists' colony**, hosting painters such as **Jean-Baptiste Camille Corot** and **Barthélemy Menn**
- The canton of Fribourg repurchased the castle in **1938** and opened it as a **museum in 1939**
- The town of Gruyères itself, with its single cobbled main street, medieval facades, and views of the Pre-Alps, is a perfectly preserved example of a fortified market town — its story is inseparable from that of Bulle, which took over as the district's administrative and commercial center

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The fire that made a star** — A child watches Bulle burn on the night of 2 April 1805; from the ashes, a mysterious architect appears with a star-shaped plan, and together they walk the wide new streets, each one pointing toward a different valley and a different story of rebuilding
2. **The bishop's castle and the count's castle** — Two rival castles (Bulle and Gruyères) argue across the valley about who truly rules La Gruyère; a child must carry a wheel of Gruyère cheese from one to the other to broker peace between the bishop's keep and the count's tower
3. **The poya that came alive** — A child touches a poya painting on a farmhouse wall near Bulle, and the painted cows step off the facade; the child must join the procession and lead the herd up to the alpage before the last bell stops ringing at sunset
4. **Six hours at the Bénichon table** — At a magical Bénichon feast, each course transports the child to a different scene: the saffron cuchaule takes them to a golden wheat field, the poires à Botzi to an autumn orchard, the meringue to a snow-covered alp — they must taste every dish to find the way home
5. **Chenaux's march** — The bronze statue of Pierre-Nicolas Chenaux comes alive one night and asks a child to help him finish the march to Fribourg he never completed; together they walk through two centuries of Gruyère history, learning why the rebel's dream of freedom eventually came true
6. **The organ that echoes from the narthex** — A child sneaks into the Church of Saint-Pierre-aux-Liens at night and touches a key of the Mooser organ; the Echo keyboard answers from across the nave, and two ghostly voices — the organ builder and the bell caster who melted cannons — tell the story of a town rebuilding itself from silence
7. **The summit of Le Moléson** — A child rides the funicular and cable car to the 2,002-meter summit, where the 360-degree panorama is alive: each mountain range tells a story — the Bernese Alps whisper about glaciers, Mont Blanc about adventurers, the Jura about ancient seas — and the child must remember all the stories before the clouds roll in
8. **The last count's cheese** — Count Michel de Gruyère, bankrupt and desperate in 1554, hides one final treasure: not gold, but the secret recipe for the perfect wheel of Gruyère; a child must follow clues from the castle cellars to the caves d'affinage to find it before the bailiffs from Fribourg arrive
