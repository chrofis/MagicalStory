## 1. Origins — The Holy Cross and the Name "Kreuzlingen"

- **Saint Conrad**, Bishop of Constance from **935 to 976**, made three pilgrimages to Jerusalem during his lifetime
- He brought back a fragment of the **True Cross** (Kreuzreliquie) and presented it to a hospital he had founded in the suburb of **Stadelhofen**, just outside the walls of Konstanz
- The hospital became known as **"Crucelin"** — derived from the Latin *Crucis Lignum* ("Wood of the Cross"), which later evolved into **Crucelingen** and finally **Kreuzlingen**
- The city's name thus literally means **"little cross"** — a direct reference to this 10th-century relic
- In **1093**, the hospital was burned down during hostilities between the Bishop of Constance and the Abbot of St. Gall
- **Bishop Ulrich I of Constance** restored the derelict hospital in about **1125** by founding a house of **Augustinian Canons** dedicated to Saints Ulrich and Afra — the Kreuzlingen Abbey
- In **1144**, Pope Lucius II took the monastery under papal protection; in **1145**, Emperor Frederick Barbarossa granted it imperial protection, making it an **Imperial Abbey**

## 2. The Twin City — Kreuzlingen and Konstanz

- Kreuzlingen (Switzerland) and Konstanz (Germany) are so intertwined that they function as **one city divided by a national border** — a situation unique in this form in Europe
- The **Swiss-German border** runs directly through shared streets, parks, and neighbourhoods; for centuries, the boundary was barely noticeable to residents
- Together, the two cities form the **largest conurbation on Lake Constance**, with a combined population of nearly **120,000**
- Historically, Kreuzlingen grew in the orbit of the much older city of Konstanz (Roman *Constantia*, founded 1st century AD), developing around its monastery and the farmland outside Konstanz's walls
- Cross-border trade was central to daily life: Swiss residents bought **tools, household goods, and clothing** in Konstanz, while Germans crossed to buy **bread, flour, sugar, coffee, and cigars** in Kreuzlingen
- In **2006**, city leaders symbolically cut through the old border fence at Klein Venedig
- In **2007**, artist **Johannes Dörflinger** installed the **Kunstgrenze** (Art Border): **22 stainless-steel sculptures**, each **8 metres tall**, painted in hummingbird red, running 300 metres across the Klein Venedig lakefront — they represent tarot trump cards such as "Magician", "Love", "Wheel of Fortune", and "Death and Rebirth"
- In **2008**, Switzerland joined the **Schengen Area**, and the border faded further into irrelevance — until 2020

## 3. The COVID Border Fence (2020) — The Dramatic Separation

- In **mid-March 2020**, German authorities erected a **metal fence** across the shared lakeside green between Kreuzlingen and Konstanz as part of COVID-19 border closures
- Weeks later, Swiss officials added a **second fence** after residents were seen **passing beers, playing cards, and kissing through the chain links** — violating the two-metre distancing rule
- The **double barrier** followed the exact route of **barbed wire that had separated the two countries during World War II**
- The images became **world-famous**: couples pressed against chain-link fencing, families organizing picnics on opposite sides, posters of protest appearing on the wire
- One widely reported story: **Josephine from Arbon (Swiss) and Josef from Singen (German)**, a couple for 33 years, could only meet three times a week at the fence
- On **15 May 2020**, the double fence was finally **dismantled**; on **16 May**, cross-border couples were officially allowed to reunite; full border reopening followed on **15 June 2020**
- The episode became a powerful symbol of how borders that had seemed invisible could suddenly, dramatically reappear

## 4. The Augustinian Monastery (Kloster Kreuzlingen) and the Basilica

- The monastery, founded **c. 1125** by Bishop Ulrich I, was one of the **earliest houses of Augustinian Canons** in the region
- During the **Thirty Years' War** (1618–1648), the monastery was **burned down in 1633** — the people of Konstanz blamed the monks for having supported the invading Swedish forces who had besieged Konstanz via Stein am Rhein
- When it was rebuilt, the new location was deliberately placed **"not less than the distance of a cannon shot"** from Konstanz's walls — a defensive precaution
- The **foundation stone** of the new buildings was laid on **4 July 1650**; the church of Saints Ulrich and Afra was consecrated on **25 October 1653**, built by architect **Michael Beer** of Vorarlberg
- The **Ölbergkapelle** (Chapel of the Mount of Olives), constructed around **1720–1730**, contains approximately **250 hand-carved statuettes** of Swiss stone pine wood (*Arvenholz*), each about 30 cm tall, depicting scenes from Christ's Passion — a masterpiece of Baroque religious art
- The relic of the **True Cross** brought by Bishop Conrad in the 10th century is still preserved in the high altar
- In **1848**, the Canton of Thurgau dissolved the monastery and seized its property during the wave of secularisation following the Sonderbund War
- The church was later elevated to a **Basilica minor** — it remains one of the most important Baroque churches in eastern Switzerland

## 5. World War II — The Lights That Saved Konstanz

- During WWII, while German cities enforced **blackouts**, Konstanz made the extraordinary decision to **keep its lights on at night**
- The idea came from neighbouring **Kreuzlingen**, which — as part of neutral Switzerland — kept its lights burning normally
- From the air, Allied bombers **could not distinguish Konstanz from Kreuzlingen**: the two cities appeared as one continuous settlement, and pilots assumed they were looking at Swiss territory
- Konstanz was therefore **never bombed**, despite manufacturing **radar parts for German submarines**, housing a **flying torpedo development facility** (Schwarzwald Flugzeugbau), and receiving relocated **Dornier aircraft production** from bombed Friedrichshafen
- This makes Konstanz one of the **few German cities to retain its original medieval architecture** — ironically preserved by its proximity to Switzerland
- For Kreuzlingen, the war years brought a different burden: a **border fence with barbed wire** was erected in **1939** between the customs posts, physically separating the twin cities for the first time
- Switzerland's refugee policy was deeply controversial: during the war, approximately **300,000 people** crossed into Switzerland, but an estimated **24,500 mainly Jewish civilians were turned away** at the border

## 6. The Seeburg — Castle and Lakeside Park

- The site's origins trace to **1598**, when **Jakob Atzenholz-Zollikofer** from Konstanz built a small manor called **Neuhorn** as a summer residence for the abbots of the Augustinian monastery
- The manor was **destroyed by fire in 1633** (the same conflagration as the monastery during the Thirty Years' War) and rebuilt in **1664** by Abbot Augustin I. Gimmi
- From **1833**, the building housed the **Thurgau teacher training seminary**, with **Johann Jacob Wehrli** as its first director
- After the monastery's dissolution in **1848**, ownership passed to the Canton of Thurgau; the seminary moved in **1850**, and the property was sold in **1852**
- In **1870**, architect **Ernst Jung** from Winterthur redesigned the building into a **historicist castle** — the Neo-Gothic/Neo-Renaissance structure visible today — for owner **Gottfried Ferdinand Ammann**
- Between **1894 and 1895**, Ammann's son **August Ammann** had the surrounding park professionally landscaped
- In **1958**, a narrow public vote resulted in the city of Kreuzlingen acquiring the property
- Today, the **Seeburgpark** is one of the most beloved public parks on Lake Constance, and since **2016**, Schloss Seeburg operates as a restaurant and event venue

## 7. Lake Constance (Bodensee) — The Shared Lake

- Lake Constance is Central Europe's **third-largest lake** (after Balaton and Geneva), with an area of **541 km²**
- It is shared by **three countries**: Germany (55% of shoreline), Switzerland (34%), and Austria (11%)
- The lake is approximately **65 km long**, up to **13 km wide**, with a maximum depth of **252 metres**
- It was formed by the **Rhine Glacier** during the Ice Age and consists of three parts: **Obersee** (Upper Lake), **Untersee** (Lower Lake), and the connecting **Seerhein**
- Kreuzlingen sits on the **southern shore of the Obersee**, directly where the Seerhein begins to flow toward the Untersee
- The lake acts as a **natural heat regulator**, creating a mild microclimate that historically supported vineyards and orchards on its shores
- Every August, the **Fantastical** (Kreuzlinger Seenachtfest) features the **largest fireworks display on Lake Constance** — a half-hour spectacle over the water with pyrotechnicians from both Germany and Switzerland

## 8. The Seemuseum — Maritime History on the Lake

- Housed in a former **granary of the Augustinian monastery**, built around **1680–1717** — originally used as a grain store, wine cellar, and wine press
- Since **1993**, the building has served as the **Seemuseum** (Lake Museum), the only museum of shipping and fishing in eastern Switzerland and southwestern Germany
- The museum covers **1,500 m²** across four floors, with permanent exhibitions on Lake Constance fishing and shipping
- Star exhibit: artefacts from the **steamship Jura**, a wooden paddle steamer that sank on **12 February 1864** after a collision with the *Stadt Zürich* off Münsterlingen in dense fog — the ship went down in **just four minutes**
- The wreck lay forgotten until **1953**, when diver **Ludwig Hain**, searching for a crashed WWII aircraft, stumbled upon it at a depth of **39 metres** — it is now called the **"best-known freshwater wreck in Europe"**
- The museum also displays approximately **50 models of Lake Constance ships** and original sailing and fishing boats

## 9. Schloss Girsberg — Count Zeppelin's Childhood Home

- **Schloss Girsberg** (Girsberg Castle) in Kreuzlingen was the childhood home of **Count Ferdinand von Zeppelin** (1838–1917), the inventor of the rigid airship
- Zeppelin was born in **Konstanz** in **1838**; his mother was the daughter of a wealthy Swiss merchant who gave the family the **Girsberg manor** across the border in Kreuzlingen
- Young Ferdinand grew up at Girsberg with his siblings, educated by **private tutors**, before entering the military academy at Ludwigsburg
- On **2 July 1900**, Zeppelin made the **first flight of his rigid airship LZ 1** — directly over Lake Constance, the lake of his childhood
- Today, the castle houses the **Puppenmuseum** (Doll Museum) on the first floor of its culture barn: **500 exhibits** of rare dolls and toys from around the world
- A special section preserves **memorabilia of Count Zeppelin**, including the original study furniture from Girsberg and personal objects from the family's possessions

## 10. The Bodensee-Radweg — Cycling Around the Lake

- The **Bodensee-Radweg** is a **260 km** cycling route circling all of Lake Constance through three countries: Germany, Switzerland, and Austria
- The route officially starts in **Kreuzlingen** and is divided into stages; **Stage 6** runs from Kreuzlingen to **Stein am Rhein** along the scenic Untersee
- The path is mostly **flat**, well-marked with black-white-blue signs, and largely separated from motor traffic — rated easy for families and beginners
- Most cyclists complete the full circuit in **4 to 7 days**, depending on pace and sightseeing
- The route passes through vineyards, medieval towns, orchards, and lakeside parks — offering views of the Alps on clear days

## 11. Wine Growing — The Lost Vineyards of Kreuzlingen

- Vineyards on the Swiss shores of Lake Constance were first documented in **850 AD**
- For nearly **half a millennium**, the landscape around Kreuzlingen was dominated by **grape vines** — an almost continuous belt of vineyards stretched from **Bottighofen** through **Kurzrickenbach** and **Egelshofen** to **Emmishofen**
- Around **1850**, the vineyard area reached its **greatest extent**; the owners included local manors, monasteries from Germany, and the **Augustinian Abbey of Kreuzlingen**
- By the **late 19th century**, viticulture in Kreuzlingen **collapsed** due to vine diseases and pests (including phylloxera)
- Today, only street and house names recall the era: **Weinstrasse**, **Weinberg**, **Torggel** (dialect for wine press)
- Recently, **two new small vineyards** have been planted in Kreuzlingen, reviving the ancient tradition
- The wider Thurgau region is famous as the birthplace of the **Müller-Thurgau** grape variety, created by **Professor Hermann Müller** from nearby **Tägerwilen**, who crossed Riesling with other varieties — it became one of the most widely planted white grapes in Germany and Central Europe

## 12. Municipal History — Three Villages Become a City

- Kreuzlingen's modern form emerged from the merger of three separate municipalities: **Kreuzlingen-Egelshofen** with **Kurzrickenbach** (1927) and **Emmishofen** (1928)
- In **1874**, the municipality of Egelshofen was renamed Kreuzlingen and became the **capital of the district**, replacing Gottlieben
- In **1870**, the three municipalities together had only **2,821 inhabitants**; by **1910**, the combined population had reached **8,155**
- In **1947**, Kreuzlingen crossed the **10,000-resident threshold**, earning official town (*Stadt*) status by Swiss convention
- Today, with approximately **22,000 inhabitants**, Kreuzlingen is the **largest Swiss city on Lake Constance** and the second-largest city in the Canton of Thurgau
- The Bernrain Chapel (**Wallfahrtskapelle Heiligkreuz Bernrain**), dating from the **14th century**, is one of the oldest pilgrimage chapels in Thurgau

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The little cross that named a city** — Bishop Conrad returns from Jerusalem with a fragment of the True Cross; a child in Stadelhofen witnesses how the hospital becomes "Crucelin" and a whole city is born from one small relic
2. **The fence that split a friendship** — Two best friends, one in Kreuzlingen, one in Konstanz, suddenly find a metal fence between them in March 2020; they find creative ways to stay connected until the fence comes down in May
3. **The night Konstanz kept its lights on** — A child in wartime Kreuzlingen watches as the neighbouring German city makes the daring decision to keep its lights burning, copying Switzerland, and wonders whether the Allied planes overhead will notice
4. **The sunken steamship** — A child dives into Lake Constance and discovers the wreck of the Jura, sunk in four minutes in 1864; the ship's ghost crew tells the story of the foggy collision
5. **Count Zeppelin's first dream** — Young Ferdinand at Girsberg Castle watches birds over Lake Constance and sketches his first flying machine; decades later, his airship floats over the very same lake
6. **The 250 wooden figures** — A child sneaks into the Ölbergkapelle at night and discovers that the 250 tiny carved statuettes come alive, re-enacting scenes from centuries past
7. **The art border treasure hunt** — A child follows the 22 red tarot sculptures along the Kunstgrenze between Kreuzlingen and Konstanz; each sculpture reveals a clue about the twin cities' shared history
8. **The last vineyard of Kreuzlingen** — A child in the 1880s tries to save the family vineyard from a mysterious vine disease; they learn about the centuries-old tradition before the vineyards disappear, leaving only street names behind
