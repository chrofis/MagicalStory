## 1. Geography — The Valley of 72 Waterfalls

- The **Lauterbrunnen Valley** is one of the deepest U-shaped glacial valleys in the Alpine chain, carved by massive glaciers during the last Ice Age roughly **25,000 years ago**
- Ice masses over **1,000 metres thick** flowed from the Jungfrau massif, grinding the original V-shaped valley into a flat-floored trough with sheer vertical cliff walls rising **300+ metres** on both sides
- The valley sits at **795 metres** above sea level in the **Bernese Oberland**, between Interlaken and the main crest of the Bernese Alps
- **72 waterfalls** cascade over the cliff edges into the valley, fed by hanging valleys that were left perched above when the main glacier gouged deeper than the tributary glaciers
- The valley floor is roughly **one kilometre wide**; the towering walls of Jurassic sedimentary rock create a dramatic corridor framed by some of the highest peaks in the Alps
- The municipality of Lauterbrunnen encompasses **164.5 km²** and comprises six villages: **Lauterbrunnen**, **Wengen**, **Murren**, **Gimmelwald**, **Stechelberg**, and **Isenfluh** (population approximately **2,330** as of 2024)
- The rear Lauterbrunnen Valley is part of the **Swiss Alps Jungfrau-Aletsch UNESCO World Heritage Site**, inscribed in **2001** — the first natural heritage site in the Alpine region

## 2. The Name — "Valley of Clear Springs"

- First documented in **1240** in a Latin letter as **"in claro fonte"** — meaning "by the clear spring(s)" or "by the bright spring(s)"
- By **1253**, the German form **Liuterbrunnon** appears; an alternate spelling **Luterbrunnen** is recorded by **1268**
- The name consists of two Middle High German elements: **lauter** (clear, bright, pure) and **Brunnen** (spring, fountain)
- A popular folk etymology translates lauter as "nothing but" in its modern German sense, yielding "nothing but springs" — a fitting description of a valley with 72 waterfalls
- Linguists note that the earlier Romance-language form "claro fonte" supports the meaning **"clear spring"** rather than "many springs," reflecting the transition from the region's earlier Romance-speaking population to Middle High German during the 13th century

## 3. Medieval History — Walser Settlers and the Monastery

- When the valley first entered the historical record in the **13th century**, it was owned by the **Freiherr (Baron) of Wadenswil**
- In **1240**, the Freiherr sold the **Sefinen Valley** to **Interlaken Monastery**, the powerful Augustinian house that controlled much of the Bernese Oberland
- Around **1300**, the **Lord of Turn** began settling **Walser-speaking people** — migrants from the **Lotschental** in the Valais — in the highlands of the Lauterbrunnen Valley
- The Walser were experienced Alpine settlers, accustomed to farming at high altitudes and establishing independent mountain communities
- By **1346**, the Walser villages of **Lauterbrunnen**, **Gimmelwald**, **Murren**, **Sichellauenen**, and **Trachsellauenen** had all established village governments with a degree of independence from the monastery
- In **1349**, much of the Bernese Oberland rose up against the monastery in an unsuccessful rebellion; the Walser villages bore the brunt of the monastery's retribution
- Between **1487 and 1488**, the villagers built a **filial church** of the parish of Gsteig bei Interlaken; in **1506**, a full-time priest was appointed for Lauterbrunnen
- In **1528**, Bern adopted the **Protestant Reformation** and imposed the new faith on the Oberland. Lauterbrunnen joined an unsuccessful resistance, but afterward became the centre of a new **Reformed parish**

## 4. Staubbach Falls — Goethe's Waterfall

- **Staubbach Falls** plunges **297 metres** in free fall from the cliff edge just west of the village — the **highest free-falling waterfall in Switzerland** and one of the highest in Europe
- The name **Staubbach** means "dust stream" — a reference to the way the falling water disperses into a fine mist long before it reaches the ground, swaying in the wind like a veil
- **Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** visited Staubbach Falls on **9-10 October 1779** during his second Swiss journey, accompanying his patron **Duke Karl August of Saxe-Weimar-Eisenach**
- Deeply moved by the sight, Goethe composed his poem **"Gesang der Geister uber den Wassern"** (Song of the Spirits over the Waters) — either at the falls or in the **Lauterbrunnen vicarage** where they lodged — and sent a copy to **Charlotte von Stein** in Weimar on **11 October 1779**
- The six-stanza poem compares the journey of the human soul to the cycle of water: rising, falling, and rising again. It was published in **1789**
- **Franz Schubert** set the poem to music multiple times between **1816 and 1821** (catalogued as D 484, D 538, D 705, D 714), most famously as a part-song for men's voices and low strings
- **Lord Byron** visited in **September 1816** with companion **John Cam Hobhouse** and described Staubbach as resembling **"the tail of a white horse streaming in the wind, such as might be conceived would be that of the pale horse on which Death is mounted in the Apocalypse."** His Alpine experiences inspired the dramatic poem **"Manfred"**
- A plaque displaying Goethe's poem can be found at the viewing area at the base of the falls; a trail to the base is open in summer

## 5. Trummelbach Falls — Inside the Mountain

- **Trummelbach Falls** are a series of **ten waterfalls inside the mountain**, the only glacier-fed underground waterfalls in Europe that are accessible to visitors
- Fed by meltwater from the glaciers on the flanks of the **Eiger**, **Monch**, and **Jungfrau**, with a catchment area of **24 km²** (half covered in snow and ice)
- The waterfalls descend approximately **140 metres** through corkscrew gorges carved into the rock, carrying up to **20,000 litres of water per second** and transporting **20,000 tonnes of rock debris per year**
- The falls have been thundering through the rock for an estimated **15,000 to 20,000 years**
- Access was first created in **1877** with walkways and bridges to the lowest falls; the lower stairs and bridges were inaugurated in **1886**
- A **tunnel lift** (funicular with counterweights) was constructed in **1913** to reach the upper falls, climbing from 819 m to 890 m elevation. It was replaced in **1964** and rebuilt by Garaventa in **1982-83**
- Visitors follow a route of approximately **600 metres** through tunnels, galleries, and stairways carved into the mountain
- Listed in the **Federal Inventory of Landscapes and Natural Monuments** and part of the **Jungfrau-Aletsch UNESCO World Heritage Site**

## 6. The Railways — Gateway to the Jungfrau

### The Wengernalp Railway (1893)
- In **1890**, **Leo Heer-Betrix** received an 80-year concession to build a rack railway from Lauterbrunnen to Grindelwald via Wengen and Kleine Scheidegg
- Construction began **20 April 1891**; the first steam locomotive reached Wengen on **18 April 1892** and Kleine Scheidegg on **10 August 1892**
- The full line from Lauterbrunnen to Grindelwald opened **20 June 1893** as a summer service
- At **19.11 kilometres**, it is the **world's longest continuous rack-and-pinion railway**
- Electrified in **1909** (Lauterbrunnen-Kleine Scheidegg) and **1910** (Grindelwald side); steam operation ceased in **1912**
- In its first year, **37,742 passengers** were transported

### The Jungfrau Railway (1896-1912)
- From Kleine Scheidegg, the **Jungfrau Railway** continues through a **7-kilometre tunnel** bored through the Eiger and Monch to the **Jungfraujoch** at **3,454 m** — the highest railway station in Europe
- Conceived by industrialist **Adolf Guyer-Zeller** on **27 August 1893**; construction began **27 July 1896**
- Stations opened in stages: **Eigergletscher** (1898), **Eigerwand** with windows into the Eiger's north face (1903), **Eismeer** (1905), and the summit terminus on **1 August 1912**
- Lauterbrunnen served as the base for the railway's power supply: the first priority of construction in **autumn 1896** was building power lines to Kleine Scheidegg

## 7. Murren — The Car-Free Village on the Cliff

- **Murren** perches on a narrow terrace at **1,650 m** above sea level, on the cliff edge high above the Lauterbrunnen Valley, with views directly across to the Eiger, Monch, and Jungfrau
- First mentioned in writing in **1257** as **"Uf dr Muren"**; originally settled by **Walser people** from the Valais in the 13th century
- The village is **car-free** — no roads connect it to the valley below. Access is only possible by cable car from Stechelberg or by the Murren railway (opened **1891**) from Lauterbrunnen via Grutschalp
- **Skiing pioneer Sir Arnold Lunn** organized the **first slalom race in the history of alpine skiing** in Murren in **1922**
- The **Kandahar Ski Club** was founded here in **1924**; the **Inferno Race** (15.8 km, 2,150 m descent) launched in **1928**
- **Switzerland's first ski school** was founded in Murren in **1930**
- The first **Alpine World Championships in slalom and downhill** were held here in **1931**
- The first ski lift in the Bernese Oberland opened on the **Schiltgrat** in **1937**

## 8. The Schilthorn and Piz Gloria — Where James Bond Met the Alps

- The **Schilthorn** rises to **2,970 m** above the village of Murren
- In **1962**, **Schilthorn Cableway Ltd.** was founded under the leadership of **Ernst Feuz** of Murren to make the summit accessible
- Construction began in **1963**: the first three cable-car sections (Stechelberg-Gimmelwald-Murren-Birg) opened by **1965**; the final section to the summit opened in **June 1967**
- Feuz conceived the idea of building **the first revolving restaurant in the Alps** at the summit — designed by Bernese architect **Konrad Wolf**
- The rotating mechanism: a **12-metre-diameter** platform that completes one full revolution approximately every hour, giving diners a 360-degree panorama of over **200 peaks**
- While construction was stalled for lack of funds, the James Bond film producers discovered the location for **"On Her Majesty's Secret Service"** (1969). They **financed the completion of the revolving platform** in exchange for the right to use the facility as Blofeld's mountain-top hideout **"Piz Gloria"**
- Filming took place from **October 1968 to May 1969**, starring **George Lazenby** as Bond
- After filming, the restaurant kept the name **Piz Gloria** and today houses a **Bond World 007 exhibition** with memorabilia and film clips

## 9. Wengen and the Lauberhorn — The World's Longest Downhill

- **Wengen** sits at **1,274 m** on a sun-drenched terrace above the Lauterbrunnen Valley, first mentioned in documents in **1268**
- Like Murren, Wengen is **car-free** — accessible only by the Wengernalp Railway from Lauterbrunnen (since 1893)
- On **28 November 1929**, local skier **Ernst Gertsch** and co-founders of the **Swiss Academic Ski Club (SAS)** signed the founding document of the Lauberhorn Race
- The **first Lauberhorn Race** took place on **1-2 February 1930**, organized with a budget of just **500 Swiss francs**. Gertsch, then 30 years old, won the slalom himself
- The **Lauberhorn downhill course** is the **longest on the World Cup circuit** at **4.48 kilometres**, with run times of roughly two and a half minutes and top speeds approaching **160 km/h** on the Haneggschuss straight
- Since the start of the **Alpine Ski World Cup in 1967**, the Lauberhorn has been one of its most prestigious fixtures, attracting up to **70,000 spectators** to the mountain slopes above Wengen

## 10. J.R.R. Tolkien — The Valley That Inspired Rivendell

- In the summer of **1911**, **J.R.R. Tolkien**, then aged **19**, joined a group of friends on a walking tour through the Bernese Oberland — a trip he would remember for the rest of his life
- The group walked from **Interlaken** to **Lauterbrunnen**, then up to **Murren** and into the head of the Lauterbrunnen Valley, sleeping rough in haylofts and cowsheds
- Tolkien later wrote: *"We went on foot carrying great packs practically all the way from Interlaken, mainly by mountain paths, to Lauterbrunnen and so to Murren and eventually to the head of the Lauterbrunnenthal in a wilderness of morains."*
- In **Letter 306** (written to his son **Michael** in **1968**), Tolkien made the connection explicit: *"The hobbit's (Bilbo's) journey from Rivendell to the other side of the Misty Mountains, including the glissade down the slithering stones into the pine woods, is based on my adventures in 1911."*
- The sheer-walled valley with its cascading waterfalls and snow-capped peaks is widely considered the primary inspiration for **Rivendell**, the hidden Elven valley in *The Lord of the Rings*
- The towering trio of **Eiger**, **Monch**, and **Jungfrau** rising at the valley's head are believed to have inspired the **Misty Mountains**
- Tolkien described 1911 as *"the annus mirabilis of sunshine in which there was virtually no rain between April and the end of October"*
- Today, Lauterbrunnen Tourism embraces the connection, and the valley draws Tolkien devotees from around the world

## 11. The Birth of Tourism — From Grand Tour to Golden Age

- In the mid-**18th century**, writers and philosophers began drawing European attention to the Alps: **Albrecht von Haller**, **Jean-Jacques Rousseau**, **Lord Byron**, and **Goethe** all praised the valley's scenery
- Early visitors lodged in the **Lauterbrunnen rectory**, which was equipped to accommodate travellers, or at the **Steinbock inn** (L'Auberge du Capricorne) at the far end of the village, or even in farmers' hay barns
- **Hotel Staubbach** — the valley's first purpose-built hotel — was constructed in **1839** by **Christian von Almen** (1815-1882)
- **Hotel Jungfrau** (De la Jungfrau) followed in **1841**; **Hotel Silberhorn** in Murren in **1857**
- The **Grand Hotel und Kurhaus** in Murren (from 1870) eventually earned a star in Karl Baedeker's **"Handbook for Travellers"**
- Locals earned money as the tourism economy grew: hiring out **horses and mules** for luggage transport, carrying guests up to the Schilthorn or Kleine Scheidegg in **sedan chairs**, and young men organizing **summer sledging** from Murren to Lauterbrunnen
- The Trummelbach Falls were made accessible in **1886**; **Hotel Trummelbach** opened in **1888**
- The arrival of the **Wengernalp Railway** in **1893** and **electrification** by 1910 transformed the valley from an adventurous destination for the wealthy into an accessible resort for the wider public
- **Felix Mendelssohn** visited the area multiple times (1822, 1831, 1842, 1847) and created what is believed to be the **oldest known drawing of Wengen**

## 12. Valley of Superlatives — Notable Waterfalls

- Beyond Staubbach and Trummelbach, the valley contains several other remarkable waterfalls:
  - **Murrenbachfall** (417 m): Switzerland's **highest waterfall**, plunging down the Murrenfluh cliff in five cascades, fed by snowmelt from Birg and the Schilthorn
  - **Schmadribachfall** (270 m): A multi-tiered cascade sourced from the Breithorn and Schmadri glaciers at the head of the valley
  - **Sprutz Waterfall**: A seasonal cascade on the path between Murren and Gimmelwald
- The Murrenbachfall is not free-falling like Staubbach — its water cascades against the rock face — which is why Staubbach retains the distinction of Switzerland's highest **free-falling** waterfall at 297 m
- The combination of 72 waterfalls, 300-metre cliff walls, glacial peaks, and a flat green valley floor is what led the valley to be called the **"most beautiful in the world"** by generations of visitors, from Romantic poets to modern travellers

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The spirits over the waters** — On a misty October evening in 1779, a child in the Lauterbrunnen vicarage watches Goethe scribble furiously by candlelight after seeing Staubbach Falls; the waterfall's spray forms ghostly shapes that whisper the poem's words before the poet writes them
2. **The valley Tolkien dreamed** — A child hiking the Lauterbrunnenthal in the footsteps of young Tolkien in 1911 discovers that when the waterfalls catch the light just so, the valley transforms into Rivendell — and a company of Elves needs help finding their way home before dawn
3. **Inside the mountain** — A child on a school trip to Trummelbach Falls gets separated from the group and follows the ten waterfalls deeper into the mountain, where the meltwater from Eiger, Monch, and Jungfrau remembers the glaciers that carved them and tells stories of 20,000 years
4. **The spy on the Schilthorn** — During the filming of James Bond in 1968, a Murren child discovers that the revolving restaurant is not just a film set — it really does spin to reveal a different secret in every direction, and one of them is real
5. **The railway through the clouds** — On the first day the Wengernalp Railway opens in 1893, a child boards the steam train in Lauterbrunnen and rides up through the clouds to Kleine Scheidegg, where the mountains are alive with the sound of falling water and the Jungfrau herself has a message for the brave
6. **Seventy-two voices** — Each of Lauterbrunnen's 72 waterfalls has a different voice and a different story; a child who can hear them must listen to all 72 before sunset to learn the valley's true name
7. **The Walser girl of Murren** — In 1300, a young Walser girl arrives on the cliff-edge terrace of Murren with her family from the Lotschental, homesick for the valley below, until the mountain goats show her a secret path and the eagles teach her that home is wherever you can see the stars
8. **Byron's pale horse** — Lord Byron sees a ghost in the Staubbach spray — the pale horse of the Apocalypse, streaming its white tail in the wind. A child must convince him it is not a sign of doom but a gift from the mountain, before the poet's dark mood infects his masterpiece
