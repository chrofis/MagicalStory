## 1. Earliest History — Waldrata's Gift to St. Gallen

- Romanshorn was probably settled in the **7th century** by Alemannic farmers on the shores of Lake Constance
- **First documented mention**: **2 February 779**, when the noblewoman **Waldrata** and her son **Waldbert** donated their property in **"Rumanishorn"** to the **Abbey of St. Gallen** — including a church dedicated to **Mary, Peter, and Gallus**
- The name likely derives from a personal name ("Romanus") combined with "Horn" (a peninsula or promontory jutting into the lake)
- The donation bound Romanshorn to the powerful **Benedictine monastery of St. Gallen** for over a thousand years — the abbots controlled taxation, the appellate court, and feudal obligations until **1798**
- In **1455**, Abbot **Kaspar von Landsberg** attempted to sell the Romanshorn estate to the city of St. Gallen, but his religious superiors forced the courts to reverse the sale
- The **Landsberg family** had owned parts of the town until **1367**, when the estate passed fully to the abbey's administration

## 2. The Alte Kirche (Old Church) — Twelve Centuries of Worship

- The church mentioned in the **779 donation** is one of the oldest documented churches in the Thurgau region
- Originally dedicated to **Mary, Peter, and Gallus** — the connection to **St. Gallus** reflects the donor family's close ties to the founding of the St. Gallen monastery around **720**
- **Archaeological excavations in the 1960s** revealed the original structure was substantial, with multiple adjoining buildings
- **10th century**: The original apse was replaced in a first major structural modification
- **14th century**: Fresco fragments in **red chalk (Rötel)** were painted on the choir's east wall — remnants survive today
- **15th century**: The imposing, fortress-like **church tower** was constructed with a massive stone base (Sockel); its original purpose is debated
- **Late 15th century**: The patron saint changed to **John the Baptist**; in **1480**, Pope **Sixtus IV** officially incorporated the church into the Abbey of St. Gallen under Abbot **Ulrich Rösch**
- **1504**: The church was significantly enlarged
- **1528–1529**: The **Reformation** reached Romanshorn. Pastor **Itelhans Bertz** and the entire congregation converted to the Reformed faith; images, altars, and reliquaries were removed
- **1563–1577**: Beginning around 1563, both **Catholics and Protestants** shared the church — a **simultaneum** arrangement confirmed by the Swiss Diet. This shared use lasted over **300 years**
- **1872**: The two denominations created separate cemeteries — the first spatial separation
- **1911**: A new **Evangelische Kirche** (Protestant church) was consecrated on **14 May 1911**, ending the simultaneum
- **1913**: A new **Catholic church (St. Johannes der Täufer)** was built
- **1963–1969**: The Alte Kirche underwent thorough archaeological investigation and careful restoration, reopening on **5 December 1969**
- Today the Alte Kirche is a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**, used for weddings, concerts, and special services, seating about 150 people

## 3. The Schloss (Castle) — From Abbey Seat to Hotel

- The castle hill (**Schlossberg**) has been a focal point of Romanshorn since the medieval period, with historical images dating back to **1760** showing its prominence above the lake
- The **"New Castle"** was built in **1404** and served from **1367 to 1798** as the seat of the **Prince-Abbot's regional administrator** (Statthalter)
- The castle was **rebuilt in 1617/18** and **completely modernized and enlarged in 1931**
- After the dissolution of abbey rule in 1798, the castle passed through various hands
- In **May 2017**, the castle reopened as the **Hotel Schloss Romanshorn**, a lakeside hotel and restaurant with terraces overlooking Lake Constance
- Both the **Old Church** and the **Castle** are listed as heritage sites in the **Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites (ISOS)** — in fact, the entire village of Romanshorn is listed

## 4. The Railway Arrives — Romanshorn Becomes a Transport Hub (1855)

- The **Winterthur–Romanshorn railway** (the **Thurtallinie** / "Thur valley line") opened on **16 May 1855**, built by the **Swiss Northeastern Railway** (Schweizerische Nordostbahn, NOB)
- It was the **fourth oldest internal railway in Switzerland**, connecting Winterthur to the Lake Constance shore at Romanshorn
- The connection from Winterthur to Oerlikon followed on **27 December 1855**, and the final section to **Zurich** opened **26 June 1856** — completing a through route from Zurich to the Bodensee
- Romanshorn station became a **railway junction** where three lines converge: the **Winterthur–Romanshorn**, the **Schaffhausen–Rorschach** (Seelinie), and the **Romanshorn–Nesslau Neu St. Johann** (Bodensee–Toggenburg railway)
- The arrival of the railway in 1855 was the single most transformative event in Romanshorn's history — the sleepy farming village of **1,408 inhabitants (1850)** began its rapid growth to **4,577 by 1900**
- A **telegraph cable** was laid across the lake in **1856**, further connecting Romanshorn to Germany
- Additional lines opened between **1869 and 1910**, linking Romanshorn to Rorschach, Constance, and St. Gallen

## 5. The Lake Constance Ferry — A Floating Bridge Between Nations

- On **22 February 1869**, the **Württemberg State Railway** and the **Swiss Northeastern Railway** opened a **train ferry** between **Friedrichshafen** and **Romanshorn** — one of the earliest international lake ferry services in Europe
- The first vessel was a **paddle steamer** designed by Scottish engineer **John Scott Russell** (famous for co-designing the SS Great Eastern with Brunel). It could carry entire railway wagons across the lake but was soon nicknamed a **"coal eater"**, consuming **600–720 kg of coal per crossing**. It was retired in **1883**
- The train ferry service continued with improved vessels until **1976**, when it was replaced by **car ferries**
- Today, three double-ended ferries — **MF Friedrichshafen**, **MF Romanshorn**, and **MF Euregia** — operate the route year-round, **365 days a year at hourly intervals**
- The **MF Euregia**, built in **1996** at the Bodan-Werft in Kressbronn, is **60 meters long** and **13 meters wide**
- The crossing covers approximately **13.6 km** (the widest part of Lake Constance) in about **41 minutes**
- The ferry is operated jointly by **Bodensee-Schiffsbetriebe (BSB)** from Konstanz (German side) and **Schweizerische Bodensee-Schifffahrt (SBS)** from Romanshorn (Swiss side)
- In **2009**, the Euregia alone transported over **586,000 passengers**, **63,300 cars**, **56,200 bicycles**, and **8,200 trucks**
- The ferry saves travellers a long drive around the lake and is often called a **"floating bridge"** between Switzerland and Germany

## 6. Locorama — The Railway Experience World

- **Locorama** is a railway museum housed in the **former SBB locomotive depot** (Lokremise) near Romanshorn station — the very place where the railway arrived in **1855**
- The depot building dates from **1898** and includes a historic **turntable** and **signal bridge with 8 signals**
- Once an important turnaround point for goods trains running between Switzerland and Germany across the lake
- The museum's foundation aims to preserve the historic buildings around Romanshorn station and make them accessible as a **living museum**
- **Key exhibits**: the **Ec 3/5 tender steam locomotive** (built 1912), a **Bavarian S 3/6 express steam locomotive**, the electric **Ae 6/6** and **Ae 4/7** locomotives, plus various postal, passenger, and goods wagons
- Interactive features: **locomotive simulator**, rides on the **garden railway** and **draisine** (hand-powered rail car), and a large **model railway layout**
- The museum connects Romanshorn's identity as a railway town to the broader history of Swiss rail — visitors stand where steam engines once turned on the turntable to begin their return journey

## 7. Zeppelins Across the Lake — Friedrichshafen's Airship Heritage

- Directly across the lake from Romanshorn lies **Friedrichshafen**, birthplace of the Zeppelin airship
- **Count Ferdinand von Zeppelin** began construction of his first rigid airship (**LZ 1**) in **June 1898** in a **floating wooden hangar** on the lake at **Manzell** near Friedrichshafen — the hangar floated so it could be turned into the wind
- On **2 July 1900**, LZ 1 made its maiden flight from the lake surface — lasting about **18 minutes** and covering roughly **6 km** over the water. It reached an altitude of **410 metres**
- In the early years, all Zeppelins were built, stored, and repaired in enormous hangars on the Bodensee shore
- The connection between Romanshorn and Friedrichshafen meant that residents of Romanshorn had a **front-row view** of the great airships rising from the opposite shore — a sight that captivated the whole lake region in the early 1900s
- The **Zeppelin Museum Friedrichshafen** houses the world's largest collection on airship history, including a partial reconstruction of the **LZ 129 Hindenburg**, and is easily reachable from Romanshorn via the ferry (41 minutes)
- Today, **Zeppelin NT** sightseeing flights still operate from Friedrichshafen — passengers can see Romanshorn and the Swiss shore from above, just as Count Zeppelin saw it in 1900

## 8. The Seepark and Harbour Promenade

- Romanshorn's **Seepark** (Lake Park) stretches along the lakefront on the **Schlossberg** hillside, offering sweeping views across Lake Constance to the Austrian and German mountains beyond
- The park features **colourful flowerbeds, green meadows, and a lakeside promenade** where families stroll while watching boats enter and leave the harbour
- The **Inseli** peninsula near the harbour served as a **bathing resort (Seebad)** from as early as **1856**, with a more established Seebad Inseli operating from **1864 to 1911**
- The harbour of Romanshorn is one of the **largest on Lake Constance**, combining ferry terminals, pleasure boat moorings, and the historic port infrastructure built in **1844** by the Canton of Thurgau
- Harbour cruises aboard the **MS Zeppelin** run from May through September, circling the Romanshorn port area
- The combination of railway station and harbour **directly adjacent** to each other gives Romanshorn a distinctive arrival experience — travellers step off trains and immediately see the lake, ferries, and the German shore in the distance

## 9. Industrialisation and Economic Growth

- Before the railway, Romanshorn's economy was agricultural: **grain, fruit, vegetables, forestry, and fishing**, with some **wine production** recorded by 1902
- **Steamboats** had operated from nearby Uttwil since **1824**; they moved to Romanshorn in **1832**, marking the first step in the town's transport evolution
- In **1844**, the Canton of Thurgau built a proper **port** and routed the **postal service to Swabia** through Romanshorn — the village began to grow
- **Fatzer AG** (founded **1836**): Joachim Fatzer started a hemp rope factory in Romanshorn at age 17. The company grew with the steamship era, transitioned to **steel wire rope production around 1900**, and by **1985** produced **2,000 tonnes of steel wire** annually. Today Fatzer supplies wire ropes for **world-record ropeways** worldwide and remains in Romanshorn
- **Max Zeller & Sons**: Pharmacist Max Zeller opened a pharmacy in **1864** and created the famous **Zeller balm** — the company grew into a pharmaceutical enterprise still known in Switzerland
- **Swiss Alcohol Board** (Eidgenössische Alkoholverwaltung): Opened a major facility in Romanshorn in **1892**, reaching a storage capacity of **30 million litres of alcohol** by 1980. The building was repurposed after the facility closed in **1996**
- **Voigt Pharmaceutical**: Founded in Romanshorn in **1904**, grew into an international pharmaceutical shipping company employing **250 people** by 2008
- **autobau erlebniswelt**: In **2009**, racing driver and entrepreneur **Fredy Lienhard** converted the former Alcohol Board warehouse (the **1892 brick building**) into a spectacular **automobile museum** housing about **120 classic and racing cars** — from Ferrari and Porsche to Formula 1 cars. The collection includes a **Sauber F1 exhibition** and the **Clay Regazzoni Honor Room**

## 10. Geography — Lake, Landscape, and Thurgau

- Romanshorn sits on the **southern shore of Lake Constance** (Bodensee) in the **canton of Thurgau**, district of Arbon
- The municipality covers **8.75 km²**: roughly **41% farmland**, **24% forest**, and **33% buildings and roads**
- The town occupies a gentle **promontory** (the "Horn") jutting into the lake — the widest part of the Bodensee stretches **13.6 km** from here to Friedrichshafen
- The surrounding Thurgau landscape is characterised by **rolling hills**, **orchards** (especially apple orchards), and **gentle green countryside** — earning the canton the nickname **"Mostindien"** (Cider India) for its prolific cider production
- Lake Constance is the **third-largest lake in Central Europe** (536 km²), shared by **Switzerland, Germany, and Austria** — Romanshorn looks across to the German shore and, on clear days, to the Austrian Alps beyond
- Population: **1,218** (1831) → **1,408** (1850) → **4,577** (1900) → **6,648** (1950) → **9,076** (2000) → **11,327** (2020)
- The hamlet areas of **Holzenstein** and **Spitz** also belong to the municipality

## 11. The Harbour Town Today — Culture, Events, and Recreation

- Romanshorn hosts the annual **Sommernachtsfest** (Summer Night Festival) along the lake promenade
- **SlowUp Bodensee**: A car-free day where the lakeside roads are opened to cyclists, skaters, and pedestrians
- **Bluescht** (spring blossom season): The surrounding orchards burst into white and pink bloom, a Thurgau-wide celebration
- The **Roxy cinema** and **Klangreich** chamber music venue provide cultural offerings
- **Ludotrail**: A family scavenger hunt with **25 stations** throughout town, designed for children ages 6+, featuring games on regional themes
- Water sports on the Bodensee: **sailing, rowing, stand-up paddling (SUP)**, and the **Seebad** (outdoor lakeside pool) with a 50-metre swimming pool
- Romanshorn serves as a hub for the **Lake Constance cycling route** (Bodensee-Radweg), one of Europe's most popular cycling routes encircling the entire lake (~260 km)

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **Waldrata's gift — the church on the horn** — A noblewoman and her son travel to the lake peninsula in 779 to dedicate their church to the monks of St. Gallen; a child discovers why the village was named for a "horn" in the lake
2. **The coal-eating paddle steamer** — 1869: a child stowaway hides aboard the first train ferry, designed by the famous engineer John Scott Russell, and helps the crew solve the mystery of why the engine devours so much coal
3. **The turntable at midnight** — In the old Locorama locomotive depot, a child discovers that at midnight the turntable still spins on its own, and the old steam engines whisper stories of the journeys they once made across the Swiss rail network
4. **Zeppelins over the Bodensee** — From Romanshorn's harbour, a child watches a strange silver shape rise from the opposite shore in 1900. Rowing across the lake, they meet the eccentric Count Zeppelin and help launch LZ 1 from its floating hangar
5. **The simultaneum — sharing a church** — In 1577, Catholic and Protestant children must learn to share the same church; a story about cooperation, tolerance, and the small acts that kept peace for 300 years
6. **The ferry that connects two worlds** — A child rides the MF Euregia back and forth across the lake, discovering that each 41-minute crossing tells a different story: of the train ferry era, of smugglers, of the people who depend on the "floating bridge"
7. **Joachim's rope** — In 1836, a 17-year-old boy starts a rope factory in Romanshorn with nothing but hemp and ambition. A story tracing the Fatzer company from hand-twisted ropes for farmers to the steel cables holding the world's greatest cable cars
8. **The secret of the Schlossberg** — A child exploring the castle hill discovers that the 1404 castle, the ancient church with its mysterious red-chalk frescoes, and the Seepark are all connected by a hidden path that reveals a different century at every turn
