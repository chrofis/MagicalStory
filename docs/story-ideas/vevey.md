## 1. Geography — The Pearl of the Swiss Riviera

- Vevey sits on the **north shore of Lake Geneva** (Lac Léman), in the **canton of Vaud**, between **Lausanne** (to the west) and **Montreux** (to the east)
- The town occupies a small **delta** jutting into the lake — one of the few stretches of flat land on an otherwise mountainous north shore
- The **Alps** rise dramatically behind the town, and on clear days the view across the lake stretches to the **French Alps** and the Dents du Midi
- The mild, south-facing climate — sheltered by mountains from cold northern winds — earned the region the name **"Riviera vaudoise"** (the Vaud Riviera), also called the Swiss Riviera
- Population: approximately **19,900** inhabitants; area: just **2.4 km²** — compact but culturally dense
- The town has drawn artists, writers, and exiles for centuries, described as a place of "calm, clarity, and inspiration"

## 2. Ancient Origins — From Viviscus to Vevey

- A **piloti settlement** (lakeside stilt houses) existed here as early as the **2nd millennium BC**
- The ancient Greek astronomer **Ptolemy** (2nd century AD) mentioned the settlement under the name **Ouikos**
- Under Rome, the town was known as **Viviscus** (or Vibiscum) — a staging post for legionaries and merchants at the junction of roads linking Italy to the strongholds of Geneva and Avenches
- In the early Middle Ages, Vevey was a station on the **Via Francigena**, the great pilgrimage route from Canterbury to Rome. Archbishop **Sigéric** recorded it in **990 AD** as stage LIII, under the name **Vivaec**
- Successive medieval names survive: Viviscum (1011), Vivesium (1017), Vivois (1163), Vives (1177), Vivex (12th c.), and Viveis (1225) — the name gradually evolving toward "Vevey"
- In the Middle Ages, the town was ruled first by the **bishopric of Lausanne**, then by the **Blonay family**, before coming under Bernese control

## 3. The Grande Place — Europe's Second-Largest Marketplace

- The **Grande Place** (main square) sits directly on the lakefront — a vast, open space with views across Lake Geneva to the Alps
- It is the **second-largest marketplace in Europe**, after Lisbon's Praça do Comércio
- Twice a week, on **Tuesday and Saturday mornings**, a traditional market fills the square with regional produce, flowers, cheeses, and wines — continuing a market tradition stretching back centuries
- During the Fête des Vignerons, the Grande Place is transformed: a temporary **arena seating 16,000 spectators** is erected on the square for the festival's performances
- The square is also home to Vevey's **Saturday folk market** (Marché folklorique), held in summer, featuring local artisans and traditional crafts

## 4. Henri Nestlé and the Birth of a Global Empire

- **Henri Nestlé** (born Heinrich Nestle, **10 August 1814** in Frankfurt am Main, Germany — died **7 July 1890** in Glion, Switzerland) was a pharmacist's assistant who settled in Vevey
- In **1867**, he developed his **Farine Lactée** ("flour with milk") — a mixture of cow's milk, wheat flour, and sugar designed as infant food for babies who could not be breastfed, at a time when infant mortality was devastatingly high
- On **1 September 1867**, Nestlé's product saved its first life: a premature baby named **Wanner** who could not digest mother's milk survived on the Farine Lactée — the news spread rapidly
- Sales exploded: from **8,600 tins** in the first year to over **one million** by 1875
- In **1875**, Nestlé retired and sold the company; the new owners retained his name as **Société Farine Lactée Henri Nestlé**
- In **1905**, the company merged with the **Anglo-Swiss Condensed Milk Company** (founded 1866 in nearby Cham) to form the Nestlé and Anglo-Swiss Condensed Milk Company
- Today, **Nestlé S.A.** remains headquartered in Vevey — the world's largest food and beverage company, still based in the small lakeside town where it all began
- The former Nestlé headquarters building (built in the 1920s) now houses the **Alimentarium** food museum

## 5. Charlie Chaplin — The Tramp's Final Home

### Exile and Arrival
- In September **1952**, Charlie Chaplin was aboard the RMS Queen Elizabeth, sailing to London to promote his film *Limelight*, when he learned that the **United States had revoked his re-entry visa** during the McCarthy era
- On the advice of his half-brother **Sydney**, the Chaplin family visited Switzerland and discovered the **Manoir de Ban**, an 1840 mansion in **Corsier-sur-Vevey**, in the hills just above Vevey
- The family moved in on **6 January 1953**. Chaplin would live there for the remaining **25 years** of his life

### Life at Manoir de Ban
- At the estate, Chaplin wrote the screenplays for *A King in New York* (1957) and *A Countess from Hong Kong* (1967), reworked the music for his earlier films at the piano, and wrote *My Autobiography* (published 1964)
- He died on **25 December 1977**, aged 88, at the Manoir de Ban. He and his wife **Lady Oona** are buried in the cemetery at Corsier-sur-Vevey

### The Body Snatching of 1978
- On **1 March 1978**, just weeks after his burial, two men — **Roman Wardas** (Polish) and **Gantscho Ganev** (Bulgarian) — stole Chaplin's coffin and demanded a ransom of **600,000 Swiss francs** from Lady Oona
- Oona refused to pay, reportedly saying her husband "would have thought the demand ridiculous"
- After a five-week investigation monitoring 200 phone kiosks in the region, police arrested the two men on **17 May 1978**; they led investigators to a **cornfield** about one mile from the Manoir de Ban
- Chaplin's body was reburied in a **concrete-encased grave** to prevent future theft

### Memorials in Vevey
- A **bronze statue of The Tramp** by British sculptor **John Doubleday** has stood on the Quai Perdonnet since **1982** — Chaplin in his iconic pose with bowler hat and cane, gazing out over Lake Geneva
- **Chaplin's World** museum opened on **17 April 2016** at the Manoir de Ban, after 15 years of development at a cost of some **CHF 60 million**. It includes the restored manor house, a Hollywood-style studio with recreated film sets, 30 wax figures, and 10 acres of parkland

## 6. The Fête des Vignerons — Once in a Generation

### The Confrérie des Vignerons (Brotherhood of Winegrowers)
- The oldest surviving document of the **Confrérie des Vignerons** is the association's "Manual" (record of minutes), dated **22 June 1647**, when it was called the **Abbaye de l'Agriculture** (Abbey of Agriculture)
- A small wooden drinking goblet, the **Abbot's Cup**, bears the name **Gaspar Rot** and the date **1618**, suggesting the brotherhood existed even earlier
- The Confrérie was not a guild of vineyard workers, but an association of **landowners** who employed workers to tend their vines. Bernese bailiffs conducted yearly vineyard inspections
- Around **1770**, the brotherhood shifted from punishing poor viticulture to **rewarding excellence** — crowning the best vineyard workers with prizes

### The Festival
- In **1797**, the first true **Fête des Vignerons** was born when stands were erected in the Grande Place for the public crowning of top vineyard workers
- The festival has been held only **12 times** in over two centuries: **1797, 1819, 1833, 1851, 1865, 1889, 1905, 1927, 1955, 1977, 1999, 2019**
- The interval between festivals varies from **14 to 28 years** — the organizing committee is free to choose, but the tradition is that it occurs roughly **once per generation**
- Each festival spans **three weeks with 15 events** and involves approximately **5,000 volunteers** who dedicate years to preparation
- Themes celebrate the **cycle of the seasons**, working the earth and vines, and the spirit of community. Traditional performances include the **Ranz des Vaches** (Alpine cow-herders' song) and processions to the neighbouring town of **La Tour-de-Peilz**
- In **2016**, the Fête des Vignerons was inscribed on **UNESCO's Representative List of Intangible Cultural Heritage** — the first Swiss tradition to receive this distinction

## 7. La Fourchette — The Giant Fork in the Lake

- **La Fourchette** (The Fork) is a monumental **stainless-steel table fork**, 8 metres tall and 1.3 metres wide at its tines, planted in Lake Geneva just **5 metres** off the Quai Perdonnet
- Designed by Neuchâtel-based artist **Jean-Pierre Zaugg** and fabricated by Vevey steelwork contractor **Georges Favre**, the fork was commissioned to celebrate the **Alimentarium's 10th anniversary** in **1995**
- Originally intended as a temporary installation (April–October 1995), its enormous popularity prompted efforts to keep it permanently — but in **1996**, cantonal authorities denied permanent authorization, and the fork was removed to Littau (Canton of Lucerne)
- In **2007**, the fork returned for a temporary exhibition; in **2008**, a **mass petition** and support from the city council finally secured its **permanent status**, with an official inauguration in **2009**
- In **2014**, it was entered into the **Guinness World Records** as the world's tallest fork
- The **Alimentarium** (food museum) behind it occupies the former 1920s Nestlé headquarters. Opened **21 June 1985**, it explores the themes of cooking, buying, eating, and digesting, and tells the story of both Nestlé and human nutrition

## 8. The Lavaux Vineyards — A UNESCO Landscape

- The **Lavaux vineyard terraces** stretch for about **30 km** along the south-facing northern shore of Lake Geneva, from the outskirts of Lausanne to the Château de Chillon — passing directly through the Vevey–Montreux region
- Although vines were grown here in Roman times, the present terraces date to the **11th century**, when **Benedictine and Cistercian monks** carved the steep hillsides into cultivable land
- The monks built a network of **stone walls stretching some 450 km**, supporting approximately **10,000 individual terraces** across **830 hectares**
- The dominant grape is **Chasselas**, representing about **60% of all plantings** — a white wine grape prized for expressing the nuances of its terroir
- Inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site in 2007**, Lavaux is recognized as "an outstanding example of a centuries-long interaction between people and their environment"
- The terraces benefit from a triple sun effect: direct sunlight, reflection off the lake surface, and heat retained by the stone walls — creating a microclimate ideal for viticulture

## 9. Literary Vevey — Rousseau, Henry James, and Graham Greene

### Jean-Jacques Rousseau (1712–1778)
- Geneva-born philosopher **Jean-Jacques Rousseau** set much of his bestselling epistolary novel **_Julie, ou la Nouvelle Héloïse_** (1761) on the shores of Lake Geneva, in and around **Vevey, Clarens, and Meillerie**
- The novel — a tale of impossible love between the tutor Saint-Preux and the nobleman's daughter Julie d'Étanges — made the region famous across Europe
- By the 1760s, "sentimental tourists" traveled to Vevey with volumes of Rousseau in hand, seeking to walk the "classic ground" described in the novel — arguably **inventing literary tourism** in the region
- James Boswell visited in **1764**, identifying himself as Saint-Preux at the very sites described in the book

### Henry James (1843–1916)
- American novelist **Henry James** stayed at the **Hôtel des Trois Couronnes** in Vevey and set his novella **_Daisy Miller_** (1878) there — one of the most famous American literary works set in Switzerland
- The hotel, built in **1842**, had already hosted Russian tsarinas, maharajahs, and composers including **Tchaikovsky** and **Camille Saint-Saëns**

### Graham Greene (1904–1991)
- British novelist **Graham Greene** spent his final years at **Corseaux**, a village directly above Vevey on the hillside
- Greene was a friend and neighbour of Charlie Chaplin; the two met at the **Hôtel des Trois Couronnes in 1960**
- His novel **_Doctor Fischer of Geneva or The Bomb Party_** (1980) was inspired by a Christmas celebration in nearby Jongny in **1979**
- Greene died on **3 April 1991** in a Vevey hospital and is buried in the **Corseaux cemetery** — a simple wooden cross marks his grave

## 10. Oskar Kokoschka and the Fondation in Vevey

- **Oskar Kokoschka** (1886–1980), the Austrian Expressionist painter, playwright, and poet, first visited Switzerland in **1910** and considered the Lake Geneva region his **"spiritual homeland"**
- In **1951**, he purchased a plot in **Villeneuve** (a few kilometres east of Vevey) and built a villa on the shore of Lake Geneva, moving in permanently in **1953**
- He lived there for **27 years** — the longest he ever resided in one place — until his death in **Montreux** on **22 February 1980**, aged 93
- The **Fondation Oskar Kokoschka** is housed within the **Musée Jenisch** in Vevey (Avenue de la Gare 2), preserving his written and artistic archives
- The Musée Jenisch holds the **world's largest collection of works by Kokoschka**

## 11. The Musée Jenisch — Art and Prints

- The museum was born from the generosity of **Fanny Jenisch** (1801–1881), widow of a Hamburg senator, who wished to express her gratitude to the city where she had spent happy holidays with her husband
- Inaugurated in **March 1897**, it was designed by architects **Maillard and Convert** in neoclassical style
- The museum houses over **11,000 drawings** and the **Cabinet cantonal des estampes** (Cantonal Print Collection), established in **1986** and transferred to the Jenisch in **1987**, containing over **35,000 prints** from the Renaissance to the present — one of the **five most prestigious print collections in Switzerland**
- Artists represented include **Courbet, Corot, Hodler, Vallotton, and Picasso**, alongside the extensive Kokoschka holdings
- The museum also includes the **Pavillon de l'estampe** (Print Pavilion), a space dedicated entirely to engraved art, and a recreation of **Kokoschka's studio**

## 12. The Hôtel des Trois Couronnes — Grand Hotel Heritage

- Built in **1842** by hotelier **Gabriel Monnet** on the ruins of the **Château des Belles Truches** (built 1376), the Hôtel des Trois Couronnes stands on the Vevey lakefront
- Around **1890**, the hotel was transformed from a neoclassical building into a **neo-baroque triple-wing structure** grouped around a northern courtyard
- Famous guests have included: Russian Tsarina **Alexandra Théodorowna** (who rented the entire hotel in **1859**), Queen Olga of Greece, the Maharajah Holkar of Indore, **Tchaikovsky**, **Camille Saint-Saëns**, the **Aga Khan** (who married there), **Charlie Chaplin**, and **Graham Greene**
- **Henry James** wrote *Daisy Miller* here, and the 1974 film adaptation by **Peter Bogdanovich** was also shot at the hotel
- The building is registered as a **Swiss cultural property of national importance**

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The baby who changed the world** — Henri Nestlé races against time to save little Wanner with his untested Farine Lactée; a child helps gather ingredients across 1867 Vevey
2. **The Tramp's last bow** — Charlie Chaplin's cane and bowler hat come alive at the lakeside statue one night; a child joins The Tramp for a moonlit tour of his beloved Vevey
3. **The festival that sleeps for a generation** — A magical vine in the Grande Place only blooms once every 25 years; a child must wake the sleeping Fête des Vignerons before it's too late
4. **The fork in the lake** — La Fourchette sinks deeper each night; a child dives into Lake Geneva to discover what the fork is stirring up on the lake floor (a hidden kitchen of the Alimentarium)
5. **The monks who built the walls** — A Cistercian monk from the 11th century needs a child's help to complete the last terrace in Lavaux before the first frost destroys the vines
6. **Julie's letters from the lake** — A child finds one of Julie d'Étanges' love letters washed ashore; following the trail leads through Rousseau's Vevey, Clarens, and Meillerie
7. **The night the coffin vanished** — A mystery-adventure retelling: a child detective must help find Chaplin's stolen coffin before it disappears forever, following clues across Corsier-sur-Vevey's cornfields
8. **The painter who couldn't leave** — Kokoschka's paintings at the Musée Jenisch come alive in swirling Expressionist colours; a child enters one painting and must find the way back through Kokoschka's memories of Lake Geneva
