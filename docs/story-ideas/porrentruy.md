## 1. Geography — The Ajoie Plain and the Allaine Valley

- Porrentruy (German: **Pruntrut**) lies in the **Ajoie** (German: Elsgau) region, the northwesternmost corner of Switzerland, in the canton of **Jura** — a bulge of Swiss territory that extends into France
- The town sits in the valley of the **Allaine** river (a tributary of the Doubs) at an elevation of about **423 meters** above sea level, at the foot of the folded **Jura Mountains**
- Only about **10 kilometers from the French border**, Porrentruy has always been a crossroads between the Swiss Confederation and France — its culture, dialect, and cuisine bear strong French influence
- The Ajoie is a **fertile limestone plateau** known as the "Orchard of the Jura" (le Verger du Jura), with rolling hills, orchards, and meadows
- Porrentruy is one of the **three historic cities of the Jura**, alongside **Delemont** (the cantonal capital) and **Saint-Ursanne**
- Population: approximately **6,400 inhabitants** (2020), making it the second-largest town in the canton after Delemont

## 2. Founding and Early History — The Counts of Pfirt

- The first historical mention of the name appears in **1136** as **"Purrentru"** — the name likely derives from the Latin **pons Ragentrudis** (Ragentrud's bridge), referring to the wife of Frankish King **Dagobert I** (7th century)
- The German name **Pruntrut** may alternatively stem from **"Bruntrutum"**, meaning an abundant spring
- The first settlement was established around **1140** near the **Eglise Saint-Germain** (Church of Saint-Germain), an early medieval church that served as the original parish
- The **Counts of Pfirt** (Ferrette) built a castle on a defensible hill overlooking the Allaine valley and made Porrentruy the **capital of the Ajoie territory**
- Two settlements grew: one at the foot of the castle (the **Faubourg de France**, toward the north) and another on the opposite hill to the south
- A **city wall** was probably built before **1283**, enclosing both settlements but not the Church of Saint-Germain
- On **20 April 1283**, King **Rudolf I of Habsburg** asked the Bishop of Basel to grant Porrentruy a **town charter** and make it a free Imperial city

## 3. The Chateau de Porrentruy — Residence of the Prince-Bishops

- The **Chateau de Porrentruy** (Schloss Pruntrut) is a castle complex that evolved between the **mid-13th century** and the **early 18th century**, listed as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**
- The oldest surviving element is the **Tour Refous** (Refous Tower), a massive **13th-century circular keep** standing **32 meters high** with a diameter of **12 meters** and walls **4.5 meters thick** at the base — its design recalls the round keeps of the **Savoy dynasty**
- The Tour Refous stands isolated on an esplanade that dominates the medieval city to the northwest; the courtyard and tower are **freely accessible** during the day
- **14th-century ramparts** survive on the western and northern sides of the castle complex
- Since **1271**, the castle belonged to the **Bishopric of Basel**; it became the **principal residence** of the prince-bishops from **1527** (some sources say 1524) after the Reformation expelled them from Basel
- Under Prince-Bishop **Jakob Christoph Blarer von Wartensee** (reigned 1575-1608), the castle underwent an **extensive reconstruction** by architect **Nicolas Frick** around **1588** — transforming it from a medieval fortress into a Renaissance princely residence
- In **1697**, a devastating fire damaged the castle; it was subsequently repaired and modified into the 18th century
- After the French invasion of **1792**, the castle lost its episcopal function; the last prince-bishop, **Joseph Sigismund von Roggenbach**, was driven out
- Today the castle houses the **headquarters of the Cantonal Court** (Tribunal cantonal) of the Republic and Canton of Jura — it remains a symbol of power in the region

## 4. Capital of the Prince-Bishopric of Basel (1528-1792)

- The **Prince-Bishopric of Basel** (Furstbistum Basel / Eveche de Bale) was an ecclesiastical principality of the **Holy Roman Empire**, ruled by prince-bishops who held the title of **Prince of the Empire**
- When the **Reformation** swept through Basel in **1528-1529**, the city adopted Protestantism and the Catholic bishop was expelled — but the prince-bishop retained control of his **territorial possessions** outside the city
- The bishop relocated his official residence to **Porrentruy in 1527/1528**, making this small Ajoie town the de facto **capital** of the bishopric's remaining Catholic domains
- For **264 years** (1528-1792), a succession of **15 prince-bishops** ruled from Porrentruy, transforming it into a small but splendid princely court
- The most influential was **Jakob Christoph Blarer von Wartensee** (1575-1608), a champion of the **Counter-Reformation** who:
  - Reformed the clergy through new statutes proclaimed at the **diocesan synod in Delemont (1581)**
  - Conducted two cycles of **parish visitations** (1586-1590, 1601-1604) to enforce Catholic orthodoxy
  - Founded a **Jesuit college** in Porrentruy in **1591** (the building later became the **Lycee cantonal**)
  - Added a **seminary** in **1606**
  - Commissioned the **reconstruction of the castle** by Nicolas Frick (c. 1588)
- Other religious orders followed the Jesuits: **Ursulines** (1619), **Sisters of the Annonciade** (1646), **Capuchins** (1663) — making Porrentruy a bastion of the Counter-Reformation
- The **Hotel-Dieu** (hospital), designed by architect **Pierre-Francois Paris** around **1765** for the last three prince-bishops, functioned as a hospital until **1956** and today houses a **museum** with a magnificent 19th-century pharmacy crafted by cabinetmaker **Jean-Baptiste Carraz** in **1846**
- The prince-bishops also built a **glaciere** (ice house) in the 18th century to preserve ice for cooling food and drinks at the episcopal court

## 5. The Medieval Old Town — Gothic, Baroque, and Renaissance

- Porrentruy's old town (la **vieille ville**) contains an exceptional ensemble of **bourgeois houses** in Gothic, Baroque, and neoclassical styles, reflecting centuries of episcopal patronage
- The **Eglise Saint-Pierre** (Church of Saint Peter), the most important **Gothic monument** in the Canton of Jura, was built between **1321 and 1349** as a basilica; it became the parish church in **1475**, replacing the older Church of Saint-Germain
- A restoration by architect **Philippe Gressot** in **1978-1982** revealed the church's original medieval structure beneath later modifications
- The town boasts several **monumental Renaissance fountains** by sculptor **Laurent Perroud de Cressier**, who also created fountains in Delemont, Neuchatel, and other Swiss cities:
  - **Fontaine du Banneret** (also called Fontaine du Suisse, **1558**): the first monumental fountain in Porrentruy, featuring a soldier bearing a banner atop a remarkable late-Renaissance sculptural shaft; the stones were quarried in Cressier, transported by boat to Bienne, and then by cart to Porrentruy
  - **Fontaine de la Samaritaine** (**1564**): depicts Christ and the Samaritan woman at the well, with richly carved pillars and a figure of the infant Saint John the Baptist at the top
  - Perroud created a total of **4 monumental fountains** in Porrentruy
- Many late-medieval and Renaissance **bourgeois houses** (maisons bourgeoises) with ornate facades line the streets of the old town

## 6. The Porte de France — Last Surviving Medieval Gate

- The **Porte de France** (France Gate) is Porrentruy's **sole surviving medieval city gate**, standing at the northern entrance to the old town on the road toward France
- Originally a medieval gateway, the current structure was **rebuilt in 1563** on older substructures and **refurbished in 1744**
- The gate features **two sturdy round towers** capped with **conical roofs** flanking a central passageway, topped by a **belfry** (bell tower)
- A **clock** (horloge) was added to the gate in the late 17th century, with clock faces on both the town and countryside sides
- The gate's name reflects Porrentruy's identity as a **border town** — it literally pointed the way to France, just 10 km to the north
- The Porte de France remains one of the most photographed landmarks of the town and a symbol of Porrentruy's medieval heritage

## 7. The French Revolution — Rauracian Republic and the End of the Prince-Bishops

- On **27 April 1792**, French Revolutionary troops invaded Porrentruy and drove out the last prince-bishop, **Joseph Sigismund von Roggenbach**, ending 264 years of episcopal rule
- On **17 December 1792**, the **Republique rauracienne** (Rauracian Republic) was proclaimed in Porrentruy — it was the **first "sister republic"** created by revolutionary France, named after the ancient Celtic tribe of the **Rauraci** who had inhabited the region
- The republic adopted a constitution modeled on French Jacobin principles, but lasted only **three months**
- On **23 March 1793**, the French Convention accepted a request from Porrentruy delegates to annex the territory, creating the **Departement du Mont-Terrible** with Porrentruy as its **chef-lieu** (capital)
- In **1800**, under Napoleon's Consulate, the Departement du Mont-Terrible was dissolved and its territory absorbed into the **Departement du Haut-Rhin**
- After Napoleon's defeat, the **Congress of Vienna (1815)** assigned the former prince-bishopric territories — including Porrentruy and the entire Ajoie — to the **Canton of Bern**, despite the region being French-speaking and Catholic while Bern was German-speaking and Protestant
- This mismatch planted the seeds of the **Question jurassienne** — the Jura separatist movement that would simmer for over 150 years

## 8. The Question Jurassienne — From Protest to New Canton

- The forced incorporation of the French-speaking, Catholic Jura into the German-speaking, Protestant Canton of Bern in **1815** created lasting tensions over language, religion, and cultural identity
- The spark came on **20 September 1947** with the **Moeckli affair**: the Bernese Grand Council refused to entrust the Department of Public Works to Jurassian politician **Georges Moeckli** on the grounds that such an important sector could not be led by a French-speaker — provoking outrage across the Jura
- In **1947**, a separatist **Comite de Moutier** was formed; by **1949**, this became the **Rassemblement jurassien** (Jura Assembly), the main separatist organization
- In **1963**, militant young separatists formed the **Beliers** ("Rams"), who carried out dramatic protests; their anti-separatist counterpart, the **Sangliers** ("Wild Boars"), formed in **1973**
- On **23 June 1974**, a cantonal referendum resulted in the decision to form a new canton — the three northern districts of **Delemont**, **Porrentruy**, and **Franches-Montagnes** voted overwhelmingly to secede from Bern
- On **24 September 1978**, **82.3%** of Swiss voters approved the creation of the new canton
- On **1 January 1979**, the **Canton of Jura** officially joined the Swiss Confederation as its **26th canton** — Switzerland's youngest
- Porrentruy, despite its historical importance as the prince-bishops' capital, was not chosen as the cantonal capital — that honor went to **Delemont**, which was more centrally located
- The canton celebrates its **independence day** on **23 June** each year, commemorating the 1974 vote
- The Jura question continued: in **2021**, the town of **Moutier** voted to leave the Canton of Bern and join the Canton of Jura

## 9. Jurassic Dinosaur Footprints — The Real Jurassic Park

- The Jura Mountains gave their name to the **Jurassic period** (201-145 million years ago), and the Ajoie region around Porrentruy contains some of the most important **dinosaur tracksites** in Europe
- Since **2002**, construction of the **Transjurane highway** (A16 motorway) has uncovered six major dinosaur tracksites in the Porrentruy district, yielding over **14,000 estimated footprints** across the wider Jura carbonate platform
- The tracks date to the **Kimmeridgian stage** of the Late Jurassic, approximately **152 million years ago**, when the region was a **shallow tropical lagoon** on the edge of the Tethys Sea
- Both **sauropod** (long-necked herbivore) and **theropod** (bipedal carnivore, likely **Allosaurus**-type) tracks have been identified, with some sauropod prints reaching **over 1 meter in diameter**
- Key sites include **Courtedoux-Bechat Bovais**, **Courtedoux-Bois de Sylleux**, **Courtedoux-Tchaffoue**, **Chevenez-Combe Ronde**, and **Porrentruy-CPP** — all located within about 6 km of the town center
- At the **Chevenez-Combe Ronde** site, a special **bridge was built in 2006** to span and protect the trackway during highway construction
- The **JURASSICA Dinotec** in Porrentruy displays over **100 tracks** uncovered in 2007 during facility renovation, protected by large glass panels — visitors can walk above real sauropod and theropod footprints; entry is **free**, open **24 hours** from April to October
- The **"Sur les traces des geants"** (In the Footsteps of Giants) educational trail at **Courtedoux** offers a one-hour walk culminating at a site with **152-million-year-old sauropod tracks** still visible in the rock

## 10. The Jardin Botanique — One of Switzerland's Oldest

- The **Jardin botanique de Porrentruy** (Botanical Garden), now part of the **JURASSICA** museum network, dates from around **1800** and is one of the **oldest botanical gardens in Switzerland**
- Located in the grounds of the **former Jesuit monastery** (the college founded by Blarer von Wartensee in 1591), the garden combines centuries of botanical heritage with the region's paleontological treasures
- Collections include: over **80 varieties of roses**, **180 varieties of iris** (one of the best iris collections in Europe), a **Jurassian Garden** with **800 species of indigenous plants**, and thematic greenhouses with **tropical plants, orchids, bromeliads, and carnivorous plants**
- Large **century-old trees** shade the wooded park, and springtime iris blooms "offer an experience of rare beauty"
- The JURASSICA Museum (the region's natural history museum) is currently closed for renovations until **2027**, but the botanical garden and Dinotec satellite remain open

## 11. Local Traditions — The Fete de la Saint-Martin and Damassine

### The Fete de la Saint-Martin (Feast of Saint Martin)
- The **Saint-Martin** is an ancestral harvest festival celebrated across the Ajoie region on the **second weekend of November**, marking the end of the agricultural year
- Originally tied to the feast day of **Saint Martin of Tours** (November 11) — the medieval date when farm rents were paid and granaries were filled — the festival has evolved into a celebration centered on **the pig**
- The highlight is the **bouchoyade**: the traditional public slaughter and nose-to-tail preparation of a pig, yielding dozens of dishes including **bouillon**, **fromage de tete** (brawn from trotters, ears, head, snout, tail), **blood sausage with cream**, **cotines** (rib cuts), **sauerkraut with lard and smoked pork**, **roast pork with rosti**, and **totche** (cream cake) for dessert
- The **totche** (from the Jurassian dialect for simply "cake") is a **sour cream tart** on leavened dough, considered **the** representative pastry of the Jura — tangy, salty, and distinctive
- A **Damassine sorbet** and the plum brandy itself accompany the feast for digestion
- When Saint-Martin falls on a Sunday, Monday becomes an **official local holiday** for Ajoie residents; the following weekend features the **Revirat** (a second celebration)
- A **market** in Porrentruy with over **55 vendors** of local products accompanies both weekends

### Damassine AOP
- **Damassine** is an eau-de-vie (fruit brandy) distilled from the **damasson rouge** — a small wild plum with intense fragrance, according to legend brought back from **Damascus** by **Crusaders in the 13th century**
- It takes over **100 plums** to produce one kilogram of fruit, and nearly **900 plums** to distill a single liter of brandy
- The fruit falls naturally from trees between mid-July and September and is collected **daily by hand** into nets; crushing and pitting are prohibited
- Distillation must be completed by **December 31** of the harvest year, and the brandy must age at least **6 months** before sale — it cannot be marketed before **Saint-Martin's Day** (November 11)
- Damassine received **AOP** (Appellation d'Origine Protegee) status in **2010**, the first such designation for a Jura spirit; all production stages must take place within the Canton of Jura

## 12. Watchmaking — Porrentruy's Industrial Transformation

- In the 19th century, Porrentruy transformed from a quiet episcopal trading town into an **important center of Swiss watchmaking**
- On **9 December 1842**, prefect **Joseph Choffat** launched a shareholders' company to introduce watchmaking to the Ajoie region — a deliberate economic development initiative for this isolated border area
- Choffat established the **first watchmaking apprenticeship workshop** in **Porrentruy Castle's hospice**, training poor and orphaned children; the workshop operated from **1842 to 1850**, producing **32 trained apprentices**
- Growth was rapid: by **1866**, there were **57 watchmaking workshops** in Porrentruy; by **1871**, the number had reached **120**
- A formal **watchmaking school** (Ecole d'horlogerie) opened in **1884**, recognizing that "without scientific knowledge it is impossible to keep abreast of new improvements"
- Notable early manufacturers included **Emile Juillard** (who in 1872 transformed a family workshop into a watch factory, creating brands like **Bulla** and **Carpe Diem**), **Phenix** (est. 1873), and **Helios** (est. 1883, employing 100 workers by 1958)
- A supporting network of specialist suppliers grew: casemakers, crystal cutters, dial manufacturers, and component producers
- Today the Jura watchmaking tradition continues with major brands operating in the broader region, and independent watchmakers maintaining the craft in Porrentruy itself

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The prince-bishop's last night** — On 27 April 1792, a child living in the castle watches as French soldiers approach the gates of Porrentruy; the prince-bishop's cat leads the child through secret passages in the Tour Refous, showing 264 years of memories hidden in the walls before the bishop flees forever

2. **The dinosaurs beneath the road** — During construction of a new road near Courtedoux, a child discovers that stepping into a 152-million-year-old sauropod footprint transports them back to the Jurassic period, when the Ajoie was a warm tropical lagoon teeming with enormous creatures

3. **The Porte de France and the two countries** — A child discovers that passing through the Porte de France at midnight lets them walk between two worlds: Porrentruy as a prince-bishop's court in 1750 and Porrentruy as a French departement capital in 1793, and they must find a way to reconcile the two

4. **900 plums for one wish** — A child must collect exactly 900 damassine plums before Saint-Martin's Day to distill a magical Damassine that grants one wish; each plum tells a story from Ajoie's history as it falls from the tree

5. **The clockmaker's apprentice** — In 1842, a poor orphan child enters Joseph Choffat's watchmaking workshop in the castle and discovers that each tiny gear they craft contains a miniature world; completing their first watch opens a door to the castle's princely past

6. **The 800 plants of the Jurassian Garden** — A child exploring the botanical garden at night finds that each of the 800 indigenous plant species whispers in a different voice; together they tell the complete story of the Jura — from tropical lagoon to limestone mountain to the canton's independence

7. **The bouchoyade mystery** — At the Saint-Martin festival, a child follows a lost piglet through the market stalls and medieval streets of Porrentruy, learning the traditions and recipes of each generation of Ajoie families, from the Middle Ages to today

8. **The tower that remembers** — The Tour Refous, 32 meters tall with walls 4.5 meters thick, has stood watch over Porrentruy since the 13th century; a child who touches the ancient stones hears the tower narrate everything it has witnessed — bishops, Jesuits, French soldiers, separatist marchers, and the joyful day in 1979 when the Jura became free
