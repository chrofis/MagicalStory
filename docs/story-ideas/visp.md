## 1. Founding and Origins — Market Town on the Hill

- First documented mention of Visp: **1214**, though settlement of the area is considerably older
- The name **Visp** (French: **Viege**) likely derives from a pre-Roman root; its exact etymology remains debated
- Old Visp developed on the **hilly terrain above the valley floor**, because the lowlands were subject to frequent and devastating **flooding** by the Rhone and Vispa rivers
- The **Bishop of Sion**, who since **999** held both spiritual and secular authority over the Valais, delegated power to a **Meier** (steward) for the **Zenden** (district) of Visp
- The stewards initially came from local nobility; around **1250**, the daughter of the then-steward married Count **Gottfried von Biandrate** from Piedmont, bringing outside feudal influence into the town
- A **Ringmauer** (ring wall) was built on the hill for better defence, forming the nucleus of the medieval old town
- The oldest surviving building is the **Meierturm** (Steward's Tower), dating from the **12th century** — a stone tower that served as the seat of the bishop's representative
- In **1354**, Emperor **Charles IV** confirmed the liberties of the seven **Zenden** (tithings), including Visp, formalising its status as one of the self-governing districts of Upper Valais

## 2. Geography — Gateway to the High Alps

- Visp lies in the **upper Rhone valley** (Rhonetal) at approximately **658 metres** above sea level, about **9 km west of Brig-Glis**
- The town sits at the **confluence of the Vispa and the Rhone** — a strategic position that has shaped its identity for centuries
- The **Vispa** (Visp river) is formed at **Stalden** by the merging of two major branches: the **Matter Vispa** (flowing from the Matterhorn valley and Zermatt) and the **Saaser Vispa** (flowing from the Saas valley and Saas-Fee)
- This makes Visp the natural **gateway** to two of Switzerland's most famous mountain destinations: **Zermatt** (home of the Matterhorn) and **Saas-Fee** (the "Pearl of the Alps")
- The town enjoys one of the **driest and sunniest climates** in Switzerland — over **2,100 hours of sunshine** per year, with only about 600 mm of precipitation annually
- The sheltered position deep within the Rhone valley creates a near-Mediterranean microclimate: vineyards, pergolas, and fig trees give Visp an almost **Italian flair** (italienisches Flair)
- Population: approximately **8,200** inhabitants, but the town provides around **12,000 jobs**, making it an economic centre far larger than its residential population suggests

## 3. The Battle of Visp (1388) — Birth of Zenden Democracy

- In autumn **1387**, Count **Amadeus VII of Savoy** launched a military campaign against the Upper Valais, seeking to extend Savoyard control over the German-speaking communities
- Count **Rudolf von Greyerz**, allied with Savoy, advanced eastward in **December 1388** with forces drawn from Saanen, Gruyere, Vaud, the Dauphine, and Savoy
- The invaders overwhelmed **Raron** and camped on the frozen Rhone plain before the fortress of Visp, confident of victory
- They issued an **ultimatum** demanding the bloodless surrender of Visp
- Instead, the defenders launched a daring **night attack**: before dawn, brave Visper crept into the enemy camp and **set fire to barns, sheds, and tents** where the Savoyard leaders were quartered
- The leaders perished in the flames; **panic** swept through the invading army, and those not killed by the Walliser fled westward
- The **Blauer Stein** (Blue Stone) — a glacial erratic boulder of blue-green serpentinite, approximately **15,000 years old** — stands in the middle of **St. Martinistrasse** as a memorial; in **1938**, for the 550th anniversary, the date "1388" was engraved into its surface
- The battle marked a turning point: it inaugurated a new era of **Zenden democracy** (Zendendemokratie) in the Upper Valais, as the communities gradually wrested secular power from the bishop and feudal lords
- The nearby **Gamsenmauer** (Gamsen boundary wall), built between **1352 and 1355**, served as a defensive **Letzi** (barrier wall) protecting the Upper Valais from western attack — it is considered the **best-preserved Letzi in Switzerland**

## 4. The Great Fire of 1518 and the Medieval Old Town

- In **1518**, a devastating **fire** (Grossbrand) destroyed almost the entire town of Visp
- Combined with earlier fires, this catastrophe left only a handful of medieval structures intact
- Despite the destruction, the old town was rebuilt and today features a beautifully restored ensemble of historic buildings, churches, and squares
- Key landmarks of the **Altstadt**:
  - **Meierturm** (Steward's Tower, 12th century): the oldest building in Visp, once the seat of the bishop's representative
  - **Lochmatterturm** (12th century): a striking medieval tower with colourful masonry and window modifications from the High Middle Ages; named after the **Lochmatter** castellan family, who owned it for approximately 200 years until about 1880
  - **Burgenerhaus** (1699): a splendid patrician house, home of Regional Governor **Johann-Jodok Burgener** (1657-1721)
  - Remnants of the **Stadtmauer** (city wall) still ring parts of the old town
- The entire small city of Visp is designated as part of the **Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites** (ISOS)

## 5. The Two Churches — Martinskirche and Burgerkirche

### Pfarrkirche St. Martin (Parish Church of St. Martin)
- The parish church sits prominently on the **Martiniplatz** in the old town
- The **tower** dates from the **14th century**, while the present church building was constructed between **1710 and 1730**
- Serves as the main parish church for Visp, Eyholz, and Baltschieder

### Burgerkirche Heilige Drei Koenige (Church of the Holy Three Kings)
- A place of worship has stood at this site since the **11th century**
- First mentioned in a document in **1220** as the **Church of Our Lady** (Kirche Unserer Lieben Frau)
- The **Gothic tower** dates from the first half of the **14th century**; a **crypt** from the **15th century** lies beneath the choir
- The current church was built at the beginning of the **18th century** by the **Burgerschaft** (citizen community) and consecrated to the **Mother of God** in **1730**
- From **1754**, both the Virgin Mary and the **Three Kings** (Heilige Drei Koenige) were listed as patron saints; eventually the Three Kings became the exclusive patrons
- Features a notable **high altar** by the Italian sculptor **Giovanni Battista Rappa**, dedicated to the Holy Three Kings
- In **1937**, the church passed from the Burgerschaft to the Visp parish community

## 6. The Earthquake of 1855 — Switzerland's Strongest in 300 Years

- On **25 July 1855**, a powerful earthquake struck the middle Valais with an **epicentral intensity of VIII** and an estimated **moment magnitude of 6.2**
- This remains the **strongest earthquake in Switzerland in the last 300 years**
- The maximum damage was concentrated in the **Vispertal** (Visp valley), where it destroyed large parts of Visp, **Stalden**, **Toerbel**, **Graechen**, and **St. Niklaus**
- **One person** was killed and dozens were injured
- Hundreds of **boulders** were dislodged, entire hillsides collapsed into the valley, roads were buried, existing springs dried up, and new ones emerged
- The earthquake was felt as far as **Paris**, **Lyon**, **Genoa**, **Ingolstadt**, and **Mainz**; it produced minor building damage in Geneva, Basel, Zurich, and Schaffhausen
- Dozens of **aftershocks** followed for months and years afterwards, some reaching intensity VII
- The earthquake became a milestone in science: researchers **G.H.O. Volger** and **A. Petermann** used the event to develop early methods of **macroseismic damage classification** — a foundational contribution to modern seismology
- The Valais remains Switzerland's most **earthquake-prone region** today, due to tectonic forces along the Rhone valley fault system

## 7. Lonza — From Electricity to Global Pharma

- **Lonza** was founded in **1897** in the nearby village of **Gampel**, initially as an **electricity producer**, taking its name from the Lonza river
- In **1909**, Lonza moved to **Visp** to scale up operations — a decision that transformed the town's economy
- The company transitioned from electricity to **chemical manufacturing**: first producing calcium carbide, then from **1915** onwards **calcium cyanamide** (a cheap nitrogenous fertiliser)
- In **1984**, Lonza opened a new **fine chemicals complex** in Visp, a landmark event attended by the Bishop of Sion
- Today, Lonza Visp is the company's **largest and oldest site** in a global network of over 30 facilities
- The site specialises in **pharmaceutical manufacturing**: mammalian and microbial biopharmaceuticals, small molecules, highly potent APIs, peptides, and antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs)
- Lonza is by far the **largest employer** in the district, with continuous expansion including new mRNA and bioconjugation facilities
- The transformation from a sleepy market town to an industrial and pharmaceutical centre is one of the defining stories of modern Visp

## 8. Railway Hub — The Loetschberg and the Road to Zermatt

- The **Visp-Zermatt railway** (Visp-Zermatt-Bahn) opened in stages: **Visp to Stalden** on 3 July **1890**, reaching **Zermatt** on 18 July **1891**
- The nine-hour journey from Visp to Zermatt by road was reduced to **two and a half hours** by rail
- In the first summer season of **1891**, an impressive **33,695 travellers** used the new line — the beginning of mass tourism to the Matterhorn
- The Visp-Zermatt railway later merged (in **2003**) with the Furka-Oberalp-Bahn to form the **Matterhorn Gotthard Bahn (MGB)**, which today operates the famous **Glacier Express** route
- With the opening of the **Loetschberg Base Tunnel** (34.6 km) on 9 December **2007**, Visp became a **standard gauge junction station**, slashing travel time to Bern by 50%
- The station was completely rebuilt for the tunnel opening; its modern building with **blue mirror glass cladding** won the inaugural **FLUX Prize** in 2007 for best-designed Swiss transport hub
- Today, Visp station is the **busiest railway station in the Valais**: approximately **230 trains** stop daily, serving around **25,300 passengers**
- Visp is the junction where travellers switch between the **SBB mainline** (Bern-Brig-Milan) and the **MGB metre-gauge** line to Zermatt and Saas-Fee

## 9. Visperterminen — Europe's Highest Vineyard

- The village of **Visperterminen** (Walliserdeutsch: **Tarbinu**), perched at **1,378 metres** above Visp, is home to the **highest vineyards in Europe**, reaching up to **1,150 metres** above sea level
- The signature wine is **Heida** (also known as **Paien** or **Savagnin Blanc**) — an aromatic white wine pressed from an ancient grape variety that thrives on the clay and sand soils, strong sun, and high elevation
- About **42 hectares** of vines are cultivated on extremely steep terraces; harvesting must be done **entirely by hand**
- Of the approximately **300,000 litres** of wine produced annually, Heida accounts for around a third
- The **Heida wine hike** (Heidaweg) from Visp up to Visperterminen is a popular excursion, passing through terraced vineyards with views of the snow-capped 4,000-metre peaks of the Valais and Bernese Alps
- Wine culture in the Visp region dates back centuries, nurtured by the same dry, sun-drenched microclimate that gives the town its Mediterranean character

## 10. Notable People and the Walliserdeutsch Identity

### Sepp Blatter
- **Joseph "Sepp" Blatter**, born **10 March 1936** in Visp, became the **eighth President of FIFA** (1998-2015)
- He grew up in Visp and remained closely connected to the town throughout his life; a school building in Visp bears his name
- Before FIFA, Blatter worked in public relations for the Valais tourist board and as general secretary of the Swiss Ice Hockey Federation

### Walliserdeutsch
- Visp lies in the heart of **Oberwallis** (Upper Valais), where the population speaks **Walliserdeutsch** — one of the most archaic Alemannic dialect groups in the German-speaking world
- Walliserdeutsch preserves features of **Old High German** that have disappeared from most other Swiss German dialects, including a functional **genitive case** (ds Vatters Huis = the father's house)
- The dialect includes numerous **French loanwords** due to centuries of proximity to the Francophone Lower Valais
- Approximately **80,000 people** in Upper Valais speak Walliserdeutsch; the dialect is a source of deep local pride and identity
- Common Walliserdeutsch terms: **Zenden** (district/tithing), **Meier** (steward), **Hergottstag** (Corpus Christi), **Suon/Bisse** (irrigation channel), **Tschaeggaettae** (masked carnival figures from the Loetschental)

## 11. Traditions and Local Customs

- **Corpus Christi** (Hergottstag / Fronleichnam): one of the most important festivals in the Catholic Upper Valais; village communities in traditional **Walliser Trachten** (costumes) lead processions through decorated streets
- The **Tschaeggaettae** tradition from the nearby Loetschental — masked, fur-clad wild figures who roam the villages during carnival — is the most famous folk custom of the Upper Valais region
- Music, brass bands, and **Alphorn** playing remain central to community life
- The **Ringkuhkampf** (cow fighting / Combat de Reines) is a beloved Valais tradition in which **Eringer** cows (a small, muscular breed unique to the region) battle for dominance; regional competitions are held throughout the valley
- Visp's proximity to both **Zermatt** and **Saas-Fee** makes it a natural staging point for mountain culture, from summer hiking and vineyard festivals to winter sports

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The night the fires burned** — A child in medieval Visp is woken before dawn on a freezing December night in 1388; they watch as brave defenders creep into the sleeping Savoyard camp and set fire to the tents — and must carry the victory signal across the frozen Rhone to the allied villages
2. **The Blue Stone's secret** — A child touches the ancient Blauer Stein on St. Martinistrasse and is pulled 15,000 years back to when a glacier deposited the boulder; they journey forward through time at each touch — Neolithic farmers, the 1388 battle, the 1518 fire, the 1855 earthquake — witnessing Visp's story through the stone's unblinking eye
3. **When the mountain shook** — On a summer day in 1855, a child in Visp feels the ground tremble; reliving the great earthquake, they race through collapsing streets, help neighbours escape falling stones, and watch in awe as new springs burst from the hillside while old ones vanish
4. **The train to the Matterhorn** — A child boards the first Visp-Zermatt train in 1891 and at each station meets a character from a different era: a Roman trader at Stalden, a medieval steward at the Meierturm, a glacier scientist near Zermatt — all connected by the Vispa river flowing alongside the tracks
5. **The vine that climbed too high** — In Visperterminen, a stubborn grapevine keeps growing higher and higher up the mountain, past 1,000 metres, past 1,100, toward the snow line; a child must convince the vine to stop before it reaches the glacier — but the vine has its own plan
6. **The steward's tower** — A child exploring Visp's old town discovers the door to the Meierturm is unlocked; inside, each floor reveals a different century — the bishop's steward dispensing justice in the 13th century, the great fire of 1518 raging outside the windows, Lonza chemists in white coats on the top floor
7. **Two rivers, two valleys** — The Matter Vispa and the Saaser Vispa, personified as twin siblings, race from their glacier sources to see who reaches Visp first; along the way they pass Zermatt and Saas-Fee, collecting stories from mountain villages, before merging at Stalden and arriving in Visp together
8. **The wall that held** — A child discovers the Gamsenmauer near Gamsen and wonders why a wall stretches across the entire valley; transported to 1355, they help the Zenden communities build the great Letzi stone by stone, and learn that a wall built together protects everyone
