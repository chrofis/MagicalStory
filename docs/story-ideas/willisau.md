## 1. Founding — The Hasenburg Brothers and a Town Between Two Hills

- **1101**: First recorded mention of the settlement as **"Willineshôwo"** in historical documents
- **1302–1303**: The three **Hasenburg brothers** — **Markward, Heimo, and Walter** — founded the fortified town of Willisau in the water-rich lowland between the hills of **Gütsch** and **Schlossberg**
- Existing village settlements were demolished to create the new planned town, which had approximately **150 residents**
- The **Freiherren von Hasenburg**, with their family seat at **Asuel in the Jura**, had acquired land in the Willisau area during the **12th century** through church patronage rights
- As privileged vassals of the **Habsburgs**, they held noble status equal to a count, though they never achieved that formal rank
- The Habsburgs granted the new town the right to build **walls and fortifications**, and later the crucial right to hold **markets**
- **1330**: Willisau officially received charter rights for **annual and weekly markets**, cementing its role as a regional trade center
- **1367**: The **Counts of Aarberg** became town lords through marriage into the Hasenburg line
- **1407**: The Hasenburg descendants **sold the town to Lucerne**, making it a territorial administrative center (Landvogtei) for the next four centuries

## 2. Geography — The Napf Foothills and the Wigger Valley

- Willisau sits at **557 meters** elevation in the **Lucerne hinterland**, about **27 km** from the city of Lucerne
- The town lies at the foot of the **Napf mountain** (1,408 m), the highest point of the subalpine ridge separating the cantons of Lucerne and Bern
- The **Wigger river**, approximately **41 km long**, rises from the northern slope of the Napf and flows northward through the valley; it is an important tributary of the Aare
- Willisau's strategic position on the **shortest route from Lucerne to Bern** and western Switzerland made it a natural crossroads for medieval trade
- The landscape is predominantly **rural**, with rolling farmland, forests, and livestock pastures characteristic of the Lucerne Mittelland
- The nearby **UNESCO Biosphere Entlebuch** (designated **2001**, Switzerland's first biosphere reserve) covers **39,000 hectares** of moor landscapes, karst terrain, and peat bogs just to the south — declared a **model biosphere for the world** by UNESCO in 2011
- Modern municipality area: **41.69 km²**, population approximately **7,800**

## 3. Four Great Fires — A Town That Refused to Die

- Willisau was destroyed by fire **four times** over its history — an extraordinary cycle of destruction and rebuilding
- **1375**: During the **Gugler invasion** (Guglerkrieg), Duke **Leopold of Austria** ordered the still poorly fortified town burned to prevent its capture by the English and French mercenaries raiding through the region
- **1386**: Just eleven years later, during the **Sempach War**, Duke Leopold again ordered Willisau burned — this time to prevent it from falling into Swiss hands. He marched south through the town on his way to the **Battle of Sempach** (9 July 1386), where he was killed and the Confederates won a decisive victory
- **1471**: A third fire destroyed much of the town, including the **Obertor (Upper Gate)**, which was rebuilt in the 16th century
- **1704**: The fourth and final fire destroyed **most of the town**. This last catastrophe ended the era of wooden construction: the townspeople rebuilt in **stone**, sourcing material from the surrounding area. The fire fundamentally transformed Willisau from a medieval timber town into the stone-built Baroque streetscape visible today
- Houses on the **Müligass** that survived the 1704 fire were plastered over to match the new stone aesthetic — some of these **Gothic-era buildings from the 1590s** still stand

## 4. The Obertor and Town Fortifications

- The **Obertor (Upper Gate)** is the western tower gate forming the upper entrance to the old town, bearing the date **1547** on the town side — it was completed around **1551**
- The Obertor **survived the 1704 fire**, making it one of the oldest standing structures in the town
- Until the end of the **19th century**, the Obertor served as a **prison**, with the original cells still preserved inside; access was only possible through a **ladder and ceiling hatch**
- The tower was renovated in **2012–2013**
- The **Untertor (Lower Gate)**, first mentioned in **1347**, was less fortunate: burned in 1471, rebuilt, burned again in 1704, rebuilt once more, then **demolished in 1854** due to dilapidation, and finally **reconstructed in 1980** based on historical models
- The **Hauptgasse** (main street) runs between the two gates, lined with painted facades, decorated signboards, and **three distinctive heptagonal (seven-sided) fountains** that divide the broad street into sections
- The **Kutzenturm**, a fortification tower built at the end of the **14th / beginning of the 15th century**, survives from the original **ring wall** — it stands open toward the city side, typical of medieval defensive architecture
- The ring wall originally connected the eastern and western rows of houses (whose rear walls doubled as town walls) to the castle hill; remnants of the free-standing wall survive on the **west side of the hillside**

## 5. The Heilig-Blut-Legende (Holy Blood Legend)

- According to tradition, **on 7 July 1392**, three men were gambling at the **Lustgarten** (pleasure garden) near Willisau
- After losing all his money, a man named **Ueli Schröter** drew his sword in rage and thrust it into the air, crying out that he wished to pierce the body of Christ
- **Five drops of blood** allegedly fell onto the gambling table — echoing the **five stigmata of Christ**
- The blasphemer was said to have been **taken by the devil** amid terrifying noise
- The two remaining gamblers tried to wash the blood from the table in a nearby stream but **could not remove the stains**; during the ensuing dispute, one killed the other, and the survivor died of lice before reaching the town gates
- The **oldest written record** of the legend dates from **1498** — but was missing three key elements: the date, the culprit's name, and the number of blood drops
- In **1564**, at the start of the **Counter-Reformation**, the local priest added the precise details: the date (1392), the name (Ueli Schröter), and the five drops — giving the legend its enduring form
- The blood drops are preserved in a **monstrance** and a major **Sühneprozession** (atonement procession) takes place annually on the second Sunday after Pentecost

## 6. The Heiligblutkapelle (Holy Blood Chapel)

- **1452**: A first chapel was built, just five years after a similar sacred host miracle at nearby **Ettiswil** (1447)
- **1497**: Replaced by a **Gothic** structure
- **1674**: The current chapel was constructed in a **Baroque/Renaissance** style — the building that stands today
- The interior houses **three ornate Early Baroque wooden altars** from the late 1600s
- **Eight oil paintings** depicting scenes from the Holy Blood Legend decorate the chapel walls (added **1684**)
- A **painted wooden ceiling** added in **1854** depicts **70 biblical scenes**, including New Testament episodes, the Apostles, and secondary patron saints
- The **Lustgarten** (pleasure garden) adjacent to the chapel has been maintained as a symbolic space: **five red-flowering or red-leaved plant beds** represent the five legendary blood drops
- The chapel remains a pilgrimage site and is listed as a heritage building of regional significance

## 7. The Willisauer Ringli — Switzerland's Famous Hard Biscuit

- The **Willisauer Ringli** is a small, ring-shaped biscuit (**3 cm diameter**) famous for its extreme hardness and its honey-lemon-orange flavor
- **Around 1850**: Baker **Heinrich Maurer** (born 1819 in Aargau), known locally as the **"Berner Beck"**, married into the Peyer family of Willisau and took over a recipe brought by his second wife **Martha Peyer** from **Castle Heidegg**, where she had worked as a housekeeper for the Pfyffer family
- Maurer gave the biscuit its definitive **shape, composition, and name**, selling them from his bakery at **Hauptgasse 26**
- **Original recipe**: 7 pounds sugar, 7 small cups water, rind of two lemons and one orange, approximately 5.5–6 pounds flour — **no fat at all**
- Ingredients today: sugar, wheat flour, honey, lemon peel, candied orange, raising agents, natural flavors — still **virtually fat-free** (0.7 g fat per 100 g)
- The biscuits are so hard they must be **broken by striking them against the palm of the hand** and then **dissolved on the tongue** rather than bitten — to avoid dental damage
- **1892**: The recipe appeared in a published cookbook, spreading its fame beyond the Lucerne region
- **1920–1975**: A **55-year legal battle** over who could produce Ringli culminated in a court ruling that biscuits bearing the Willisauer name may only be manufactured **in the town of Willisau** — an early form of geographic product protection
- Today, the **HUG/Wernli** company operates a **Ringli factory** in Willisau, producing nearly **500 tonnes annually**; artisanal bakeries produce about **14,000 pieces (140 kg) per week**

## 8. The Landvogteischloss (Bailiff's Castle)

- Built **1690–1695** by the City of Lucerne on a hill south of the old town as the residence and administrative seat of the **Landvogt** (bailiff)
- The first bailiff to reside there was **Franz Bernhard Feer**
- From 1407 to 1798, Willisau was administered by a Lucerne bailiff who dispensed justice, executed government orders, and collected taxes; before 1651 the bailiff commuted from Lucerne, but from **1651** onward he maintained a **permanent residence** in Willisau
- The castle is among the most significant **secular Baroque buildings** in central Switzerland
- Interior features include **stucco decoration** by Ticino artists **Augusto Giacomo** and **Pietro Neurone**, as well as notable woodwork and paintings in the courtroom
- Remnants of the original **city wall** are visible on the castle hill
- The building is a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**

## 9. Jazz Festival Willisau — The Mecca of Free Jazz

- **1966**: A young **Niklaus Troxler** (born **1 May 1947** in Willisau) began organizing jazz concerts at the **Hotel Mohren** and **Restaurant Kreuz** in Willisau — a remarkable undertaking for a small town of a few thousand
- By **1974**, these informal concerts had already hosted major artists including **Ornette Coleman**, **Keith Jarrett**, and **Chick Corea**
- **August 1975**: The first official **Jazz Festival Willisau** took place over three days with five concert blocks, featuring **Cecil Taylor, Archie Shepp, John Tchicai, Albert Mangelsdorff**, and **Chris McGregor's Brotherhood of Breath**
- Troxler directed the festival for **35 years** (1975–2009), building it into an internationally renowned forum for **free jazz and improvised music** — earning Willisau the nickname **"The Mecca of Free Jazz"**
- Beyond organizing, Troxler **designed the festival posters** for 35 years; his graphic work is held in the collections of **MoMA New York**, **MoMA Toyama**, the **Stedelijk Museum Amsterdam**, and the **Cooper Hewitt Smithsonian Design Museum**
- Troxler was professor of **communication design** at the **State Academy of Fine Arts Stuttgart** (1998–2013) and member of the **AGI (Alliance Graphique Internationale)** since 1989
- **2009**: Troxler handed the festival to his nephew **Arno Troxler**, who continues the tradition while adding new focal points
- **2009**: Troxler donated his private collection to the **Hochschule Luzern** music library; sound recordings are preserved at the **Swiss National Sound Archives** in Lugano
- Over **1,000 concerts** have been documented since 1966, spanning free jazz, modern jazz, and avant-garde improvisation — a remarkable cultural legacy for a small Swiss market town

## 10. The 1653 Peasants' War and Willisau's Rebel Legacy

- The **Swiss Peasants' War of 1653** (Schweizer Bauernkrieg) was a major popular revolt against the Ancien Regime, sparked by currency devaluation in Bern
- The uprising spread from the **Entlebuch valley** (just south of Willisau) across the cantons of Lucerne, Bern, Solothurn, and Basel
- Willisau was an **important meeting point of the insurgents**, given its position between the Entlebuch and the wider Mittelland
- Key rebel leaders included **Christian Schibi** of Escholzmatt and **Hans Emmenegger** of Schüpfheim — both from the Entlebuch, Willisau's immediate hinterland
- Local figure **Johann Ulrich Amstein**, innkeeper of the Sternenwirt in Willisau, was punished with **ten years of galley service** for his role
- The revolt was crushed; Schibi was tortured and killed, Leuenberger (the Emmental leader) was executed
- **1942–1943**: Facade paintings on the **Gasthaus Adler** in Willisau were created to commemorate the revolt, depicting four rebel leaders: **Antoni Farnbühler, Niklaus Leuenberger, Christian Schibi**, and **Johann Jakob Peyer**

## 11. The Parish Church of Saints Peter and Paul

- The **Romanesque tower** dates from after **1200**, making it the oldest surviving element of the church
- **1647**: An additional storey with a **Baroque dome** was added to the tower
- **1804–1810**: The church was rebuilt in a **Neoclassical** style by architect **Josef Purtschert** — one of the largest parish churches in the Lucerne region
- Artist **Xaver Hecht** (who settled in Willisau in 1803) created the **altarpiece and ceiling frescoes**; Hecht also founded a **painting school** in Willisau that trained notable artists including **Lorenz Justin Ritz** and sculptor **Anton Ritz**
- **1929**: A monumental **copper bell tower** was installed
- The church is listed as a **cultural property of national significance**
- The **Willisau Society of Theatre- and Music-Lovers**, founded in **1804**, created a theatre auditorium on the first floor of the Merchants' Hall (Kaufhaus) in 1811 — evidence of the town's longstanding cultural ambitions

## 12. The Merchants' Hall and Modern Willisau

- The **Kaufhaus (Merchants' Hall / Town Hall)** was erected in **1720** in the middle of the Hauptgasse — an unusual placement that underscores the town's mercantile identity
- The ground floor served as the **town slaughterhouse** until the 1950s
- A **Jugendstil (Art Nouveau) facade** was added in **1912**
- **2006**: The municipalities of **Willisau-Stadt** and **Willisau-Land** were reunified into a single municipality
- **2021**: The former municipality of **Gettnau** merged into Willisau
- Today Willisau maintains its character as a small, well-preserved market town — home to the Ringli factory, the jazz festival, pilgrimage traditions, and a remarkably intact medieval streetscape between two gate towers

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The five drops on the table** — A child discovers the Lustgarten and accidentally time-travels to 1392, witnessing the fateful card game; must find the Holy Blood monstrance to return home, following clues through the chapel's eight oil paintings
2. **The Ringli that wouldn't break** — A magic Willisauer Ringli grants wishes when you crack it, but this one is unbreakable; the child journeys from the bakery on Hauptgasse 26 to Castle Heidegg to learn the original secret recipe from Martha Peyer's ghost
3. **Jazz in the Obertor** — Strange music echoes from the old prison cells; a child discovers Niklaus Troxler's first jazz poster has come alive, and famous musicians (Cecil Taylor, Ornette Coleman) step out of the posters for one magical midnight concert
4. **The town that burned four times** — A fire salamander guardian has protected Willisau since 1302; the child helps it through four fires (1375, 1386, 1471, 1704), each time saving one precious thing — the Obertor, the monstrance, the recipe, the church tower
5. **The Kutzenturm watch** — A child becomes the night watchman of the last surviving tower in the ring wall, seeing through its arrow slits into different centuries of Willisau's history
6. **The rebels of the Sternenwirt** — It is 1653 and the innkeeper's child must carry a secret message from the rebel leaders at the Gasthaus Adler to the peasant army in the Entlebuch, slipping past Lucerne's bailiff at the Landvogteischloss
7. **The Lustgarten's five flowers** — Each of the five red plant beds in the chapel garden hides a riddle; solving all five reveals a hidden passage under the Hauptgasse connecting the Obertor to the Untertor
8. **The poster that played music** — A child visiting the Willisau Jazz Archive touches one of Troxler's famous posters and is pulled into a world where colors become sounds; must compose a piece using Willisau's landmarks as musical notes to find the way back
