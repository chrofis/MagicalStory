## 1. Roman Origins — Lousonna on the Lakeshore

- The Celtic peoples of the region fell under Roman rule in **15 B.C.**, and the vicus (civilian settlement) of **Lousonna** was established on the shore of **Lac Léman** at what is today the Vidy district
- The name "Lousonna" is of **Celtic origin**, likely meaning "spring" or "water" — a reference to the natural springs in the area
- At its peak in the **1st–2nd centuries A.D.**, Lousonna housed over **1,500 inhabitants** and featured a **forum with basilica**, a **sanctuary and temple**, a **theatre**, and a **port with dam** for lake trade
- The settlement prospered due to its position on the main **Roman road connecting Italy to Gaul**, linking the Rhine and Rhone trade networks
- By the **4th century**, as Roman stability crumbled and Alemanni raids intensified, the population abandoned the lakeshore and took refuge on the fortified hilltop of the **Cité** — a castrum (fortified camp) that would become the nucleus of modern Lausanne
- Today, the **Musée Romain de Lausanne-Vidy** preserves the archaeological remains of Lousonna, including foundations of a Roman house with original wall paintings

## 2. Geography — A City Built on Three Hills

- Lausanne is built on **three hills** — **Cité**, **Bourg**, and **Saint-Laurent** — with deep valleys carved between them by the rivers **Flon** and **Louve**
- The terrain rises steeply from **370 meters** at the lakeshore to **870 meters** in the forests of **Jorat** to the north, making Lausanne one of Switzerland's steepest cities
- This dramatic topography shaped the city's character: **bridges, staircases, and terraces** connect the different quarters, and the **Escaliers du Marché** (Market Stairs), a medieval covered wooden stairway, links the Palud to the Cité
- The city rests on **molasse** (compacted sandstone) typical of the Swiss Plateau, overlaid by moraines deposited during alpine glaciation
- **Lac Léman** (Lake Geneva), Europe's largest Alpine lake, stretches **73 km** along its northern shore. The French call it Lac Léman; the English name "Lake Geneva" is technically a misnomer, since the lake extends far beyond the city of Geneva
- The southern district of **Ouchy** sits directly on the waterfront, offering views across the lake to the French town of **Évian-les-Bains** and the peaks of the **Chablais Alps**

## 3. Medieval Power — The Bishops of Lausanne

- The **diocese of Lausanne** was established by **517 A.D.**, when the Bishop relocated from Avenches to the fortified Cité hilltop
- In **1011**, King **Rudolph III of Burgundy** granted the **County of Vaud** (comté de Vaud) with all its rights and privileges to **Bishop Henri of Lausanne**, making the bishops temporal lords over the city and surrounding territory
- The bishops were granted the title of **Prince of the Holy Roman Empire** in **1273** — the deed was requested by **Pope Gregory X** himself, on the very day he presided at the consecration of the cathedral
- At its medieval peak in the **13th century**, Lausanne had approximately **9,000 inhabitants** and was a major pilgrimage destination thanks to the cathedral and the cult of the **Virgin Mary**
- The bishops' temporal power was constantly challenged by their nominal protectors (avoués): first the **Counts of Genevois**, then the **Dukes of Zähringen**, and finally the **Counts and Dukes of Savoy**, who steadily encroached on episcopal authority
- In **1481**, the citizens obtained a **municipal charter** (Plaict Général) granting limited self-governance — the beginning of the city's struggle for independence from both bishop and foreign lords

## 4. The Cathédrale Notre-Dame — Switzerland's Finest Gothic Church

- Construction began around **1170–1175** under the direction of multiple master builders, including **Jean Cotereel**, who may have had English origins — explaining the cathedral's unusual blend of French and English Gothic elements
- **Pope Gregory X** consecrated the cathedral to the **Blessed Virgin Mary** on **20 October 1275**, in the presence of **King Rudolf I of Germany** and the former Bishop **Guillaume de Champvent** — one of the grandest ceremonies of the medieval era
- The **Rose Window** (Rosace) on the south transept, created **1220–1230**, measures **8 meters in diameter** and is attributed to **Pierre d'Arras**, a master glazier from northern France. Its 105 panels depict a medieval cosmology: the **four seasons, four elements, four winds, four rivers of paradise**, and the **twelve signs of the zodiac**
- The architect **Villard de Honnecourt** sketched the rose window in his famous notebook around 1230 — one of the earliest architectural drawings in European history
- The **Portail Peint** (Painted Portal, 1215–1230) on the south side features remarkably well-preserved **polychrome sculptures** depicting the Coronation of the Virgin and scenes from the life of Christ — it was the main entrance until the Reformation
- During the **Reformation of 1536**, the cathedral was stripped of its Catholic decorations; the beloved **Vierge Dorée** (Golden Virgin) statue was **melted down**
- The 19th-century architect **Eugène Viollet-le-Duc** (famous for restoring Notre-Dame de Paris) began restoration work on the cathedral, which continues to this day
- **225 steps** lead to the top of the belfry tower, offering panoramic views of the city and lake

## 5. Le Guet — The Night Watchman of the Cathedral

- The tradition of the **guet** (night watchman) dates to **1405**, when a devastating fire prompted the Archbishop to decree a permanent watch from the cathedral tower
- Every night of the year, from **10 p.m. to 2 a.m.**, the watchman climbs **153 steps** to the belfry and calls out to the four cardinal directions: **"C'est le guet; il a sonné l'heure!"** ("This is the nightwatch; the hour has struck!")
- The role was originally practical: the watchman warned of **fires** and rang bells to announce the hours alongside ground guards
- By the 20th century, technology rendered the function obsolete — fire alarms were installed in **1907**, and bells became automated. When discontinuation was proposed in **1960**, a public outcry via letters to regional newspapers saved the tradition
- The current watchman, **Renato Häusler**, has held the position since **2002**, supported by eight deputies. His predecessor, **Philippe Becquelin** (known as cartoonist "Mix et Remix"), was equally beloved
- Lausanne's guet is one of only **seven ceremonial night watchmen** remaining in Europe, and the **only one who calls the hours year-round** from a church tower
- Listed in Switzerland's **Inventory of Living Traditions** (Lebendige Traditionen) as an intangible cultural heritage

## 6. The Bernese Conquest and the Reformation (1536)

- In **January 1536**, the Protestant canton of **Bern** invaded the Pays de Vaud under field captain **Hans Franz Nägeli**, ostensibly to protect Geneva from the Duke of Savoy
- On **13 May 1536**, the first Bernese bailiff (bailli) arrived in Lausanne, ending centuries of episcopal rule. Bishop **Sébastien de Montfaucon** fled to Savoy — the last Bishop of Lausanne to reside in the city
- The **Disputation of Lausanne** (Dispute de Lausanne) took place in the cathedral from **1 to 8 October 1536** — a formal theological debate between Catholics and Protestants. The reformer **Pierre Viret** led the Protestant side; the outcome was predetermined, and the Bernese authorities declared victory for the Reformation
- Catholic worship was made **illegal** throughout the Pays de Vaud; churches were stripped of images, altars, and relics
- The Bernese founded a **theology academy** (Académie de Lausanne, **1537**) — the first Protestant theological school in the French-speaking world, from which Reformed thought spread to congregations across French-speaking Europe
- Bernese rule lasted from **1536 to 1798**, governing through a system of bailiffs and a local oligarchic council called the **Vingt-Quatre** (Twenty-Four)
- In **1723**, Major **Abraham Davel** led a failed uprising against Bernese authority and was executed — he is remembered as a Vaudois patriot and martyr

## 7. Famous Residents and Visitors

- **Voltaire** (1755–1759): Stayed multiple times in Lausanne and staged theatrical performances in a private amphitheatre near the **Villa de Mon-Repos**. He declared Lausanne gave him his **"happiest days"**
- **Edward Gibbon** (1753–1758, 1783–1793): The English historian spent formative years in Lausanne as a teenager, then returned to complete his masterwork **The History of the Decline and Fall of the Roman Empire** (final volumes written **1783–1788**). He famously laid down his pen one evening and walked onto his terrace overlooking the lake under the stars
- **T.S. Eliot** (November 1921 – January 1922): Composed most of **The Waste Land** while under the care of psychiatrist **Dr. Roger Vittoz** at a sanatorium near Lausanne. Vittoz's method of "brain control" — focusing on specific objects, words, and images — has striking parallels to modern mindfulness
- **Coco Chanel**: The fashion empress lived in upper Lausanne for **ten years**, first in the city's palace hotels, then in a house in the **Sauvabelin** area. She is buried in the **Cimetière de Bois-de-Vaux**, her grave adorned with white flowers and **five lions** — her favourite animal
- **David Bowie** (1982–1997): Lived at the **Château du Signal** in the Sauvabelin woods; his civil wedding was celebrated in the **Hôtel de Ville** (Town Hall) by the Place de la Palud
- **King Bhumibol of Thailand**: Arrived in Lausanne aged six and lived there until age 23; in **2007**, Thailand gifted a **gold-leaf Jaturamuk-style pavilion** to Denantou Park in his honour

## 8. The Olympic Capital

- On **10 April 1915**, **Pierre de Coubertin** (founder of the modern Olympics) and **Baron Godefroy de Blonay** met with Lausanne's mayor **Paul Maillefer** and signed an agreement making the city the **permanent headquarters** of the International Olympic Committee (IOC)
- Coubertin chose neutral Switzerland to protect the Olympic movement during **World War I** — the IOC was previously based in Paris, which was under threat
- The first IOC offices were at the **Casino de Montbenon**, high above the city. In **1922**, the city allocated the **Villa Mon-Repos** — the same 18th-century mansion where Voltaire had staged plays — to house the IOC secretariat and a small museum of Olympic artefacts
- The **Olympic Museum** (Musée Olympique) opened on **23 June 1993** (Olympic Day) in a striking building overlooking Lake Geneva in the **Ouchy** waterfront district, designed by architects **Pedro Ramírez Vázquez** and **Jean-Pierre Cahen**
- Lausanne was officially designated the **"Olympic Capital"** (Capitale Olympique) in **1994**
- Today, over **50 international sports federations** and organizations are based in Lausanne and the surrounding region, including **FIFA** (in nearby Zurich) and the **Court of Arbitration for Sport** (TAS/CAS)

## 9. The Flon — From Industrial Valley to Cultural Quarter

- The **Flon** was originally a natural valley with a river running through it; around **1740**, the **Mercier family** established a tannery on its banks, launching an industrial era of sawmills, fullers, and leather works
- In **1832**, a cholera epidemic prompted the city to bridge and cover the Flon and Louve rivers, and the **Pont Pichard** was built with its dramatic **25-meter arches**
- In **1868**, entrepreneurs **Louis Gonin** and **Jean-Jacques Mercier-Marcel** conceived a bold plan: a goods station linked to a Lausanne–Ouchy railway, with warehouses filling the levelled valley
- The **Lausanne–Ouchy funicular** (Switzerland's first public funicular railway) and the Flon station were inaugurated in **1877**; soil from railway tunnels was used to fill and level the valley floor
- By the **1950s**, the depot area fell into decline; warehouses were converted into cheap studios, attracting **artists and craftsmen** who gave the quarter its bohemian character
- A **District Development Plan** adopted in **June 1999** sparked the modern transformation: architects preserved the orthogonal industrial layout and square-plan warehouse buildings while introducing bold contemporary architecture
- Today, **Le Quartier du Flon** is Lausanne's vibrant cultural heart — home to cinemas, galleries, restaurants, clubs, boutiques, and the **Espace Arlaud** exhibition space

## 10. The Lausanne Métro — A Small City, A Big Idea

- Lausanne is the **smallest city in the world** (population approximately **140,000**) with a full **grade-separated metro system**
- The system evolved from the **1877 Lausanne–Ouchy funicular**, Switzerland's first public funicular railway, which connected the city centre to the lakeside
- In **1959**, the funicular was converted into a **rack railway** and renamed the "métro" — the first use of that term in the city
- **Line M2**, a fully automated, driverless, **rubber-tyred metro**, opened on **27 October 2008**, replacing Rennes (France) as the smallest metro city in the world
- The line runs **5.9 km** from **Ouchy-Olympique** on the lakefront to **Croisettes** in the northern suburb of Épalinges, crossing the entire city north to south
- The steep topography presented unique engineering challenges: the line has an **average incline of 5.7%** and reaches **12% in places** — the steepest slopes of any adhesion-worked metro in the world
- The rubber-tyred technology (based on the **Paris Métro** system) was specifically chosen for its superior grip on the extreme gradients

## 11. Sauvabelin — The Forest Tower and Urban Wilderness

- The **Bois de Sauvabelin** (Sauvabelin Forest) is a large wooded area directly above the city centre, offering an escape from the urban bustle within minutes
- The **Tour de Sauvabelin**, a **35-meter wooden observation tower**, was built in **2003** by architect **Julius Natterer** using an innovative **double-helix staircase** design
- To minimize environmental impact, the tower was constructed from **dying Douglas fir** trees (exterior) and local **fir and spruce** (interior) — avoiding unnecessary felling
- Visitors climb **151 steps** to the platform for panoramic views of Lausanne, Lac Léman, and the Alps; a maximum of **30 people** are allowed at the top simultaneously
- The surrounding park includes a **small lake**, **farm animals**, and forest paths — making it one of the city's most popular family destinations
- The Sauvabelin area was also home to **Coco Chanel** and **David Bowie**, who appreciated its peaceful, elevated seclusion above the city

## 12. Knowledge Capital — EPFL and the University of Lausanne

- The **Académie de Lausanne**, founded in **1537** as a Protestant theology school after the Reformation, is the direct ancestor of the modern **Université de Lausanne** (UNIL)
- In **1853**, the **École Spéciale de Lausanne** opened with just eleven students — it would evolve into the **EPFL** (École Polytechnique Fédérale de Lausanne), Switzerland's second federal institute of technology
- EPFL was officially established as a federal institution on **1 January 1969**, separating from the university. A 25-year, **CHF 700 million** initiative relocated the entire campus to **Dorigny-Écublens** on the shores of Lac Léman
- Together, EPFL and UNIL form a vast lakeside campus with over **31,000 students and staff** — one of Europe's most important centres for science, technology, and innovation
- EPFL's iconic **Rolex Learning Center** (2010), designed by the Japanese firm **SANAA**, is an undulating open-plan building that has become an architectural landmark
- The **Treaty of Lausanne** (24 July 1923), which resolved the post-World War I conflict between Turkey and the Allied powers, was signed at the **Palais de Rumine** — today home to several cantonal museums and the university library

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The watchman who heard too much** — A child sneaks up the 153 cathedral steps at night and joins the guet; from the tower, they hear whispered secrets carried on the wind from all four directions — each one a mystery from a different century of Lausanne's history
2. **The lost mosaic of Lousonna** — A Roman child in Lousonna buries a treasure mosaic before fleeing uphill to the Cité; 1,700 years later, a modern child finds the first tile near the Musée Romain and follows the trail down to the ancient lakeshore
3. **The rose window's secret cosmos** — When moonlight hits the cathedral's great rose window at a precise angle, the four seasons, four elements, and zodiac signs come alive; a child must restore the cosmic balance before dawn
4. **Olympic dreams in Ouchy** — A child discovers Pierre de Coubertin's forgotten notebook in the Villa Mon-Repos; each page describes an imaginary sport, and the child must compete in all of them across Lausanne's landmarks to earn a place in a secret ceremony
5. **The ghost metro of Lausanne** — On the steepest stretch of the M2, the automated train stops between stations at midnight; a child steps out into a hidden underground world where the old funicular of 1877 still runs, connecting past and present
6. **Voltaire's last curtain call** — In the gardens of Mon-Repos, a child stumbles upon an 18th-century stage hidden behind the hedges; Voltaire's ghost needs one more actor to finish a play he never completed — and the audience is all of Lausanne's famous former residents
7. **The double helix of Sauvabelin** — A child climbing the wooden tower discovers that the two intertwined staircases lead to different versions of Lausanne: one as it is today, one as it was when the forest covered the three hills before the Romans came
8. **Eliot's unfinished line** — A poet's notebook washes up on the shore at Ouchy, its pages half-blank; a child must travel to Dr. Vittoz's old sanatorium and piece together the missing words of a poem that, when completed, calms a great storm over the lake
