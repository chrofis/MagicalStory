## 1. Founding — The Zähringer and the Bear

- **1191**: Duke **Berchtold V of Zähringen** founded the city of Bern (according to the Cronica de Berno)
- First documentary mention of the name "Bern": **1208**
- **The founding legend**: Berchtold V declared the city would be named after the **first animal killed** in the surrounding forest during a hunt. That animal was a **bear (Bär)**
- **The more likely etymology**: "Bern" probably derives from the Celtic word **"berna"**, meaning "cleft" or "gorge" — referring to the dramatic Aare peninsula
- **Strategic location**: Berchtold IV (his predecessor) had already built **Nydegg Castle** at the tip of the Aare peninsula to protect the river crossing. Berchtold V founded the city behind it
- The **Zähringerbrunnen** (Zähringer Fountain) honors the founder — but depicts not the duke himself, but a **large bear in knightly armor** standing in for the city's founder
- The city could only expand in **one direction** (westward along the peninsula ridge), which produced its distinctive long, narrow layout with broad market streets instead of plazas — a characteristic of **all Zähringen cities**

## 2. Geography — The Aare Peninsula

- Bern sits on a dramatic **elevated peninsula** formed by the Aare river bending sharply around the old town — creating a **natural fortress** surrounded by water on three sides
- The Aare drops about **30 meters** in steep cliffs below the old town
- This geography made Bern virtually **impregnable** in medieval times and shaped the entire city layout
- The river encircles the city "like a protective belt" (swissinfo)
- Today: **Aare swimming** (Aareschwimmen/Aareböötle) is a beloved summer tradition — locals and tourists float down the turquoise river in inflatable rafts, a free activity that defines Bernese summer life

## 3. The Great Fire of 1405 and Sandstone Rebuilding

- In **1405**, a devastating fire destroyed **two-thirds of the city**
- The medieval wooden houses burned rapidly
- Aftermath: The Bernese rebuilt their city in **local sandstone** — the warm, honey-colored Bernese Molasse sandstone that defines the city's appearance today
- During the rebuild, they created the famous **6 kilometers of Lauben (arcades)** — covered walkways running along both sides of the main streets, with shops behind the arcade pillars
- These form **Europe's longest covered shopping promenade**
- **UNESCO World Heritage since 1983**: The Altstadt was inscribed primarily because of this remarkably well-preserved medieval urban layout with the characteristic Lauben

## 4. The Zytglogge (Clock Tower) — Bern's Mechanical Heart

### Construction History
- Built around **1200** as the **western city gate** (Zeitglockenturm) — the entry to the original Zähringer city
- As Bern expanded westward, the gate lost its defensive function and became a **clock tower**
- After the **1405 fire**, rebuilt in **sandstone** (the current structure)
- The name "Zytglogge" (Zeitglocke = "time bell") came when a bell was hung in the tower and a clockwork installed

### Kaspar Brunner's Masterwork (1530)
- By the early 1500s, the old clockwork had broken down beyond repair
- In **1527**, the city council commissioned **Kaspar Brunner**, a German weapons locksmith, to build a new mechanism for the price of **1,000 Gulden**
- Brunner's clockwork was completed in **1530** — it is one of the **oldest functioning mechanical clocks in Switzerland**
- The clock is not just a timepiece: it is an **astronomical instrument** showing hours, minutes, day of the week, date, month, zodiac signs, moon phases, sunrise, and sunset

### The Figurenspiel (Figure Play)
The clock features several animated mechanical figures that perform at the top of every hour:

- **The Golden Rooster** (Goldener Hahn): Crows **three times** before each hour strikes — powered by bellows inside the mechanism
- **Chronos / "Hans von Thann"**: A larger-than-life bearded figure (the Greek personification of time) who **turns an hourglass** as the minutes pass. The Bernese nicknamed him "Hans von Thann"
- **The Knight**: A ritterliche Figur (knightly figure) at the tower top who **strikes the hour bell with a gilded hammer**
- **The Dancing Bears** (added **1610**): Bears dressed in **Bern's colors (red and black)** that dance in a circle — symbolizing the city's power and honoring the founding legend. They were added 80 years after the clock, making them a later but beloved addition

### Cultural Impact
- Einstein reportedly was inspired by the Zytglogge: imagining what would happen if a streetcar **raced away from the clock at the speed of light** — the clock would appear frozen while the rider's watch kept ticking. This thought experiment was supposedly part of his path to the **special theory of relativity**

## 5. The Berner Münster (Cathedral)

- **Switzerland's largest late-medieval church** and its **tallest cathedral** (100.6 meters / 330 feet)
- Construction began in **1421** under the direction of **Matthäus Ensinger**, a master builder from Strasbourg who had already built three other cathedrals
- Construction continued for over a century with successive architects: **Vincenz Ensinger**, then **Erhard Küng** (who created the famous portal sculptures)
- The tower was only completed in **1893** — nearly 500 years after construction began
- A **344-step spiral staircase** leads to the top for panoramic views

### The Last Judgment Portal
- The main entrance features one of the most important **Late Gothic sculptural programs** in Europe
- Depicts the **Last Judgment**: Christ separating the blessed from the damned
- **47 large free-standing statues** (today replaced by replicas; originals in the Bernisches Historisches Museum)
- **170+ smaller figures** — all originals, still in place
- Total: **234 human figures** from all ranks of society — from the pope to peasants, religious leaders to laypeople — all awaiting judgment
- The sculptural program was created by **Erhard Küng**

## 6. The Kindlifresserbrunnen (Child Eater Fountain)

- Located at **Kornhausplatz** in the old town
- Created **1545–1546** by sculptor **Hans Gieng**, replacing an older wooden fountain from the 15th century
- Original name: **"Platzbrunnen"** (Plaza Fountain); the name "Kindlifresserbrunnen" first appears in **1666**
- Depicts a seated **ogre devouring a naked child**, with a bag containing **more children** at his side — presumably next to be eaten
- The frieze may have been designed by **Hans Rudolf Manuel Deutsch** (son of the famous Bernese artist Niklaus Manuel)

### Interpretations (all debated, none confirmed)
- **Greek mythology**: The Titan **Kronos** (Saturn) devouring his children to prevent a prophecy
- **Warning to children**: A medieval "bogeyman" figure to frighten naughty children into obedience
- **Anti-Semitic blood libel**: A theory linking the figure to medieval anti-Jewish caricatures (controversial, no direct patronage records support this)
- **Carnival figure**: A Fastnacht character representing excess and gluttony
- **Political allegory**: Representing the power of the Swiss cantons consuming the smaller territories
- The **true meaning remains one of Bern's great mysteries** — no contemporary records explain the iconography

## 7. The Bears of Bern — From Battle Trophy to BearPark

- **1513**: Swiss mercenaries fighting in the **Holy Alliance of Pope Julius II** against the French King captured a **live bear** after the Battle of Novara in Northern Italy. **Bartholomäus May**, Bern's most prominent citizen, led the captured bear ceremoniously into the city
- **1515**: The first **bear pit** (Bärengraben) opened at the current **Bärenplatz**
- The bear became the living embodiment of the city's name and coat of arms
- **1764 onwards**: The bear pit had to move multiple times due to city expansion — from Bärenplatz to Golattenmattgass-Tor, then to Grosse Schanze (from 1820)
- **1857**: Bears moved to the current location at the lower end of the old town, below the **Nydeggbrücke** — a two-part pit where they were kept for over **100 years**
- **October 22, 2009**: The modern **BärenPark** opened — a **6,000 square meter** park on the Aare riverbank where bears can roam freely 365 days a year, 24 hours a day, exhibiting natural behavior
- Current bears: Björk, Ursina, and others — the tradition of naming them is a civic affair
- The bear is everywhere in Bern: on the cantonal coat of arms, on fountains, on buildings, as the Zytglogge's dancing figures, as chocolate bears in shops

## 8. Einstein in Bern — The Miracle Year

- Einstein moved to Bern in **1902** after graduating from ETH Zurich, to work at the **Swiss Federal Patent Office** (Eidgenössisches Amt für geistiges Eigentum)
- His title: **"Technical Expert, Third Class"** — evaluating patent applications for electromagnetic devices
- He and his wife **Mileva Marić** rented an apartment at **Kramgasse 49** (second floor) at the end of October **1903**
- They lived there until **1905** — the period of his greatest breakthroughs

### The Annus Mirabilis (Miracle Year) of 1905
- Between March and September 1905, Einstein published **five groundbreaking scientific papers** in three different areas:
  1. **Photoelectric effect** (light quanta / photons) — this earned him the **1921 Nobel Prize**
  2. **Brownian motion** — proving the reality and size of atoms
  3. **Special theory of relativity** — E = mc²
- All written in his spare time, evenings and weekends, after full days at the patent office
- In mid-September 1905, he finally received **permanent employment** and a salary raise at the Patent Office

### The Kramgasse 49 Connection
- Today the **Einsteinhaus** museum at Kramgasse 49 preserves his apartment and tells the story of his Bernese years
- The apartment overlooks the **Kramgasse** — one of Bern's main old town streets, directly in view of the **Zytglogge** clock tower
- The proximity to the Zytglogge is part of the mythology: Einstein reportedly contemplated what would happen if one **raced away from the clock face at the speed of light**

## 9. The Bundeshaus (Federal Palace)

- Built in **1902** (current building), housing the **Swiss Federal Assembly** (Parliament)
- Located on the western edge of the old town, on the high bank of the Aare
- The **dome** features the cantonal coats of arms of all Swiss cantons in mosaic
- The Bundesplatz (Parliament Square) in front serves as a market, events venue, and features 26 fountains representing the cantons
- Bern became the **federal city** (Bundesstadt) in **1848** when the modern Swiss federal state was established — though Switzerland has no official "capital" in the formal sense

## 10. Historic Fountains — The City of Fountains

- Bern has **over 100 public fountains**, many dating from the **16th century**
- **11 historic fountains** in the old town are particularly notable, created in the 1540s by **Hans Gieng** (the same sculptor who made the Kindlifresserbrunnen)
- Notable fountains:
  - **Zähringerbrunnen**: Bear in armor representing the city's founder
  - **Kindlifresserbrunnen**: The ogre fountain (see above)
  - **Gerechtigkeitsbrunnen** (Justice Fountain): Blindfolded Justice with sword and scales
  - **Mosesbrunnen** (Moses Fountain): Moses holding the Ten Commandments
  - **Simsonbrunnen**: Samson and the lion
  - **Schützenbrunnen**: A musketeer with banner
- Each fountain was originally a **practical water supply** — only later recognized as works of art
- The fountains are maintained and repainted regularly — their bright colors are iconic

## 11. The Gurten — Bern's House Mountain

- Height: **858 meters**, Bern's local "Hausberg"
- Accessible by **funicular railway** (Gurtenbahn) since **1899**
- Panoramic views of the city, the Aare loop, and the entire **Bernese Alps** chain (Eiger, Mönch, Jungfrau visible on clear days)
- Home to the annual **Gurtenfestival** — one of Switzerland's largest open-air music festivals

## 12. Notable Historical Facts

- **1353**: Bern joined the **Swiss Confederation** as the eighth member — one of the most powerful cantons
- **1528**: Bern adopted the **Reformation** — making it a Protestant city
- Bern was for centuries the **largest city-state north of the Alps** — its territory stretched from the Jura to the Bernese Oberland
- **1798**: Napoleon's troops conquered Bern, ending the old regime. The famous **Berner Bärenschatz** (Bear Treasury — gold coins) was seized by the French
- **1848**: Bern became the seat of the Swiss federal government

6. **Swimming with the Aare** — Nervous child's first Aareschwimmen, guided by a friendly otter past the bears, bridges, and Bundeshaus
7. **The Münster's 500 steps** — Child climbs the 344 steps of the cathedral tower, each landing revealing a different century of Bern's history
8. **The lost bear of 1513** — A bear captured at the Battle of Novara escapes and must be found across medieval Bern before Bartholomäus May's parade
