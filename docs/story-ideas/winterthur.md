## 1. Roman Origins — The Vicus of Vitudurum

- The name **Winterthur** traces back to the Celtic settlement **Uitoduro** (reconstructed as *Uitodurō*), from Celtic **"uito"** (pasture) and **"durōn"** (gate/marketplace) — meaning roughly "pasture gate"
- The Romans Latinised the name to **Vitudurum** around **280 AD**, applying it to their **vicus** (civilian settlement) at present-day **Oberwinterthur**
- Roman timber buildings have been **dendrochronologically dated to around 4 BC**; in **AD 7** the Romans rebuilt the passageway into a proper road
- The open settlement stretched about **500 metres** from the church hill (**St. Arbogast**) as a street village with multiple **insulae** (building blocks)
- Vitudurum flourished in the **1st and 2nd centuries AD**, with a **temple, thermal baths, and a water supply system**
- During the **Alemannic invasions** (late 3rd century), the vicus was replaced by a fortified **castrum** on the St. Arbogast hill; the fortification wall is dated to **294 AD** by an inscription stone
- The archaeological remains are listed as a **Class A object of national importance** in the Swiss inventory of cultural property
- Alemannic settlers (from the **6th century**) adapted the name to **"Wintarduro"** (attested **856 AD**); over time "Vitu" was reinterpreted as "wintar" (winter) and "duro" as "Thur" (the nearby river) — a folk etymology, since the Thur does not actually flow through Winterthur
- Today locals call the city simply **"Winti"**

## 2. The Kyburg Counts and the Founding of the City

- The **Counts of Winterthur**, a cadet branch of the Counts of Bregenz, built **Kyburg Castle** in the **10th century**; first historical mention as **"Chuigeburg"** dates to **1027**
- In **1053** the counts of Winterthur died out; the castle passed to the **Counts of Dillingen**, then to the **Kyburg dynasty**
- Duke **Ulrich III von Kyburg** ordered the construction of the modern castle complex around **1200** — tower, palace, curtain wall, and chapel
- The Kyburgs founded **Winterthur as a city around 1175**, granting it a charter with extensive privileges to consolidate their power in the region
- **Kyburg Castle** overlooks the **Töss river** about 3 km south-east of Winterthur; it is the **best-preserved feudal keep in north-eastern Switzerland**
- When the Kyburg male line went extinct in **1264**, their entire holdings — including Winterthur — passed to the **House of Habsburg**

## 3. Habsburg Rule and Joining Zurich (1467)

- From **1264**, the Habsburgs controlled Winterthur and established the comital line of **Neu-Kyburg**
- From **1415 to 1442**, Winterthur was **reichsfrei** (Reichsfreiheit — subject only to the Holy Roman Emperor), a privileged status
- During the **Old Zurich War** (Alter Zürichkrieg, 1440–1446), Winterthur lost its imperial freedom and returned to Austrian Habsburg control
- In **1460**, the Swiss Confederation conquered the **Thurgau**; the Habsburgs grew financially and militarily weaker
- In **1467**, Duke **Sigismund of Habsburg** sold Winterthur to the city of **Zurich** (Zürich) — ending centuries of Habsburg rule
- Under Zurich's leadership, Winterthur's economic freedoms were **restricted**: the city lost many of its market rights and trading privileges
- Despite these restrictions, Winterthur adopted the **Reformation** following Zurich's lead in the **1520s–1530s**, stripping Catholic churches of liturgical objects

## 4. The Stadtkirche — 1,300 Years of Sacred Architecture

- The **Stadtkirche** (City Church) traces its origins to the **7th/8th century**: a simple wooden hall, nine metres long and six wide, built near the Roman road to Vitudurum
- The wooden church was replaced by a **solid stone building** in the **9th century**, later extended with transepts around **1000 AD**
- **Bernard of Clairvaux** preached in the church during his passage in **1146**
- In the **13th century**, the church gained **three naves** and a tower; the **Romanesque-Early Gothic chancel** survives as the oldest part, modelled on Zurich's **Fraumünster**
- Fire damage in **1313** led to enlarged side aisles; the **second tower** was built **1486–1490**
- The distinctive **twin towers** reach about **55 metres**; the south tower received a baroque dome in **1659**, and in **1794** the north tower was raised to match
- During the **Reformation**, all liturgical objects were stripped; the **organ pipes were melted down**, leaving the church without an organ for nearly **400 years** (a new one arrived in **1809**)
- Five cast bells by **Johann Jakob Keller** (1869) span both towers; the largest weighs **3,999 kg**
- Interior murals by **Paul Zehnder** (1923–1930) depict Old Testament prophets and New Testament scenes in a Romanesque style

## 5. The Altstadt and Marktgasse — Medieval Heart of the City

- Winterthur's **Altstadt** (old town) is one of the best-preserved medieval town centres in Switzerland
- The city boasts the **largest continuous pedestrian zone in Switzerland** (grösste zusammenhängende Fussgängerzone der Schweiz)
- The **Marktgasse** (Market Street) is the central artery: a broad, cobblestoned pedestrian street lined with cafes, boutiques, and speciality shops
- Traditional **markets** have been held on the Marktgasse since medieval times; today a market takes place every **Tuesday and Friday**
- The **Weihnachtsmarkt** (Christmas market) evolved from a small artisan gathering into one of the region's most popular festive events
- In **1989**, Winterthur received the prestigious **Wakker Prize** (Wakkerpreis) from the Swiss Heritage Society for the development and preservation of its architectural heritage
- The **Stadthaus** (town hall), designed by the famous architect **Gottfried Semper** and built **1865–1869** (inaugurated **30 October 1870**), anchors the old town; it has served as the city's **concert hall since 1934**

## 6. Industrialisation — Engines, Looms, and Locomotives

- In the **19th century**, Winterthur transformed from a small market town into one of Switzerland's most important industrial centres
- **Rieter** (Johann Jacob Rieter & Cie.): Founded **15 April 1795** on the Marktgasse as a trading house for spices and cotton. When Napoleon's **Continental Blockade (1806)** cut off British machine parts, Rieter began building its own textile machinery (**1810**). By acquiring the former **Kloster Töss** (Töss Abbey) in **1833**, the company expanded massively; today Rieter is the **world's leading supplier of spinning-mill systems**
- **Sulzer**: Founded in **1834** by **Johann Jakob Sulzer-Neuffert** and his sons as a foundry and locksmith workshop. **Rudolf Diesel** worked at Sulzer in **1879**; Sulzer built its first diesel engine in **1898**. Sulzer diesel engines powered ships and locomotives worldwide; at its peak, Sulzer employed thousands in Winterthur
- **SLM** (Schweizerische Lokomotiv- und Maschinenfabrik): Founded in **1871** by the British engineer **Charles Brown**. SLM produced over **5,000 locomotives** exported to more than **40 countries**, including the iconic **"Crocodile" electric locomotives** for steep Alpine gradients. SLM operated until **2005**; its cog-railway division was sold to **Stadler Rail** in 1998
- These three companies made Winterthur a powerhouse of **mechanical engineering** (Maschinenbau) and earned it a reputation as an industrial city of European significance

## 7. The Musikkollegium — Nearly 400 Years of Music

- The **Musikkollegium Winterthur** (Winterthurer Musikkollegium) was founded in **1629** by clergyman **Hans Heinrich Meyer** — making it one of the **oldest continuously operating musical institutions in Europe**
- Originally a **choir and music society** of 30–40 members who performed at church services; each member had to sing and play an instrument
- In the **18th century**, as church music declined, the group shifted to **instrumental compositions**
- In **1875**, conductor **Georg Wilhelm Rauchenecker** relaunched it as the **Stadtorchester Winterthur** (City Orchestra)
- The early **20th century** was a golden age: patron **Werner Reinhart** and conductor **Hermann Scherchen** (permanent guest conductor **1922–1950**) turned Winterthur into a European musical hub, hosting **Igor Stravinsky, Richard Strauss**, and **Anton Webern**
- The ensemble performs in the **Stadthaus**, Gottfried Semper's concert hall — inaugurated in the same era as Vienna's **Musikverein**
- In **2000**, the orchestra readopted the historic name **Musikkollegium Winterthur**
- Today the orchestra performs over **40 concerts per season** under Chief Conductor **Roberto Gonzalez-Monjas**

## 8. Museums — From Old Masters to Hands-On Science

- **Sammlung Oskar Reinhart "Am Römerholz"** (Oskar Reinhart Collection): A world-class art museum housed in the collector's **1915 villa** on Haldenstrasse. Contains **207 works** spanning Late Gothic to early 20th century — including **Cranach the Elder, Bruegel the Elder, El Greco, Renoir, Cezanne, Manet, van Gogh**, and **early Picasso**. Reinhart (**1885–1965**), son of trading magnate Theodor Reinhart, bequeathed the collection to the **Swiss Confederation**; it has been open to the public since **1970**. Operated by the Federal Office of Culture (BAK)
- **Kunst Museum Winterthur — Reinhart am Stadtgarten**: Opened in **1951** as the **first private museum in Switzerland**; houses approximately **600 paintings and drawings** of Swiss, German, and Austrian art including **Ferdinand Hodler, Caspar David Friedrich**, and **Carl Spitzweg**
- **Fotomuseum Winterthur**: Founded in **1993** by photographer **George Reinhart** (Oskar's nephew), writer **Urs Stahel**, and publisher **Walter Keller**. Located in the **"Kultursagi"**, a former rubber-band weaving mill from **1877**. One of Europe's leading museums for **photography and visual culture**
- **Swiss Science Center Technorama**: The **only science centre in Switzerland** and one of the **largest in Europe**, with over **500 hands-on exhibits** across **6,500 m2** on three floors. Over **64,000 schoolchildren** visit annually in organised groups. Located in **Oberwinterthur**, expanded in **2021** with "Technorama Outdoors"
- Winterthur calls itself the **Museumsstadt** (museum city) — with **17 museums** in a city of around 120,000 people

## 9. Castles — Kyburg, Hegi, and Mörsburg

- **Schloss Kyburg**: Perched above the Töss valley, 3 km south-east of Winterthur. First fortified in the **10th century**; the modern castle dates to **ca. 1200** (Duke Ulrich III). Passed to the Habsburgs in **1264**; opened as a **castle museum in 1865** by Matthäus Pfau. Today it is one of the **most-visited castles in the canton of Zurich**
- **Schloss Hegi**: First mentioned in **1225** in connection with the **ministerials of Hegi**; the core is a **defensive residential tower with a moat**, built around **1200** to secure the property of the **Bishopric of Constance**. The **Lords of Hohenlandenberg** expanded it from **1460** with ring walls, round towers, and a half-timber residence with chapel. The **bishop's living room** (1496) is one of the **most important medieval rooms in eastern Switzerland**. Purchased by the city of Winterthur in **1947**; now a museum and cultural venue, listed as a **heritage site of national significance**
- **Mörsburg**: First mentioned in **1241** as property of the Counts of Kyburg; the early Gothic chapel dates to about **1250**. Ownership passed to the **Habsburgs (1273)**, the **Lords of Goldenberg (1363)**, and finally the **city of Winterthur (1598)**. Today it displays domestic culture from the **17th–18th centuries**, weapons, and glass

## 10. Geography — The Töss Valley and Surrounding Vineyards

- Winterthur sits at approximately **440 metres** above sea level in a **basin** where several valleys converge, at the foot of the **Lindberg** and **Eschenberg** hills
- The **Töss** (river) flows through the western part of the city; the **Tösstal** (Töss valley) extends south-eastward into the **Tössbergland** — a landscape of narrow valleys, meadows, and forested ridges
- The region stretches from the Töss valley to the **Rhine Falls** (Rheinfall) near Schaffhausen
- **Vineyards** dot the hillsides around Winterthur, part of the broader **Zurich wine region** (607 hectares); the dominant grape is **Pinot Noir** (Blauburgunder), producing nuanced red wines
- The city is surrounded by **forests and farmland**, with gentle rolling hills to the north towards the **Thurgau**
- Winterthur's location made it a natural crossroads: the Roman road from **Lake Geneva to Lake Constance** (Bodensee) passed through Vitudurum, and the city remains an important **railway junction** today

## 11. Famous People of Winterthur

- **Jonas Furrer** (1805–1861): Born in Winterthur, he became the **first President of the Swiss Confederation** in **1848**. A lawyer and key drafter of the **1848 Federal Constitution** that established modern Switzerland as a federal state
- **Joan Gamper** / Hans-Max Gamper (1877–1930): Born in Winterthur, he co-founded **FC Zurich** in **1896** and then founded **FC Barcelona** in **1899**. He served as Barcelona's president five times and scored over 100 goals in 48 matches. The annual **Trofeo Joan Gamper** pre-season tournament honours his legacy
- **Oskar Reinhart** (1885–1965): Art collector and patron from the Reinhart trading dynasty (Volkart Brothers). Left his world-class collection to the Swiss nation; two major museums in Winterthur bear his name
- **Werner Reinhart** (1884–1951): Oskar's brother, an industrialist and **patron of modern music** who helped attract Stravinsky and other composers to Winterthur
- **Alfred Buchi** (1879–1959): Born in Winterthur, he invented the **exhaust-gas turbocharger** (patent **1905**); his work at **Sulzer** and later the **Buchi Syndicate** revolutionised diesel engines worldwide. The city named **Alfred-Buchi-Weg** in his honour in 2012
- **Niklaus Wirth** (1934–2024): Born in Winterthur, computer scientist who created the **Pascal programming language** and won the **Turing Award** in **1984** — the highest distinction in computer science
- **Charles E. L. Brown** (1863–1924): Machine designer, co-founder of **Brown, Boveri & Cie** (BBC, later ABB), and founder of **SLM**

## 12. Modern Winterthur — The Sixth City

- With over **120,000 residents**, Winterthur is **Switzerland's sixth-largest city** and the **second-largest city in the canton of Zurich** (after Zurich itself)
- The city has successfully transitioned from its industrial past; former factory sites now house **creative businesses, startups**, and cultural venues
- Home to the **ZHAW** (Zurcher Hochschule fur Angewandte Wissenschaften / Zurich University of Applied Sciences), one of Switzerland's largest universities of applied sciences
- The **Sulzer-Areal** (former Sulzer factory grounds) has been redeveloped into a mixed-use neighbourhood with housing, offices, and cultural spaces — a model of industrial-to-urban conversion
- Winterthur's location — just **20 minutes by train from Zurich** — makes it both a commuter city and an independent cultural centre
- The **pedestrian-friendly old town**, 17 museums, lively market traditions, and green surroundings give Winterthur a small-city charm despite its size

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The lost road to Vitudurum** — A child discovers Roman coins near St. Arbogast church and follows the ancient road back in time to the bustling vicus, meeting a Roman potter, visiting the thermal baths, and witnessing the construction of the castrum wall in 294 AD
2. **The bear on the Kyburg tower** — A young squire in Duke Ulrich's castle must warn the people of Winterthur about an approaching danger; the child races down the Toss valley, discovering what it means to protect a newly founded city (ca. 1200)
3. **The day the music started** — Set in 1629, a child joins clergyman Hans Heinrich Meyer's new Musikkollegium, learning to sing and play an instrument; each rehearsal unlocks a different room in the Stadtkirche that echoes with centuries of music
4. **The turbocharger kid** — Young Alfred Buchi is fascinated by the roaring engines at his father's Sulzer factory; a magical gear transports him inside a diesel engine where he must figure out how to harness the wasted exhaust — and invents the turbocharger
5. **The painter's villa** — A child visiting the Am Roemerholz collection steps into a painting by Bruegel and must find their way home by travelling through canvases — from Cranach's forests to Renoir's gardens to Van Gogh's starry hospital in Arles
6. **500 experiments** — During a school trip to Technorama, a child accidentally activates all 500 exhibits at once; to set things right, they must solve scientific puzzles across three floors before the museum opens the next morning
7. **The goalkeeper from Winterthur** — Young Hans Gamper kicks a ball through the Marktgasse and dreams of a football club; the story follows him from the cobblestones of Winterthur to founding FC Barcelona in 1899, showing how a Swiss boy changed world football
8. **The secret room in Schloss Hegi** — A child exploring the 800-year-old castle discovers the bishop's hidden living room from 1496; inside, a medieval riddle sends them on a quest through all three of Winterthur's castles — Hegi, Kyburg, and Morsburg
