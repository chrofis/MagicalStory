## 1. Founding — Berchtold IV and the Zähringen Dynasty

- **1157**: Duke **Berchtold IV of Zähringen** founded the city of Fribourg — the name derives from German **"frei" (free)** and **"Burg" (fort)**, meaning "free fortress"
- The Zähringen dukes were the same dynasty that founded **Bern (1191)**, **Burgdorf**, **Murten**, and **Thun** — Fribourg is the **oldest** of their Swiss city foundations
- **Strategic purpose**: Berchtold IV chose the site to control a **ford across the Sarine/Saane river** and to strengthen Zähringen power on the Swiss Plateau between the Aare and the Sarine
- The original settlement occupied a **natural peninsula** formed by a tight meander of the Sarine — protected on three sides by **steep molasse cliffs** dropping up to **100 meters** to the river below
- **1218**: The Zähringen line died out; Fribourg passed to the related **Counts of Kyburg**
- **1249**: The Kyburgs granted Fribourg its **Handfeste** — a charter establishing the city's legal, institutional, and economic framework
- **1277**: The Kyburgs sold the city to the **Austrian Habsburgs**
- **1452**: The **House of Savoy** assumed control of Fribourg
- The city's early growth produced four distinct quarters: **Bourg** (the original hilltop settlement), **Au/Auge** (across the river), **Neuveville** ("new town," actually the oldest of the lower districts), and **Spital**

## 2. Geography — The Sarine/Saane Gorge

- Fribourg is built on and around a **dramatic gorge** carved by the **Sarine (French) / Saane (German)** river as it cuts through the Swiss Plateau
- The gorge reaches depths of up to **100 meters** below the surrounding plateau — the old town perches on cliff edges and slopes above this chasm
- The river's tight **meanders** created natural peninsulas that served as defensive positions — much like the Aare at Bern, but with even steeper, more dramatic cliffs
- The **molasse sandstone** (compressed sand and clay from an ancient seabed) forms the distinctive grey-green cliff faces and was used as building material throughout the city
- The old town cascades from the upper plateau (**Bourg** quarter) down steep cobbled streets to the riverside districts of **Neuveville**, **Auge**, and **Planche**
- **14 bridges** span the gorge and river at various levels — from medieval stone and timber crossings at the bottom to the high-level **Pont de Zaehringen** (1924, concrete, 230 meters long, 65 meters above the river) and the **Pont de la Poya** (2014, cable-stayed, 852 meters long)

## 3. The Medieval Old Town — One of Europe's Best Preserved

- Fribourg's old town is recognized as the **largest ensemble of medieval architecture in Switzerland** and one of the finest in Europe
- Over **200 Gothic facades** from the 15th century line the narrow streets — an extraordinary concentration of original medieval urban fabric
- The old town is on the **Swiss Inventory of Heritage Sites of National Importance** and has been proposed for the **UNESCO World Heritage tentative list**
- Three distinct lower-town districts retain their medieval character:
  - **Neuveville** ("newest town of the old part"): thrived with water-intensive **tanning and textile industries**, surrounded by a fence in the 13th century and a fortified wall by the 14th
  - **Auge**: a working-class riverside quarter with original Gothic houses
  - **Planche** (Matte): the lowest district along the river
- The **Town Hall** (Hôtel de Ville / Rathaus), built **1501-1522** with a tower added in **1642**, sits on the Place de l'Hôtel-de-Ville and features a distinctive **exterior double staircase** in late Gothic style
- The **Tilleul de Morat** (Linden Tree of Murten) stands on the square before the Town Hall — according to legend, a messenger ran from the **Battle of Murten (1476)** to bring news of the Swiss victory over Charles the Bold, only to **collapse dead from exhaustion** on arrival. Onlookers planted a **linden twig from his hat** — and the tree grew for over **500 years** until a drunk driver destroyed it in **1983**. A cutting was replanted in **1984** and stands today

## 4. The Cathédrale Saint-Nicolas (Kathedrale Sankt Nikolaus)

- **Construction**: begun in **1283** in Gothic style, with major building continuing through the 14th and 15th centuries
- The **tower** was completed around **1490** — it rises **74 meters** (242 feet) above the city
- Visitors can climb **365 steps** (one for each day of the year) to the top for panoramic views of the old town, the Sarine gorge, and the Pre-Alps
- The cathedral was elevated from collegiate church to **cathedral status** in **1924** by papal bull, when Fribourg became part of the **Diocese of Lausanne, Geneva, and Fribourg**

### The Last Judgment Portal
- The main entrance features an elaborate **14th-century tympanum** depicting the **Last Judgment** — one of the finest Gothic sculptural programs in Switzerland
- The composition shows Christ separating the blessed from the damned, flanked by statues of **Saint Nicholas** and the **twelve apostles**
- The portal reflects the same theological theme as the Bern Minster's famous Last Judgment, though in a different artistic style

### The Great Organ — Aloys Mooser's Masterpiece
- Built by local organ builder **Aloys Mooser** between **1824 and 1834**
- The instrument has approximately **61 stops** distributed across **four manuals and pedal**
- The fourth manual is an **echo manual** — its pipes speak out into the narthex, creating an extraordinary effect of spatial distance
- In **1836**, **Franz Liszt** visited the cathedral during a Swiss tour with writer **George Sand**; Sand wrote that they had entered to hear "the most beautiful organ that had been built up to that point"
- Liszt played a fragment of Mozart's **"Dies Irae"** on the organ — Sand described him as transfixed in "mystical and religious sadness"
- The composer **Anton Bruckner** also performed on the Mooser organ

## 5. Medieval Fortifications — Switzerland's Finest

- Fribourg preserves the **most significant ensemble of medieval military architecture in Switzerland**
- The system was built between the **13th and 15th centuries** and comprises **six towers**, **five gates**, **seven rampart sections**, and **one large bulwark**, stretching over **2 kilometers**
- The fortifications were built primarily on the **western side** of the city — the steep Sarine gorge cliffs provided natural defense on the other sides

### Notable Towers and Gates
- **Porte de Morat / Murtentorturm** (1411-1414): the most monumental gate tower — **34 meters high**, rectangular (10.5 x 12 m), originally open on the town side so an enemy who captured it could not use it as a fortress
- **Tour Rouge / Roter Turm** (Red Tower, mid-13th century): the **tallest fortification tower at 38 meters**, its distinctive red brick color visible from across the gorge
- **Tour des Chats / Katzenturm** (Cats Tower, second half of the 16th century): built against the oldest rampart section, **33 meters** high
- **Porte de Bourguillon** (1350-1367): guards upper access to the Maigrauge loop, the only section built in **freestone and quarry stone** rather than molasse, raised to **29.5 meters** at the end of the 16th century
- **Porte de Berne / Berntor** (1270-1290): the oldest surviving gate, **24 meters** high, built in grey molasse stone, heightened in the 1380s

## 6. The Three Medieval Bridges

- Three bridges over the Sarine date their origins to the **13th century**, shortly after the city's founding — they are among the oldest river crossings in Switzerland

### Pont de Berne / Bernbrücke
- Originally constructed around **1250** — built to connect Fribourg to the road toward Bern
- Before the bridge, travelers crossed the Sarine **by boat**
- In **1340**, the bridge was **sawn down** by Fribourgeois defenders to ward off an attack by encroaching **Bernese invaders**
- The current structure dates from **1653** (central stone pillar and deck replaced **1853-54**, roof replaced **1885**)
- It is Fribourg's **last remaining covered wooden bridge** — approximately **40 meters** long, with a wooden shingle roof
- Listed in the **1995 inventory of cultural property of national importance**

### Pont du Milieu (Middle Bridge)
- The current stone structure was built in **1720** using local **tufa** (calcareous stone)
- Nearly **70 meters** long with **four arches**, it is a distinctive **humpback bridge** paved with cobblestones

### Pont de Saint-Jean
- First documented shortly before **1259** — originally a **timber-truss bridge with a roof and sidings**
- Rebuilt in **stone** in **1746**
- The last of the three medieval bridges to be constructed

## 7. The Funicular — Powered by Wastewater

- The **Funiculaire de Fribourg** connects the lower **Neuveville** district to the upper **Saint-Pierre** district — bridging a height difference of **56 meters**
- **Inaugurated**: February 4, **1899**; built by the **Von Roll ironworks** of Bern at a cost of **CHF 140,000**
- Initiated by **Paul-Alcide Blancpain** (1839-1899), owner of the **Cardinal brewery** in the upper town, who wanted to give his workers easier access between the brewery and the city center

### The Water-Ballast Mechanism
- The funicular runs on **wastewater** from the upper town's sewer network — a **2,700-liter tank** beneath each car's axles is filled with filtered effluent at the upper station
- The heavier loaded car **descends by gravity**, pulling the lighter car up via a **cable-and-pulley** system — no engine or pump required
- **Track length**: 121 meters; **gradient**: 54%; **speed**: just over 4 km/h; **journey time**: 2 minutes; **capacity**: 20 passengers per car

### Heritage Status
- The **last water-ballast funicular in Switzerland** and one of the last in Europe
- Major renovation in **1998** (CHF 2 million), reopened **July 3, 1998** — still running on filtered wastewater today
- Listed in the **Inventory of Cultural Property of National Importance**
- In its first full year (1900), it carried **174,776 passengers**

## 8. The Röstigraben — Life on the Language Border

- Fribourg sits precisely on the **Röstigraben** — the informal cultural and linguistic boundary between French-speaking and German-speaking Switzerland, named after the **Rösti** potato dish favored in German-speaking regions
- The **Sarine/Saane river** itself roughly marks the language border through the canton
- At its founding in **1157**, Fribourg was a **German-speaking** city — German remained the official language until **1800**
- French gradually became dominant through industrialization and immigration from French-speaking rural areas; today the city is approximately **two-thirds French-speaking** and **one-quarter German-speaking**
- Both French and German are **official languages** of the canton of Fribourg

### The Bolze — A Vanishing Mixed Language
- In the **Basse-Ville** (lower town), a unique mixed dialect called **Bolze** (or Boltz) developed from the 19th century onward
- It fuses **Senslerdeutsch** (the Swiss-German dialect of the Sense district) with **Fribourg Patois** (a local French dialect) — speakers switch between French and German within a single sentence without transition
- Born from the immigration of German-speaking landless farmers from the Sense district who settled in the French-speaking lower town
- By 2019, Bolze was reportedly spoken by only a **handful of locals** — a vanishing piece of Fribourg's bilingual heritage

## 9. The University — Switzerland's Only Bilingual University

- The roots trace to **1580**, when the Jesuit **Peter Canisius** (1521-1597) founded the **Collège Saint-Michel** in Fribourg
- In **1763**, an **academy of law** was created by the state, forming the nucleus of the future law faculty
- The **University of Fribourg** was officially established in **1889** by an act of the cantonal parliament, championed by politician **Georges Python**
- It is **Switzerland's only officially bilingual university**, offering full curricula in both **French and German** — students are expected to have passive knowledge of both languages
- **Five faculties**: Catholic Theology, Law, Natural Sciences, Humanities, Economics and Social Sciences
- Approximately **10,000 students**, 200 tenured professors, and 700 other academic staff
- The **Miséricorde campus** (1939-1942) was designed by architects **Denis Honegger** and **Fernand Dumas**, students of **Le Corbusier**

## 10. Catholic Stronghold — Peter Canisius and the Counter-Reformation

- While neighboring **Bern** adopted Protestantism in **1528**, Fribourg **remained staunchly Catholic** throughout the Reformation — becoming a fortress of Catholic faith on the Swiss Plateau
- The city became a **refuge for exiled bishops** from the Protestant dioceses of Geneva and Lausanne
- **Peter Canisius** (1521-1597), a Dutch Jesuit and one of the foremost figures of the Counter-Reformation, spent his **last 20 years in Fribourg** (from 1580 until his death)
- Canisius founded the **Collège Saint-Michel** (1580), which trained generations of young men and became a bulwark of Catholic education
- His **Triple Catechism** became the most famous catechism of the Counter-Reformation, going through **400 editions in 150 years**
- Canisius was called the **"Second Apostle of Germany"** (after St. Boniface) for his defense of Roman Catholicism in Central Europe
- He died in Fribourg on **December 21, 1597**; he was **canonized** and declared a **Doctor of the Church** in **1925** by Pope Pius XI
- In **1846**, Fribourg joined the reactionary **Sonderbund** (alliance of Catholic cantons) against Protestant liberalism — the Sonderbund lost the brief civil war, and **Jesuits were expelled** from Switzerland as a consequence
- Fribourg remains the seat of the **Diocese of Lausanne, Geneva, and Fribourg** and is considered the center of Swiss Catholicism

## 11. The Historic Fountains

- Fribourg's old town features at least **11 historic fountains** from the medieval and Renaissance periods — many by sculptor **Hans Gieng**, the same artist who created Bern's famous fountains
- The fountains served as the city's **practical water supply** before being recognized as works of art

### Notable Fountains
- **Fontaine de Samson** (1547, by Hans Gieng): depicts the biblical **Samson** from Judges 15; originally located at Place de Notre-Dame, moved to **Place des Ormeaux** in 1958; octagonal Jura stone basin from 1857
- **Fontaine de la Vaillance** (Bravery Fountain): first mentioned in **1394** as the fountain of the cattle market; depicts a **bearded warrior in armor** with a lion-muzzle helmet and sword, symbolizing courage
- **Fontaine de la Fidélité** (Loyalty Fountain): first mentioned in **1404** on Rue de la Palme; features the most beautiful **fluted column shaft** in Fribourg, decorated with **music-making cherubs** — the work of **Hans Gieng (1552-1553)**
- **Fontaine de Saint-Georges**: depicts **Saint George fighting a dragon**, located on the square before the Town Hall
- **Fontaine de la Samaritaine** (Samaritan Woman Fountain): biblical theme
- Other fountains include the **Fontaine de Saint-Jean**, **Fontaine de Saint-Pierre**, and **Fontaine de Sainte-Anne**

## 12. Gruyère Cheese and the Bénichon Festival

### Gruyère AOP — A Fribourg Heritage
- **Gruyère** cheese is named after the town of **Gruyères** in the canton of Fribourg — production in the region is documented from as early as **1115**
- Medieval chronicles praise the expertise of Fribourgeois inhabitants who turned Alpine milk into full-fat cheese that was **exported to France and Italy**
- The **17th century** marked the official recognition of the Gruyère name and a boom in exports; first measures to **protect the denomination of origin** were introduced
- Gruyère received **AOC (Appellation d'Origine Contrôlée)** status in **2001**, upgraded to **AOP (Appellation d'Origine Protégée)** in **2013**
- Production spans Fribourg, Vaud, Neuchâtel, Jura, and parts of Bern — but the heartland remains the **district of La Gruyère** in Fribourg canton

### The Bénichon (Kilbi) — Fribourg's Harvest Thanksgiving
- The **Bénichon** (from Latin *benedictio*, "blessing") is a centuries-old harvest festival unique to the canton of Fribourg — comparable in cultural importance to American Thanksgiving
- Originally a **religious celebration** marking the anniversary of the local church's consecration; it evolved into a **folk festival** celebrating the end of the harvest and the **return of cattle herds from Alpine pastures**
- For many Fribourgeois, the Bénichon gathering is **larger than even Christmas**
- The traditional **Bénichon menu** is a legendary multi-course feast:
  - **Cuchaule**: a golden plaited bread flavored with **saffron**
  - **Moutarde de Bénichon**: a sweet-sour mustard unique to the festival
  - **Pot-au-feu**: a rich meat soup
  - **Lamb ragout** and **smoked ham**
  - **Poires à Botzi**: pears caramelized in spiced wine syrup
  - **Bricelets**: thin, rolled waffle cookies
  - **Meringues with double cream** (crème de la Gruyère)
- According to tradition, you **eat and drink for six hours straight** — and if you can still stand, you dance

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The messenger's linden tree** — A child follows the legendary runner from Murten to Fribourg in 1476; the dying messenger whispers a secret to the linden twig before it is planted, and the tree remembers every event in the city for 500 years
2. **Two words for everything** — A child discovers they can only cross the Pont du Milieu by speaking both French and German; each bridge in Fribourg requires a different language, and the Bolze-speaking cat on the Pont de Berne speaks them all at once
3. **The wastewater express** — A child shrinks small enough to ride inside the funicular's water tank; the 2,700 liters of water carry them on a wild underground journey from the upper town sewers down through 125 years of Fribourg history
4. **365 steps, 365 days** — Each step of the Cathedral tower reveals a different day in Fribourg's history; at step 1283 (the founding year), a Gothic stone-cutter asks the child for help finishing the Last Judgment portal
5. **The organ that made Liszt cry** — A child sneaks into the Cathedral at night and touches the Mooser organ's echo manual; the pipes in the narthex summon the ghost of Franz Liszt, who plays a piece that makes the stone saints weep
6. **Peter Canisius and the book that wouldn't burn** — During the Reformation, a magical catechism keeps reappearing in Protestant Bern no matter how many times it is confiscated; a child must deliver it back to Canisius in Catholic Fribourg
7. **The Bénichon feast that never ended** — At a magical Bénichon, every dish tells the story of a Fribourg season: the saffron cuchaule is golden summer, the poires à Botzi are autumn, the lamb is spring; a child must eat all courses to unlock the secret of winter
8. **The towers that talk** — Fribourg's six fortification towers argue about which is the bravest; the Tour Rouge (tallest at 38m) boasts of seeing farthest, but the Porte de Morat remembers the Burgundian Wars — a child mediates their quarrel by climbing each one
