## 1. Geography — The End of the Road

- Arosa lies at **1,775 meters** (5,823 ft) above sea level in the canton of **Graubünden**, at the head of the **Schanfigg valley** — a narrow Alpine valley that climbs steeply from the cantonal capital, Chur
- The village is accessible by a single road, the **Schanfiggerstrasse**: a 30-kilometer route from Chur featuring approximately **360 curves** and an elevation gain of **1,320 meters** — a dramatic ascent that takes about 40 minutes by car
- Arosa is literally **the end of the road**: there is no pass, no through route — the valley terminates in a high Alpine bowl surrounded by peaks on three sides
- The municipal area is vast: **154.79 km²**, one of the largest in Graubünden, mostly uninhabited mountain terrain
- Two small lakes sit at the heart of the village: the **Obersee** (Upper Lake, at 1,734 m, surface area 7.1 hectares) and the **Untersee** (Lower Lake, at 1,691 m) — used for swimming, boating, and fishing in summer, and ice skating and curling in winter
- The village consists of two areas: **Ausserarosa** (the main resort around the train station and Obersee) and **Innerarosa** (the older settlement at the valley's upper end)
- The highest point in the municipality is the **Aroser Rothorn** at **2,980 m**; the most prominent local peaks are the **Aroser Weisshorn** (2,653 m) and the **Hörnli** (2,512 m)
- Permanent population: approximately **3,160** (2024), fluctuating seasonally between about 2,000 in May and 4,600 in January

## 2. Early History — Walser Settlers at the Valley's End

- The Schanfigg valley first appears in records under the name **"Scanavico"** in **765 AD**
- Earlier settlements in the lower valley are documented: **Lüen** (1084), **Castiel** (1132), **Calfreisen** (1156) — all Romansh-speaking communities
- Archaeological evidence at **Carschlingg** near Castiel shows continuous occupation from the **Bronze Age** through the 6th century AD
- Arosa itself is first mentioned around **1330** as **"Araus"**; in 1383 as "Orossen"; and in **1428** the name "Arosa" first appears
- After **1300**, German-speaking **Walser settlers** crossed the **Strela Pass** from Davos and established themselves in the upper Schanfigg valley, replacing the original Romansh-speaking population
- Approximately **30 Walser families** settled in scattered hamlets: **Fondei**, **Sapün**, **Medergen**, and around what is now Arosa
- Their economy was pure **Alpine pastoralism**: tributes paid around 1330 consisted of cheese, sheep, wool, and butter
- Until **1851**, Arosa politically belonged to the municipality of **Davos** — at that point it had only **56 inhabitants**
- In **1851**, Arosa separated from Davos to become an **independent municipality** — one of the youngest communes in Switzerland
- On **1 January 2013**, seven surrounding villages (Calfreisen, Castiel, Langwies, Lüen, Molinis, Peist, and St. Peter-Pagig) merged into the municipality of Arosa

## 3. The Bergkirchli — Arosa's Oldest Building

- The **Bergkirchli** (Mountain Chapel), completed in **1493**, is the oldest surviving building in Arosa and the oldest church in the resort
- Located in **Innerarosa** at approximately **1,900 m** above sea level, near the valley station of the Carmenna chairlift
- Notable features include a **late Gothic vaulted wooden ceiling**, a large **Maria bell from 1492**, and a house organ with four registers from the first half of the 18th century
- Today it is a **protected monument**, accessible only during services, concerts, weddings, and guided tours — summer and winter concert series are held inside

## 4. Dr. Otto Herwig and the Sanatorium Era

- In **January 1882**, a young German physician named **Otto Herwig** arrived in Davos as a patient, suffering from a lung condition caused by overwork
- In **1883**, while exploring the mountains south of Chur, Herwig discovered the isolated community of Arosa — and recognized its potential as a site for tuberculosis treatment: high altitude, dry air, intense sunshine, and a sheltered valley
- He built a house in **1883** on the south slope of the **Tschuggen**, the spot with the longest sunshine duration in the valley
- In **1888**, Herwig opened the **Berghilf Sanatorium**, the first sanatorium in Arosa, together with his sister **Marie**, who managed the institution from 1892 until her death in 1922
- Herwig married a local resident, **Magreth Hold**, and became a citizen of Arosa in **1923**; he was named **honorary citizen** of Arosa in **1922**
- Another physician, **Dr. Carl Rüedi** (1848–1901), also advocated for Arosa as a health resort and organized a key stakeholder meeting on **9 February 1901** to promote its development
- By the early 1900s, multiple sanatoriums had opened: the **Waldsanatorium Arosa** opened on **10 October 1910** as a treatment facility for lung patients
- The sanatorium era transformed Arosa's population: from **61 residents** in 1870 to **1,071** in 1900, peaking at **3,466** in 1930
- The **Berghilf Sanatorium** became the **Tschuggen Grand Hotel** for the 1929/1930 winter season, pivoting from medicine to tourism
- The **Waldsanatorium** was converted to the **Neues Waldhotel** in **1932**, later serving as a **Swiss Federal Military Sanatorium** (1938–1954), before becoming the **Waldhotel National** in 1961 — today's **Waldhotel Arosa**

## 5. Thomas Mann — The First Days of Exile

- **Thomas Mann** (1875–1955), the Nobel Prize-winning German author, was a regular guest in Arosa with his wife **Katia** — they spent at least **eight extended vacations** at the Waldhotel (formerly the Waldsanatorium)
- Katia Mann was treated at the Waldsanatorium for a lung condition; Mann began composing **"Der Zauberberg" (The Magic Mountain)** while visiting her there — though the novel's setting became the fictional Berghof sanatorium in **Davos**
- In **February 1933**, Mann was on a lecture tour through Amsterdam, Brussels, and Paris when Adolf Hitler seized power on 30 January. His eldest children, **Klaus and Erika**, warned him by telephone that it would not be safe to return to Munich
- Mann retreated to Arosa, staying at the **Neues Waldhotel** — where he and Katia made the momentous decision in **March 1933** not to return to Germany
- His controversial **Wagner lecture** (delivered 10 February 1933 in Munich, just before his departure) had provoked a furious Nazi backlash; the "Protest der Richard-Wagner-Stadt München" condemned him publicly
- From Arosa, the Manns moved to **Sanary-sur-Mer** in southern France, then to **Küsnacht** near Zurich (autumn 1933–1938), and eventually to the United States. Mann's German citizenship was revoked in **1936**; he never lived in Germany again
- An exhibition titled **"Erwin Schrödinger & Thomas Mann — Lebenswende in Arosa"** was displayed at the Arosa museum, commemorating the two great figures whose lives took decisive turns in this small Alpine village

## 6. Schrödinger's Wave Equation — Physics Born at 1,800 Meters

- **Erwin Schrödinger** (1887–1961), then a professor of theoretical physics at the **University of Zurich**, traveled to Arosa for the Christmas holidays in **December 1925**
- He stayed at the **Villa Frisia** (part of Dr. Herwig's former sanatorium complex) at **Sonnenbergstrasse 14**
- During this holiday, Schrödinger worked on a problem posed by his colleague **Peter Debye**: to find a proper wave equation for Louis de Broglie's "matter waves"
- On **27 December 1925**, Schrödinger wrote to physicist Wilhelm Wien that he had found a model whose "self-frequencies" matched those of the hydrogen atom — the breakthrough
- On **27 January 1926**, his paper **"Quantisierung als Eigenwertproblem"** (Quantization as an Eigenvalue Problem) was received by the journal *Annalen der Physik* — the first of four papers laying the foundations of **quantum wave mechanics**
- Upon returning to Zurich, he told a colleague he had **"barely gone skiing because he had been working on a few calculations"**
- Albert Einstein wrote to him: **"The idea behind your work is proof of true genius!"**
- Schrödinger shared the **1933 Nobel Prize in Physics** with Paul Dirac for this work
- In **2026**, the Swiss Academy of Sciences honored Arosa as a **"Chemical Landmark"** on the 100th anniversary, recognizing the Schrödinger equation as one of the ten most important publications in physics

## 7. The Rhaetian Railway to Arosa — Engineering Through the Schanfigg

- By 1900, it was clear the winding **Schanfiggerstrasse** (completed 1890) could not handle the growing traffic to the booming health resort
- Construction of the **Chur–Arosa railway** began on **1 August 1912** under chief engineer **Gustav Bener** and his deputy **Hans Studer**
- The line opened on **12 December 1914**, delayed from the planned 15 November due to unstable terrain — built in just over two years
- Technical specs: **25.7 km** long, **metre-gauge** (1,000 mm), climbing **1,155 meters** from Chur (585 m) to Arosa (1,740 m), with a maximum gradient of 6%
- The line passes through **19 tunnels** and crosses **41 bridges and viaducts**
- Construction cost: **CHF 402,700 per kilometer** — the most expensive line on the entire Rhaetian Railway network
- The crown jewel is the **Langwieser Viaduct**: designed by **Hermann Schürch**, built by **Eduard Züblin** between 1912 and 1914, at a cost of CHF 625,000
- The viaduct is **287 meters long** with a main arch span of **100 meters** rising **62 meters** above the Plessur valley — it was the **world's first railway bridge built entirely of reinforced concrete** and, at completion, the longest in the world
- A total of **7,469 cubic meters of concrete** and **250 tons of steel** were used in its construction
- The Langwieser Viaduct is now a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**
- In **1942**, the Chur-Arosa-Bahn was incorporated into the **Rhaetian Railway** (RhB)
- Today, the one-hour journey from Chur to Arosa remains one of Switzerland's most scenic railway experiences

## 8. Arthur Conan Doyle — Skiing into Arosa

- On **23 March 1894**, **Arthur Conan Doyle** — creator of Sherlock Holmes — set out at 4 AM from Frauenkirch near Davos with two local guides, the **Branger brothers** (Tobias and Johannes), to cross the **Maienfelder Furgga pass** on skis to Arosa
- The Branger brothers had first completed this crossing exactly one year earlier, on **23 March 1893** — they were among the earliest ski mountaineers in the Alps
- The journey took approximately **7 hours** and was not without peril: Conan Doyle later wrote about the precipices with typical British understatement
- His humorous account described the toll on his clothing: **"My tailor claims that Harris Tweed does not wear out. That is pure theory that does not stand up to thorough scrutiny. Between the Furgga Pass and Arosa he can inspect numerous samples of his fabric."**
- The article, **"An Alpine Pass on 'Ski'"**, was published in **The Strand Magazine** in **December 1894** and is credited with helping popularize Alpine skiing among the British public

## 9. Arosa Bärenland — The Bear Sanctuary in the Mountains

- The idea for a bear sanctuary in Arosa was born in **2010**, conceived by tourism director **Pascal Jenny** with a vision to create a species-appropriate habitat for rescued bears
- An autumn 2010 legacy donation provided initial funding
- In **November 2012**, the international animal welfare organization **FOUR PAWS** contacted Arosa Tourism to propose collaboration
- In **June 2015**, the civic community approved the location near the **Weisshorn cable car's middle station**, at over 2,000 meters elevation
- In **November 2016**, Arosa residents voted in favor of the project with over **78% support**; the **Arosa Bären Foundation** was established in December 2016
- Construction began **August 2017**; the sanctuary officially opened **3–5 August 2018**
- **Napa** (born 12 December 2006), a former circus bear who had lived for years in a tiny, rusty cage in the Corona circus in Serbia, arrived on **4 July 2018** as the first resident; he was euthanized in November 2020 due to epilepsy complications
- **Amelia and Meimo**, two restaurant bears from Albania who had spent years in a 60-square-meter concrete enclosure, arrived in **February 2019**
- **Jambolina**, a former circus bear from Ukraine, arrived in **December 2020**
- **Sam and Jamila**, siblings who spent nearly two decades in separate concrete pits at a zoo in Skopje, North Macedonia, were rescued in **2021**
- The sanctuary welcomed its **100,000th visitor** on **2 July 2020**
- An **adventure trail with suspension bridges** opened on **17 July 2020**, and the **Arosa Bear Academy** educational program launched in July 2020

## 10. The Arosa–Lenzerheide Ski Connection

- Arosa's winter sports history began with the arrival of Europe's ski-tourism pioneers: the first **ski lifts** were installed in **1938**, and the **Hörnli skilift** followed in **1940**
- **David Zogg** (1902–1977), raised in Arosa, won the first-ever **FIS World Championship in Slalom** in **1931** and the **World Championship in Downhill** in **1934**; after retiring from racing, he led the **Arosa ski school** for many years
- For decades, Arosa and Lenzerheide operated as separate ski areas, separated by the **Urdental valley**
- In **December 2013**, the **Urdenbahn** opened — an aerial tramway connecting the Arosa Hörnli to the Urdenfürggli on the Lenzerheide side
- The Urdenbahn, built by Austrian-Swiss manufacturer **Steurer Seilbahnen**, spans **1,682 meters** with a single unsupported rope span (no pylons) — an engineering feat surmounting just 70 meters of altitude difference
- The connection created the **largest contiguous ski area in Graubünden**: **225 kilometers** of slopes with 43 cable cars and lifts, at elevations between 1,225 m and 2,865 m
- Arosa alone offers approximately **60 km of pistes**, predominantly blue and red runs, plus **92 km of cross-country trails**

## 11. Cultural Life — The Humor Festival and Traditions

- The **Arosa Humorfestival**, Switzerland's largest comedy and cabaret event, has been held annually since **1992**
- Performances take place in a heated circus tent beside the **Tschuggenhütte** at 2,000 meters above sea level, over eleven days in December
- The festival has hosted Swiss comedians including **DivertiMento**, **Marco Rima**, **Claudio Zuccolini**, and **Massimo Rocchi**, as well as international acts like **Josef Hader**, **Michael Mittermeier**, and **Helge Schneider**
- **Chalandamarz**: a traditional spring festival blending pagan and Christian elements, in which children process through the village ringing large cowbells to drive away winter — a Walser and Romansh tradition practiced across Graubünden on **1 March**
- The **Arosa Bärenlauf**: an annual 42 km cross-country ski marathon held in January
- **Arosa Electronica**: a March electronic music festival on the slopes
- The **Heimatmuseum Arosa-Schanfigg**: a local history museum documenting the valley's transformation from farming community to world-famous resort
- The **Alpine Garden Arosa** preserves approximately **500 vascular plant species** native to the region

## 12. Notable People and Events

- **Dr. Otto Herwig** (discoverer of Arosa as a health resort, 1883; honorary citizen, 1922)
- **Erwin Schrödinger** (formulated the wave equation in Arosa, Christmas 1925; Nobel Prize 1933)
- **Thomas Mann** (regular guest; made the decision to go into exile in Arosa, March 1933)
- **Arthur Conan Doyle** (skied into Arosa from Davos in March 1894; wrote the famous Strand Magazine article)
- **David Zogg** (raised in Arosa; first FIS Slalom World Champion, 1931; Downhill World Champion, 1934)
- **Christian Morgenstern** (German poet; was a patient at the Waldsanatorium)
- **Alfred Michael Koch** (born in Arosa, 1894; became a Canadian flying ace with ten aerial victories in World War I)
- **Paul ten Bruggencate** (born in Arosa, 1901; became a German astronomer and astrophysicist)
- **Alex Fiva** (born 1986 in nearby Parpan; freestyle ski cross world champion 2021; Olympic silver medalist, Beijing 2022)
- **Prince William and Prince Harry** of England have visited Arosa as holiday guests
- In **2026**, the Swiss Academy of Sciences designated Arosa a "Chemical Landmark" on the centenary of Schrödinger's wave equation

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The equation in the snow** — A child staying at Villa Frisia finds a forgotten notebook filled with mysterious formulas; following the scribbled clues through Arosa, they meet the ghost of a physicist who barely went skiing because he was "working on a few calculations" — and discover how waves can describe the whole universe
2. **The bears who found a home** — A bear named Napa escapes a tiny circus cage and dreams of mountains; guided by an Alpine chough, he travels from Serbia across Europe to a sanctuary high in the Swiss Alps, where he sees real trees, real snow, and real sky for the first time
3. **360 curves to the sky** — A child rides the Rhaetian Railway from Chur to Arosa; at each of the great viaducts and tunnels, the train reveals a different chapter of the valley's history — Walser farmers, sanatorium patients, a physicist scribbling equations, bears padding through snow
4. **The detective who learned to ski** — Arthur Conan Doyle arrives in Davos and accepts a dare to cross the Maienfelder Furgga on skis; a local child joins as guide, and along the way they must solve a mystery worthy of Sherlock Holmes before tumbling into Arosa in shredded Harris Tweed
5. **The Magic Mountain's secret** — A child discovers a hidden room in the Waldhotel where Thomas Mann once sat writing; stepping through the door, they enter the world of "The Magic Mountain" and must find their way back before seven years pass in seven hours
6. **The bell of 1492** — The ancient Maria bell in the Bergkirchli begins to ring by itself on a winter night; a child follows the sound through Innerarosa and meets the Walser families who built the chapel, learning why they crossed the mountains to make a home at the end of the valley
7. **Napa and the stars** — At the bear sanctuary, a rescued bear looks up at the night sky above the Weisshorn for the first time; a child explains the constellations, and together they discover that freedom is learning to see what was always there — the whole wide world above
8. **The bridge that couldn't fall** — In 1913, young apprentice workers on the Langwieser Viaduct must solve the mystery of why the concrete needs so much more material than planned; spanning the Plessur gorge 62 meters above the river, they build what becomes the world's first reinforced-concrete railway bridge — and a child from today walks across it on the train, connecting past and present
