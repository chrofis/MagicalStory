## 1. First Mention and Founding — From Rotbald's Gift to the Counts of Toggenburg

- **754**: Wil appears in its earliest written record, the **Henau document** of August 6, 754, which describes free farmer **Rotbald** donating his property to the **Monastery of St. Gallen**. As part of the lease agreement, Rotbald was to deliver **30 barrels of beer** annually, along with grain and pigs — one of the oldest documented beer levies in Switzerland
- The settlement grew over centuries at a strategic location on an **elongated hilltop** overlooking the Thur valley
- Around **1200**, the **Counts of Toggenburg** formally founded Wil as a town, establishing it as a political and economic outpost to control regional trade
- **1226**: Count **Diethelm I of Toggenburg** transferred the town and surrounding territory to the **Prince-Abbey of St. Gallen**, beginning over 570 years of abbatial rule
- Wil remained the undisputed property of the prince-abbots from 1226 onward, interrupted only briefly in **1407** when rebellious **Appenzellers** temporarily seized the town during the Appenzell Wars

## 2. Geography — Gateway to the Toggenburg

- Wil sits on a prominent **hilltop** at approximately **571 meters above sea level**, with the Hof at the summit reaching about 603 meters
- Located in the canton of **St. Gallen**, roughly halfway between the city of **St. Gallen** (about 20 km east) and **Winterthur** (about 30 km northwest), on the main railway line connecting Zurich to St. Gallen
- The town overlooks the **Thur valley** and marks the entrance to the **Toggenburg** region — the long valley of the Thur river stretching southeast toward the Churfirsten mountains
- The surrounding area is called the **Fuerstenland** ("Prince's Land"), named after the prince-abbots who ruled it
- Wil is the **third-largest population center** in the canton of St. Gallen (after St. Gallen and Rapperswil-Jona), with about **25,000 inhabitants** and an agglomeration of over 74,000 — the second-largest conurbation in Eastern Switzerland

## 3. The Hof zu Wil — The Abbot's Hilltop Palace

- The **Hof zu Wil** is one of the most significant **secular Gothic buildings** in Switzerland, dominating the old town from its highest point
- At its core stands a **residential tower from the late 12th century**, built by the Toggenburg family, featuring **herringbone-pattern masonry** and ground-floor walls **four meters thick**
- After the **1226 transfer** to the abbey, the abbots gradually expanded the structure with additional wings and a palace (Palas) on the eastern side
- **1391**: A major expansion added new building complexes above the existing fortification walls
- **Fuerstabt Ulrich Roesch** (abbot 1463–1491), a baker's son from Wangen im Allgaeu who became one of the abbey's most important leaders, gave the Hof its present external form in **1481–1482**, unifying all earlier structures under a single mighty **hip roof**
- The Hof served for over 500 years as the **most important court and administrative center** of the Prince-Abbey outside St. Gallen, housing the **Reichsvogt** (imperial advocate managing finances and law) and the **high court** (Blutgericht), which held jurisdiction over life and death from **1463**
- **Abbot Diethelm Blarer** added a chapel and servants' quarters around **1540**; Renaissance-era abbots expanded it with grand halls and murals
- **Abbot Coelestin Sfondrati** (1687–1696), a Milanese nobleman who later became a **cardinal**, introduced Baroque interior modifications
- The last abbot to reside here was **Pankraz Vorster** (1796–1805), born in Naples to a Fuerstenland family, who was forced to flee in **1798** when the French invasion ended the old regime
- **1978**: The Hof was placed under **federal heritage protection**; designated a monument of **national significance** in 1990
- **1988**: The citizens of Wil voted to purchase the Hof; a foundation (Stiftung Hof zu Wil) was established in 1990 to restore and maintain it
- Today the Hof houses a **restaurant**, the **Stadtmuseum Wil**, a library, event spaces, and tourist information

## 4. The Altstadt — Eastern Switzerland's Best-Preserved Old Town

- Wil's Altstadt is recognized as **one of the best-preserved medieval old towns in Eastern Switzerland**, featuring picturesque timber-framed houses, arcaded walkways, and narrow medieval lanes
- **1984**: The town was awarded the **Wakker Prize** by the Swiss Heritage Society for its careful preservation and revitalization of historic buildings as a living environment
- The main old town street is the **Marktgasse**, lined with colorful burgher houses and leading up to the Hofplatz
- Key landmarks on the Hofplatz include the **Hof zu Wil**, the **Baronenhaus**, the former town hall (which stood between 1505 and 1854), and the **Pankratiusbrunnen** (fountain)
- Swissminiatur in Melide describes it simply as "the best-preserved historical centre in eastern Switzerland"

## 5. Town Walls, Gates, and the Schnetztor

- The earliest documented **town wall** dates to **1260**, encircling only the hilltop core between the Schnetztor and Schneckentor gates
- After the **1292 destruction** and the 1301 rebuilding, a new surrounding wall was erected; medieval foundations remain embedded in buildings' basements, with walls reaching approximately **6 meters high** on the northern side
- After another fire in **1312**, the town expanded eastward and southward. New districts documented in **1338–1340** received their own walls, approximately **1 meter thick** and **6–8 meters tall**
- The **Obere Vorstadt** (Upper Suburb) had three external gates and a corner tower; the eastern wall survives largely intact with distinctive **keyhole-shaped gun ports**
- The **Niedere Vorstadt** (Lower Suburb) had five external gates and a southeastern corner tower
- The **Schnetztor**, the upper city gate, was rebuilt under Fuerstabt Ulrich Roesch (1463–1491) as a **closed gate tower** and later served as a prison. It remains a **landmark** and the most prominent surviving element of the medieval fortifications
- Most other gates were demolished in the 19th century: the Schneckentor in **1835**, the Pankratiustor in **1834**

## 6. The Destruction of 1292 and Rebuilding

- In **1287**, citizens of Wil set fire to the nearby Habsburg town of **Schwarzenbach**, provoking retaliation
- **1292**: Duke **Albrecht von Habsburg** besieged and burned Wil, ordering its inhabitants to relocate to Schwarzenbach
- This occurred during a power struggle between the prince-abbots, who were expanding Wil as their western stronghold, and King **Rudolf of Habsburg**, who had founded Schwarzenbach as a rival settlement
- A peace settlement in **1301** returned Wil to the Prince-Abbey; Schwarzenbach was to be dismantled, and Wil was rebuilt and regained its **market rights**
- A second devastating fire struck in **1312**, leading to further reconstruction and the expansion of the suburbs

## 7. The Defensionale von Wil (1647) — Birthplace of Swiss Armed Neutrality

- In **1647**, during the final years of the **Thirty Years' War** (1618–1648), the Swiss war council assembled in the Hof zu Wil to agree on a federal defense ordinance
- The **Defensionale von Wil** was the **first comprehensive military ordinance** of the Swiss Confederation, containing obligations on mobilization, organization, and armament
- It allowed the establishment of a war council that could immediately mobilize **12,000 men**, with the capacity to repeat mobilization up to three times for a total force of **36,000**
- In 1638, the thirteen cantons had already committed to **armed neutrality**; the Defensionale formalized this into a concrete military framework
- The Defensionale is considered the **foundation of Swiss armed neutrality** — a principle that still defines Switzerland's foreign policy today
- After the **Peace of Westphalia** (1648), the Defensionale provided the basis for further border security measures

## 8. The Toggenburgerkrieg (1712) — When Wil Fell to Protestant Cannon

- The **Toggenburg War** (also known as the Second War of Villmergen) erupted in **1712** as a civil war between Catholic inner cantons allied with the Prince-Abbey and Protestant cantons of Zurich and Bern supporting Toggenburg rebels
- **May 21–22, 1712**: Zurich and Bern artillery **bombarded Wil**, a key Abbey outpost near the Toggenburg border where the abbot had concentrated his forces
- The town surrendered rapidly after minimal resistance
- The war ended with a decisive Protestant victory at the **Battle of Villmergen** on July 25, 1712, fundamentally shifting the balance of power within the Confederation in favor of the Protestant cantons
- For Wil, the bombardment marked the beginning of the end of the prince-abbots' temporal power, which would be fully extinguished by the **French invasion of 1798**

## 9. The Stadtweier and Market Tradition

### The Stadtweier (Town Pond)
- The **Stadtweier** was probably artificially created and first documented around **1370**
- In **1470**, Fuerstabt **Ulrich Roesch** created a second, upper pond to ensure the **fish supply during Lent** — reflecting the practical importance of fish ponds for medieval ecclesiastical communities
- From the **15th century** onward, contracts with professional fishermen are documented; in **1719**, fisherman Lorenz Braecker built a dwelling by the ponds
- Today the Stadtweier is an idyllic park at the eastern foot of the old town, with meadows, a playground, ducks, swans, and pygmy goats

### Market Tradition
- Markets have been held in Wil since the town's founding in the late **12th century**
- After the 1301 rebuilding, Wil was granted rights for **two annual markets**, a **weekly market**, and specialty markets for lard and salt
- In **1472**, Abbot Ulrich Roesch obtained permission from Emperor **Friedrich III** for two additional annual markets
- The four great markets fell on **Candlemas**, the **first Tuesday in May**, **Pentecost**, and **St. Othmar's Day**
- The cattle market, first documented in **1474**, was held in the Upper Suburb
- Wil's **Saturday weekly market** continues today, with local producers selling regional products

## 10. Notable Buildings Beyond the Hof

### The Baronenhaus
- Built in **1795** by **Josef Pankraz von Grueebler** (1737–1803), the last imperial advocate (Reichsvogt) appointed by the prince-abbot
- Grueebler was ennobled in May 1795, receiving the title of **baron** — giving the house its name
- Art historian Bernhard Anderes described it as "the last stately large-scale construction in the Canton of St. Gallen," combining **Baroque architectural principles** with **Neoclassical furnishings**
- Features a prominent tower, arcades, and **illusionistic facade paintings**
- Listed as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance**
- Sold to the **Ortsgemeinde Wil** in 1952; restored 1993–94 and now serves as a municipal administrative building

### Dominikanerinnenkloster St. Katharina
- Originally founded in **1228** in the city of St. Gallen, with an active scriptorium
- Forced to leave St. Gallen during the **Reformation**; relocated to Wil in **1607** after Prioress secured approval from the papal nuncio in 1606
- The monastery church was consecrated on **July 26, 1607**, featuring an elegant **Baroque roof tower** with an onion dome
- The nuns took on **girls' education** from 1809, running the well-known school "Kathi" for two centuries
- Listed as a **heritage site of national significance**; the community continues contemplative Dominican life today

### Maria Dreibrunnen (Pilgrimage Church)
- Likely founded in the first half of the **13th century** by the Counts of Toggenburg; the name comes from **three springs** that rise nearby
- First mentioned in **1275** as "Tueffenbrunnen"
- In **1289**, Count Diethelm of Toggenburg donated it to **Monastery Rueti**; after the Reformation dissolved Rueti, Zurich sold it to the **Holy Spirit Hospital of Wil** in 1526
- Received a magnificent **Rococo interior** in **1761–1763** by painter Jakob Joseph Mueller
- Listed as a **cultural property of national significance**; has served as an official pilgrimage site since 1938

## 11. The Hofchilbi and the Beer Tax of 754

- In **1540**, Abbot **Diethelm Blarer von Wartensee** built a chapel at the Hof, consecrated on **July 25** — the annual celebration of this consecration became the **Hofchilbi** festival
- The Hofchilbi was celebrated every year until **1722**, when it lapsed due to difficult economic times
- In **1972**, the **Hofbrauerei Wil** revived the festival; it has been held annually since, on the last Saturday in July
- The festival ceremonially commemorates the **beer tax of 754** — Rotbald's ancient obligation to deliver 30 barrels (approximately **1,125 liters**) of beer to the monastery
- Today the beer is distributed to festival-goers, with proceeds supporting the **Stiftung Hof zu Wil**
- The Hofchilbi has featured celebrity beer-tappers including Olympic champions **Vreni Schneider** (2002) and **Denise Biellmann** (2003)

## 12. The Wiler Turm and Modern Wil

- The **Wiler Turm**, opened in **2006** on the partly forested **Hofberg** near Wil, is a striking **38-meter timber tower** designed by engineer **Julius Natterer**
- Built entirely of wood with **X-shaped supports**, its floor plan is an equilateral triangle with a 12-meter base
- A **189-step double spiral staircase** leads to a covered viewing platform at 34 meters, offering panoramic views from Lake Constance to the Alps
- Wil has a **Chocolarium** (the Munz & Minor chocolate factory experience) and is near the **Walter Zoo** in Gossau
- **Karin Keller-Sutter**, born in 1963 in nearby Uzwil and long a resident of Wil, served as a municipal councillor in Wil (1992–2000) before rising to become **President of the Swiss Confederation** in 2025 — the most prominent political figure associated with the town

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The beer barrel of 754** — A child discovers the ancient Henau document and must deliver 30 barrels of beer to the abbey, traveling through 1,200 years of Wil's history along the way
2. **The abbot's secret tower** — Exploring the Hof zu Wil, a child finds a hidden staircase inside the 12th-century tower with its four-meter-thick walls and discovers the rooms where prince-abbots once decided matters of life and death
3. **The Defensionale drummer** — In 1647, during the Thirty Years' War, a child drummer at the Hof zu Wil must rally the Swiss cantons to sign the defense pact that will secure Swiss neutrality for centuries to come
4. **The Schnetztor prisoner** — A child locked in the old gate tower overnight discovers the ghosts of medieval travelers, Habsburg soldiers, and Appenzeller rebels who all once passed through this gate
5. **The Stadtweier fish miracle** — When Abbot Ulrich Roesch's Lenten fish pond runs dry, a child must find a way to fill the Stadtweier before the Lenten fast, befriending the fish, ducks, and swans along the way
6. **The bombardment of 1712** — During the Toggenburgerkrieg, a child living in Wil must protect the town's treasures as Zurich and Bern cannons thunder against the walls — told from both sides of the confessional divide
7. **The dancing sisters of St. Katharina** — When Dominican nuns arrive in Wil in 1607, fleeing the Reformation in St. Gallen, a local child helps them build their new home and discovers the power of their ancient illuminated manuscripts
8. **The double spiral** — A child races a friend up the Wiler Turm's two intertwining spiral staircases (189 steps each), and at every landing, a window reveals a different era of Wil's history stretching out below
