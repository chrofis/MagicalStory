## 1. Geography — The Left Bank of Lake Zurich

- Wädenswil sits on the **left (western/southern) bank of Lake Zurich**, roughly halfway between the city of Zurich and Rapperswil, in the **Horgen district** of Canton Zurich
- Approximately **60% of the municipal area** is used for farming, 10% for forests, and 30% is developed — reflecting its agricultural character despite urban growth
- With around **25,700 inhabitants**, it is the **largest municipality on the left bank** of the lake
- The opposite (eastern) shore is the famous **Goldküste** ("Gold Coast"), named for its sunny exposure and wealthy residents. The left bank was historically dubbed the **Pfnüselküste** ("Sniffle Coast") — a teasing reference to its north-facing aspect lying in the shadow of its own hills, which meant less evening sun and supposedly more colds
- In recent years, the left bank has rebranded itself as the **"Silver Coast" or even "Platinum Coast"**, with brisk construction activity and rising real estate values overtaking the Gold Coast in some segments
- On **1 January 2019**, the former municipalities of **Hütten and Schönenberg** merged into Wädenswil, expanding the municipal area from 17.4 km² to **35.64 km²**
- The town is accessible via the **A3 motorway** and by rail (S2, S25, S8 to Zurich in approximately 29 minutes), as well as by **Lake Zurich boat service** in summer

## 2. Early History — The Freiherren von Wädenswil

- The earliest written reference to Wädenswil appears in a **foundation charter of the Monastery of Fahr** (dated around **1130**), which lists witnesses including **"Waldhere de uvadensuvilere"** — though the authenticity of this document is disputed among historians
- In the High Middle Ages, the settlement was the seat of the **Freiherren (barons) von Wädenswil**, a noble family whose castle (**Alt-Wädenswil**) controlled the territory between the Mülibach and Meilibach rivers down to Lake Zurich
- The last baron, **Rudolf III** (born c. 1233), had no male heir. In **1287**, he sold the castle and the entire Herrschaft (lordship) to the **Johanniter Commandery at Bubikon** (Knights Hospitaller) for **650 Mark Silber** (roughly 152.75 kg of silver), retaining the right to live in the castle and an annual pension of 20 marks silver until his death
- Rudolf III died around **1300**. The Johanniter paid his heirs an additional 270 marks silver to settle inheritance claims, and the Order ruled the Herrschaft Wädenswil as a largely **independent domain for over 250 years**

## 3. From Johanniter to Zurich — The 1549 Purchase

- In **1549**, the Johanniter Order sold the Herrschaft Wädenswil with all rights — castle, high and low jurisdiction, patronage rights over the churches of Wädenswil and Richterswil — to the **city of Zurich** for **20,000 Gulden** (negotiated down from the initial asking price of 32,000)
- Wädenswil became a **Landvogtei** (bailwick) administered by a Zurich-appointed Landvogt. A new castle for the administrator was constructed between **1550 and 1555**
- However, the neighbouring canton of **Schwyz** felt threatened by Zurich's expanding power. At the **Tagsatzung** (Federal Diet), a decision was reached that Zurich had to **demolish the fortified castle** in **1557** and replace it with an unfortified administrative building
- This was the site of the later **Schloss Wädenswil**, which housed the Landvogt and eventually became the home of the federal research station in 1890

## 4. The Reformation and the Anabaptists

- The Reformation reached Wädenswil in **1529**, following Huldrych Zwingli's movement in Zurich. The existing medieval church was converted for Reformed worship. By **1540**, all altars, images, and Catholic furnishings were removed from the church, completing the transition to **Reformed Protestantism**
- Alongside the official Reformed Church, Wädenswil became an important centre of **Anabaptism** (Täuferbewegung) from the early 1500s. By **1635**, at least **71 Anabaptists** lived in the community
- On **26 January 1613**, a formal disputation took place in Wädenswil between Reformed clergy and fifteen Anabaptists led by elder **Hans Landis** of Horgerberg
- Landis, born around 1568 in Hirzel near Zurich, refused to recant or emigrate, declaring that "the earth was the Lord's" and no authority could drive them out. After multiple imprisonments and escapes, he was **beheaded on 30 September 1614** in the Wellenberg tower in the Limmat River in Zurich — the **last Anabaptist martyr** executed in Switzerland
- His execution is commemorated in **hymn No. 132** of the Ausbund, the oldest hymnbook of the Swiss Brethren, with 46 stanzas
- Many Wädenswil Anabaptists eventually emigrated, first to the **Palatinate** and then to **Pennsylvania** in the 1700s, becoming part of the Mennonite and Amish communities in North America

## 5. The Reformed Church — Grubenmann's Masterwork

- The first recorded mention of a church in Wädenswil dates to **1270**. The medieval building was originally a **Romanesque hall church** with a rectangular east-facing choir
- After the Reformation in 1529, the church served the Reformed community. By the 17th century, the growing population necessitated expansions — in **1638**, the church was extended by two meters and fitted with a gallery
- By **1760**, the medieval structure was beyond repair. In **1763**, the community commissioned **Hans Ulrich Grubenmann** (1709-1783), the famous master carpenter and bridge builder from **Teufen in Appenzell Ausserrhoden**, to design a new church
- Grubenmann's creation, completed between **1764 and 1767**, is considered his **masterwork**: a late-Baroque cross church spanning **36 meters in length and 20 meters in width** with a roof supported by **no interior columns** — a remarkable feat of structural engineering
- According to legend, Grubenmann convinced the sceptical church council by building a **wooden scale model** and standing on it to demonstrate the roof's load-bearing capacity
- The church was inaugurated on **23 August 1767** with cantatas composed by **Johann Jakob Nägeli**
- Major renovations took place in 1862-64, 1916, 1935, 1951-52, 1983-84, and 1998-99; the building remains a vibrant place of worship and one of Grubenmann's most celebrated structures

## 6. The Stäfner Handel (1794-1795) — Demands for Democratic Rights

- By the late 18th century, the communities along Lake Zurich — Wädenswil, Stäfa, and Horgen — had grown wealthy through their **textile industry** (cotton and silk). A prosperous rural upper class of merchants and entrepreneurs emerged, but they lacked the **legal and political rights** enjoyed by citizens of the city of Zurich
- Inspired by the **French Revolution**, potter **Heinrich Neeracher** from Stäfa authored the **Stäfner Memorial** in 1794, a document cataloguing the countryside's historic grievances and demanding **legal equality** with the city
- In **November 1794**, the Memorial was first read and discussed in Stäfa. On **19 November**, delegates from lake communities held a secret conference in **Meilen**
- Zurich's government responded harshly: Neeracher and co-authors **Pfenninger and Staub** were **exiled** in January 1795, and over **sixty others** received monetary fines and penalties to their civic honour
- In Stäfa, inhabitants erected a **Freiheitsbaum** (liberty tree) during the night of **21-22 March 1795**
- In Wädenswil, a **Revolutionary Club** of leading entrepreneurs considered supporting Stäfa's patriots, but two government-loyal figures — Undervogt **Hans Jakob Hauser** and teacher **Heinrich Leuthold** — prevented unified action. When land militias assembled in Wädenswil to suppress the unrest, leather merchant **Hauser** publicly called for **passive resistance**, was arrested, imprisoned for twenty days, and fined
- The suppression only deepened resentment. In **February 1798**, as the old order collapsed, over **70 communes** assembled in Wädenswil's church. On **5 February 1798**, Zurich finally proclaimed **legal equality** between city and countryside — a direct consequence of the Stäfner Handel
- Wädenswil itself erected a **liberty tree** at the Eidmatt meadow during **Carnival 1798** — pointedly at the same spot where rural representatives had suffered humiliation during a conflict in **1646**

## 7. Textile Industry — From Cotton Spinning to Silk for Dior

- Between **1730 and 1760**, textile home work in Wädenswil evolved from a supplementary income into a full **putting-out industry**. Local families — the Blattmanns, Diezingers, and Theilers — operated as independent entrepreneurs, manufacturing mousseline (wool-cotton blend), cotton yarns, and printed fabrics
- By **1770**, more than **half of all Wädenswil residents** lived from textile work. In **1787**, among 3,620 inhabitants there were **435 cotton spinners** and **516 looms** in operation
- In **1811**, the firm Blattmann, Diezinger & Co. established a mechanized cotton spinning and weaving facility in what is now the town hall building
- **August Gessner-Theiler** entered the silk trade in **1841**, founding what would become **Gessner AG** — a global silk-weaving company headquartered in Wädenswil. His son **Emil Gessner** mechanized operations in **1881-82** with a new facility in the Neuwiesquartier
- At its peak before World War I, Gessner employed **2,200 workers** and exported silk fabrics throughout Europe and to America, with factory branches in Waldshut, Rovereto, Villeurbanne, and **Dunfermline, Scotland**. Its tie fabrics supplied fashion houses including **Dior, Karl Lagerfeld, Versace, and Yves Saint Laurent**
- After the Great Depression and post-war decline, the workforce shrank to 227 by 1991. **Gessner AG closed in 2012** as the **last silk-weaving operation in Canton Zurich**
- Other industrial enterprises included: a tannery (Johannes Hauser, **1813-14**, with 70 vats), a horsehair factory (Schnyder, **1826**), and a cloth factory (Pfenninger, wool goods). Infrastructure followed: the **railway** arrived in **1875-1877**, gas service in **1874**, and hydroelectric power in **1895**

## 8. Viticulture — The Wine Capital of Lake Zurich

- Vine-growing on the Au peninsula is documented as far back as **1484**. The warm, south-facing slopes above the lake created ideal conditions for viticulture, and Wädenswil became a centre of the Zurich wine region
- The **Experimental Station for Fruit, Wine and Horticulture** was founded in Wädenswil in **1890**, on the initiative of **Colonel Viktor Fehr** (1846-1938) and Director Lutz of the Strickhof Agricultural School. Originally a concordat effort among German-speaking cantons, the **federal government took over the station in 1902**
- The station's first director was **Hermann Müller-Thurgau** (1850-1927), a Swiss botanist and oenologist who had already bred the world's most widely planted "new" grape variety at the Geisenheim Research Institute in Germany — a **Riesling x Madeleine royale** cross that was formally named **"Müller-Thurgau"** in **1913**. In **1891**, 150 plants were shipped to Wädenswil where trials continued under Heinrich Schellenberg
- Müller-Thurgau served as director until his retirement in **1924**. The variety he created is now cultivated on over **22,000 hectares worldwide** and remains the second most important white grape in German viticulture after Riesling
- During the great **phylloxera and mildew crisis** of the late 19th century, Wädenswil's vines were severely damaged. The Au peninsula vineyards were cleared entirely in **1912**, but replanting began in **1950** under the direction of the Fachschule für Obst- und Weinbau Wädenswil
- Since **2018**, the historic Au vineyards are cultivated by the **Wädenswil Viticulture Centre** on behalf of the ZHAW. The wine is marketed under the **Dreistand** brand

## 9. The Au Peninsula — Nature Reserve and UNESCO Site

- The **Au peninsula** rises from Lake Zurich between Horgen and Wädenswil, a green oasis of **meadows, forests, vineyards**, and the small **Ausee** lake
- The peninsula is designated as a **Naturschutzgebiet** (nature reserve) and features a strong rural character — woodland on the lake-facing side, vineyards on the sun-facing interior slopes
- **Wädenswil-Vorder Au** is one of the **111 serial sites** of the UNESCO World Heritage Site **"Prehistoric Pile Dwellings around the Alps"** (inscribed 2011). Because the lake has risen over millennia, the original wooden pilings now lie **4 to 7 metres below the water surface**. The site has yielded special pottery from the transitional period between the **Pfyn and Horgen cultures** (late Neolithic, c. 3900-2800 BC)
- The **Weinbaumuseum** (Winemaking Museum) on the peninsula opens every Sunday from April through October, with an exhibition in a historic barn illustrating traditional and modern winemaking
- **Schloss Au** stands on the peninsula: in the **1650s**, **Hans Rudolf Werdmüller** built a villa in the Venetian style on the site. This was replaced in **1928-29** by a **neo-Baroque villa** designed by architect Johann A. Freytag, incorporating the 1720s Werdmüller-Trotte (wine press house). In **1985**, the last owner **Eric Alex von Schulthess** donated the estate to the Canton of Zurich. Since 1989 it has served as the **Tagungszentrum Schloss Au** (conference centre), operated by the cantonal college of education (PHZH)
- The estate is listed in the **Swiss inventory of cultural property** as a Class B object of regional importance

## 10. ZHAW and Agroscope — Science Campus on the Lake

- The **Zurich University of Applied Sciences** (ZHAW) operates its **School of Life Sciences and Facility Management** from the Wädenswil campus, with sites at **Grüental and Reidbach**
- The institution traces its roots to the former **Fachschule für Obst- und Weinbau** (School for Fruit and Wine Growing), which evolved through the 20th century into the **University of Applied Sciences Wädenswil**. In **September 2007**, this merged with institutions in Winterthur and Zurich to form the ZHAW
- Today the campus enrols approximately **1,525 Bachelor** and **421 Master** students across **seven Bachelor's and five Master's programmes** in fields including food science, biotechnology, chemistry, natural resource sciences, and facility management, with around **645 staff members**
- A **Master in Life Sciences** programme (jointly with BFH and FHNW) offers specialisation in **viticulture and oenology** — continuing Wädenswil's centuries-old tradition
- In 2024, construction began on the **"Future of Food" campus**, reflecting the institution's focus on sustainable, healthy nutrition research
- Directly adjacent sits **Agroscope**, the Swiss federal centre of excellence for agricultural research. The Wädenswil site, in continuous operation since **1890**, conducts experiments from vineyard to finished wine, including sensory analysis, fruit-growing research, and horticultural innovation. Researchers and students benefit from this unique **co-location** of academic and federal research institutions

## 11. The Brewery and the Kulturgarage

- In **1826**, Heinrich Rusterholz purchased the property "zum grünen Hof" in Wädenswil; in **1833** he established a **brewery** with malt house and vaulted cellar. Extensions in 1837 and 1840 added further beer cellars to meet growing demand for lager beer
- The **Brauerei Wädenswil** operated continuously from 1833 until **1990**. The former brewery building was partially demolished in 2003
- The brewing tradition was revived in **1992** by the **Wädi-Brau-Huus AG**, founded in July 1991 with the first beer brewed on **27 January 1992** and a gastropub opening on 29 February 1992. The brewery-restaurant became a beloved local institution, though it filed for bankruptcy in **December 2024**
- Wädenswil's cultural life centres on the **Kulturgarage** at Florhofstrasse 15, a former Delta-Autogarage **converted in 2012** into a cultural venue staged as a factory hall
- The Kulturgarage hosts the **Historische Gesellschaft Wädenswil** (Historical Society) with its rotating themed exhibitions drawn from a collection of around **4,000 objects** documenting Wädenswil's cultural, social, and economic history
- The venue also houses the **Volkstheater Wädenswil** (community theatre) and is available for public events, assemblies, and cultural programming throughout the year

## 12. Notable People

- **Hans Ulrich Grubenmann** (1709-1783): Though from Teufen, his masterwork — the Reformed Church — stands in Wädenswil
- **Johann Gottfried Steffan** (1815-1905): Swiss landscape painter, born in Wädenswil, known for his Alpine scenes
- **Walter Hauser** (1837-1902): Born in Wädenswil, served as member of the **Swiss Federal Council**
- **Hans Wilhelm Auer** (1847-1906): Born in Wädenswil, architect of the **Swiss Federal Palace** (Bundeshaus) in Bern — the parliament building completed in 1902. A pupil of Gottfried Semper at ETH Zurich, he won the design competition that shaped the most iconic government building in Switzerland
- **Hermann Müller-Thurgau** (1850-1927): Director of the Wädenswil research station from 1891 to 1924, creator of the world-famous grape variety bearing his name
- **Robert Haab** (1865-1939): Politician from Wädenswil, served on the **Swiss Federal Council**
- **Otto Hauser** (1874-1932): Prehistorian from Wädenswil, known for his archaeological excavations in the Dordogne region of France
- **Paul Wild** (1925-2014): Born in Wädenswil, astronomer and director of the Astronomical Institute of the University of Bern. Studied mathematics and physics at ETH Zurich (1944-1950) and worked with Fritz Zwicky at Caltech. Discovered **94 asteroids** (including near-Earth objects Sisyphus, Beltrovata, and Don Quixote), multiple comets, and numerous supernovae during his career at the Zimmerwald Observatory near Bern

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The last baron's silver bargain** — Rudolf III has no heir and must decide the castle's fate; a child helps him negotiate with the Johanniter knights, discovering secret passages in Alt-Wädenswil before the handover in 1287
2. **The vine that crossed the Alps** — Hermann Müller-Thurgau carries his precious grape cuttings from Geisenheim to Wädenswil in 1891; a child accompanies him, learning about patience and cultivation as the first vines are planted on the Au peninsula
3. **Grubenmann's impossible roof** — The master builder bets that he can span 36 metres without a single column; a child apprentice helps him build the scale model and must stand on it with Grubenmann to convince the doubting church council
4. **The liberty tree of Wädenswil** — During the Stäfner Handel, a child from a textile-worker family plants a secret seedling that grows into the liberty tree at the Eidmatt meadow, carrying messages of freedom between lake communities
5. **Hans Landis and the hidden hymn** — An Anabaptist child must memorise 46 stanzas of a forbidden hymn before the community emigrates to the Palatinate, running through the forests above Wädenswil to deliver it to the next congregation
6. **The silk thread to Paris** — A child follows a single silk thread from the looms of Gessner AG all the way to a Dior fashion show in Paris, discovering how Wädenswil's factories clothed the world
7. **Stars over the Sniffle Coast** — Young Paul Wild lies on the Au peninsula one clear night, looking through a homemade telescope at the sky above Lake Zurich; a comet whispers its name and dares him to find it when he grows up
8. **The pile-dwellers of Vorder Au** — A child dives into Lake Zurich and discovers the ancient pilings 4 metres below the surface, slipping back in time to a Neolithic village where the Pfyn and Horgen cultures meet on the Au peninsula
