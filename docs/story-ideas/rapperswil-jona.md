## 1. Founding Legend — The Deer on the Castle Hill

- According to legend, **Count Rudolf of Altendorf** was hunting along the lakeshore with his wife **Countess Mechthild** when their dogs chased a **deer** into a cave on a rocky promontory — today's castle hill
- Inside the cave, two deer calves were hiding. Mechthild took pity and persuaded Rudolf to spare their lives
- At midday the deer returned and **laid its head in Mechthild's lap** in gratitude — the count took this as a divine sign
- He decided to build a **castle on the rock** and a town on its southern slope — the founding of Rapperswil
- The **deer park (Hirschpark)** on the north flank of the castle hill has housed **fallow deer since 1871**, a living reminder of the founding legend. A gamekeeper tends the 10–15 animals daily
- Historically, the **Counts of Rapperswil** relocated from **Altendorf** (Alt-Rapperswil) on the opposite shore of the lake to the new settlement around **1200–1220**

## 2. Schloss Rapperswil — The Castle Above the Lake

- Built circa **1200–1220** by **Count Rudolf II** and his son **Count Rudolf III von Rapperswil**; first documented mention in **1229** on the occasion of the foundation of **Kloster Rüti** (Rüti Abbey)
- The castle formed an **almost equilateral triangle** with three towers, dominating the town and controlling the narrow waterway between the Obersee and the lower Zürichsee, as well as the medieval **Gotthard Pass route** between Lombardy and Zurich
- **1283**: The Rapperswil dynasty became **extinct** with the death of 18-year-old **Count Rudolf V**; Emperor Rudolf I of Habsburg acquired their fiefs
- **1350**: Zurich's first independent mayor **Rudolf Brun** attacked and **destroyed** Rapperswil's fortifications and castle; Count Johann II could not afford to rebuild and sold everything to the **Habsburgs**
- **1352–1400**: **Duke Albrecht II of Habsburg-Austria** rebuilt the castle on the original ruins, creating the silhouette that endures today — an "ostentatious facade" deliberately facing Switzerland to project Habsburg power
- After the extinction of the Habsburg-Laufenburg line in **1442**, the castle passed to the **citizens of Rapperswil**
- Since **1870**, the castle has housed the **Polish National Museum** (see section 5)
- Today: exhibitions on the castle's history, the **Great Knights' Hall** for events, a panoramic tower, and an official civil wedding venue

## 3. The Rosenstadt — City of Roses

- Rapperswil has been called the **"Rosenstadt" (City of Roses)** for over a century, with more than **16,000 rose bushes** of over **600 varieties** blooming between June and October
- The connection to roses runs deep: the coat of arms of the **House of Rapperswil** shows **three roses**; the city of Rapperswil adopted **two roses** — first documented on the oldest known city seal from **1306**
- As early as the **1830s**, numerous gardens were laid out in the filled-in city moat and planted with roses
- In **1959**, gardener **Dietrich Wössener** single-handedly selected the varieties and planted most of the roses in the present city rose gardens
- **Four public rose gardens** adorn the old town today:
  - **Schlossberg rose garden**: around 3,000 plants from 150 varieties, gifted by the **Curti family** in 1965
  - **Capuchin monastery rose garden**: at the foot of the vineyard near the Kapuzinerkloster
  - **Einsiedlerhaus rose garden**: near the historic Einsiedlerhaus
  - **Duftrosengarten (Fragrant Rose Garden) on the Schanz**: established **October 1984** on the roof of the subterranean municipal parking garage — the **only rose garden for the blind in Switzerland**, with names written in Braille on guide panels
- A heraldic curiosity: when **Diethelm VI of Toggenburg** married **Guta of Rapperswil** in the late 12th century, her dowry included one rose from Rapperswil's coat of arms — which is why the town of **Uznach** displays one rose to this day

## 4. The Holzbrücke — Walking Across Lake Zurich

- The **Holzbrücke Rapperswil-Hurden** is a **841-metre** wooden pedestrian bridge crossing the Obersee — the **longest wooden bridge in Switzerland**
- People have been crossing the lake at this narrow point for **millennia**: the oldest remains at the **Hurden Rosshorn** archaeological site date to the **Horgen culture** (late Neolithic, roughly 3rd millennium BC)
- Timber piles from **1523 BC** (Bronze Age) were found at the site, along with remains from the **Hallstatt period (647 BC)**, the **Roman era (AD 165)**, and the **Early Middle Ages (AD 741)**
- These prehistoric lake-crossing structures are part of the **UNESCO World Heritage Site** "Prehistoric Pile Dwellings around the Alps" (inscribed 2011)
- The first documented medieval wooden bridge was built **1358–1360** by **Duke Rudolf IV of Austria**, partly to accommodate the growing stream of **pilgrims to Einsiedeln**, but mainly to secure Habsburg control over eastern access to the Gotthard route
- The **Heilig Hüsli** (Holy House), a tiny bridge chapel measuring just 2 x 3 metres on a 4-metre-high base, was first mentioned in **1485** and rebuilt in stone in **1551**; it provided a spiritual refuge for pilgrims on the **Jakobsweg** (Way of St. James)
- After the **Seedamm** (a railway causeway) was built in **1878**, the old bridge fell into disuse and the Heilig Hüsli stood isolated in the lake for over a century
- On **6 April 2001**, the modern wooden footbridge was inaugurated, reviving the centuries-old tradition of walking across the lake

## 5. The Polish Connection — A Museum of Exile and Memory

- In **1868**, Polish Count **Wladyslaw Broel-Plater** — a post-November 1830 Uprising emigre who had been in Switzerland since 1844 — erected the **Bar Column** (Kolumna Barska) on the Lindenhügel, largely at his own expense
- The column, designed by Zurich University professor **Julius Stadler** and sculpted by **Louis Wethli**, commemorated the **100th anniversary of the Bar Confederation (1768)** — a confederation of Polish magnates that resisted Russian dominance and symbolized Poland's struggle for independence
- On **23 October 1870**, Plater founded the **Polish National Museum** in the castle, at the urging of activist **Agaton Giller**, as "a refuge for Poland's historic memorabilia dishonored and plundered in the occupied homeland"
- The museum was supported by Swiss figures including the writer **Gottfried Keller**
- Collections grew to remarkable size: by **1927**, the museum held approximately **3,000 artworks**, 2,000 historical relics, 20,000 prints, 9,000 medals and coins, **92,000 books**, and 27,000 archived documents — with rooms dedicated to **Kosciuszko**, **Mickiewicz**, and **Copernicus**
- After Poland regained independence, the collections were **transported to Warsaw in 1927** — tragically, roughly **95% were destroyed** during World War II
- The museum was re-established in the castle after 1952 and continues today on the castle's second floor, documenting Polish-Swiss relations and contemporary Polish culture

## 6. The Medieval Old Town — Herrenberg, Rathaus, and Stadtpfarrkirche

- Rapperswil's **Altstadt** (old town) clusters on and around the castle hill, with medieval alleys connecting the **Hauptplatz**, **Engelplatz**, **Fischmarktplatz**, and the harbour
- The **Herrenberg** is an archaeological hillside south of the Lindenhof, where remains of a **Roman castle (1st–3rd century AD)** and an **Early Medieval fortress** granted by **King Ludwig the German** around **870** have been excavated
- The **Rathaus** (town hall) at Hauptplatz was first mentioned in **1419**; the present building dates from around **1470** and served as the seat of Rapperswil's council and mayor until the merger with Jona in 2007
- The **Stadtpfarrkirche** (parish church of St. John) on the Herrenberg was built as a Romanesque hall church around **1220/29** by Count Rudolf II; the parish was formally founded by **Count Rudolf III in 1253**
- A smaller southern tower was added in **1441**; the church was rebuilt as a **tripartite Gothic choir** with arched ceilings and tracery windows after collection campaigns in **1493/97**
- A devastating fire in **1882** caused extensive damage; reconstruction under architect **Xaver Müller** and art historian **Johann Rudolf Rahn** was completed by **1885**

## 7. Centum Prata — The Roman Settlement at Kempraten

- The **Gallo-Roman vicus** of **Centum Prata** (Latin for "100 meadows"), from which the name **Kempraten** derives, is the **most important Roman archaeological site in Canton St. Gallen**
- First mentioned as "Centiprata" in **863**; sporadic archaeological discoveries began as early as **1531**, but the settlement was not formally recognised until **1941**
- The vicus served as a **stage town** at the intersection of Roman roads to **Turicum** (Zurich), **Vitudurum** (Winterthur) via Irgenhausen, and **Curia Rhaetorum** (Chur), and as a **transshipment harbour** for goods moving between the lake, the Linth, and the Walensee
- It measured roughly **300 x 200 metres** and contained massive stone buildings, half-timbered houses, forums, baths, and pottery workshops
- Major excavations from **2009 to 2013** revealed a **Mithraeum** (temple to Mithras), a **Gallo-Roman sanctuary to Mater Magna**, over **500 coins**, and lead curse tablets — evidence of cult practices from the mid-2nd century AD onward

## 8. Knies Kinderzoo — Circus and Children's Zoo

- The **Knie family** traces its circus origins to **1803**, when **Friedrich Knie** (1784–1850), the son of an Austrian doctor in Innsbruck, founded a company of rope dancers and acrobats
- After a decade travelling through Germany, Austria, and Switzerland, the family settled in Switzerland and established **Circus Knie**, which became the **Swiss National Circus**
- In **1907**, the Knie family settled in **Rapperswil**; by **1919**, they built their **winter quarters** there and transitioned from an open arena to a covered tent circus
- **10 June 1962**: Brothers **Fredy** and **Rolf Knie Sr.** (fifth generation) inaugurated **Knies Kinderzoo** in Rapperswil, designed by architect **Wolfgang Behles**
- **15 February 1963**: The first birth of an **Asian elephant** in Switzerland — **Sahib-Fridolin**, son of elephants Ceylon and Siam
- **19 June 1965**: Trained dolphins **Sindbad and Skipper** from Florida became the first trained dolphins shown in an inland European country
- **25 July 1970**: The first permanent **dolphinarium** in Switzerland opened, featuring an 800,000-litre pool
- **1976**: Record year with **399,331 visitors**
- The zoo serves as the winter home for circus animals not on tour and remains one of the most popular family attractions in Switzerland

## 9. The Capuchin Monastery (Kapuzinerkloster)

- Founded in **1606** with just four priests and three brothers, on the **Endingerhorn** peninsula on Lake Zurich's shore, west of the city
- Established as a **Roman Catholic counterpart** to the centre of the Reformation in Zurich
- Dedicated on **23 September 1607** by Bishop **Johannes V Flugi von Aspermont**
- The monastic buildings were constructed by the citizens of Rapperswil, though the land (Endingen) belongs to **Einsiedeln Abbey**
- In **1662**, the monastery was **fortified**: a small fort was built at Endingerhorn, and the monastery became part of Rapperswil's town walls as a defensive tower
- In **1734**, growing numbers of friars required a major expansion; a new south wing created the characteristic enclosed courtyard
- The friary remains **active to this day** — one of the few still-functioning Capuchin monasteries in Switzerland

## 10. Eis, Zwei, Geissebei — The Carnival Food Throw

- **Eis-zwei-Geissebei** ("one, two, goat-leg") is a Carnival tradition held on **Shrove Tuesday** (Fasnacht Tuesday) in Rapperswil
- The tradition may date back to **24 February 1350**, when Zurich's mayor **Rudolf Brun** attacked and besieged Rapperswil — according to legend, compassionate wealthy citizens **handed food through their windows** to hungry children and neighbours during the siege
- Today: after the traditional **"Herrenessen"** (council dinner) in the **Rathaus**, hundreds of children gather in the Hauptplatz. At exactly **15:15**, the town hall windows open and a fanfare sounds
- A council member calls out: **"Sind alli mini Buebe doo?"** (Are all my boys here?) — the children shout back **"Ja! Eis, zwei, Geissebei!"**
- Then for approximately 30 minutes, **sausages, bread rolls, and Biberli** (gingerbread) rain down from the banqueting hall windows onto the cheering crowd below

## 11. The Merger of 2007 — Rapperswil-Jona Is Born

- On **30 November 2003**, citizens of both Rapperswil and Jona approved a referendum to merge the two municipalities
- On **1 January 2007**, the merger took effect, creating the municipality of **Rapperswil-Jona** — the **second-largest town in Canton St. Gallen** after the cantonal capital
- At the time of merger, the combined population was **25,777** (17,799 from Jona and 7,601 from Rapperswil)
- Jona was **12 times the area** of Rapperswil, with 17,000 inhabitants — the two communities had grown together so thoroughly that their boundaries were no longer recognisable
- Today Rapperswil-Jona has approximately **28,000 inhabitants** and is known as the **"pearl on the Obersee"**
- The university campus of the former **HSR (Hochschule für Technik Rapperswil)**, now part of **OST — Ostschweizer Fachhochschule** since 2020, sits on the lakeshore with around 2,200 students

## 12. Geography — Where the Lake Divides

- Rapperswil-Jona sits at the point where **Lake Zurich** is divided into two basins by the **Seedamm**, a natural ice-age moraine
- The downstream (northwestern) part is **Zürichsee** proper; the upstream (southeastern) part is called the **Obersee** ("Upper Lake")
- The Seedamm marks the lake's **narrowest and shallowest point** — the reason people have been crossing here for over 4,000 years
- The main tributary of the Obersee is the **Linth**, rising in the glaciers of the **Glarus Alps**; to the southeast, the **Speer** (1,950 m), **Chüemettler**, and **Federispitz** overlook the lake
- The **Jona** river flows through the municipality and once formed the boundary between the former communes of Rapperswil and Jona
- The surrounding landscape is characterised by **glacial moraines**, rolling pre-Alpine hills, and vineyards on the castle slopes
- From the castle viewpoint: panoramic views over both lake basins, the Seedamm and wooden bridge, the **Frauenwinkel** nature reserve, and towards the **Bachtel** mountain and the distant **Glarus Alps**

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The deer who founded a city** — Retelling of the founding legend from the deer's perspective: chased by hounds, saved by kind Mechthild, then gratefully choosing the castle hill as home for a future town
2. **Walking across the lake** — A child crosses the wooden bridge and at the Heilig Hüsli chapel, slips through time to walk alongside medieval pilgrims heading to Einsiedeln, encountering Bronze Age lake dwellers along the way
3. **The roses that remember** — Each of the 600 rose varieties in Rapperswil holds a memory from the town's history; a child who can "listen" to roses discovers stories from the castle, the monastery, and the old town
4. **Poland's treasure in the castle** — A child finds a mysterious old painting in the castle and learns about Count Plater, the Polish exiles, and why a small Swiss town became the guardian of a nation's memory — and how 95% was lost in WWII
5. **Eis, Zwei, Geissebei!** — A child travels back to 1350 during the siege of Rapperswil, helps citizens throw food to hungry children, and discovers the origin of the beloved Carnival tradition
6. **The circus comes to town** — Set in 1962, a child befriends the Knie family as they build the Kinderzoo; helps care for baby elephant Sahib-Fridolin, the first Asian elephant born in Switzerland
7. **The Roman harbour at Kempraten** — A child discovers a Roman coin at the lakeshore and is transported to the bustling vicus of Centum Prata, where traders, boatmen, and mysterious Mithras worshippers cross paths
8. **The castle's three towers** — Each of the castle's three towers holds a secret from a different era: the Counts of Rapperswil (1229), the Habsburgs (1352), and the Polish exiles (1870) — a child must solve all three to unlock the castle's heart
