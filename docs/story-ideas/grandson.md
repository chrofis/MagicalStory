## 1. Origins — From Menhirs to Medieval Lords

- The region around Grandson has been inhabited since **prehistoric times**. A **Neolithic menhir** (standing stone, *pierre levée*) dating to **3000–2000 BC** stands on the commune’s territory — roughly **4 metres tall** with views across Lake Neuchâtel
- Nearby megalithic sites include the **alignements de Clendy** (45 standing stones erected over 6,000 years ago) and the menhirs of Gorgier, Saint-Aubin-Sauges, and Vauroux
- Grandson is home to one or more **prehistoric pile-dwelling settlements** (*habitations lacustres*) that are part of the **UNESCO World Heritage Site** “Prehistoric Pile Dwellings around the Alps” (inscribed 2011)
- When the level of Lake Neuchâtel was **artificially lowered in the 1870s** (the Jura Water Correction), a great number of ancient artefacts came to the surface. The nearby late Iron Age culture was named **La Tène** after a village on the lake — one of the defining archaeological periods of European prehistory
- The town was **first mentioned in 993** in historical records
- The **Lords of Grandson** (*Seigneurs de Grandson*) appear in the second half of the **11th century**, when the castle was first built on the lakeshore to **control the coast road** along Lake Neuchâtel

## 2. Geography — Between Lake and Jura

- Grandson sits on the **southwestern shore of Lake Neuchâtel** (*Lac de Neuchâtel*), Switzerland’s largest lake entirely within its borders
- Located at the **foot of the Jura Mountains** in the northern part of Canton Vaud, in the district of **Jura-Nord vaudois**
- The territory rises steeply from the lakeshore up to about **500 m elevation**, with the highest point **Là Outre** at **540 m**
- The commune includes the villages of **Les Tuileries-de-Grandson** and **Corcelettes**
- Population: approximately **3,400 inhabitants**
- The area of the commune is **3.63 km²**, compact but richly layered with history
- Grandson is accessible by boat in summer, with a port and **public beaches** (*plages*) along the lake
- The town lies just **3 km north of Yverdon-les-Bains**, the regional capital known for its thermal baths and Roman history

## 3. The Castle of Grandson — One of Switzerland’s Greatest Fortresses

- The **Château de Grandson** is one of the **largest and best-preserved medieval castles in Switzerland**, a site of **national importance**
- Construction began in the **late 11th century** by the Lords of Grandson. It was significantly rebuilt in the **13th century** by **Othon I de Grandson** (Otto I)
- The castle was strategically placed on the shore of Lake Neuchâtel to **control the coast road** — a key military and commercial route
- The castle features massive **round towers**, a large **inner courtyard**, and walls up to several metres thick — designed to withstand prolonged siege
- After the Burgundian Wars, the castle passed through various owners, eventually falling into private hands
- In the 20th century, the castle housed a **vintage automobile museum** (*musée automobile*), opened in **1983** by the Foundation du Château de Grandson. The collection included more than 20 classic cars ranging from a **Delahaye (1898)** to a **1927 Rolls-Royce Phantom** once owned by **Greta Garbo**, valued at over **$3 million**. The auto museum has since **permanently closed**
- The castle underwent a **major renovation** valued at over **40 million Swiss francs** — the **“Château de Grandson 2026” project** — carried out jointly by the Stiftung für Kunst, Kultur und Geschichte and the Castle of Grandson Foundation
- The new museum reopened in **March 2026**, coinciding with the **550th anniversary** of the Battle of Grandson, with exhibitions on the **Burgundian Wars**, the history of the fortress, and a **centre dedicated to the crossbow** (*arbalète*)
- The castle is classified as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance** and is listed in the Swiss inventory of cultural property

## 4. Othon I de Grandson — Crusader Knight and Friend of the English Crown

- **Othon I de Grandson** (c. **1238–1328**) is the most famous of the Lords of Grandson — a knight, diplomat, and one of the closest associates of **King Edward I of England**
- He likely travelled to England in the company of **Peter II of Savoy** by **1252** and entered the service of Prince Edward — a journey that took this lakeside knight across Europe to serve a foreign crown
- In **1268**, both prince and servant were **knighted**, and in **1271** Othon accompanied Edward on the **Ninth Crusade** to the Holy Land, serving at **Acre**
- He reportedly **saved Edward’s life** during an assassin’s attempt. Decades later, he returned to lead the **defence of Acre** in **1291** — the last Crusader stronghold — commandeering Venetian ships to evacuate the wounded as the city fell
- Back in England, he served as **Chief Justiciar of Wales** (1284–1294), based at **Caernarfon Castle**, and was active in Edward’s campaigns in Scotland and Wales
- After Edward I’s death in **1307**, Othon returned permanently to the continent. He served as England’s representative at the **Papal Curia** until **1317**
- A separate figure, **Oton de Granson** (also spelled Grandson), was a notable **medieval French-language poet** — author of **122 extant poems**, including some of the earliest **Valentine’s Day love poems**. Chaucer called him *“the flour of hem that make in France”* and translated several of his ballades. Together, Granson and Chaucer are credited with **inventing the link between St. Valentine’s Day and romantic love**

## 5. The Battle of Grandson (1476) — Turning Point of the Burgundian Wars

- The **Battle of Grandson** (*Bataille de Grandson*) was fought on **2 March 1476** and was the first major engagement of the **Burgundian Wars** (*Guerres de Bourgogne*)
- **Charles the Bold** (*Charles le Téméraire*), Duke of Burgundy, advanced through the Jura with approximately **12,000 men** after failed negotiations over the territory of Vaud
- He seized **Yverdon** and laid siege to **Grandson Castle**, which surrendered on **28 February 1476**
- After the surrender, Charles broke his promise of mercy and **executed the entire garrison of 412 men** — hanging them from trees or drowning them in Lake Neuchâtel in an execution that lasted **four hours**. Swiss sources are unanimous that the defenders surrendered only after Charles assured them they would be spared
- Two days later, the **Swiss Confederate army** arrived and attacked near the village of **Concise**. The Burgundian cavalry could not hold the Swiss vanguard, and when Charles ordered a tactical withdrawal, the arrival of a **second, larger Swiss force** caused confusion and panic
- The Burgundian army **broke and fled**, abandoning their entire camp. The Swiss achieved a decisive victory
- The defeat shattered Charles the Bold’s **aura of invincibility** and gave the Swiss Confederation aggressive momentum. Charles went on to lose at the **Battle of Morat** (June 1476) and was killed at the **Battle of Nancy** (January 1477)
- The three battles are remembered together in Swiss history: *"Grandson le butin, Morat la tuerie, Nancy le tombeau"* — at Grandson the booty, at Morat the slaughter, at Nancy the grave

## 6. The Burgundian Treasure — A Fortune Abandoned

- When Charles the Bold’s army fled Grandson, the Swiss captured one of the most **extraordinary treasure hoards of the late Middle Ages** (*le butin bourguignon*)
- The booty included:
  - **The Three Brothers** (*Les Trois Frères*) — a legendary jewel featuring three enormous spinels and three pearls, inherited from John the Fearless
  - The **Sancy diamond** (or its ancestor) — a famous 55-carat pale-yellow diamond
  - Charles’s **golden ducal hat**, encrusted with rubies, diamonds, and ancient Roman intaglios
  - His **personal seal**, made from a kilogram of pure gold
  - A **silver bathtub**
  - Magnificent **tapestries**, including the celebrated *Tapisseries d’Alexandre* (Carpets of Alexander the Great)
  - Illuminated manuscripts, embroidered vestments, and a substantial **artillery train** of cannons
- The sheer value of the booty initially **overwhelmed the Swiss**, who reportedly underestimated its worth — soldiers are said to have **sold precious gems for small sums**, not recognising what they had
- The battle is remembered in Swiss popular history as the one where the Confederates won *“le butin de Grandson”* — the Burgundian booty that enriched the soldiers and the cantons
- Charles's solid golden ducal hat featured more rubies, diamonds, and ancient Roman intaglios than any crown in Europe — a symbol of Burgundian excess
- Several of these treasures are today held in museums across Switzerland, including the **Bernisches Historisches Museum**
- The famous Burgundian tapestries are among the finest surviving examples of 15th-century Flemish weaving

## 7. The Church of Saint-Jean-Baptiste — Romanesque Jewel

- The **Église Saint-Jean-Baptiste** (Church of St. John the Baptist) is a **monument of national significance** standing in the heart of Grandson’s old town
- A jewel of **12th-century Romanesque art** (*art roman*), the church is renowned for its remarkable **sculpted Romanesque capitals** — among the finest in the region
- Since the **12th century**, it was under the authority of the **Benedictine congregation of La Chaise-Dieu** (Auvergne, France)
- At the beginning of the **14th century**, Othon I de Grandson commissioned the **reconstruction of the apse in Gothic style**
- In **1554**, the priory was abolished and the church was given over to **Reformed (Protestant) worship** following the Bernese Reformation
- It became the property of the **Canton of Vaud** in **1803** and was listed as a **historic monument in 1900**
- A thorough **restoration** was carried out between **1999 and 2006** under the responsibility of the State of Vaud
- The church remains a functioning place of worship and is open to visitors — its quiet interior offers a striking contrast to the military history of the castle just metres away

## 8. Medieval Town and Cultural Heritage

- Grandson’s **old town** (*vieille ville*) preserves a remarkably intact medieval character with narrow streets, stone houses, and historic facades
- The **Hôtel de Ville** (town hall) is adorned with the commune’s coat of arms
- In **2017**, Grandson received the **Award of Merit** from the association *Les Plus Beaux Villages de Suisse* (Most Beautiful Villages of Switzerland) for its enhancement of historical heritage and renovation of the old town
- Walking through Grandson means immersing oneself in an atmosphere that weaves together stories of knights, battles, and centuries-old traditions
- The town hosts annual festivals including a **medieval festival** (*fête médiévale*) and the **Brandons** — a traditional carnival (*carnaval*) with parades and fire
- The **open cellars days** (*journées des caves ouvertes*), held in late May or early June, celebrate the local wine tradition with tastings across the village
- Grandson’s **port** (*port*) offers boat connections on Lake Neuchâtel in summer, and the commune’s lakeside beaches are popular with locals
- The nearby village of **Corcelettes** has a charming lakeside promenade and is part of the commune’s territory, adding to the recreational appeal of the area

## 9. The Garrison Massacre — A Story of Betrayal

- The execution of the Grandson garrison in **February 1476** is one of the most dramatic and tragic episodes of the Burgundian Wars
- The castle’s garrison, under the command of **Brandolf vom Stein**, held out against Charles the Bold’s siege but eventually surrendered after receiving **assurances of mercy**
- Charles broke his word. On **28 February 1476**, all **412 defenders** were led past the duke’s tent and systematically executed — **hanged from trees** along the lakeshore or **drowned in Lake Neuchâtel** — in a spectacle that lasted four hours
- The papal nuncio **Panigarola**, who witnessed the event, described the scene as *“shocking and horrible”*
- Far from intimidating the Swiss as Charles intended, the massacre **enraged the Confederation** and stiffened their resolve. Just two days later, the Swiss army arrived and routed the Burgundians
- Brandolf vom Stein survived — he later commanded Bernese forces at the **Battle of Nancy in 1477**, where Charles the Bold was killed
- The garrison massacre became a rallying cry for the Swiss throughout the Burgundian Wars — a symbol of treachery avenged by courage on the battlefield

## 10. Unique Features and Modern Life

- Grandson lies at the heart of a region rich in **Neolithic heritage** — the menhir, nearby pile dwellings, and the Clendy stone alignments make it a crossroads of **6,000 years of human history**
- The town’s position on Lake Neuchâtel offers **water sports**, sailing, and swimming, with views across the lake to the distant Alps
- The **550th anniversary celebrations in 2026** have brought renewed international attention, with the reopened castle museum, historical re-enactments, and a programme of festivities throughout the year
- The crossbow centre (*centre de l’arbalète*) in the renovated castle connects Grandson to the wider Swiss tradition of marksmanship and the legend of William Tell
- The commune was for **210 years** the chief-town (*chef-lieu*) of its district before the administrative reorganisation that created the district of Jura-Nord vaudois
- Despite its small size, Grandson punches far above its weight historically — it is a place where **prehistoric standing stones, Crusader knights, Burgundian treasure, and Reformation churches** all coexist within walking distance
- The town's French-speaking character, lakeside setting, and medieval silhouette make it one of the most atmospheric small towns in western Switzerland

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The knight who crossed the sea** — Young Othon de Grandson leaves his lakeside castle to follow Prince Edward on the Crusade; homesick, he carries a pebble from Lake Neuchâtel as a talisman through the Holy Land and back
2. **The treasure in the tent** — After the Battle of Grandson, a Swiss farm boy wanders into Charles the Bold’s abandoned camp and discovers a glittering golden hat, tapestries, and a diamond — but has no idea what any of it is worth
3. **The garrison’s promise** — Told from the perspective of a young drummer boy inside the besieged castle, who watches the garrison surrender on a promise of mercy, then must escape across the lake when the promise is broken
4. **The menhir’s memory** — A child touches the ancient standing stone of Grandson and is transported back 5,000 years to watch Neolithic people erect it on the lakeshore, then forward through time: Romans, medieval knights, the great battle, and today
5. **The dancing bears of Grandson** — During the 2026 medieval festival, a girl in costume discovers a secret passage in the castle that leads to a hidden chamber where Charles the Bold’s lost silver bathtub still sits, guarded by a very old cat
6. **Valentine’s Day at the castle** — A child learns that the poet Oton de Granson helped invent Valentine’s Day, and sets out to write the perfect love poem using clues hidden in the castle’s Romanesque capitals and carved stones
7. **The last ship from Acre** — Othon de Grandson commands the final evacuation of Acre in 1291; a child stowaway on the Venetian rescue ship watches the old knight save the wounded as the city burns behind them
8. **The crossbow contest** — At the new crossbow centre in the castle, a nervous child enters a marksmanship competition and discovers that patience and focus matter more than strength — with the ghost of William Tell offering quiet encouragement from the ramparts
