## 1. Geography — The Swiss Riviera (Riviera vaudoise)

- Montreux sits on the **eastern shore of Lake Geneva** (Lac Léman), extending about **6 km** along the waterfront at the foot of the Alps
- The municipality rises from the lakeshore up to the summit of **Rochers-de-Naye** at **2,042 metres** above sea level
- Montreux comprises three merged resort communities: **Le Châtelard-Montreux**, **Les Planches-Montreux**, and **Veytaux-Montreux** (consolidated in **1962**)
- The region is known as the **Riviera vaudoise** (Swiss Riviera) thanks to a remarkable **Mediterranean microclimate**: the mountains behind the town shield it from northerly and easterly winds, creating conditions mild enough for **palm trees, cypresses, and umbrella pines** — highly unusual for Switzerland
- The name "Montreux" derives from a small monastery (**monasteriolum** in Latin) that stood on the site by the **11th century**, evolving through forms like Mustruel, Muistruum, and Moutru
- Nearby **Lavaux vineyard terraces**, stretching 30 km along the lake from Chillon to Lausanne, have been a **UNESCO World Heritage Site since 2007** — 800 hectares of terraced vineyards dating back to the **11th century**, when **Benedictine and Cistercian monks** first cultivated them
- The Lavaux vines benefit from a unique triple sun exposure: direct sunlight, reflection off **Lake Geneva**, and stored heat radiating from the **ancient stone terrace walls**

## 2. Château de Chillon — The Most Visited Castle in Switzerland

- The **Château de Chillon** (Castle of Chillon) stands on a rocky island on Lake Geneva, between Montreux and Villeneuve — **Switzerland's most visited historic monument**, attracting over **400,000 visitors** annually (record: **432,000 in 2019**)
- Archaeological evidence shows occupation since the **Bronze Age**; the first written mention dates to **1150**
- The name "Chillon" derives from a word meaning **"rocky platform"** (plate-forme rocheuse)
- From the **12th to 16th centuries**, the castle belonged to the **Counts of Savoy** (Comtes de Savoie), who used it as a summer residence, fortress, and prison — and profited from tolls on merchants traveling the lake route
- **Peter II of Savoy** expanded the castle significantly in the **13th century**, giving it its present form
- In **1536**, Bernese forces conquered Vaud and seized the castle after a three-week siege, ending Savoyard control
- The castle served as a **Bernese administrative seat** for 260 years until the **Vaudois Revolution of 1798**
- Restoration began in the late **19th century** under architect **Albert Naef**; the Association for the Restoration of Chillon Castle was founded in **1887**

## 3. The Prisoner of Chillon — Byron and Bonivard

- **François Bonivard** (1493–1570), a Genevan nobleman and **prior of the Priory of Saint-Victor** in Geneva, was imprisoned at Chillon for **six years (1530–1536)** by **Duke Charles III of Savoy**
- Bonivard's crime: he supported **Geneva's independence** from Savoy and affiliated with the **Eidgenots** (Swiss nationalists)
- The first two years, his noble status earned him rooms within the castle; the duke then ruled this too lenient, and Bonivard spent the remaining **four years in the underground dungeon**, chained to a pillar
- Bonivard claimed he **"wore away the floor, creating a path in the stone"** by walking in circles — though archaeologists found no physical evidence supporting this
- On **June 22, 1816**, Lord **Byron** and **Percy Bysshe Shelley** sailed across Lake Geneva and visited the castle. Byron carved his name into a pillar in the dungeon (still visible today)
- Byron composed **"The Prisoner of Chillon"**, a 392-line narrative poem, in just a few days while staying at a hotel in **Ouchy** (Lausanne)
- The poem became a huge international success, translated into **20 languages**, and brought worldwide fame to the castle — transforming it into one of Europe's most celebrated Romantic-era destinations

## 4. Belle Époque and the Grand Hotels

- Montreux's rise as a fashionable resort began in the **19th century**, fuelled by its mild climate, lake views, and Byron's literary fame
- The **Fairmont Le Montreux Palace** (originally Hôtel du Cygne, then Le Montreux Palace) was built in a record **18 months** by architect **Eugène Jost**, opening on **March 19, 1906** — it became known as the **"Pearl of the Swiss Riviera"** (Perle de la Riviera)
- Commissioned by hoteliers **Alexandre Emery** and **Ami Chessex**, who had operated the adjacent Hôtel du Cygne since **1881** and fused the two buildings together
- The **Grand Hotel Suisse Majestic**, the **Hôtel Masson** (the oldest hotel in the Montreux-Vevey region), and the **Caux Palace Hotel** (built 1902, above Montreux) are among the other grand Belle Époque properties
- Notable guests through the decades: **Victor Hugo**, **Gustave Eiffel**, **Mikhail Gorbachev**, **Miles Davis**, and **Aretha Franklin**
- Composer **Richard Strauss** composed his **Vier letzte Lieder** (Four Last Songs) at the Montreux Palace

## 5. Vladimir Nabokov — The Writer in the Palace

- **Vladimir Nabokov** (1899–1977) and his wife **Véra** moved into the **Montreux Palace Hotel** in **October 1961**, taking a sixth-floor suite in the **Cygne wing** (Aile du Cygne)
- Nabokov chose Montreux for peace and privacy after the worldwide sensation of **Lolita** (1955)
- He lived there for **16 years** — the rest of his life — writing on **index cards** at a standing lectern
- Works written at the Montreux Palace include: **Pale Fire** (1962), **Ada, or Ardor** (1969), **Transparent Things** (1972), and **Look at the Harlequins!** (1974)
- When not writing, Nabokov pursued his lifelong passion for **lepidopterology** (butterfly collecting) in the surrounding mountains
- Nabokov died on **July 2, 1977**, in Montreux. His remains were cremated and buried at the **Clarens cemetery** (Cimetière de Clarens) in Montreux

## 6. Igor Stravinsky — The Rite of Spring, Composed on the Riviera

- **Igor Stravinsky** (1882–1971) lived in **Clarens**, a lakeside village now part of the Montreux municipality, from **1910 to 1920**
- In a tiny rented room — barely 2.5 by 2.5 metres, with just an upright piano, a table, and two chairs — Stravinsky composed **Le Sacre du printemps** (The Rite of Spring) during the winter of **1911–1912**
- He signed the completed orchestral score **"Clarens, March 8, 1913"**
- The premiere at the **Théâtre des Champs-Élysées** in Paris on **May 29, 1913**, conducted by **Pierre Monteux** for Diaghilev's **Ballets Russes**, caused one of the most famous scandals in music history — the audience rioted
- Stravinsky also composed much of **Petrushka** (1911) at Clarens, the second of the three revolutionary ballets commissioned by impresario **Sergei Diaghilev**
- In **1985**, Clarens honoured the composer by naming a street **Rue du Sacre du Printemps**

## 7. The Montreux Jazz Festival — Claude Nobs' Vision

- Founded in **1967** by **Claude Nobs** (1936–2013), together with **René Langel** and **Géo Voumard**, with considerable support from **Ahmet and Nesuhi Ertegun** of Atlantic Records
- The first edition: **three days**, a budget of just **10,000 Swiss francs**, with headline acts including **Charles Lloyd**, **Keith Jarrett**, and **Jack DeJohnette**
- Today: a **16-day** summer festival with approximately **250,000 attendees**, over **700 concerts** across **15 stages** (13 of which are free to access)
- The festival is the **second-largest annual jazz festival in the world** (after Montreal)
- Past performers include **Nina Simone**, **Miles Davis**, **Ella Fitzgerald**, **Prince**, **David Bowie**, **Elton John**, **Kendrick Lamar**, and **Lady Gaga** — long since expanding beyond jazz into rock, pop, blues, and electronic music
- In **2013**, the festival's audiovisual archives — more than **11,000 hours** of live recordings spanning from 1967 — were inscribed on **UNESCO's Memory of the World Register**
- After Nobs' death in **January 2013**, **Mathieu Jaton** has directed the festival

## 8. Deep Purple and "Smoke on the Water" — The Casino Fire of 1971

- On **December 4, 1971**, during a concert by **Frank Zappa and the Mothers of Invention** at the **Montreux Casino**, someone in the audience fired a **flare gun** that hit the wooden ceiling
- The fire spread rapidly, destroying the entire Casino; remarkably, **no one was killed** — most injuries were minor cuts and burns
- **Claude Nobs** heroically ran in and out of the burning building to help concertgoers escape
- **Deep Purple** were in Montreux to record their album **Machine Head** at the Casino using the **Rolling Stones' mobile recording studio** — they watched the fire from across the lake
- Bassist **Roger Glover** saw the layer of smoke drifting over Lake Geneva and coined the phrase **"smoke on the water"**
- The resulting song, released in **1972** on the album **Machine Head**, immortalised the event — its lyrics reference "Funky Claude" (Claude Nobs) "running in and out"
- The Casino was rebuilt and reopened in **1975**

## 9. Freddie Mercury and Queen — Mountain Studios

- **Queen** first came to Montreux in **July 1978** to record the album **Jazz** at **Mountain Studios**, located inside the Montreux Casino
- In **1979**, the band purchased Mountain Studios, making it their private recording base
- Six studio albums were produced there: **Jazz** (1978), **Hot Space** (1982), **A Kind of Magic** (1986), **The Miracle** (1989), **Innuendo** (1991), and posthumously **Made in Heaven** (1995)
- **Freddie Mercury** increasingly made Montreux his home from **1987 onward**, eventually purchasing a lakefront apartment in **1991**
- His famous quote: **"If you want peace of soul, come to Montreux"** (said to soprano Montserrat Caballé)
- In his final months, knowing he was losing his battle with AIDS, Mercury devoted himself to recording as much material as possible at Mountain Studios so his bandmates could complete arrangements after his death
- Mercury died on **November 24, 1991**
- A **3-metre bronze statue** by Czech sculptor **Irena Sedlecká** (1928–2020) was unveiled on the **Place du Marché** (Market Square) on **November 25, 1996**, in the presence of **Montserrat Caballé**, **Brian May**, **Roger Taylor**, and Mercury's parents **Jer and Bomi Bulsara**
- The epitaph, written by Brian May, reads: **"Lover of life — singer of songs"**
- Today, the free museum **"Queen: The Studio Experience"** inside the Montreux Casino offers an interactive journey through Queen's recording history at Mountain Studios

## 10. Charlie Chaplin — The Last Act on the Riviera

- **Charlie Chaplin** (1889–1977) lived in **Corsier-sur-Vevey**, a village 9 km from Montreux, for the last **25 years** of his life
- In **September 1952**, while sailing to Europe to promote his film **Limelight**, Chaplin learned that the United States had revoked his re-entry visa (during the McCarthy era). He never returned to America
- On **January 6, 1953**, the Chaplin family moved into the **Manoir de Ban**, a neoclassical manor house on a **14-hectare estate** overlooking Lake Geneva and the Alps
- At the Manoir, Chaplin wrote the screenplays for **A King in New York** (1957) and **A Countess from Hong Kong** (1967), reworked the music for his films, and wrote **My Autobiography** (1964)
- Chaplin died on **Christmas Day, December 25, 1977**, aged 88, and is buried in the **Corsier-sur-Vevey cemetery**
- In **April 2016**, after 15 years of development, **Chaplin's World** museum opened at the Manoir de Ban — the only museum in the world dedicated entirely to Charlie Chaplin, featuring over **30 wax figures** by Grévin and recreated film sets

## 11. Rochers-de-Naye — The Mountain Above the Lake

- **Rochers-de-Naye** (2,042 m) towers above Montreux, offering panoramic views of Lake Geneva, the Savoy Alps, and the Jura mountains
- A **cogwheel railway** (chemin de fer à crémaillère) connects Montreux to the summit — the first section (Glion to Rochers-de-Naye) opened in **1892**, built by the Chemin de fer Glion–Rochers-de-Naye company using the **Abt rack system** and steam locomotives
- The lower section (Montreux to Glion) opened in **1909**, electrically operated from the start; the upper section was electrified in **1938**
- At the summit: the **Alpine Garden La Rambertia** (Jardin alpin La Rambertia), with over **1,000 varieties** of alpine flowers and plants
- The **Paradis des Marmottes** (Marmot Paradise) houses **14 species** of marmots from around the world — a favourite with children
- During the Christmas season, the summit hosts the **Maison du Père Noël** (House of Father Christmas), accessible by a special "Santa Train" from Montreux

## 12. The Narcissus Festival and the Montreux Christmas Market

### Fête des Narcisses (Narcissus Festival)
- From **1897 to 1957**, the Fête des Narcisses was **Montreux's flagship event** — the highlight of the Belle Époque social calendar
- Each **May**, when wild narcissi blanketed the heights above Montreux in what locals called **"la neige de mai"** (May snow), the town celebrated with international orchestras, ballets, operas, and lavishly decorated **floral parade floats** (chars fleuris)
- In **1954**, the festival became the **first event broadcast on Eurovision** television
- After a long hiatus, the festival was revived in **May 2015** with the help of the **Jeune Chambre Internationale Riviera**, featuring over **40 floats and brass bands**
- The narcissus meadows above **Les Avants** (a village in the Montreux municipality) remain a beloved destination for spring excursions

### Montreux Noël (Christmas Market)
- The **Marché de Noël de Montreux** has been held since **1994** and is classified as one of **the most beautiful Christmas markets in Europe**
- For one month, the lakefront promenade hosts more than **170 illuminated chalets** (chalets illuminés) with merchants, artisans, and food vendors
- The market attracts approximately **550,000 visitors** annually
- A signature spectacle: **Le Père Noël volant** (Flying Santa) soars over the lakefront promenade in his sleigh at 17:00, 18:00, and 19:00 each evening

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The prisoner who walked in circles** — Bonivard's dungeon comes alive: a child touches the worn stone floor at Chillon and is pulled back to 1534, where they must help the chained prisoner keep hope alive until the Bernese arrive
2. **Smoke on the water** — On the night of December 4, 1971, a child at the Montreux Casino concert must help "Funky Claude" rescue concertgoers from the fire while smoke drifts over the lake like a ghost
3. **The piano in the tiny room** — A child discovers Stravinsky's cramped room in Clarens and accidentally plays a chord on the old upright; suddenly the Rite of Spring comes to life — wild dancers, a sacrificial maiden, and pagan rhythms that shake the walls
4. **Freddie's last song** — A child sneaks into Mountain Studios at night and meets the ghost of Freddie Mercury, who needs help recording one final song before dawn; together they explore Montreux's moonlit lakefront
5. **The narcissus queen** — During the 1954 Fête des Narcisses, a child rides atop a floral parade float and discovers the white flowers are alive, each one whispering a secret about the mountains above Les Avants
6. **Nabokov's butterflies** — A rare butterfly escapes from the writer's collection at the Montreux Palace; a child chases it through the hotel's Belle Époque corridors, the Lavaux vineyards, and up to Rochers-de-Naye
7. **The Tramp's last Christmas** — On Christmas Eve 1977 at the Manoir de Ban, a child meets an old gentleman in a bowler hat who performs one final silent comedy routine amid the snow — a farewell from Charlie Chaplin
8. **The castle on the rock** — Byron's ghost returns to Chillon to finish an unwritten poem; a child must guide him through all 25 rooms of the castle before sunrise, each room revealing a different century of Swiss history
