## 1. Geography — A Terrace Above the Valley

- Soglio sits on a **sunny natural terrace at 1,090 meters** above sea level on the north side of the Val Bregaglia (German: **Bergell**), in the canton of Graubuenden
- The village faces south across the valley toward the dramatic granite peaks of the **Sciora group**, the **Pizzo Badile** (3,308 m), and the **Pizzo Cengalo** (3,369 m) — one of the most famous mountain panoramas in the Alps
- Below the village, the land drops steeply into the **Val Bondasca**, a wild glacier-carved side valley that cuts into the Bregaglia Range
- The Val Bregaglia runs from the **Maloja Pass** (1,815 m) down to the Italian border at **Castasegna** (697 m) — a dramatic altitude drop of over 1,100 meters in just 25 kilometers, creating a transition from Alpine to almost Mediterranean climate
- Soglio is one of the highest settlements in the valley, connected by a narrow road that climbs from **Promontogno** on the valley floor
- Population: approximately **166 residents** — a compact, intimate community that has barely changed in centuries

## 2. Early History — First Mention and Medieval Roots

- Soglio is **first mentioned in 1186** as "de Solio" in medieval documents — the name derives from the Latin **solium**, meaning "threshold" or "seat"
- The settlement's position on a broad, south-facing ledge made it a natural site for habitation: protected from valley floods, warmed by the sun, and commanding views of approaching travelers
- In **1219**, Soglio appears again in Graubuenden State Archives, confirming the settlement's continuous habitation
- The noble **de Salis** family first appears in Soglio between **1285 and 1293**, when a certain **ser Rodolfus de Salice de Solio** is documented — the family likely descended from the patrician **Salici family of Como**, traceable to 1202
- During the Middle Ages, the village prospered from trade along the routes connecting the **Engadin** to **Chiavenna** and northern Italy, with the elite families — the **Salis**, **Castelmur**, **Torriani**, and **Prevosti** — enriching themselves through tolls on goods crossing the Alpine passes

## 3. The Salis Family — Lords of the Three Leagues

- The **von Salis** family became one of the most powerful noble dynasties in the **Free State of the Three Leagues** (Drei Buende), the predecessor of the Canton of Graubuenden
- By the **16th century**, the Salis had outmaneuvered their rivals, the **Planta** family, to become the predominant political force in the region
- **Herkules von Salis-Gruesch** (1566–1620) was a propagator of Reformed Protestantism and a military leader during the turbulent **Buendner Wirren** (Revolt of the Leagues, 1618–1639)
- His son **Ulysses von Salis** (1594–1674) allied with France and Venice, serving as a mercenary commander who guarded the strategically vital Alpine passes connecting northern and southern Europe
- Ulysses von Salis and the legendary **Juerg Jenatsch** used French money to hire an **8,000-man mercenary army** to drive the Austrians out of Graubuenden
- The family contributed **25 governors, 44 mayors, and 16 vicars** to the region over the centuries
- Their power was finally broken when **Napoleon** separated the **Valtellina and Chiavenna** from the Grisons Free State in **1797**, stripping the Salis of their Italian possessions and income from foreign military service
- The family's political dominance ended with the **Helvetic Republic** in **1798**, which dissolved the Three Leagues' sovereignty

## 4. The Palazzo Salis — A Baroque Palace in an Alpine Village

- The **Palazzo Salis** was built in **1630** by Knight **Baptista von Salis** as a family residence — the original structure was known as the **Casa Battista**
- In **1701**, his descendants extended the building to its present stately appearance, adding the grand baroque facade that dominates the village
- The palace features **historic furniture, elegant parquet floors, murals, stucco work, and ancestral portraits** — a startling display of aristocratic wealth in a remote mountain village
- The **baroque garden**, originally designed with geometric patterns in the 18th century, later incorporated both domestic and exotic trees including **sequoias** — from this garden, Giovanni Segantini reportedly gazed at the mountains and called Soglio the "threshold to paradise"
- After the Salis family's decline, the palazzo was converted into a **hotel in 1876** — it has operated as a guesthouse ever since, making it one of the oldest continuously operating hotels in Graubuenden
- Famous guests include the painter **Giovanni Segantini**, the poet **Rainer Maria Rilke**, the sculptor **Alberto Giacometti**, and the writer **Hermann Burger**
- In **1998**, ICOMOS designated Palazzo Salis as **Historic Hotel of the Year**; it became a founding member of **Swiss Historic Hotels** in 2004
- Today the palazzo has **14 rooms** and large halls, serving as the architectural focus of Soglio's preserved townscape
- The oldest von Salis palace in the village dates to the **14th century** (now housing the B&B Pension Piazza), while other family residences include **Casa Mezzo** (1696) — in total, **seven von Salis palaces** cluster around the cobblestone streets

## 5. The Reformation — An Italian-Speaking Protestant Enclave

- Val Bregaglia's transformation began with the **Articles of Ilanz** (1524, reaffirmed 1526), which granted each community in Graubuenden the right to **choose its own religious confession**
- In **1532**, **Bartolomeo Maturo**, a former Dominican from Cremona, arrived as the first Italian reformer to preach in the valley
- The decisive figure was **Pietro Paolo Vergerio** (c. 1498–1565), a former papal nuncio and bishop who fled the Venetian Inquisition and arrived in Val Bregaglia in **1549**
- From 1550 to 1553, Vergerio served as **pastor of Vicosoprano**, converting the towns of **Soglio, Stampa, and Bondo** to Protestantism within two years
- In **1552**, with Soglio's vote, **all communes of the Bregaglia** united in adopting the Reformation — a remarkable act of communal self-determination
- **Castasegna** was fully converted by **1555**
- The valley attracted thousands of **Italian Protestant refugees** fleeing the Inquisition — educated theologians, merchants, and former clergy who strengthened the communities
- Among the refugees was **Michelangelo Florio** (1515–1566), a former Franciscan friar turned Protestant, who arrived in Soglio on **27 May 1554** to serve as pastor of the Chiesa San Lorenzo after the death of Michele Lattanzio
- In Soglio, Michelangelo educated his son **John Florio** (born c. 1553 in London), who later became one of the great humanists of the English Renaissance — compiler of the first comprehensive **Italian–English dictionary** (*A Worlde of Wordes*, 1598) and translator of **Montaigne's Essais** into English
- John Florio's translations are documented as a direct source for **Shakespeare's plays** — the fringe "Florian theory" goes further, proposing that Florio himself authored the plays (the name "Crollalanza," linked to Florio's mother, can be parsed as "shake-spear" in Italian); mainstream scholars regard this as unproven, but the textual influence is widely accepted
- Today, Bregaglia remains the **only Italian-speaking municipality in Switzerland where the majority of the population is Protestant** — a unique cultural distinction
- The village's unique linguistic character adds to its identity: the local **Bregagliotto** dialect is a transitional idiom between **Lombard** and **Rhaeto-Romance**, with vocabulary borrowed from Romansh (Puter, Vallader) and grammar closer to Lombard — in Soglio specifically, the dialect features distinctive **double consonants** and a vibrant rolled "r" unlike other villages in the valley

## 6. The Church of San Lorenzo

- The **Chiesa evangelica riformata San Lorenzo** was first mentioned in **1354**, though the lower part of the adjacent tower is certainly from **Romanesque times**, indicating a much earlier foundation
- The church was **restored and enlarged in 1506**, reflecting the village's medieval prosperity
- After the Reformation in 1552, San Lorenzo became the village's **Reformed Protestant church**
- The church received its present appearance during a major renovation in **1735**, which added a new vaulted ceiling and extended the tower to its current height
- A further reconstruction of the **baroque facade** was completed in **1760** after fire damaged the earlier structure
- Today San Lorenzo anchors the village center and hosts **summer concerts** — its bell tower rising above the stone rooftops is one of the defining silhouettes of Soglio

## 7. The Chestnut Forests — The Fruit of the Valley

- Chestnut trees were brought to Val Bregaglia by the **Romans**, who introduced the species along the remnants of a Roman road still visible in the lower valley
- Intensive cultivation began during the **medieval period** (roughly from 400 AD onward), as chestnuts became a dietary staple — rich in protein, carbohydrates, iron, potassium, and vitamins
- Between Soglio and Castasegna lies one of the **largest chestnut groves in Europe**, a cultivated landscape that has been maintained for centuries
- Four traditional varieties survive in Bregaglia: **Ensat, Marun, Lueina**, and **Vescuv**
- The harvest is traditionally dried in **cascine** — small stone smoking huts where the chestnuts are preserved over slow-burning fires, a practice unchanged since the Middle Ages
- The chestnut forests below Soglio create a remarkable microclimate zone: sheltered, south-facing slopes where fruit trees also thrive at surprising altitude
- Every autumn, the valley celebrates the **Festa della Castagna** (Chestnut Festival) — a gathering that honors the tree that sustained generations through long Alpine winters
- The groves also serve an ecological function, stabilizing the steep hillsides and creating a distinctive cultural landscape recognized by heritage organizations

## 8. Giovanni Segantini — "La soglia del paradiso"

- **Giovanni Segantini** (15 January 1858 – 28 September 1899) was one of the most famous painters in Europe in the late 19th century, known for his luminous Alpine landscapes painted in a **Divisionist** technique
- In **1894**, Segantini and his family settled in **Chalet Kuoni in Maloja** at the top of the Val Bregaglia — the family spent the winters in **Soglio**, at the Palazzo Salis
- It was reportedly while sitting in the **garden of the Palazzo Salis** that Segantini declared Soglio to be **"la soglia del paradiso"** — "the threshold to paradise" — a phrase that has defined the village's identity ever since
- The wordplay is deliberate: **Soglio** and **soglia** (threshold) share the same Latin root, *solium*
- Segantini's monumental **Alpine Triptych** (*Werden – Sein – Vergehen* / *La Vita – La Natura – La Morte*) draws directly on the Bregaglia landscape: the left panel, **"La Vita"** (1898–1899), depicts the last rays of daylight on the high plains near **Soglio**
- He commissioned the Soglio firm **Torriani** to build a round wooden pavilion attached to his Maloja studio — originally a 1:10 scale model for a colossal Engadine panorama intended for the **1900 Paris World Exhibition**, later used as his atelier and library
- On **28 September 1899**, Segantini died of **acute peritonitis** at the Segantini Hut (2,731 m) on the **Schafberg** above Pontresina, where he had gone to work on the central panel of his triptych
- He was buried on 1 October 1899 in **Maloja's cemetery**, with the inscription: *"Arte e amore vincono il tempo"* — "Art and love conquer time"
- The **Segantini Museum** in St. Moritz, opened in 1908, houses the Alpine Triptych and was designed to echo Segantini's own pavilion sketches

## 9. The Giacometti Dynasty — A Valley of Artists

- The Val Bregaglia produced an extraordinary artistic dynasty: the **Giacometti family** of Stampa, just a few kilometers down the valley from Soglio
- **Giovanni Giacometti** (1868–1933), the father, was born in Stampa and became one of Switzerland's most important Post-Impressionist painters, deeply influenced by his friend **Giovanni Segantini** and by the work of Cezanne and Van Gogh
- **Alberto Giacometti** (10 October 1901 – 11 January 1966) was born in **Borgonovo** near Stampa — his birthplace is known as **Ca d'Dolf**; the family moved to Stampa in 1904
- Alberto became one of the 20th century's greatest sculptors, known for his elongated bronze figures that became icons of **existentialism** and postwar art
- His brother **Diego Giacometti** (1902–1985), also born in Borgonovo, became a renowned furniture designer and sculptor in his own right
- Their cousin **Augusto Giacometti** (1877–1947) was a pioneer of **abstract painting** and Art Nouveau stained glass — one of the first abstract painters in Switzerland
- Alberto maintained a lifelong connection to the valley, returning regularly from Paris to **Stampa and Maloja** throughout his life; he died in Chur in 1966 and was buried in the **San Giorgio cemetery** near his birthplace
- Today the **Ciasa Granda** museum in Stampa and the **Sentiero Giacometti** (Giacometti Trail) through the valley celebrate the family's legacy

## 10. Stone and Silence — The Architecture of Soglio

- Unlike the Engadin's famous **sgraffito-decorated** houses, Soglio's architecture is defined by **compact stone construction** — tight clusters of houses built from local granite and gneiss, with thick walls designed to retain heat through harsh winters
- The village is a labyrinth of **narrow cobbled alleyways** (vicoli), stone-paved with original **porphyry**, featuring carved lintels, wooden balconies, and houses with stables attached below
- Stone-slab roofs (**piode**) are a distinctive feature — heavy flat stones laid overlapping to shed snow and rain, a roofing technique shared with the Italian side of the Alps
- The village layout preserves a medieval pattern: houses cluster tightly together for mutual warmth and protection, with small piazzas featuring **fountains and communal washhouses** (lavatoio)
- In **2015**, Soglio was voted **"Switzerland's prettiest village"** in a survey with over 35,000 voters
- That same year, the municipality of **Bregaglia** (formed in 2010 by merging Soglio, Bondo, Castasegna, Stampa, and Vicosoprano) was awarded the prestigious **Wakker Prize** by the Swiss Heritage Society (Schweizer Heimatschutz), recognizing a valley community that "draws its strength from its architectural heritage to promote independent development"
- A symbol of Soglio's quiet vitality: **Soglio Produkte AG**, founded in **1979** by Walter Hunkeler, produces natural body-care products from local goat butter, sheep's whey, herbs, and alpine flowers — the company won the **Prix Montagne** in 2019 and operates from a workshop in the village, proving that even a community of 166 can sustain a living enterprise

## 11. The Bondasca and the Peaks — Mountain Drama

- The **Val Bondasca** opens directly across from Soglio, offering the village its legendary panorama: the jagged granite spires of the **Sciora group** rising above ancient forests and glaciers
- **Pizzo Badile** (3,308 m), whose name means "shovel peak" in Italian, is one of the most famous rock walls in the Alps — its smooth granite northeast face drops nearly 800 meters and has attracted mountaineers since the **first ascent on 27 July 1867** by **W. A. B. Coolidge** with guides Francois and Henri Devouassoud
- The legendary **Via Cassin** on the northeast face was first climbed in **1937** by **Riccardo Cassin** and his team — it remains one of the great classic Alpine routes
- **Pizzo Cengalo** (3,369 m) towers beside the Badile; on **23 August 2017**, approximately **3 million cubic meters** of rock broke from its eastern face, triggering a devastating debris flow that destroyed parts of the village of **Bondo** below Soglio and killed eight people
- The **Sciora Hut** (Capanna di Sciora, 2,118 m), accessible from Bondo, serves as the base for climbing the Sciora peaks and is reached in 2–3 hours from the valley floor
- The catastrophic **1618 Piuro landslide** further down the valley buried the prosperous trading town of **Piuro** (on the Italian side), killing at least 1,000 people — a reminder that this dramatic landscape carries real danger

## 12. Rainer Maria Rilke and the Literary Legacy

- The poet **Rainer Maria Rilke** arrived at Palazzo Salis in **July 1919**, fleeing the chaos of post-World War I Munich and seeking a place to resume work on his **Duino Elegies**
- During his stay, Rilke met **Gudi Noelke** at the palazzo, beginning a lasting friendship and extensive correspondence
- **Room 15** at Hotel Palazzo Salis is dedicated to Rilke, with a page of his letter to **Yvonne von Wattenwyl** (21 August 1919) affixed to the wall
- Soglio was part of Rilke's extended search for a suitable Swiss residence — he moved through Soglio, Locarno, and Berg am Irchel before finding his permanent home at **Chateau de Muzot** in the Valais in mid-1921
- The village's literary resonance extends beyond Rilke: the Swiss writer **Hermann Burger** was also a guest at Palazzo Salis, and the village continues to attract writers and artists drawn by the same light and silence that captivated Segantini

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The threshold to paradise** — A child staying at Palazzo Salis discovers an old paintbrush in the garden; each brushstroke opens a window onto a different season in Soglio, painted in Segantini's luminous Divisionist light, until the child must paint their own view to find the way home

2. **The seven palaces of the Salis** — A treasure hunt through Soglio's seven von Salis palaces: each palazzo holds a clue from a different century (1285, 1400s, 1630, 1701...), leading to a hidden room where the family's story comes alive through ancestral portraits that whisper their tales

3. **The chestnut keeper** — An old cascina (smoking hut) in the chestnut forest comes alive during the harvest; a child meets the spirit of the grove — half tree, half grandmother — who teaches them the four ancient varieties (Ensat, Marun, Lueina, Vescuv) and the secret of keeping a village fed through winter

4. **The sculptor's valley** — A child walking the Sentiero Giacometti finds a piece of Bregaglia granite that wants to become a sculpture; following clues from young Alberto's footsteps between Borgonovo and Stampa, the stone slowly reveals its hidden form — tall, thin, and reaching upward like a Giacometti figure

5. **The bell tower's view** — From the Romanesque tower of San Lorenzo, a child sees not just the mountains but through time: the arrival of Vergerio the preacher in 1549, the village's vote for the Reformation in 1552, the Italian refugees finding safety in this tiny Protestant enclave above the clouds

6. **The mountain that fell** — After the 2017 Bondo rockfall, a child in Soglio must help a young chamois separated from its herd find a safe path through the changed landscape of the Val Bondasca, learning that mountains are alive and always moving, however slowly

7. **Rilke's lost verse** — A child discovers a half-finished poem tucked behind the wallpaper in Room 15 of Palazzo Salis; to complete it, they must visit each place Rilke saw during his 1919 summer — the garden with its sequoias, the chestnut groves, the view of the Sciora peaks at sunset

8. **The stone roof's secret** — During a thunderstorm, a child shelters under the heavy piode (stone-slab roof) of an old Soglio house and hears the stones humming; each slab remembers the hands that quarried and laid it, telling stories of the village stretching back to 1186 — the year Soglio first had a name
