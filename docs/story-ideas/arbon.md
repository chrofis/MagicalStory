## 1. Geography — A Peninsula on Lake Constance

- Arbon sits on a **peninsula** on the southwestern shore of **Lake Constance (Bodensee)**, the third-largest inland lake in Central Europe
- Located in the canton of **Thurgau**, between **Romanshorn** to the west and **Rorschach** to the east, about **10 km from St. Gallen**
- The town's elevated position on a promontory jutting into the lake gave it **strategic importance** from Roman times onward — visibility and control over lake traffic
- Population: approximately **14,000** inhabitants
- The official language is German; the spoken dialect is **Alemannic Swiss German**
- Thurgau is known as **"Mostindien"** (Apple Juice India) — the canton produces roughly one-third of Switzerland's eating apples and nearly half of its juicing apples

## 2. Prehistoric Pile Dwellings — UNESCO World Heritage

- The shores of Lake Constance near Arbon have been inhabited since the **Neolithic period**, with settlement traces reaching back over **5,500 years**
- **Arbon-Bleiche 3** is one of the most thoroughly investigated Neolithic lakeshore settlements in central Europe
- Rescue excavations between **1993 and 1995** uncovered nearly half the village: **27 houses** (25 dwellings plus 2 storehouses), with the full settlement estimated at about **50 houses** housing **300–500 people**
- The houses were built using posts of **white fir**, arranged in rows with their long sides facing the lake, each approximately **4 x 8 metres**
- Construction began in **3384 BC** (dated precisely by dendrochronology). In **3370 BC**, the entire village **burned down** after only 15 years of existence and was never rebuilt
- The fire paradoxically created superb preservation: a **single-layer settlement** sealed under protective sand from later flooding, with organic materials — food remains, textiles, tools, seeds — remarkably intact
- Analysis of approximately **70,000 animal bones** and botanical remains revealed an economy based on cereals, hazelnuts, and hunting (red deer provided about 50% of consumed meat)
- Chemical analysis of pottery residues provided **direct evidence of dairying** in late Neolithic central Europe
- In **2011**, the **Arbon-Bleiche 2 and 3** sites were inscribed as part of the UNESCO World Heritage Site **"Prehistoric Pile Dwellings around the Alps"** — a serial designation covering 111 sites across six countries

## 3. Roman Arbor Felix — The Happy Tree

- The Latin name **Arbor Felix** ("happy tree" or "fortunate tree") first appears in the **Itinerarium Antonini** around **300 AD**, a Roman road guide listing travel stations
- Arbor Felix served as a **fortified post station** on the Roman road from **Vindonissa** (Windisch) via **Ad Fines** (Pfyn) to **Brigantium** (Bregenz) — a key east-west route along the southern shore of Lake Constance
- The fort was probably constructed under Emperor **Diocletian** in the **late 3rd century**, built on the peninsula's elevated position
- The fortification had an **irregular pentagonal** shape (approximately 150 x 65–80 m, covering about 1 hectare), surrounded by the lake on three sides, with a rampart and ditch protecting the landward side
- Walls measured **2.6 m thick** above foundations, with towers at corners and sides in rectangular, semicircular, and segmented shapes
- Arbor Felix is mentioned in the **Notitia Dignitatum**, the late Roman administrative register, as the station of a **numerus barcariorum** — a military flotilla unit patrolling Lake Constance
- The Roman historian **Ammianus Marcellinus** records that Emperor **Gratian** passed through Arbor Felix in **378 AD**, hurrying eastward to join his uncle Valens before the catastrophic **Battle of Adrianople**
- The fort was occupied **until the late 5th century**, towards the end by the **Cohors Herculea Pannoniorum**
- A **Roman bathhouse** was discovered during 1986 excavations beneath the Church of St. Martin
- Remains of a late Roman **watchtower** (built c. 280 AD) survive in the old town and are visible today

## 4. Saint Gallus — The Irish Monk and the Bear

- **Saint Gallus** (c. 550–c. 650 AD), also known as Saint Gall, was an Irish monk from **Bangor** in present-day Northern Ireland, and one of the twelve companions of **Saint Columbanus** on his Christian mission to the European continent
- In **612**, when Columbanus departed Bregenz for Italy, Gallus stayed behind due to **illness** and was nursed back to health **at Arbon**
- After recovering, he did not rejoin Columbanus but withdrew with several companions into the forests southwest of Lake Constance, near the source of the **River Steinach**
- **The bear legend**: One evening, as Gallus warmed himself at a fire in the wilderness, a **bear** charged out of the forest. Gallus rebuked the animal, and the awed bear stopped its attack, retreated to the trees, gathered **firewood**, and returned to share the fire. The bear is said to have followed Gallus as a companion for the rest of his days
- Gallus died around **645–650 AD** at the age of about ninety-five **in Arbon**
- In **719**, Abbot **Otmar** expanded Gallus's hermitage cell into the **Abbey of St. Gall**, which became one of the most important intellectual centers in medieval Europe and the nucleus of the city and canton of St. Gallen
- The **Galluskapelle** (Gallus Chapel) in Arbon, adjacent to the Church of St. Martin, was built in his honour — the present structure dates to the **late 13th century**, though it likely had a Carolingian predecessor
- The chapel is listed as a **Swiss heritage site of national significance** and belongs to the Catholic Parish of St. Martin Arbon
- It is closed to the public for preservation but can be viewed through its windows

## 5. The Schloss (Castle) — Bishops, Towers, and a Museum

- A castle in Arbon was first mentioned in **720** in the chronicles of the Abbey of St. Gall, built on or near the foundations of the Roman fort
- Around **700 AD**, Arbon and its castle came under the ownership of the **Bishopric of Constance** — the bishops ruled Arbon through ecclesiastical overseers for centuries
- The oldest surviving part of the present castle is a **residential tower dating to 993**
- Bishop **Hugo von Hohenlandenberg** (c. 1457–1532), Bishop of Constance from 1496, rebuilt and expanded the castle between **1515 and 1518** into its current form — converting the tower into a **U-shaped complex** with added gables and a hip roof, aligned to the Roman fortress's northwest corner
- In **1460**, Thurgau was taken over by the Swiss Confederation, but Arbon was kept by the Bishop of Constance; after the **Swabian War of 1499**, the bishops lost their civil rights in Arbon, retaining only ecclesiastical authority
- The castle today houses the **Historisches Museum Arbon** (opened **1967**), the largest local and regional history museum in Thurgau
- The permanent exhibition takes visitors through **5,500 years of settlement history**: from Neolithic pile dwellers through Roman Arbor Felix, the medieval bishopric, and the industrial age
- Exhibits include original finds from the Arbon-Bleiche UNESCO sites, a Roman lead ingot, an early medieval bronze belt buckle, and Biedermeier furniture
- The castle's **Landenberg Hall** serves as a venue for cultural events

## 6. The Reformation in Arbon — A Town Between Faiths

- In **1525**, the first stirrings of the **Protestant Reformation** reached Arbon, as they did across much of the Swiss Confederation
- The town's population largely embraced the Reformed faith, but Arbon's political situation was unique: it remained under the authority of the **Catholic Bishop of Constance**
- In **1537**, the reformed congregation was **forced to return** the Church of St. Martin to the Catholic Church and was relegated to a chapel in Erdhausen — even though only a small minority of the population remained Catholic
- This created centuries of religious tension in the small lakeside town
- In **1712**, Thurgau finally declared **equality for the various faiths**
- In **1803**, Arbon became part of the newly created **Canton of Thurgau** under Napoleon's Act of Mediation, ending centuries of dependence on the Bishops of Constance
- The adoption of the Swiss federal constitution further solidified Arbon's independence from episcopal rule

## 7. The Church of St. Martin and the Galluskapelle

- **St. Martin's Church** is probably the **oldest Christian church** in the Arbon region
- The oldest part of the current structure is the **chancel, dating to 1450**
- During 1986 excavations beneath the church, archaeologists discovered the remains of a **Roman bathhouse**, confirming settlement continuity from Roman times through the Christian era
- A **Christian sanctuary** may have existed on this site as early as the **4th century**, predating the current church by a millennium
- Both the Church of St. Martin and the adjacent **Galluskapelle** are listed as **Swiss heritage sites of national significance**
- The old town walking tour identifies **nine historical stations**, including the Roman fort Arbor Felix, the Roman bath complex, and the chapel with its connection to St. Gallus

## 8. Saurer — From Pots and Pans to World-Famous Trucks

- In **1853**, **Franz Saurer** (1806–1882) from Veringenstadt, Germany, established an iron foundry in **St. Gallen**, initially producing domestic pots and pans
- In **1863**, Saurer relocated the foundry to **Arbon** on Lake Constance, pivoting to **textile machinery** — Jacquard looms and embroidery machines — to serve eastern Switzerland's booming embroidery industry
- The firm was formally established as **"F. Saurer und Söhne"** in **1869**
- In **1896**, the eldest surviving son **Adolph Saurer** (1841–1920) took over; his son **Hippolyt Saurer** (1878–1936) began developing **petrol engines** that same year
- The first Saurer truck — a **five-ton model** powered by a 30 hp petrol engine with four-speed transmission — was built in **1903**
- In **1904**, Saurer developed the **world's first exhaust brake** for commercial vehicles
- Saurer became a pioneer of **direct-injection diesel technology**, collaborating with MAN from **1924** and launching its first viable diesel truck in **1928**
- Before World War I, Saurer was **probably the leading European producer of heavy commercial vehicles**
- International expansion: subsidiaries in **Austria (1906)**, **France (1910)**, **Germany (1915)**, **UK (1927)**, and the **Saurer Motor Truck Co.** in the USA (**1911**)
- At its peak in the post-war period, Saurer employed **over 5,000 people** in Arbon — the town's dominant employer
- The company produced over **10,000 hand embroidery machines** during its textile era
- Commercial vehicle production ended in **1982–1983**; production of **Swiss Army trucks** continued until **1987**
- The **Saurer Museum** (established 1993, relocated to lakeside premises in 2010) at **Weitegasse 8** displays about **20 historic vehicles** — trucks, buses, fire engines, military vehicles, and PostBuses — most still in working order, alongside a complete collection of Saurer petrol and diesel engines and original textile machinery

## 9. The Lakefront Promenade and Old Town

- Arbon's **lakeside promenade** stretches for **three kilometres** along the shore of Lake Constance, lined with a distinctive **chestnut avenue**, park areas, a concert pavilion, children's playgrounds, and a public lido
- The promenade offers views across the lake to **Constance**, the German shore, and the distant **Alps**
- The **Altstadt** (old town) features colourful **half-timbered houses**, graceful **Rococo facades**, and medieval structures — many built atop the thick walls of the former city fortifications
- **Untertorgasse** preserves a particularly impressive row of houses whose foundations incorporate the medieval defensive walls
- **Fischmarktplatz** (Fish Market Square) is the social heart of the old town, featuring a **shell-shaped fountain with a bronze fisherwoman** — the square hosts markets, concerts, and festivals throughout the year
- The **Posthof**, a large residential block from **1905/1906**, borders the square
- Arbon's harbour offers pedalo and boat rentals, and the **"White Fleet"** of Lake Constance excursion ships stops here

## 10. Mosterei Möhl and Thurgau's Apple Heritage

- **Mosterei Möhl**, founded in **1895** in Arbon, has been producing apple juice, cider, and apple wine for **five generations**
- It is one of only **two large-scale apple juice companies** in all of Switzerland
- Thurgau's nickname **"Mostindien"** reflects the canton's status as Switzerland's primary apple-producing region
- In **2018**, the Möhl family opened the **MoMö** (Museum of Modern Öpfel) — the first Swiss cidery and distillery museum, spread over **1,000 square metres**
- The interactive museum traces the journey from **orchard to bottle**, exhibiting antique presses, fruit mills, and laboratory equipment alongside the history of the Möhl family
- Arbon's **original cider** is a local specialty recommended to visitors along the Lake Constance cycle path

## 11. Notable People

- **Saint Gallus** (c. 550–c. 650): Irish monk, founder of the hermitage that became the Abbey of St. Gall, died in Arbon
- **Jacques Gerschwiler** (1898–2000): Born in Arbon, Swiss figure skater and coach; trained World and European champions including Cecilia Colledge and Jeannette Altwegg; elected to the **World Figure Skating Hall of Fame** in **1976** as part of its inaugural class; lived to age 101

## 12. Notable Historical Facts

- Arbon claims **5,500 years of continuous settlement history** — from Neolithic pile dwellers through Roman legionaries, medieval bishops, and industrial pioneers
- Emperor **Gratian** passed through in **378 AD** — one of the last Roman emperors to traverse this part of the empire before its collapse
- The name evolved from the Latin **Arbor Felix** through medieval forms to the modern **Arbon**
- The **Caminhão** — a 1911 Saurer truck discovered in **Brazil** — was returned to Arbon in **2003** and is now a centrepiece of the Saurer Museum
- Arbon lies on the popular **Lake Constance Cycle Path** (Bodensee-Radweg), a 273 km circuit around the lake crossing through Switzerland, Germany, and Austria
- The town has **three museums** within walking distance: the Historisches Museum in the castle, the Saurer Museum on the lakefront, and the MoMö cidery museum

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The village that burned in the night** — A child living in a pile dwelling on the lake in 3370 BC must rescue their family's most treasured seed basket before the flames consume the entire village
2. **Arbor Felix and the lucky tree** — A Roman child in the lakeside fort befriends a tree said to bring good fortune; when Emperor Gratian arrives, the child must prove the tree's magic to save it from being felled for firewood
3. **Gallus and the bear who brought firewood** — Retelling of the classic legend: a sick monk nursed back to health in Arbon wanders into the forest, confronts a bear, and gains a lifelong companion who helps him build a hermitage
4. **The bishop's tower** — A child in 1515 watches Bishop Hugo rebuild the crumbling Roman fort into a grand castle; each stone reveals a different layer of Arbon's history — Roman, Alemannic, medieval
5. **The first Saurer truck** — In 1903, a child whose family works in the embroidery factory watches in wonder as a strange, noisy machine rolls out of the workshop — the very first five-ton Saurer truck, destined to change the world
6. **The secret of the Fischmarktplatz** — The bronze fisherwoman on the fountain comes alive at night and leads a child on a tour through Arbon's old town, revealing stories hidden behind every half-timbered facade
7. **The apple that rolled into the lake** — A child from a Thurgau apple orchard chases a runaway apple all the way to Arbon's lakefront, discovering the MoMö museum and learning how apples shaped an entire canton
8. **The flotilla of Arbor Felix** — A child joins the Roman barcarii (lake patrol) on Lake Constance, sailing past pile dwelling ruins and watching for signals from the watchtower on the peninsula
