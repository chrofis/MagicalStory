## 1. Saint Fridolin — The Irish Monk and Patron of Glarus

- **Saint Fridolin of Säckingen** (died c. 538–540) was an **Irish missionary** who became the apostle of the Alamanni people along the Upper Rhine
- According to tradition, Fridolin belonged to a noble Irish family, first preached in Ireland, then crossed to **France** where he found the relics of **Saint Hilarius** in Poitiers
- Commanded by a vision of Hilarius, he journeyed to an **island in the Rhine** at Säckingen (today in Baden-Württemberg, Germany), where he founded **Säckingen Abbey** and a nunnery
- He established numerous churches dedicated to Saint Hilarius throughout the region, including in the **Linth valley** — the territory that became the canton of Glarus
- **The Ursus legend** (recorded in the 13th century): Fridolin converted a wealthy landowner named **Ursus**, who bequeathed his lands in the Linth valley to Fridolin. When Ursus's brother **Landolf** challenged the gift in court at Rankweil, Fridolin **summoned Ursus from the dead** to confirm the donation. The terrified Landolf then gave his own lands to Fridolin as well
- From the **9th century**, the area around Glarus was owned by Säckingen Abbey; the place name **"Glarus"** likely derives from churches dedicated to Saint Hilarius ("Clarona")
- A banner depicting Saint Fridolin was carried into the **Battle of Näfels in 1388**, rallying the Glarner troops
- Since **1395**, when Glarus bought its freedom from Säckingen Abbey, the canton has displayed **Saint Fridolin on its coat of arms** — shown as a pilgrim figure with a bible, often accompanied by the skeleton of Ursus

## 2. Geography — The Dramatic Alpine Valley

- The canton of Glarus lies in **east-central Switzerland**, dominated by the deep valley of the **Linth River** flanked by the towering peaks of the **Glarus Alps**
- The Linth rises near the village of **Linthal** in the south, fed by Alpine glaciers, and flows approximately **50 km** northward through the valley toward **Walensee** (Lake Walen)
- The highest point is the **Tödi** at **3,614 m** (11,857 ft), the most prominent peak of the Glarus Alps
- The **Glärnisch massif** (with four peaks above 2,700 m) rises dramatically above the cantonal capital, its summit snowfield visible from as far as Zurich
- **Klöntalersee**: a natural lake at **848 m** elevation in the Klöntal side valley, formed by a prehistoric rockslide, covering **3.3 km²** — famous for its mirror-like surface reflecting the surrounding mountains; it has served as a hydroelectric reservoir since **1908**
- Total canton area: **264 square miles** (684 km²), with over 50 square miles of forest; population approximately **38,000**, predominantly German-speaking
- The valley's narrow geography — mountains pressing close on both sides — shaped the town's dense medieval layout and made the **Föhn wind** a constant presence and danger

## 3. The Battle of Näfels (1388) — Victory Against the Habsburgs

- On **9 April 1388**, a force of roughly **400 men from Glarus** and a few dozen allies from **Schwyz and Uri** faced an Austrian Habsburg army of approximately **6,500 soldiers** at the northern entrance to the Linth valley
- **Background**: Glarus had joined the Swiss Confederation in **1352** but was returned to Habsburg control in 1355. In **1387**, the valley council declared itself free, destroying **Burg Windegg** (the Habsburg fortress)
- After the expiration of a truce in February 1388, **Duke Albert III of Austria** sent forces to crush the rebellion
- The Austrians, led by **Count Donat von Toggenburg** and the **Count of Thorberg**, captured the **Letzi** (fortifications) at Näfels, forcing the Glarner defenders to retreat into the hills
- The decisive moment: the Glarner emerged from **snow and fog** to counterattack while the Austrians were occupied looting. The disorganized Habsburg forces broke and fled toward **Weesen**, but a **bridge over the Maag collapsed** under their weight, plunging hundreds into the river where they drowned
- **Casualties**: approximately **54 Swiss** killed versus **1,700 Austrians** — a staggering disparity
- The battle was the **last major Swiss-Austrian conflict** of the 14th century. In **April 1389**, a seven-year peace was signed in Vienna, leaving the Confederation in undisputed possession of its gains
- A **banner of Saint Fridolin** was carried into the battle, cementing the saint's role as protector of Glarus

## 4. Näfelser Fahrt — Switzerland's Oldest Memorial Celebration

- The **Näfelser Fahrt** is the annual commemoration of the Battle of Näfels, held on the **first Thursday in April** (except when it falls in Holy Week)
- It is recognized as **Switzerland's oldest state celebration** still regularly observed; the first battle chapel was likely built as early as **1389**, and the oldest version of the **Fahrtsbrief** (processional document) dates to between **1404 and 1443**
- The procession begins at **7:15 AM** at the **Zeughaus** (armory) in Glarus, with a military honor guard, singing societies, and marching bands
- The route leads from Glarus through **Netstal** to **Schneisigen**, passing **eleven memorial stones** spaced at intervals of 123 to 261 meters, marking stopping points for prayers and readings
- At each stone, passages from the **Fahrtsbrief** are read aloud — the document details the events leading to the battle and names every military casualty
- The procession ends at the **Schlachtdenkmal** (Battle Memorial) near the **Church of St. Hilarius** in Näfels, followed by a solemn high mass
- Participants include cantonal officials, military units, the **Näfels Harmoniemusik** band, and the **Glarus cantonal choir**; the event draws up to **1,000 participants**
- The celebration is listed in Switzerland's **Living Traditions** (Immaterielles Kulturerbe) program under "Social practices"

## 5. The Landsgemeinde — Open-Air Democracy Since 1387

- The **Landsgemeinde** is the canton's annual open-air assembly, with roots documented to **11 March 1387** — one of the oldest democratic institutions in Switzerland
- It takes place on the **first Sunday in May** at the **Zaunplatz**, Glarus's central square, which transforms from a car park into an open-air parliament
- The **Landammann** (canton president) leads the assembly, carrying the **cantonal sword** — a symbol of judicial and military authority dating back centuries
- Historically, the only proof of citizenship men needed to enter the voting area was to show their **ceremonial sword or Swiss military sidearm** (bayonet), proving they were freemen allowed to bear arms and vote
- Today, citizens vote by **raising orange cards** in a public show of hands; the Landammann visually estimates whether a majority supports a proposal; if too close, cards are raised and counted a second time
- In **2016**, Glarus voters **rejected** electronic voting aids, preferring the traditional method
- Any citizen may **take the microphone** and propose amendments to laws, which are voted on immediately
- The Landsgemeinde exists today in only **two Swiss cantons**: **Glarus** and **Appenzell Innerrhoden**
- Notable decisions: in **2006**, the Landsgemeinde voted to consolidate the canton's **25 municipalities into 3** (effective 1 January 2011); on **6 May 2007**, Glarus became the **first Swiss canton to lower the voting age to 16** — decided by raised hands in three rounds of voting in pouring rain
- Average attendance: approximately **3,000 citizens**; debates are accompanied by **simultaneous sign language interpretation**

## 6. The Great Fire of 1861 — Destruction and Rebirth

- On the night of **10–11 May 1861**, a catastrophic fire destroyed **two-thirds** of the cantonal capital
- The fire broke out after **9:30 PM** in a barn near the **Landsgemeindeplatz**, next to the house of Councilman Christoph Tschudi
- Driven by a violent **Föhn wind** (the hot, dry south wind channeled through the narrow valley), the fire spread to nearly **150 buildings within half an hour**
- In total, approximately **600 buildings** were destroyed, **2,300 people** (47% of residents) left homeless, and **eight to ten people** killed (the exact number unknown, as some died later from smoke poisoning)
- The church bells **melted** from the heat in the narrowest part of the main street, the "Meerenge"
- Financial losses exceeded **8 million Swiss francs**, with the canton's compulsory fire insurance holding only **250,000 francs** in reserves — exposing a critical insurance gap
- **Reconstruction** (1861–1864): architects **Bernhard Simon** (1816–1900, who had designed residences for Russian aristocrats in **Saint Petersburg**) and **Johann Caspar Wolff** (1818–1891, Zurich state building inspector) designed a modern town with a **checkerboard grid layout**, wide straight streets, and stately public buildings — a deliberate fireproofing strategy
- The rebuilt Glarus is considered one of the **most accomplished 19th-century urban planning achievements** in Switzerland
- A major challenge: removing the **Tschudirain**, a 23-meter mound from an ancient landslide, to create the new grid
- The disaster directly led to the founding of **Swiss Re** (Schweizerische Rückversicherungs-Gesellschaft), established by **Moritz Ignaz Grossmann** on **19 December 1863** in Zurich — now one of the world's largest reinsurers

## 7. The Textile Industry — A Global Printing Powerhouse

- In **1740**, **Johann Heinrich Streiff** opened the first **Indiennes** (printed cotton textile) workshop in Glarus, engaging an expert colourist of **Huguenot** descent
- **Indiennes** — brightly printed cotton fabrics imitating Indian imports — had become wildly popular across Europe; Swiss producers were among the leading manufacturers
- Several institutional factors favored Glarus: **low taxes**, the **absence of a guild system** with its restrictive regulations, and no salt tax until the late 18th century
- After **1750**, cotton and linen printing flourished, and the canton of Glarus alongside **Mulhouse (Alsace)** became the **most important centres of textile printing in Europe**
- At its peak, **22 printing plants** employed **6,250 workers** out of approximately 35,000 cantonal inhabitants — more than half the workforce being women, girls, and boys
- By hand, they printed approximately **48 million metres of fabric per year**
- The famed **Glarner Tüechli** (Glarus cloth) was exported via Italian trading bases to the **eastern Mediterranean**, throughout the **Ottoman Empire**, and later to **Asia and Africa**
- The industry drove the canton's prosperity but also created harsh working conditions — especially **child labor** — that eventually led to the pioneering factory law of 1864

## 8. The Fabrikgesetz of 1864 — Switzerland's First Factory Law

- On **22 May 1864**, the Landsgemeinde voted to adopt the revised **"Law on Factory Police"** (Fabrikgesetz) — the **first factory law in continental Europe** prescribing a standard working day for adult men
- Key provisions: daily working hours limited to a maximum of **12 hours**; **night work abolished**; factory work forbidden for **school-age children** (under 13); "female persons" exempted from factory work for **six weeks before and after childbirth**
- The law was revolutionary because it challenged the previously sacrosanct **freedom of contract** — recognizing for the first time that the state had a responsibility for working conditions in private enterprises
- It was born from the harsh realities of the textile industry, where children as young as 7 had worked long hours in printing factories
- The Glarner Fabrikgesetz served as the direct model for the **Swiss Federal Factory Law of 1877**, and also influenced worker protection legislation in **Bismarck's Germany**

## 9. The Linthkanal — Taming the River (1807–1816)

- In the late 18th century, **industrial deforestation** in the Glarus valley caused increasing amounts of gravel to wash downstream with spring floods, raising the level of **Walensee** by several meters and turning the Linth plain into **malarial swampland**
- Agriculture became impossible, poverty increased, and diseases including **tuberculosis and malaria** were rampant among the population
- **Hans Conrad Escher** (1767–1823), a Zurich merchant, naturalist, engineer, and statesman, developed the plan to tame the river
- **The Escherkanal** (completed **1811**): diverted the Linth into **Walensee**, where gravel could be deposited without damage
- **The Linthkanal** (completed **1816**): a second channel connecting Walensee to **Lake Zurich**, replacing the former Maag river course
- Results: the flooding ended, the level of Walensee dropped by **five meters**, swamps dried up, and **20 km² of arable land** was reclaimed in the Linth plain — transforming the region
- The project (1807–1823) was the **earliest engineering project of such scale** undertaken by the new Swiss state, and a landmark act of inter-cantonal solidarity
- Following Escher's death in 1823, the Swiss state took the highly unusual step of appending the honorific **"von der Linth"** to his family name — an honor that persists to this day (his grandson was the famous industrialist Alfred Escher, founder of Credit Suisse and the Gotthard railway)

## 10. Glarner Schabziger — The World's Oldest Branded Cheese

- **Schabziger** (also called **Ziger** or **Sap Sago** abroad) is a hard, cone-shaped green cheese produced exclusively in the canton of Glarus
- Its origins trace to **Säckingen Abbey** (Fridolin's monastery), possibly as far back as the **9th century**; the first written mention dates to **1310**, with documented sales at Zurich markets recorded in **1429**
- Made from **skimmed cow's milk** flavored with **blue fenugreek** (Trigonella caerulea), also called blue melilot — a herb believed to have arrived in Europe during the **Crusades** from the Middle East
- The distinctive green color and pungent flavor come entirely from the fenugreek herb, originally grown by nuns in the Säckingen monastery gardens
- On **24 April 1463**, the Landsgemeinde approved a decree titled **"Von Ziger ze machen"** — establishing precise quality controls, mandating that the cheese bear a **stamp of origin** with the cantonal coat of arms, and granting exclusive production rights to Glarus
- This 1463 decree makes Schabziger **Switzerland's oldest protected brand** and one of the earliest examples of product protection and quality control anywhere in the world
- The cheese is pressed into distinctive **cone shapes** (Zigerstöckli), dried for 2–6 months, and typically **grated** over pasta, vegetables, or mixed with butter as a spread
- By the **17th century**, Schabziger was exported to Holland, Germany, France, Italy, England, and the United States
- Today it is produced exclusively by **Geska** (Gesellschaft Schweizer Kräuterkäse-Fabrikanten), with 70 of the canton's 400 farms contributing milk

## 11. Vrenelisgärtli — The Legend of the Alpine Garden

- **Vrenelisgärtli** ("Little Verena's Little Garden") is the **2,904 m** summit of the Glärnisch massif, the most prominent panorama peak of the range — its distinctive **triangular snowfield** is visible from Zurich on clear days
- **The legend**: a spirited young woman named **Vreneli** declared she would plant a flower garden on the summit, defying all warnings. She said: "Now I'll go up there to prove the opposite, whether it pleases the Lord God or not"
- She placed a **copper kettle** on her head for protection, trudged through snow and wind, and cleared a patch of meadow with her bare hands
- But the weather turned, a heavy snowfall set in, and the kettle filled with water and snow until it became so heavy that Vreneli **could no longer free herself** — she was buried beneath the mounting snow
- To this day, a small **nearly square field of snow** lies on the summit — the so-called Vrenelisgärtli, where the "arrogant gardener lies buried beneath"
- **An alternative version** tells of Vreneli as the daughter of a mighty mountain spirit who tended a colourful garden on the peak; when her father caught her with a boy she loved, he threw the boy down the mountain, turned Vreneli into a boulder, and turned the garden into a desert of snow and ice
- The snowfield **completely melted for the first time** in recorded history during the hot summer of **2003** — a widely reported symbol of climate change in the Alps

## 12. Zwingli and the Confessional Division

- **Huldrych Zwingli**, the great Swiss reformer, served as **parish priest in Glarus from 1506 to 1516** — his first clerical appointment, before he moved to Einsiedeln and then launched the Reformation in Zurich
- During his decade in Glarus, Zwingli twice served as **military chaplain** with Glarner mercenary troops in Italy, an experience that turned him against the mercenary system and began his path to reform
- Glarus adopted the Reformation but remained **religiously divided** — both Catholics and Protestants shared the same church
- From **1623**, the canton maintained **separate Catholic and Protestant assemblies** (Landsgemeinden) and courts, a dual system that lasted until **1836** when a single unified Landsgemeinde was reestablished
- This confessional coexistence makes Glarus unusual among Swiss cantons — the parish church is **still jointly used** by both denominations

3. **The sword of the Landsgemeinde** — A child inherits a miniature wooden sword and discovers it grants the power to speak at the Landsgemeinde; learns that democracy means every voice counts, even the smallest
4. **Vreneli's frozen garden** — A child climbs the Glärnisch and finds Vreneli's ghost tending her snow garden; together they must plant one real flower before the summer heat melts the last patch of snow
5. **The fog of Näfels** — On the battlefield, a child from Glarus and a child from Austria meet in the fog and must decide: fight or make peace? Retelling the 1388 battle through children's eyes
6. **The green cheese mystery** — A child follows a trail of fenugreek herbs from the Säckingen monastery to the Landsgemeinde of 1463, where the secret recipe is about to be sealed forever
7. **Escher and the drowned valley** — A child discovers the Linth plain is sinking back into swamp; must travel back to 1807 to help young Hans Conrad Escher convince the cantons to build the canal together
8. **The printing girl of Glarus** — A child works in a textile printing factory in 1860, stamping 48 million meters of cloth; when the Landsgemeinde debates the factory law, she finds the courage to speak up for children's rights
