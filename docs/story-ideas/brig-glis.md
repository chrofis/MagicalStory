## 1. Geography — Gateway Between the Alps

- Brig-Glis lies in the **upper Rhône valley** at an average altitude of **691 meters**, in the canton of **Valais** in southern Switzerland
- The town sits at the **foot of the Simplon Pass** (2,005 m), the historic gateway between Switzerland and Italy
- It occupies a strategic position between the **Bernese Alps** to the north and the **Pennine Alps** (Valais Alps) to the south, not far from the **Aletsch Glacier** — the largest glacier in the Alps
- The **Saltina**, a powerful mountain torrent descending from the Simplon area, cuts through the town in a dramatic gorge before joining the **Rhône** — its name derives from the Latin "saltare" (to jump), reflecting the violent boulders it carries during floods
- The area covers **38 km²** with a population of around **13,000**, making it the service centre of the German-speaking **Upper Valais** (Oberwallis)
- Named **Alpine Town of the Year** in **2008** for its commitment to sustainability and cross-border cooperation with neighbouring Domodossola in Italy

## 2. Early History — From Bronze Age to Bishop's Fortress

- The earliest evidence of habitation near Brig comes from **Bronze Age** finds: a bracelet and a dagger
- The area was inhabited through the **La Tène** (Celtic) period; the name **Brig** derives from the Celtic word **"briga"**, meaning "hill fortress" or "fortified height"
- During the **Roman period**, the Simplon route was already used, and Roman influence reached the upper Rhône valley. In **2020**, excavations in Brig-Glis uncovered remains of an exceptionally large Roman masonry building — only the second such structure identified in Upper Valais — suggesting organized administrative or commercial functions rather than mere transit
- Brig is **first mentioned** in documents in **1215** as "Briga"
- The town was likely founded by the **Bishop of Sion**, who built a tower and curtain wall in the **12th century**, establishing it as a fortified settlement within the **Prince-Bishopric of Sion**
- Brig was not formally recognized as a **town** until the **17th century**, and in **1518** it became the capital of the **Zenden** (district) of Brig
- On **1 October 1972**, the former municipalities of **Brig**, **Glis**, and **Brigerbad** merged to form the modern municipality of **Brig-Glis**

## 3. The Pilgrimage Church of Glis — 1,500 Years of Faith

- The **Church of Our Lady** in Glis is one of the most remarkable sacred buildings in the Upper Valais, with layers spanning over a millennium
- Excavations in **1984** discovered an **early Christian aisleless church from around 500 AD**, along with a **baptistery** (rectangular baptismal font, built around **615**), side rooms, and traces of **four additional churches** built on the same site
- Since the **14th century**, the church has been a **pilgrimage centre** of the Upper Valais
- The present building is a palimpsest of styles: a **Romanesque tower**, a **Gothic choir** and side chapels, a **Golden Gate**, and a **Renaissance portal**
- From **1648 to 1668**, the powerful Kaspar Jodok von Stockalper had the **baroque pillar basilica** built by the **Bodmer brothers** — the same master builders who worked on the Stockalper Palace
- Glis was raised to an **independent parish** only in **1642**

## 4. Kaspar Jodok von Stockalper — The King of the Simplon

- Born **14 July 1609** in Brig into an already wealthy family of probable Italian origins; died **29 April 1691**
- The most powerful merchant prince in 17th-century Switzerland, known as the **"King of the Simplon"** and the **"Fugger of the Alps"**
- Revitalized the **Simplon Pass**, which had fallen into disuse, transforming it into a major **trading highway** with improved mule tracks and warehouses along the route
- Held **monopolies on salt** (essential for food preservation and cheesemaking), as well as tinder polypore, larch resin, and snails — importing salt and silk from Italy, exporting iron, leather, cattle, and cheese
- Built a trading network spanning from **Geneva to Vienna** and **Milan to Brussels**, conducting business in **six languages**
- Also operated **mines** (iron near Brig, gold at Gondo, plus lead and copper mines), ran a **mercenary hiring** business, and held the **postal monopoly**
- Employed **several thousand** hauliers, carriage drivers, roadworkers, administrators, and builders — at a time when Brig itself had only about **900 residents**
- Held political offices from age **20**, rising to **Landeshauptmann** (highest office in Valais) for eight years from **1670**
- Showered with titles and honours by popes, emperors, and kings — including **Louis XIV** of France, who called him "King of the Simplon"
- In **1678**, jealous communities of Visp, Leuk, Sierre, and Sion forced him from power; in **1680** he fled to **Domodossola** in Italy, fearing for his life
- Returned to Brig in **1685** under a negotiated agreement but never regained his former power; died in his palace in **1691**
- Married twice, had **14 children** — but only one son survived to continue the family line
- Founded the **Jesuit college** (Kollegium Spiritus Sanctus, 1662) and an **Ursuline convent** with a girls' school (1661) in Brig
- Buried in the **church of Glis**, the pilgrimage church he had rebuilt

## 5. The Stockalper Palace (Stockalperschloss) — Baroque Colossus

- The **largest private secular Baroque building** in Switzerland, built by Stockalper between **1658 and 1678**
- A massive rectangular complex with **three towers** on three corners (northwest, southwest, southeast), each topped with a gilded **onion dome**
- The towers are named after the **Three Wise Kings**: **Kaspar**, **Melchior**, and **Balthasar** — Stockalper called the building the "Houses and Chapel of the Three Kings"
- Features an elegant **Renaissance-style arcaded courtyard** (approximately 40 m x 32 m) with Tuscan arcades, blending Italian Renaissance with Northern European Baroque
- The garden spans **13,000 m²** — among the largest historic gardens in the Valais
- Served Stockalper as both a **showpiece of wealth** and a practical **warehouse** for his trading empire
- The builders were **Christian, Balthasar, and Peter Bodmer** (the same family who built the Kollegiumskirche and the Glis church), with stonemasons **Uli Jordan** and **Johann Baptist Miletto**
- After Stockalper's exile and death, the palace fell into decline
- In **1948**, the Municipality of Brig purchased the palace; comprehensive **renovation began in 1956**, supported by a Swiss trust
- Today owned by a **public foundation** (Swiss Confederation, Canton of Valais, Municipality of Brig, and cultural associations); houses the municipal administration, district court, an Alpine research institute, and the **"Passage Simplon"** museum exhibition
- The courtyard hosts **concerts, film festivals, and exhibitions**

## 6. The Kollegiumskirche and Kollegium Spiritus Sanctus

- The **Kollegium Spiritus Sanctus** (College of the Holy Spirit) was founded on **19 October 1662**, financed by Stockalper who donated land and building materials to convince the **Jesuits** to relocate from Venthône to Brig
- The **college building** was constructed from **1663 to 1673**; the **Kollegiumskirche** (Collegiate Church) followed from **1673 to 1687**
- The church was designed by **Mathäus Koller** from Augsburg and Jesuit friar **Heinrich Mayer**, built by the **Bodmer brothers** from Priszell
- Architecturally a representative of the **German Jesuit church type**: rectangular walls, a recessed choir with triple end and **fan vault**, a **barrel-vaulted nave**, and a choir flank tower with **octagonal bell storey and onion dome**
- The Jesuits ran the school from 1662 to 1773, and again from 1814 to 1847
- During **Napoleon's occupation** (1812–1814), the college building served as **military barracks**
- After the **papal suppression** and Switzerland's federal expulsion of Jesuits in **1848**, priests from the **Diocese of Sion** took over the school
- The college continues today as a **secondary school** (gymnasium and sports school), one of the most important educational institutions in Upper Valais

## 7. The Simplon Pass — First Highway over the Alps

- Altitude **2,005 metres** (some sources: 2,009 m); connects **Brig** (691 m) to **Domodossola** (270 m) over **63 km**
- Archaeological evidence suggests the pass was already a thoroughfare in the **Bronze and Iron Ages**
- By the **12th century**, it formed the heart of the trade route between **Upper Italy** and the **Champagne** fairs in France; a hospice is first documented in **1235**
- Around **1320**, traffic shifted eastward to the **Gotthard**, and the Simplon declined — until Stockalper revitalized it in the **1630s**
- **Napoleon Bonaparte** ordered the construction of a proper road in **1800** — "Pour faire passer les canons" (to pass the cannons) — to transport his artillery between Paris and Milan
- Built by engineer **Nicolas Céard**; completed and opened on **9 October 1805** as a **63 km highway** with **eight large bridges** and **seven galleries** carved into the rockface — considered the **first true highway across the Alps**
- Peak stagecoach era: **1850–1906**; in **1876 alone**, 28,190 passengers were transported using over 150 horses daily
- The first motorized **PostBus** crossed the pass in **1919**; today the pass remains open year-round with winter safety improvements since **1957**

## 8. The Simplon Railway Tunnel — Engineering Marvel of 1906

- Runs beneath the Simplon massif from **Brig** to **Iselle** (Italy), more than **1.6 km below Monte Leone** at its deepest point
- **First tube**: **19,803 m** (12.3 miles) long — the **longest railway tunnel in the world** from its opening in 1906 until **1982** (when Japan's Daishimizu Tunnel surpassed it)
- Construction began in **1898** under the direction of **Alfred Brandt**, head of the German engineering firm Brandt, Brandau & Company, who invented an efficient hydraulic rock drill; Brandt died before the tunnel's completion
- The two halves met on **24 February 1905**; the tunnel was officially inaugurated on **19 May 1906** by **King Victor Emmanuel III** of Italy and Swiss Federal President **Ludwig Forrer** at Brig
- Construction faced extreme challenges: temperatures up to **49 °C** (120 °F) inside the rock, and water inrushes of up to **49,270 litres per minute**
- An average of **3,000 workers** laboured daily; **67 workers** died in accidents, and many more perished from silicosis and other diseases
- A **second tube** was begun in **1912** and opened in **1921** (delayed by World War I), at **19,824 m**
- The tunnel made Brig an **international railway hub**: the **Simplon Orient Express** (from 1919) passed through Brig — the only place where this legendary train traversed German-speaking territory

## 9. Brig as Railway Junction — Crossroads of the Alps

- **Brig railway station** opened in **1878**, adjacent to the northern portal of the Simplon Tunnel
- The **Lötschberg railway line** (Kandersteg–Goppenstein–Brig) was completed in **1913**, connecting Brig to **Bern** and northern Switzerland through a **14.6 km tunnel** beneath the Bernese Alps — construction claimed **64 workers' lives**, including 25 killed when the tunnel broke into a glacial fissure in **July 1908**
- The **Lötschberg Base Tunnel** (34.6 km), opened in **December 2007**, now provides a high-speed connection reducing travel time dramatically
- Together, the Lötschberg and Simplon lines form one of Europe's most important **north-south railway axes**
- The **Glacier Express**, the world-famous "slowest express train," has connected Brig to **Zermatt** and **St. Moritz** since **30 June 1930**
- Brig station serves as the junction between three major Alpine routes: the **Simplon**, the **Lötschberg**, and the **Furka** lines — making it one of Switzerland's most important mountain railway hubs

## 10. The Saltina Flood of 1993 — Disaster and Resilience

- On **24 September 1993**, after weeks of rain followed by three days of extreme precipitation, the **Saltina** burst its banks in Brig
- Meteorologists recorded **precipitation and discharge values** that were the highest in the Saltina catchment for the **entire 20th century**: 150 litres of water per m² and **1.5 tonnes of bedload per second**
- Debris and alluvial wood **blocked the Saltina bridge** in the town centre, causing the water to dam up and flood both the **inner city of Brig** and the less densely built neighbourhoods in **Glis**
- Approximately **250,000 m³ of mud, sand, and debris** were deposited across roads, squares, and buildings — burying the city centre metres deep
- **Two women** lost their lives; material damage totalled over **CHF 650 million**
- The reconstruction was completed **within one year** — a remarkable feat of civic determination
- Protective measures implemented afterwards: **bank protection**, **bedload management** in the Grindji, and a revolutionary **hydraulic lift bridge** that automatically raises itself by up to **three metres** within minutes when water levels rise
- These measures proved their worth in **2000**, when river volumes **exceeded 1993 levels** but damage was kept to a minimum
- The Saltina has a documented flood history stretching back to **1331**, with major events in **1755** and **1828**

## 11. Brigerbad — Roman Hot Springs

- **Brigerbad**, incorporated into Brig-Glis since 1972, is home to thermal springs known since **Roman times** — a history stretching back roughly **2,000 years**
- The hot springs supply **lithium-containing sodium-calcium-sulphate water** at a rate of **50 litres per second**, valued for its effects on rheumatic and neurological conditions
- Today, the **Brigerbad Thermal Bath** is one of the **largest open-air thermal baths in Switzerland**, with over **2,600 m²** of water facilities
- Kaspar Jodok von Stockalper himself **owned the thermal baths** in the 17th century, incorporating them into his vast business empire
- Highlights include a **grotto bath** carved into rock, a **184-metre thermal water slide**, and multiple pools

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The King of the Simplon** — A mule driver's child follows Stockalper's salt caravan over the Simplon Pass, discovers how one man built an empire from salt, silk, and courage — and learns why the three golden towers are named after the Wise Kings
2. **The tunnel that beat the mountain** — Child travels back to 1905 and joins the workers boring through Monte Leone; faces the heat (49 °C!), the floods underground, and the moment the two halves of the Simplon Tunnel finally meet
3. **When the Saltina jumped** — On a peaceful day in Brig, a child notices the river rising. Retelling of the 1993 flood from a child's perspective: the bridge that blocked, the mud that buried the town, and the neighbours who rebuilt everything in one year
4. **The palace of three towers** — Stockalper's ghost shows a child the palace at night; each tower (Kaspar, Melchior, Balthasar) contains a different era of Brig's history — Roman traders, medieval pilgrims, and Baroque merchants
5. **Napoleon's road** — A child finds a carved stone ("Pour faire passer les canons") on the Simplon road and is transported to 1805, witnessing the construction of the first highway over the Alps alongside engineer Nicolas Céard
6. **The Roman bath that remembers** — At Brigerbad, a child dives into the warm springs and surfaces in Roman times, meeting the first people to discover the healing waters 2,000 years ago
7. **The slowest express in the world** — A child boards the Glacier Express in Brig and, at each stop, steps off into a different century: 1930 (first journey), 1906 (Simplon opening), 1913 (Lötschberg completion)
8. **The church beneath the church** — In Glis, a child discovers a hidden staircase leading down through layers: the Baroque basilica, the Gothic chapel, the Romanesque tower, and finally the tiny early Christian church from 500 AD, where a baptismal font still holds water
