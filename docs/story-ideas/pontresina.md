## 1. Geography — A High Alpine Village at the Foot of the Bernina

- Pontresina lies at **1,805 meters** above sea level in the **Val Bernina**, the highest-altitude side valley branching off the Upper Engadin
- The village sits in the canton of **Graubünden** (Grisons), in southeastern Switzerland, just 6 km from St. Moritz
- The **Bernina massif** rises directly to the south, with the only four-thousander in the Eastern Alps — **Piz Bernina (4,049 m)** — and the iconic **Piz Palü (3,900 m)** dominating the skyline
- The **Roseg valley** (Val Roseg) and the **Morteratsch valley** (Val Morteratsch) extend southward from the village into the glaciated heart of the Bernina group
- Population: approximately **2,100** permanent residents (2024), though the village hosts thousands of guests year-round
- The Upper Engadin is a **high plateau** at roughly 1,800 m, one of the highest permanently inhabited valleys in the Alps, blessed with over **300 days of sunshine** per year
- The valley's language was historically **Romansh** — specifically the **Putèr** dialect of the Upper Engadin

## 2. Name and Origins — The Bridge of Saracenus

- First documented mention: **1137** as "ad Pontem Sarisinam" in medieval Latin records
- Second mention: **1237** as "de Ponte Sarraceno"
- The name combines the Latin **pons** (bridge) with the personal name **Saracenus** — a name widely known in the Engadin valley
- An older theory linked the name to the **Saracens** (medieval term for Arabs), suggesting an early medieval Muslim engineering presence in the Alps; modern scholars consider it more likely derived from a **local person's name** rather than an ethnic group
- Until the 19th century, the entire population spoke the **Putèr dialect** of Upper Engadin Romansh. In 1880, 45.7% still spoke Romansh as their first language; by 1900, this had fallen to 33.6% as tourism brought German speakers
- The village was historically a modest farming and pastoral community, serving as a waypoint for travelers crossing the **Bernina Pass**

## 3. The Spaniola Tower — Medieval Stronghold of the Pontresina Family

- The **Spaniolaturm** (Spaniola Tower) is a well-preserved pentagonal residential tower built around **1210** for the noble **Pontresina family**
- The family was first mentioned in **1244** when they were stripped of the office of chancery over the Upper Engadin by the **Bishop of Chur**
- The tower's unusual **five-sided shape** may have been designed to deflect rockslides or avalanches, with the pointed side facing the mountain
- Built from irregular quarry stone and erratic boulders, the tower still stands **four stories tall** with an original entrance on the second story (a defensive feature requiring a ladder)
- The Pontresina family's fortunes declined rapidly; the last family member is mentioned in **1307**, after which the castle passed to the **Murell family**
- By the **15th century**, the castle was abandoned; by **1550**, chronicler Ulrich Campell recorded it as a ruin
- The tower was repaired and structurally strengthened in **1994** and is now a Pontresina landmark

## 4. The Church of Santa Maria — Medieval Frescoes

- The **Chiesa Santa Maria** is the oldest church in Pontresina and one of the most precious sacred buildings in the **canton of Graubünden**
- The church contains remarkable **Byzantine-Romanesque frescoes** dating from the **1100s** — high-quality fragments that represent some of the oldest painted art in the Engadin
- Three frescoes from approximately **1230** survive on the west wall, depicting the **Epiphany** (the Three Kings visiting the infant Jesus, with Mary at the right)
- After an extension in **1495**, the church was completely repainted with an **informative Lombardian cycle**, which covered the older medieval frescoes
- Between **1962 and 1976**, a painstaking restoration uncovered a **Mary Magdalene cycle consisting of 18 frescoes** on the west wall and tower — hidden for nearly five centuries beneath the 1495 layer
- The first restoration was carried out in **1913** by Christian Schmidt of Zurich, continued later by Charles Lefébure of Brussels
- The church also serves as Pontresina's burial church and cemetery

## 5. The Bernina Pass — Ancient Alpine Crossing

- The **Bernina Pass** (2,328 m) connects the Upper Engadin with the **Val Poschiavo** and the Italian **Valtellina** beyond
- Stone Age **menhirs** found in the pass area indicate it has been used for travel for **thousands of years**
- Unlike the Splügen and Septimer passes, the Bernina was not a major European transit route in the Middle Ages; it gained importance in the early **1500s** when the Three Leagues conquered the **Veltlin** (Valtellina) in **1512**
- In **1410**, communities north and south of the pass agreed to cooperate on path maintenance
- Around **1550**, a constant **French messenger service** between Lyon and Venice used the Albula, Bernina, and Aprica passes
- After a fatal **avalanche** closed the western route in winter, engineer **Rudolf Albertini** of Zuoz led the widening of the eastern road between **1842 and 1865**, including a hospice at 2,307 m
- The pass has been open **year-round** since 1965

## 6. Mountaineering Capital — Piz Bernina, Piz Palü, and the Mountain Guides

- Pontresina became the **mountaineering base camp** of the Engadin from the mid-19th century onward

### Piz Bernina — The Only Four-Thousander in the Eastern Alps
- On **13 September 1850**, topographer **Johann Wilhelm Coaz** (age 28, from S-chanf) and his assistants **Jon and Lorenz Ragut Tscharner** made the first ascent of **Piz Bernina (4,049 m)** via the east ridge
- They departed the Bernina Inn (2,050 m) at 6 a.m. with surveying instruments, crossed the Morteratsch Glacier, and reached the summit around 6 p.m.
- Coaz recorded in his diary: stepping on "soil that no human had trodden upon before"
- The **Spallagrat** (south ridge) was climbed in **1866** by Francis Fox Tuckett and Frederick Augustus Yeats Brown — it became the normal route
- The legendary **Biancograt** (Crast' Alva, "White Ridge") — often called the most beautiful snow ridge in the Alps and nicknamed the **"Himmelsleiter" (Ladder to Heaven)** — was first climbed in **1878** by Paul Güssfeldt with guides Hans Grass and Johann Gross
- First winter ascent: **February 1880** by Cecil Watson with guides Christian Grass senior and Valentin Kessler

### Piz Palü (3,900 m)
- The east summit was climbed as early as **12 August 1835** by naturalist **Oswald Heer** with guides Johann Madutz and Gian Marchet Colani
- The first complete traverse of all three summits came on **22 July 1868**
- The mountain gained worldwide fame through the **1929 film "Die weisse Hölle vom Piz Palü"** (The White Hell of Pitz Palu)

### The Mountain Guide Tradition
- The **Pontresina Mountain Guide Association** was founded in **1871** — the **third mountain guide association in Switzerland**
- In **1963**, six mountain guides established the **Bergsteigerschule Pontresina** (Pontresina Mountaineering School), the **oldest and largest** mountaineering school in Graubünden

## 7. The Morteratsch Glacier — A Retreating Giant

- The **Morteratsch Glacier** is the **largest glacier** in the Bernina Range by area, and the **third largest** in the Eastern Alps by volume (1.2 km³)
- It originates beneath Piz Bernina and Piz Palü, flowing northward into the Val Morteratsch
- In **1860**, the glacier's tongue reached all the way to what is now the **Morteratsch railway station** — visitors stepping off the train stood directly at the ice front
- Since then, the glacier has retreated over **3 kilometers**
- Systematic length measurements have been recorded since **1878**; from 1878 to 1998, the average annual retreat was approximately **17.2 meters**
- In recent decades, the retreat has accelerated dramatically: **30 meters per year** from 1999 to 2005, and **20–50 meters per year** in some recent periods
- The **Morteratsch Glacier Trail** — one of the most popular hikes in Switzerland — follows the 3 km path from the railway station to the current glacier snout, with **16 information stops** and date markers showing where the ice stood in different years
- In **2017**, researchers from Utrecht University began developing an **artificial snowmaking system** to slow the glacier's retreat, estimating that a decade of use might preserve 400–500 meters of length
- Climate models project that under high-emission scenarios, the glacier could lose **80–90% of its volume by 2100**

## 8. The Rhaetian Railway and the Bernina Express — UNESCO World Heritage

- The **Bernina railway line** was built between **1906 and 1910** by thousands of Italian workers under challenging Alpine conditions
- Opening in stages: **Pontresina to Morteratsch** on 1 July 1908; the full line from **St. Moritz to Tirano (Italy)** inaugurated on **5 July 1910**
- The line was **electrified from the start** at 750 V DC, powered by hydroelectric stations on the south side of the pass
- Winter service began in **1913**
- The **Bernina Express** crosses the Bernina Pass at **2,253 meters** — the highest railway crossing in the Alps without a tunnel
- The journey from Chur to Tirano traverses **196 bridges** and **55 tunnels** in approximately 4 hours
- Key engineering marvels: the iconic **Landwasser Viaduct**, helical (spiral) tunnels, and the unique **Brusio circular viaduct** — a 360-degree loop where the train crosses over itself
- In **July 2008**, the Albula and Bernina lines were jointly inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** — recognized as "an outstanding technical, architectural and environmental ensemble" that harmonizes with the Alpine landscapes

## 9. The Ibex Colony — Return of the Alpine King

- The **Alpine ibex** (Steinbock) was hunted to near-extinction across the Alps; by the early 19th century, only about **100 individuals survived** in the Gran Paradiso region of northwest Italy
- King **Victor Emmanuel II** declared Gran Paradiso a royal hunting reserve in **1854**, saving the species
- All Alpine ibex alive today descend from this single surviving Gran Paradiso population
- The first successful **reintroduction of ibex to Switzerland** took place in the **1920s** on the slopes of **Piz Albris (3,166 m)**, directly above Pontresina
- The Albris colony has grown spectacularly to approximately **1,700–1,800 animals** — one of the **largest ibex colonies in the Alpine region**
- Each spring, when the first grass sprouts, ibex migrate down toward the **upper edge of the village**, where they can be observed grazing, basking, and play-fighting at remarkably close range
- Pontresina has embraced its identity as the **"Steinbock-Paradies"** (Ibex Paradise), with an **Ibex Promenade** (1 km trail with 7 information stops), an **Ibex Gallery** at the Languard chairlift station (2,332 m), and a children's **Ibex Playground** at Alp Languard

## 10. The Diavolezza — She-Devil of the Glaciers

- The **Diavolezza** (2,958 m) is a mountain pass and viewing platform southeast of Pontresina, offering one of the most spectacular panoramas in the Alps: **Piz Bernina, Piz Palü, and the Pers and Morteratsch glaciers** spread out below
- The name means **"she-devil"** in Romansh/Italian, from an Engadin legend: a beautiful mountain fairy named Diavolezza enchanted young men, luring them to their deaths in the glacier. A youth named **Aratsch** disappeared into the icefalls of the Pers Glacier; legend says Diavolezza never found peace until the glacier advanced and covered the entire alp with ice and scree
- A cable car was first built to the summit in **1956** (replacing earlier funicular plans from 1954); by **1980**, a larger 125-person cable car was installed
- The Diavolezza is famous for the **longest glacier ski run in Switzerland**, descending over the Pers and Morteratsch glaciers
- The **Glacier Experience Trail** on the summit ridge offers educational stops about glaciology and the Bernina ice fields

## 11. Muottas Muragl and Segantini — Art on the Mountain

### Muottas Muragl
- **Muottas Muragl** (2,456 m) offers an iconic panoramic view over the entire **Upper Engadin lake landscape** and the Bernina massif
- The **funicular railway** was built between **1905 and 1907** by the firm Von Roll — it is the **oldest mountain railway in the Engadin**, with a length of 2,199 m and an altitude gain of 709 m
- The **Romantik Hotel Muottas Muragl**, reopened on 18 December 2010, became the first **plus-energy hotel** in the Alps — producing more energy than it consumes

### Giovanni Segantini
- The Italian painter **Giovanni Segantini** (1858–1899) moved to the Engadin in **1894**, settling in Maloja
- He became the region's most famous artistic interpreter, painting monumental **Alpine landscapes** with a luminous Divisionist technique
- In **1897**, he was commissioned to create a vast panoramic painting of the Engadin for the **1900 Paris World's Fair**, which evolved into his legendary **Alpine Triptych**: "La Vita – La Natura – La Morte" (Life – Nature – Death)
- While working on the middle panel from the **Schafberg** (Munt da la Bês-cha) high above Pontresina, Segantini fell ill with acute **peritonitis** and died on **28 September 1899**, age 41, in the mountain hut now named the **Segantinihütte**
- The hut is reachable in about 90 minutes from Muottas Muragl, and offers the exact panorama depicted in his painting "La Natura"
- The **Segantini Museum** in St. Moritz (opened 1908) houses the world's most comprehensive collection of his work, including the Alpine Triptych

## 12. Engadin Architecture and Romansh Culture

### Sgraffito — The Scratched Art of the Engadin
- **Sgraffito** (from the Italian "sgraffiare" — to scratch) is the distinctive wall decoration technique that gives Engadin villages their unmistakable character
- Italian master builders brought the technique to Graubünden in the **16th century**
- The method: ornaments are etched with styli and knives into a damp layer of plaster covered with light lime, revealing the **darker lime mortar beneath** — creating designs in two tones
- Popular motifs: **geometric patterns, wavy ribbons, rosettes, and mythological figures**
- Sgraffito reached its **heyday in the 17th and 18th centuries** and experienced a revival at the beginning of the 20th century
- Pontresina's **Via Maistra** (main street) features outstanding examples on traditional Engadin houses — massive stone structures with deep-set windows, bay windows, and heavy entrance gates

### Chalandamarz — Driving Out Winter
- **Chalandamarz** is a Romansh spring festival celebrated on **1 March** throughout the Engadin, including in Pontresina
- Children parade through the village with the **largest cowbells** they can carry, cracking whips and singing to **drive away the evil spirits of winter** and awaken the spirits of spring
- The tradition dates to **Roman times**, when the new year began on 1 March and young men of Rhaetia rang bells to mark the occasion
- The festival became known internationally through the children's book **"Schellen-Ursli" (A Bell for Ursli)** by Selina Chönz and Alois Carigiet (1945)

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The glacier that remembers** — A child follows the 16 date markers along the Morteratsch Glacier Trail; at each sign, the glacier whispers memories of what it looked like in that year — 1900, 1930, 1960 — until the child reaches the ice and makes a promise to help it endure
2. **The Biancograt dare** — Young Johann Coaz (age 28) bets his surveying companions they can reach a summit no one has climbed; told from the perspective of his assistant Lorenz, who nearly slips on the Morteratsch Glacier on that September day in 1850
3. **The ibex who came home** — An ibex born in a zoo in the lowlands is released on Piz Albris in the 1920s; frightened at first, it must learn to trust the mountains and find its herd — a story of belonging and wildness reclaimed
4. **Diavolezza's secret** — A child riding the cable car hears the legend of the beautiful she-devil who enchanted young men; exploring the glacier ridge, the child discovers that Diavolezza wasn't evil — she was the mountain's guardian, warning people away from crevasses
5. **The painter's last mountain** — Segantini climbs to the Schafberg above Pontresina to paint the Engadin one final time; a child from the village brings him supplies and watches as the painter captures light, clouds, and eternity on canvas before the mountain claims him
6. **The scratched house** — A child discovers that the sgraffito decorations on Pontresina's old houses are actually a secret code; following the patterns from house to house along the Via Maistra, they decode a message left by a 17th-century Italian master builder
7. **Bells for spring** — A shy child in Pontresina must find the biggest cowbell in the village for the Chalandamarz parade on 1 March; the quest leads through old Engadin houses, the Spaniola Tower, and up to the ibex on Piz Albris — arriving just in time to ring in spring
8. **The train that crossed the sky** — In 1910, a child rides the very first Bernina Express from Pontresina over the pass to Tirano; at each of the 196 bridges and 55 tunnels, the train reveals a different world — from glaciers and ibex at the top to palm trees and Italian gelato at the bottom
