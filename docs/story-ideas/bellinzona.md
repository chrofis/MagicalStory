## 1. Geography — The Gateway to the Alps (Porta delle Alpi)

- Bellinzona sits at an elevation of **240 metres** in the valley of the **Ticino river** (Fiume Ticino), surrounded by the southern ranges of the **Lepontine Alps**
- Its position commands a narrow valley passage — barely **one kilometre wide** — where the Ticino valley can be completely sealed off, making it one of the most strategically important sites in the entire Alpine arc
- The town controls access to **four major Alpine passes**: the **Gotthard** (Passo del San Gottardo), **San Bernardino**, **Lukmanier** (Passo del Lucomagno), and **Nufenen** (Passo della Novena)
- This position earned Bellinzona the title **"Porta delle Alpi"** (Gateway to the Alps) — whoever held Bellinzona controlled north-south traffic across the central Alps
- Canton **Ticino** is the only Swiss canton where **Italian** is the sole official language; the culture, cuisine, and architecture are deeply **Lombard-Italian**, with a Mediterranean climate protected from northern weather by the Alps
- The Ticino river flows southeast through the Leventina valley from the Gotthard, receiving tributaries from the Blenio and Mesolcina valleys before curving west above Bellinzona to enter **Lake Maggiore** (Lago Maggiore)

## 2. Ancient Origins — From Neolithic Settlement to Roman Fortress

- The **rocky hill** on which Castelgrande stands has been inhabited for over **6,000 years**
- **Archaeological excavations (1984-85)** unearthed evidence of **Neolithic settlements** dating to **5500-5000 BC** — making it one of the oldest continuously occupied sites in Switzerland
- The site was repeatedly inhabited during the **Bronze Age** and **Iron Age**
- Emperor **Augustus** brought the region into the Roman Empire, and the Romans established a **basecamp** on the Castelgrande hill around **1 AD**
- In the **4th century AD**, the Romans **refortified** and greatly expanded the site with a large wall and gate, housing approximately **1,000 soldiers** at its peak
- The first documentary mention of the town: **590 AD**, recorded in Latin as **"Belitio"** or **"Bilitio"** by the Frankish historian **Gregory of Tours**
- The name is **Lepontic** (pre-Roman Celtic) in origin, possibly from *belitio* meaning "juniper" — the town's name evolved through medieval variants: Berinzona (721), Beliciona (901), Belinzona (1055)

## 3. Castelgrande — The Oldest and Largest Castle (Castello Vecchio)

- **Castelgrande** dominates Bellinzona from a rocky peak overlooking the entire Ticino valley — the oldest of the three castles and the largest fortified complex
- Historical names: **Castello Vecchio** (Old Castle), then **Castello di Uri** (1506, under Swiss rule), then **Castello di San Michele** (1818, after Ticino's independence)
- The present medieval fortifications date primarily to the **13th-15th centuries**, with a major Milanese building phase during **1473-1486**
- Two prominent towers define the skyline:
  - **Torre Bianca** (White Tower): **27 metres** tall, dating to the **13th century** (ca. 1250-1350)
  - **Torre Nera** (Black Tower): **28 metres** tall, dating to **1310** (14th century)
- During peacetime, a **castellano** (castellan) and at least **twenty infantrymen** guarded the fortress; entry required **a document with a ducal seal**
- The south wing today houses an archaeological-historical museum covering **"6,500 years of human presence on the hill"** — from the first Neolithic village to the 20th century

### Aurelio Galfetti's Renovation (1984-1991)
- Swiss architect **Aurelio Galfetti** (1936-2021) — one of Ticino's foremost 20th-century architects alongside Mario Botta, Luigi Snozzi, and Livio Vacchini — directed the castle's modern restoration
- Galfetti's philosophy: **"Conservare = Trasformare"** (Preserve = Transform) — believing restoration should continue historical layering, not freeze a structure in time
- He carved a **modern entrance** into the hillside at **Piazza del Sole**: a wedge-shaped gap in rock leading to a concrete-lined rotunda, from which **lifts transport visitors 40 metres up** to the castle
- The intervention juxtaposed **brutalist concrete** geometry against medieval stone walls, cleared overgrowth to emphasize the fortified character, and created new public courtyards — giving the restoration "an urban dimension"

## 4. Castello di Montebello — The Middle Castle

- **Montebello** sits approximately **90 metres** above Bellinzona, within sight of Castelgrande
- Historical names: **Castello Piccolo** (Small Castle) or **Castello di Mezzo** (Middle Castle), then **Castello di Svitto** (Schwyz Castle, 1506), then **Castello di San Martino** (1818)
- Most probably built by the **Rusca family of Como** in the **13th-14th centuries** during their conflicts with the Visconti of Milan (**1284, 1292, 1303** sieges)
- The Rusca retained Montebello even after losing Como (1335) and Castelgrande (1340) to the Visconti
- External fortifications were expanded by **Sforza engineers** in the second half of the **15th century**
- Acquired by **Canton Ticino** in **1903** and restored between **1902-1910**
- Today houses the **Museo Civico** with an extensive collection of archaeological artefacts from the Bellinzona region

## 5. Castello di Sasso Corbaro — Built in Six Months

- **Sasso Corbaro** perches **230 metres** above the city on an isolated rocky promontory — the smallest and highest of the three castles
- Historical names: **Castello di Unterwalden** (1506, under Swiss rule), then **Castello di Santa Barbara** (1818)
- The only castle with a **precise construction date**: built in **1479**, immediately after the **Battle of Giornico** (28 December 1478), where roughly **600 Swiss troops** ambushed and routed a larger Milanese army in a narrow icy valley by rolling boulders down the hillside
- Alarmed by this defeat, Duke **Gian Galeazzo Sforza** commissioned Florentine military engineer **Benedetto Ferrini** to complete the defensive barrier
- Ferrini designed and built the castle in the **incredibly brief time of six months** — then **died of plague** shortly before its completion
- Features massive **walls up to 7 metres thick** and a square courtyard design optimized for defence

## 6. The Murata — A Wall Across the Valley (La Murata)

- **La Murata** is the fortified defensive wall that once **sealed off the entire Ticino valley** from west to east, connected to Castelgrande's western flank
- During the second half of the **14th century**, after the Visconti conquest of 1340, a long wall was built across the valley floor — approximately **one kilometre wide** at Bellinzona
- The wall allowed Milan to **control and tax commercial traffic** heading to and from the St. Gotthard Pass and regional fairs; a dedicated **castellano** oversaw operations to prevent unauthorized military or smuggling passage
- The Murata featured **dual walls** creating two passageways: a covered passage approximately **4 metres high** and an upper external walkway with **battlements and embrasures** (merlature e feritoie) — approximately **800 metres** in total length
- Significantly reinforced and expanded during the **1480s** under the Sforza dynasty
- In **1489**, Duke **Ludovico il Moro** Sforza ordered construction of the **Ponte della Torretta** to extend the barrier from Castelgrande across the Ticino river to the right bank
- The ensemble is the **only surviving example** in the entire Alpine arc of medieval military architecture comprising multiple castles linked by a wall that closed off an entire valley — this uniqueness was central to the **UNESCO inscription in 2000**

## 7. Visconti, Sforza, and the Swiss Conquest (1340-1503)

- **1340**: The **Visconti family** of Milan seized Castelgrande from the Rusca family, beginning nearly **160 years** of Milanese domination
- **1396**: King **Wenceslaus** granted the Visconti formal feudal rights over Bellinzona
- **1447**: Death of the last Visconti duke, **Filippo Maria**, without a male heir — brief **Ambrosian Republic** (Repubblica Ambrosiana, 1447-1450), then **Francesco Sforza** became Duke of Milan
- **1478**: **Battle of Giornico** (Battaglia di Giornico) — Swiss troops defeated a Milanese army; Sforza response was the rapid construction of Sasso Corbaro (1479)
- **1499**: King **Louis XII of France**, claiming Visconti descent, invaded Milan and seized Bellinzona, garrisoning **1,000 troops** in Castelgrande
- **Winter 1499-1500**: Armed **revolt of Bellinzona's citizens** drove out the French
- **14 April 1500**: Seeking protection from France, Bellinzona joined the **Swiss Confederation** as a **condominium** (territorio comune) under the joint administration of **Uri, Schwyz, and Nidwalden** (Unterwalden)
- Each canton received one castle: Uri got Castelgrande, Schwyz got Montebello, Unterwalden got Sasso Corbaro — hence the castles' Swiss-era names
- **1503**: The **Treaty of Arona** (Trattato di Arona) between France and the Confederation confirmed Swiss control of Bellinzona, the Leventina, and the Blenio valley
- **1516**: A further treaty definitively affirmed Swiss sovereignty — Bellinzona and its castles have remained Swiss ever since

## 8. Capital of Canton Ticino (Capitale del Cantone)

- **1798**: During the Napoleonic **Helvetic Republic**, Bellinzona became capital of the short-lived Canton of Bellinzona
- **1803**: Under Napoleon's **Act of Mediation**, the modern **Canton Ticino** was formed, with a rotating capital system: **Bellinzona, Lugano, and Locarno** each served as capital for **six years** in rotation, established by the **Constitution of 1814**
- **1878**: Bellinzona became the **permanent sole capital** of Canton Ticino, ending 65 years of rotation
- **1882**: Opening of the **Gotthard Railway** (Ferrovia del Gottardo) spurred economic growth and tourism
- **1 April 2004**: The **Federal Criminal Court** (Tribunale penale federale) was established in Bellinzona — one of Switzerland's federal courts, handling cases of organized crime, money laundering, terrorism, and offences against federal interests
- **30 November 2000**: The three castles, the Murata, and the town ramparts were inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site**

## 9. The Collegiata and Piazza Collegiata

- The **Collegiata dei Santi Pietro e Stefano** (Collegiate Church of Saints Peter and Stephen) stands on the **Piazza Collegiata**, Bellinzona's most popular square
- Designed by **Master Tomaso Rodari of Maroggia** — the same architect-sculptor who created **Como Cathedral** (Duomo di Como)
- Constructed **1515-1543** in the **Renaissance** style
- The white stone facade uses stone from quarries in nearby **Castione**; the portal bears the **town's coat of arms** and is flanked by two smaller 15th-century doors
- Above the portal: a magnificent **twelve-segment rose window** (rosone), dating from the late 16th to early 17th century, measuring **five metres in diameter**
- Interior: a **single nave** (navata unica) with two rows of side chapels, richly decorated with **17th-century stucco**
- Notable artworks include paintings by **Pier Francesco Mazzuchelli**, **Camillo Procaccini**, and **Bartolomeo Roverio** ("Il Genovesino")
- The apse altarpiece, a **Crucifixion**, is attributed to **Simone Peterzano** of Bergamo — historically noted as the **teacher of Caravaggio**
- The fresco **"Angeli musicanti"** (Musical Angels) by **Giuseppe Felice Orelli** of Locarno dates to ca. **1770**
- The **Fontana Trivulziana** (holy water stoup) once belonged to the 15th-century nobleman **Gian Giacomo Trivulzio**

## 10. The Carnival of Bellinzona — Rabadan

- **Rabadan** is Bellinzona's carnival and one of the **largest in Switzerland** (after Basel's Fasnacht)
- Officially founded in **1862**; the name "Rabadan" first appeared in **1874**
- The word **"Rabadan"** comes from the **Milanese/Lombard dialect**, meaning **"noise"** or "racket" — possibly linked etymologically to Crusaders' encounters with Ramadan nighttime celebrations
- Draws approximately **150,000 visitors** over five days of celebration
- Key traditions:
  - **Giovedì Grasso** (Fat Thursday / "Dirty Thursday"): The mayor of Bellinzona hands the **symbolic key to the city** (chiave della città) to **Re Rabadan** (King Rabadan), elected by the local carnival society
  - **Friday**: The **children's procession** (corteo dei bambini) through the old town
  - **Sunday**: The **Corteo** — the carnival's highlight parade, featuring around **fifty floats** and **Guggenmusik** brass bands from across Ticino and German-speaking Switzerland
  - **Martedì Grasso** (Fat Tuesday / Mardi Gras): The final night of celebration
- Festivities include mask contests, tug-of-war competitions, street theatre, and servings of **risotto** in carnival tents until the early morning hours

## 11. The Saturday Market and Villa dei Cedri

### Mercato del Sabato (Saturday Market)
- Bellinzona's **Saturday market** dates back to **1878** and takes place **every Saturday, year-round**
- Stretches from **Piazza Nosetto** through the narrow streets and alleyways of the **old town** (centro storico)
- Approximately **120 stalls**, about half offering traditional Ticino food: **artisanal salumi** (cured meats), **local cheeses** from the valleys, **Ticino bread**, sun-ripened fruit, and regional specialties
- The other stalls sell crafts, antiques, clothing, and household goods — some artisans build their wares on the spot for visitors to watch
- Attracts thousands of visitors every week and is considered one of the **finest traditional markets in Ticino**

### Villa dei Cedri (Museo Villa dei Cedri)
- A **neoclassical suburban villa** built around **1860**, reworked in **Palladian** style around **1931** by Milanese architect **Nelusco Mario Antoniazzi** for banker **Arrigo Stoffel**, who added a loggia, veranda, and belvedere tower
- Acquired by the **City of Bellinzona** in **1978** and opened as a civic art gallery (pinacoteca civica) in **1985**
- The permanent collection focuses on art from **Ticino, Lombardy, and Switzerland** from the second half of the 19th century to the present — with a particular strength in **works on paper** (two-thirds of the collection)
- Set in a **19th-century English-style park** (parco all'inglese) with a **vineyard** behind the villa that produces the museum's own **Merlot** wine

## 12. The Buzza di Biasca — A Catastrophe That Shaped the Valley (1515)

- On **30 September 1513**, a gigantic rockslide detached from the western flank of **Pizzo Magno** (2,329 m), burying houses and creating a **dam of boulders over 60 metres high** that blocked the entrance to the **Blenio valley** and dammed the **Brenno river**
- On **20 May 1515**, the dam catastrophically burst — the **Buzza di Biasca** (from Lombard *buzza*, "burst") sent enormous flood waves down through the Riviera to Bellinzona and on to **Lake Maggiore**
- In Bellinzona alone, over **100 people died** (including 20 Swiss soldiers), and the tidal wave destroyed approximately **400 buildings**
- The **Murata wall** and the **Ponte della Torretta** temporarily withstood the water pressure before bursting, causing the water to back up and flood the town even more severely
- Total casualties across the valley: between **200 and 600 people** — it was one of the greatest natural disasters in Swiss history
- The destroyed bridge near Bellinzona was not rebuilt until **1813** — nearly 300 years later

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The castle that grew in six months** — Benedetto Ferrini races against time (and plague) to build Sasso Corbaro before the Swiss return; told from his apprentice's perspective as they haul stones up the 230-metre hill
2. **The key to the city** — A child discovers the real golden key of Bellinzona hidden in one of the three castles; each castle holds a riddle linked to its Swiss-era name (Uri, Schwyz, Unterwalden)
3. **The wall across the valley** — A merchant child must find a way through the Murata to deliver goods to a fair, learning how the wall controlled an entire valley and all the passes beyond
4. **When the mountain fell** — The Buzza di Biasca: a child living in Bellinzona in 1515 feels the ground shake when the dam bursts and must escape to the highest castle (Sasso Corbaro) as floodwaters surge through the town
5. **The rose window's secret** — A beam of light through the five-metre rosone of the Collegiata illuminates a hidden fresco by Peterzano (Caravaggio's teacher); the child follows the light through Piazza Collegiata across different centuries
6. **Re Rabadan for a day** — A shy child accidentally becomes King Rabadan and must lead the carnival parade; along the way, learns the meaning of "noise" and community through mask-making, risotto-cooking, and Guggenmusik
7. **The towers that talk** — Torre Bianca and Torre Nera of Castelgrande argue about who is taller (27 vs. 28 metres) and who has seen more history (13th vs. 14th century); a child mediates by showing them what they've witnessed together over 700 years
8. **The lift inside the mountain** — A child enters the mysterious entrance carved into the rock at Piazza del Sole (Galfetti's modern passageway) and rides the elevator 40 metres up through 6,000 years of history — each level revealing a different era of the hill's inhabitants, from Neolithic farmers to Roman soldiers to Sforza dukes
