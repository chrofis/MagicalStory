## 1. Geography — At the Foot of the Rothorn

- **Brienz** is a village and municipality on the **northern shore of Lake Brienz** (Brienzersee), at the eastern end of the lake, in the **Bernese Oberland** region of the Canton of Bern
- It sits at approximately **566 metres** above sea level, at the foot of the **Brienzer Rothorn** (2,350 m), the highest peak in the Emmental Alps
- The municipality covers **47.99 km²**, with a population of approximately **3,250** residents
- Brienz lies in the **Interlaken-Oberhasli** administrative district, roughly **20 km** east of Interlaken along the lake
- The village is nestled between the turquoise lake to the south and steep, forested mountain slopes to the north — a compact settlement of dark-timbered chalets lining the lakeshore
- The **Aare** river enters Lake Brienz at the eastern end near Brienz, having flowed from the Haslital valley through Meiringen

## 2. Early History — From the Celts to Bernese Rule

- The area around Brienz has been inhabited since the **Neolithic** and **Bronze Ages**
- In the **5th century BC**, the **Celts** (Helvetii) settled in the alpine valleys of the region
- At the end of the **1st century BC**, the Romans conquered the area; Roman settlements were later destroyed by the **Alamanni** around **259/260 AD**
- The **Alamanni** eventually settled permanently around **450 AD**, with archaeological evidence of a settlement from the **7th century**
- The first documented mention of Brienz is in **1146**, recorded as **"Briens"**
- Under **Habsburg** dominion during the High Middle Ages, the economy relied primarily on **cattle herding, fishing, and forestry**
- Following regional conflicts, Brienz was integrated into **Bernese authority** around **1528**, becoming part of the Oberland bailiwicks
- The **Reformed Church** of Brienz stands on a site of much older origins; the village's oldest church foundations date back centuries before the Reformation

## 3. Lake Brienz (Brienzersee) — The Turquoise Gem

- **Lake Brienz** is a glacial lake in the Bernese Oberland with famously **intense turquoise** water — among the most striking lake colours in Switzerland
- Dimensions: approximately **14 km** long, **2.8 km** wide, surface area **29.8 km²**, maximum depth **260 metres**, surface elevation **564 m** above sea level
- The striking colour comes from **glacial flour** — tiny rock particles ground by glaciers and washed into the lake by the **Aare** and the **Lütschine** rivers; these particles remain suspended and scatter light, producing the characteristic turquoise
- The lake was formed by the **Aare Glacier** during the Pleistocene ice ages, which carved an overdeepened basin into the limestone
- Approximately **10,000 years ago**, Lake Brienz and **Lake Thun** (Thunersee) were a single body of water called the **Wendelsee**; over millennia, sediment from the **Lütschine** and **Lombach** rivers built up the **Bödeli** (the plain where Interlaken sits), splitting the giant lake in two — the separation was complete by roughly the **10th century**
- The **Aare** enters at the eastern end (near Brienz) and exits at the western end (near Interlaken), connecting via a short river stretch to Lake Thun
- Other tributaries include the **Giessbach** on the southern shore and the **Lütschine** (both the Schwarze and Weisse Lütschine from Grindelwald and Lauterbrunnen) at the southwestern corner

## 4. The Wood Carving Tradition — Switzerland's Carving Capital

- Brienz is known as Switzerland's undisputed **"village of carvers"** (Schnitzlerdorf), a tradition stretching back over **200 years**
- The craft began during the **hunger years of 1816–1817** (following the eruption of Mount Tambora in 1815 and the resulting "Year Without a Summer"), when wood turner **Christian Fischer** (1790–1848) began decorating everyday objects with carvings and selling them to the growing number of tourists visiting the Bernese Oberland
- To meet rising demand, Fischer **taught the skill** to others in the region — within one generation, wood carving had become a thriving economic sector
- By **1843**, there were approximately **300 woodcarvers** working in and around Brienz
- Production took place in home workshops called **"Bbudiigg"** — small rooms where entire families carved figures, animals, ornamental brackets, and souvenir items
- In **1851**, Brienz woodcarving achieved **international recognition** at the **Great Exhibition in London** (the Crystal Palace exposition)
- By around **1880**, approximately **2,000 woodcarvers** are believed to have worked across the Berner Oberland region
- Major enterprises shaped the industry: **Ed. Jobin Inc.** (founded 1835) became the largest producer with around 200 employees and branches in France and Germany; **Huggler-Wyss Inc.** (1900) became known for sophisticated figure carvings and nativity sets; **Albert Schild Inc.** (1898) specialized in toys and souvenirs
- The early 20th century brought decline: economic depression and changing tastes in design caused severe hardship for woodcarvers
- After **1974**, a renaissance began as younger generations rediscovered the craft, drawn by appreciation for nature and traditional skills

### The Schule für Holzbildhauerei (Woodcarving School)

- Founded in **1884**, the **Schule für Holzbildhauerei** is the only institution in Switzerland where the art of wood carving can be learned as a vocation
- In **1928**, it became an official educational institution and training workshop of the **Canton of Bern**
- Since **2009**, the school has expanded to include vocational training for **woodturners, basket and wickerwork designers, coopers, and white-wood coopers**
- Today it awards the **Federal Certificate of Competence (EFZ)** in five specialisations, with apprentices attending four two-week teaching blocks annually in Brienz
- The **Schweizer Holzbildhauerei Museum** (Swiss Woodcarving Museum), founded in **2009** by the Association for the Collection and Exhibition of Brienz Woodcarvings, documents the craft's 200-year history

## 5. The Brienz Rothorn Railway — Steam on the Mountain

- The **Brienz Rothorn Bahn** (BRB) is the only regularly operating **steam-powered cog railway** in Switzerland — and one of the last in Europe
- **7.6 km** long, built to **800 mm gauge**, using the **Abt rack system** (double lamella), climbing from Brienz (566 m) to the summit station at **2,244 m** — a height difference of approximately **1,678 metres**
- The initiative came from citizens of Brienz, led by manufacturer **Karl Brück** and engineer **Alexander Lindner**, who submitted a concession application on **15 October 1889**
- Approval came just **65 days** later; construction contractor **Theodor Bertschinger** from Lenzburg mobilised approximately **700 workers** (primarily from Italy) who completed the line in **16 months** at a total cost of **2.2 million Swiss francs**
- The railway opened on **16 June 1892** (some sources cite 17 June)
- Mountain railway pioneer **Roman Abt** was responsible for equipping the line with his newly developed rack system

### Financial Struggles and Survival

- The line was designed for **25,000 passengers per year** but attracted only **5,000** in its first year
- Wars in South Africa and China, high coal prices, and the lack of a rail link between Brienz and Interlaken worsened the situation
- A **public limited company** was founded in **1900** to provide new capital
- **World War I** devastated operations, and the railway was **mothballed** for years
- Operations resumed in **June 1931** with renewed capital and community support
- In **1958**, a general meeting voted to **demolish** the railway, but community opposition saved it
- Crucially, the wartime closure is the reason the BRB was **never electrified** — while all other Swiss mountain railways modernised during this period, the BRB slept through the electrification era

### The Steam Fleet

- The railway owns **11 steam locomotives** and **3 diesel locomotives**; eight steam engines are in regular service
- **Five original locomotives** from **1891/92** survive — of these, Nos. **2** and **5** are still in regular operation, making them among the **oldest continuously operating steam locomotives** in scheduled service anywhere in the world
- Each original engine consumes **300 kg of coal** and **2,000 litres of water** per round trip, carrying up to 40 passengers with a 3-person crew
- Modern oil-fired steam locomotives (Nos. 12, 14, 15, 16) were added between **1992 and 2004**
- In August **2024**, severe thunderstorms destroyed **2.2 km of track**, causing an estimated **5 million francs** in damage

## 6. The Giessbach Falls and the Grand Hotel

### The Waterfall

- The **Giessbachfälle** are among Switzerland's most dramatic waterfalls: the Giessbach stream plunges approximately **500 metres** through **14 cascades** before reaching Lake Brienz on the southern shore
- The water originates from the **Faulhorn–Sagis** area and descends through alpine pastures and forest — visitors can walk behind the water curtain on a section of the path
- The falls were made accessible around **1817** by **Johannes Kehrli** (1773–1854), a schoolmaster from Brienz who created a footpath and offered hospitality to visitors
- Kehrli built a simple wooden refreshment house in **1822**; young women, including the locally famous **Elisabetha Grossmann** (the "beautiful boatwoman"), ferried guests across the lake

### The Grand Hotel Giessbach

- **Conrad von Rappard**, a former Frankfurt parliament member who had fled after the 1848 revolution, purchased the land for **70,000 francs** and opened the first hotel on **1 July 1857**
- In **1875**, the **Hauser** hotelier family acquired the property and commissioned Bernese architect **Horace Edouard Davinet** to design a grand hotel in **French Baroque palace** style, completed around **1875**
- In **1879**, engineer **Roman Abt** built the **Giessbachbahn** — a funicular railway from the boat landing to the hotel, the **oldest tourist funicular in Europe** still in operation; originally powered by water ballast, electrified in **1948**
- By **1914**, the Giessbach had become a meeting point for European **royalty, diplomats, statesmen, and celebrated artists** — a pinnacle of Belle Epoque tourism
- The hotel declined through two World Wars and changing ownership; it closed in **1979** and faced demolition — the owners planned to replace it with a modern "Jumbo Chalet" apartment complex

### The Rescue Campaign

- Environmentalist **Franz Weber** and his wife **Judith**, working with the **Fondation Franz Weber** and **Helvetia Nostra**, launched the foundation **"Giessbach dem Schweizervolk"** (Giessbach for the Swiss People) in **1983**
- An unprecedented public fundraising campaign raised **3 million Swiss francs** from over **80,000 donors** to purchase the **22-hectare estate** and register it as a protected monument
- The hotel reopened provisionally in **June 1984** and definitively in the **summer of 1989** after five winters of restoration
- Today the **Grandhotel Giessbach** operates under the Stiftung Giessbach, a landmark of Swiss heritage preservation

## 7. The Ballenberg Open-Air Museum

- The **Freilichtmuseum Ballenberg** (Ballenberg Swiss Open-Air Museum) is Switzerland's only open-air museum, located in **Hofstetten bei Brienz**, just east of Brienz village
- In **1963**, the Swiss Federal Council appointed an expert commission to evaluate creating a national open-air museum, following the Scandinavian model (Stockholm's Skansen, founded 1891)
- The **Swiss Open-Air Museum Foundation Ballenberg** was established in **1968**
- The museum opened on a **66-hectare** site in **1978** with **16 buildings**; by **1980** there were 25; by **1985**, 61; today, over **100 historic buildings** from across all regions of Switzerland
- Buildings are **dismantled at their original locations** — stone by stone, beam by beam — and reconstructed on site, arranged by Swiss region
- The oldest building: a **residential dwelling from 1336** from Schwyz (inaugurated at Ballenberg in 1997); the largest: a **Ticino farmstead with over 50 rooms** (added 2003)
- Over **200 native farm animals** populate the grounds, and traditional crafts are demonstrated daily: **basket-weaving, blacksmithing, spinning, weaving, cheese-making, and wood carving**
- Annual visitors experience a living panorama of Swiss rural life from the **14th to 19th centuries**

## 8. The Swiss Violin Making School (Geigenbauschule)

- The **Schweizer Geigenbauschule Brienz** (Swiss School of Violin Making) is one of only a handful of dedicated violin-making schools in the world
- Founded on **1 July 1944** through the **"Association of Friends of the Swiss Violin"** (established 1943), initiated by **Prof. Dr. Heinrich Hanselmann** (University of Zurich professor and collector of over 200 Swiss violins) and **Dr. Max Gafner** (Bern Government Councillor)
- The school was conceived during the economic crisis of the first half of the 20th century: tourism had collapsed, many woodcarvers were unemployed, and the founders believed violin making could offer **new economic potential** for the region by building on existing woodworking skills
- **Adolf König** (1908–2000) served as the first principal from **1944 to 1973**
- Early conditions were precarious — "very basic working conditions and a bleak financial situation" — but prospects improved in the **1950s**
- In **1952**, the school was transferred to **state operation** under the Canton of Bern, housed in the "Schnätzi" school building alongside the woodcarving school
- In **1982**, the school moved to its current location at **Oberdorfstrasse 94**
- In **1996**, the Canton threatened closure; a private committee formed to save the school, leading to the establishment of the **Brienz Violin Making School Foundation** in **2000**
- A major building renovation was completed in **2016–2018**
- Today the school operates as an independent foundation, training students in the complete art of building stringed instruments by hand

## 9. The Aare Gorge (Aareschlucht) — Nearby

- The **Aareschlucht** lies approximately **15 km** east of Brienz, near **Meiringen** in the Haslital
- The gorge cuts through the **Kirchet**, a formation of extremely hard **Cretaceous limestone** deposited **130–160 million years ago** as ocean sediment in the ancient **Tethys Sea**
- The Swiss Alps uplift occurred approximately **15 million years ago**; beginning **1–2 million years ago**, repeated glacial and interglacial cycles carved gorges through the limestone
- The current gorge formed approximately **10,000 years ago** as torrential meltwater from retreating glaciers eroded a deep, narrow chasm
- Geologist **Franz Muller** identified **five earlier gorges** (completely or partially filled with moraine) in addition to the current one — each carved during a different interglacial period
- Dimensions: approximately **1,400 metres** long, with sheer cliffs up to **50 metres** high and narrows as tight as **1 metre** across
- Walking paths were built in **1887** and the gorge has been open to tourists since **1889**
- From **1912 to 1956**, a tramway linked the gorge's western entrance to Meiringen

## 10. The 2005 Flooding Disaster

- On the night of **22–23 August 2005**, extreme rainfall triggered catastrophic **debris flows** from two mountain torrents — the **Glyssibach** and the **Trachtbach** — that struck Brienz and neighbouring Schwanden bei Brienz
- Warning sirens sounded at **11:30 pm** on 22 August; a wall of mud and boulders **up to eight metres high** swept through the village
- A second debris flow followed within **two hours**
- **Two women were killed**; **eight houses were completely destroyed**; **thirteen more were damaged**, some buried under up to **three metres** of mud
- Approximately **400 residents** were evacuated in the night
- A **250-person team** — including army personnel, emergency services, and volunteers — worked around the clock to clear mud and rubble
- The event was part of the broader **August 2005 European floods**, described as the **most destructive natural event recorded in Switzerland** since systematic recording began in 1972, with total damage across the country estimated at **3 billion Swiss francs** and six lives lost nationwide
- The disaster led to significant investments in **flood protection infrastructure** in Brienz and across the Bernese Oberland

## 11. The Brienzer Rothorn Summit and Alpine Panorama

- From the summit of the **Brienzer Rothorn** (2,350 m), reached by the steam railway, visitors enjoy one of the most celebrated **360-degree panoramas** in the Bernese Oberland
- To the south: the full chain of the **Bernese Alps** — the **Eiger** (3,967 m), **Monch** (4,107 m), and **Jungfrau** (4,158 m), as well as the **Blumlisalp** massif (3,661 m)
- To the north and east: views across the **Emmental** foothills, the **Haslital** valley, and on clear days as far as the **Black Forest** in Germany
- Below: the entire length of the **turquoise Brienzersee** and the village of Brienz at the foot of the mountain
- The summit features two restaurants and the **Rothorn Kulm** mountain lodge
- The journey by steam train takes approximately **one hour** each way, passing through alpine meadows, forests, and increasingly dramatic terrain

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The carver in the hunger year** — In the famine winter of 1816, young Christian Fischer discovers he can trade carved wooden animals for bread; a child helps him carve a bear that brings luck to the village and launches a 200-year tradition
2. **The little steam engine that refused to die** — In 1958, when the railway faces demolition, a child befriends the oldest locomotive (No. 2, built 1891) and rallies the village to save it; the engine puffs back to life and carries its first passengers in years up through the clouds to the Rothorn summit
3. **The turquoise secret** — A child wonders why Lake Brienz glows turquoise while other lakes are blue; a journey upstream with a tiny glacier sprite reveals how ancient ice grinds mountains into flour that colours the water — visiting the Aare, the Lutschine, and the lake's hidden depths
4. **The waterfall hotel** — In 1983, when the Grand Hotel Giessbach is about to be demolished, a child discovers that the 14 cascades of the Giessbach Falls each hold a memory from the hotel's golden age; collecting all 14 memories convinces 80,000 people to save the hotel
5. **The house that moved** — A centuries-old farmhouse from the Valais learns it will be demolished; instead, it is carefully dismantled beam by beam and rebuilt at Ballenberg, where it meets houses from every corner of Switzerland and discovers it is part of a much larger family
6. **The violin and the wood** — A young apprentice at the Geigenbauschule must find the perfect piece of spruce for her first violin; she travels to the forests above Brienz where the trees sing in the wind, and the oldest tree offers a branch — but only if she can hear its song
7. **The night the mountain moved** — On the night of the 2005 floods, a child is evacuated as mud engulfs the village; guided by the sound of the church bells and the lights of rescue workers, the child discovers that even after the worst disaster, a community rebuilds stronger than before
8. **Steam, sparks, and 700 workers** — It is 1891 and 700 Italian workers are building an impossible railway up the steepest mountain; a local child befriends a young Italian stonemason, and together they witness the first locomotive push its way to the clouds on opening day
