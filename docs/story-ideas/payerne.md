## 1. Geography — The Broye Valley

- Payerne lies in the broad, fertile **Broye Valley** (vallée de la Broye), in the canton of **Vaud**, at an elevation of **456 meters** (1,496 ft)
- The town sits on the banks of the **Broye** (la Broye), a **68 km** river that rises at **Semsales** in the Fribourg Pre-Alps, flows through the agricultural plain, enters **Lake Morat** (lac de Morat), and continues via the **Broye Canal** into **Lake Neuchâtel** (lac de Neuchâtel)
- Around Payerne the valley opens into a **wide, intensively cultivated plain** — historically one of the key agricultural zones of western Switzerland, with tobacco cultivation once strongly established
- The town sits roughly **halfway between Bern and Lausanne**, making it a natural crossroads between French-speaking and German-speaking Switzerland
- Population: approximately **10,725** (2024); area: **24.18 km²**
- Payerne was the capital of the **Payerne District** until 2006, when it became part of the new **district of Broye-Vully** (district de la Broye-Vully)
- The demonym for inhabitants is **les Payernois**

## 2. Early History — Bishop Marius and the First Church

- The earliest recorded history of Payerne dates to the **6th century**: in **587 AD**, **Bishop Marius** of Aventicum (today Avenches, just 6 km away) built a **villa** (a large country estate) and a **chapel dedicated to St. Mary** (Sainte-Marie) at a place called **Paterniacum** — the Latin origin of the name Payerne
- Archaeological evidence goes much further back: **Neolithic stone tools**, **Bronze Age** artifacts, and remarkable **gold objects from the Hallstatt period** have been found at Le Bois de Roverex near Payerne
- The first written mention of the name Payerne appears in **961** (as *ecclesie sancte Marie Paterniacensis*), though this record survives only in a 12th-century copy
- The strategic position in the Broye Valley, on the road linking the former Roman capital **Aventicum** to the Burgundian heartlands, ensured Payerne's early importance

## 3. Queen Bertha — La Bonne Reine Berthe

- **Bertha of Swabia** (Berthe de Souabe, c. **907** – after 2 January **966**) was queen of Burgundy from 922 and queen of Italy from 922 to 926, through her marriage to **King Rudolf II** of Burgundy
- After Rudolf's death in **937**, she married **King Hugh of Italy** — an unhappy union. When Hugh died in **947/948**, Bertha returned to Burgundy
- Between **950 and 960**, Bertha **founded the priory at Payerne**, establishing a religious community and calling **Benedictine monks from Cluny** to build and manage it
- She was buried at the priory church in Payerne, though her actual remains have never been conclusively identified (see section 5)

### The Spinning Legend
- Bertha is venerated as **"la bonne reine Berthe"** (the good Queen Bertha) throughout the Romandy region, especially in Vaud
- In legend, she is depicted riding through her domain on a **white palfrey**, spinning wool as she went — she reportedly had a **special saddle built with a hole for her distaff** (quenouille) so she would not lose spinning time during her rides
- She taught local women to spin, giving them a source of income, and looked after the welfare of farmers, women, and children
- The **French proverb** *"Ce n'est plus le temps où la reine Berthe filait"* ("It is no longer the time when Queen Bertha was spinning") means "those golden days are long gone" — a testament to her legendary status
- A painting by **Albert Anker**, *La reine Berthe et les fileuses* (Queen Bertha and the Spinners, 1888), in the collection of the **Musée cantonal des Beaux-Arts** in Lausanne, depicts the queen instructing peasant women in the art of spinning

## 4. The Abbatiale — Switzerland's Largest Romanesque Church

### Foundation and the Cluny Connection
- After Queen Bertha's death, her daughter **Empress Adelaide** (Adélaïde, c. 931–999), wife of Holy Roman Emperor **Otto I**, and her son **King Conrad of Burgundy** richly endowed the priory
- In **965**, Empress Adelaide formally placed the priory under the authority of **Cluny Abbey** (abbaye de Cluny) in Burgundy, making it one of the most important **Cluniac priories** (prieurés clunisiens) north of the Alps
- The great Cluniac abbots **Maiolus** (Mayeul) and **Odilo** (Odilon) personally managed and developed the house

### Architecture
- The current abbey church was largely built in the **first third of the 11th century**, with major construction around **960** expanding an earlier 10th-century church following the model of **Cluny II**
- A new, larger **nave** was added approximately **1070–1080**
- The nave extends over **60 meters in length** and rises **15 meters** high, covered by a **barrel vault** (voûte en berceau)
- Built in **Jura limestone with a slight reddish tint** — the warm "blondness" of the stone creates a distinctive atmosphere
- The apse features **three windows on the lower level** and **five on the upper level**, in the characteristic Cluniac **en échelon** plan
- A **Gothic bell tower** (clocher gothique) with **four turrets** and a spired crown rises above the transept crossing — a later medieval addition to the Romanesque body
- **Magnificent sculpted capitals** (chapiteaux) adorn the columns throughout the nave
- **Mural paintings** (peintures murales) from the **11th, 12th, and 14th centuries** survive in the church and narthex, including frescoes near the narthex dating to approximately **1200**
- The **Chapel of the Resurrection** contains wall paintings from the **14th century**; the **Chapel of Grailly**, built in the **15th century**, features a **rib vault** (voûte à nervures)

### Coronation of Conrad II
- On **2 February 1033**, **Emperor Conrad II** of the Holy Roman Empire held an assembly at the priory and was **crowned King of Burgundy** in the church — a pivotal moment when the Kingdom of Burgundy was absorbed into the Empire

## 5. The Mystery of Queen Bertha's Tomb

- In **1818**, bones found in the nave of the Abbey Church were **identified as Queen Bertha's remains** and solemnly transferred to a tomb in the **Gachet Chapel** (chapelle de Gachet) of the adjacent parish church
- A **marble slab** closing the tomb recounts the queen's legendary past
- On **20 May 2021**, the tomb was opened for modern scientific examination by a team of archaeologists, anthropologists, and art historians
- The results were definitive and surprising: the bones belonged to a **male individual**, aged between **30 and 60** at death, and **carbon-14 dating** placed them in the **15th century** (1420–1495, 94% confidence) — ruling out Queen Bertha entirely
- The true location of Bertha's remains remains **one of Payerne's unsolved mysteries**
- The monks of the priory themselves contributed to the confusion: in the **12th century**, they **falsified documents** purporting to be the "Testament of Queen Bertha" to claim additional rights and privileges

## 6. From Priory to Granary — The Reformation and Bernese Rule

### Medieval Town Development
- Before **1302**, the prior granted citizens the right to establish councils and create town seals
- In **1344**, Payerne signed agreements with **Bern**; in **1349**, with **Fribourg**
- The **town charter** (charte communale) was officially recognized in **1348**, though the prior's actual power was already limited
- A **hospital** was built in **1362**; a **schoolmaster** is recorded from **1395**, and a secondary school opened in **1449**

### The Reformation
- In **1531**, the reformer **Guillaume Farel** attempted to preach in Payerne but was refused entry to the church; he preached in the cemetery instead, provoking a mob that threatened to throw him into the Broye — the assistant bailiff had to smuggle him into prison for his own safety
- By **1532–1533**, Payerne adopted the **Protestant Reformation** — notably **before** Bern's conquest of Vaud

### Bernese Conquest (1536–1798)
- In **1536**, Bern conquered the **Pays de Vaud** and dissolved the priory, seizing its buildings and property
- The abbey was closed definitively in **1562**
- Payerne was given a **privileged legal status** by Bern: the Schultheiss (local representative of Bernese authority) was a **citizen of Payerne** rather than an imposed Bernese Vogt — a rare distinction
- In **1640**, a **castle** (château) was built on the monastery grounds as the residence of the Bernese representative; it later served as a secondary school into the 20th century
- The abbey church was put to a succession of **humiliating uses**: a **bell foundry** in the 17th century, a **grain store** in the 18th, then a **prison**, **military barracks**, and **fire engine depot** — with interior floors subdividing the once-majestic nave

### The Walls Come Down
- In the **1830s and 1840s**, Payerne's medieval **city walls and three gates** were demolished in the course of urban modernization
- **Four medieval towers** survive to this day as remnants of the old fortifications

## 7. The Restoration — A Monument Reborn

- In **1900**, the Abbey Church was listed as a **historic monument** (monument historique)
- Scholarly interest and restoration work began in the early **1920s**
- A major, decade-long restoration campaign was undertaken in the 2010s, jointly funded by the **municipality of Payerne**, the **canton of Vaud**, the **Swiss Confederation**, and numerous private donors
- On **11 July 2020**, the fully restored Abbey Church reopened to the public as a **museum and cultural site** — the floors that had subdivided the nave were removed, restoring the original majestic volume
- The museum now features **20 interactive stations** along a 90-minute route, including **5 immersive experiences**, **5 interactive terminals**, and **3 elevated vantage points** on the building, all enhanced by a curated soundscape
- An **organ by German builder Jürgen Ahrend** was installed in **1999** and is used for concerts throughout the year

## 8. The Place du Marché — Heart of the Town

- The **Place du Marché** (Market Square) has been the civic heart of Payerne for centuries, located directly in front of the Abbey Church at **Place du Marché, 1530 Payerne**
- The square hosts Payerne's traditional **markets**: a spring market, a summer market, and one of the region's largest **flea markets** (marché aux puces) from Good Friday to Easter
- The **Fontaine des Serruriers** (Locksmiths' Fountain) stands on the square
- Payerne's new **City Hall** (Hôtel de Ville) was inaugurated in **1964**
- The town's coat of arms features a shield **divided vertically into silver (white) and red**

## 9. The Swiss Air Force Base — Jets and Solar Dreams

### Military Aviation
- **Payerne Air Base** (Base aérienne de Payerne; ICAO: **LSMP**, IATA: **VIP**) opened in **1921** and became one of the most important military airfields in Switzerland
- Home to the **F/A-18 Hornet** squadrons: **Fliegerstaffel 17 "Falcons"** and **Fliegerstaffel 18 "Panthers"**, plus the militia **F-5E Tiger II** squadron **Fliegerstaffel 6 "Ducks"** and the transport squadron **Lufttransportstaffel 1**
- The base handles **Quick Reaction Alert / air policing** duties for Swiss airspace and serves as an alternate airport for **Bern-Belp** for foreign government flights
- The base also hosts training for ground crews: aircraft mechanics, airport firefighters, and other specialists

### Solar Impulse — A New Kind of Flight
- On **8 July 2010**, pilot **André Borschberg** took off from Payerne in **Solar Impulse 1** and completed the world's first **crewed 26-hour solar-powered flight**, including nearly 9 hours of night flying, reaching an altitude of **9,235 meters**
- Payerne became the home base for **Solar Impulse 2** (HB-SIB), which made its first flight from Payerne on **2 June 2014**
- Solar Impulse 2, piloted alternately by Borschberg and **Bertrand Piccard**, completed the first **solar-powered circumnavigation of the globe** on **26 July 2016**

### Clin d'Ailes Military Aviation Museum
- The **Clin d'Ailes** (musée de l'aviation militaire) museum, located on the air base, was inaugurated in **2003** and has welcomed over **150,000 visitors**
- Displays **13+ aircraft and helicopters** across two exhibition halls, including de Havilland Vampires, Hawker Hunters, Dassault Mirages, Northrop F-5 Tigers, and Pilatus PC-7 trainers
- Features **three flight simulators**, including an F/A-18 Hornet simulator

## 10. Notable People of Payerne

### Antoine-Henri Jomini (1779–1869)
- Born in Payerne on **6 March 1779** into a prominent local family — both his father and grandfather served as **mayor of Payerne** (syndic de Payerne)
- Became one of the most celebrated **military theorists** of the 19th century, considered one of the **founders of modern strategic thought**
- Served under **Napoleon** and **Marshal Ney** at battles including Ulm, Jena, and Eylau; later defected to **Russian service** in 1813
- His masterwork *Précis de l'art de la guerre* (1838) distinguished between **strategy, tactics, and logistics** and became required reading at military academies worldwide, notably **West Point**
- A permanent exhibition dedicated to Jomini is housed at the Abbey Church museum

### Jacques Chessex (1934–2009)
- Born in Payerne on **1 March 1934**, Chessex was one of Switzerland's most prominent writers and painters
- In **1973**, he won the **Prix Goncourt** for his novel *L'Ogre* — the **first non-French citizen** to receive France's most prestigious literary prize
- His 2008 novel *Un Juif pour l'exemple* (A Jew Must Die) recounts a dark chapter of Payerne's history: the **murder of Arthur Bloch** (see section 11)
- Chessex died of a heart attack on **9 October 2009** in Yverdon-les-Bains

### Other Notable Figures
- **Frédéric-Louis Allamand** (1736–1809): botanist
- **Daniel Rapin** (1799–1882): pharmacist and botanist
- **Charles Estoppey** (1820–1888): Swiss politician, member of the **Federal Council** (1875)
- **Hélène Monastier** (1882–1976): peace activist
- **Léon Savary** (1895–1968): writer and journalist

## 11. The Dark Chapter — The Murder of Arthur Bloch (1942)

- On **16 April 1942**, a small cell of **Swiss Nazis** in Payerne, led by **Fernand Ischi**, lured **Arthur Bloch**, a Jewish cattle merchant, into a stable and murdered him with an iron bar
- The killing was intended as a demonstration — an "example" of what would happen once the Nazis took control of Switzerland
- Bloch's body was dismembered, placed in **three milk cans**, and sunk in a local lake
- Jacques Chessex, who grew up in Payerne and **sat next to the murderers' children in school**, first wrote about the crime in **1967** and returned to it definitively in his 2008 novel *Un Juif pour l'exemple*
- Payerne's twin town is **Paray-le-Monial**, France — another town with deep Cluniac connections

## 12. Local Traditions — The Saucisson de Payerne

- Payerne is renowned for the **saucisson de Payerne**, a traditional **smoked pork sausage** (saucisson fumé) that has been a regional specialty since the mid-1800s
- The region is considered the **cradle of Vaud charcuterie** (charcuterie vaudoise): pig farming developed here from the **15th century** after the **Duke of Savoy** granted inhabitants the right to raise pigs in surrounding forests
- Local legend attributes the sausage's distinctive flavor to a particular breed of **red pigs** (cochons rouges) fed on acorns and the special waters of Payerne
- The designation "saucisson de Payerne" was restricted after **December 2016** due to overlap with the protected **Saucisson vaudois IGP** (Indication Géographique Protégée) designation
- The broader culinary tradition lives on as a point of local pride and identity

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The queen who spun the world** — A child finds an ancient distaff in the Abbey Church museum; touching it summons the ghost of Queen Bertha on her white horse, who takes the child on a ride through the Broye Valley, spinning thread that magically weaves the landscape into existence — fields, rivers, villages
2. **The bones that lied** — A child sneaks into the Gachet Chapel at night to visit Queen Bertha's tomb, but the bones whisper that they are not hers. Following clues left by 12th-century monks who falsified documents, the child hunts through Payerne's four surviving towers to find the queen's real resting place
3. **The day the sun flew** — On the night of July 8, 2010, a child at Payerne Air Base watches Solar Impulse take off and imagines flying alongside it; the plane's solar wings carry them silently over the Broye, Lake Morat, and Lake Neuchâtel through an entire night and day — 26 hours of seeing Switzerland from above
4. **The abbey that remembered** — When the nave floors are removed during the 2020 restoration, a child discovers that each layer holds a frozen moment: the 17th-century bell foundry, the 18th-century grain store, the medieval monks chanting — the building remembers every use it has endured
5. **The general's first battle** — Young Antoine-Henri Jomini, son of the mayor of Payerne, stages elaborate mock battles with tin soldiers on the Place du Marché; a mysterious stranger (who turns out to be a future Marshal Ney) watches and says "one day you will write the book of war itself"
6. **The emperor's crown** — It is February 1033, and the Abbey Church fills with nobles as Emperor Conrad II arrives to be crowned King of Burgundy. A child serving as a page must carry the heavy crown through the majestic nave without dropping it — each of the 60 meters feels like a mile
7. **Two churches, one song** — A child discovers that the Abbey Church's Ahrend organ and the parish church's organ are in a secret conversation; at midnight, each plays a note the other answers, and together they tell the story of Payerne in music — from Bertha's spinning song to the roar of jet engines
8. **Farel and the river** — It is 1531, and the reformer Guillaume Farel is preaching in the cemetery after being refused entry to the church. A child in the crowd must choose: join the mob threatening to throw him in the Broye, or help the bailiff smuggle him to safety through Payerne's medieval streets
