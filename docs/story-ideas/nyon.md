## 1. Colonia Iulia Equestris — Switzerland's First Roman Colony

- Around **45 BC**, shortly after the Gallic Wars, **Julius Caesar** founded the **Colonia Iulia Equestris** at the site of a Celtic settlement called **Noviodunum** (from Celtic *nowyo* = "new" and *dun* = "hillfort")
- The colony was established to settle **cavalry veterans** (*equites*), giving it the name **Equestris** — making Nyon one of the oldest Roman colonies in all of Switzerland
- The colony followed Caesar's defeat of the **Helvetii** at the **Battle of Bibracte** in 58 BC; the settlement helped control the Helvetii, who were resettled in the region against their will
- The territory of the Colonia stretched along the **right bank of the Rhone**, between the Pays de Gex, the Jura, and the Cote vaudoise (La Cote)
- At its height, the Roman town boasted a **forum**, **basilica**, **thermal baths**, an **aqueduct**, a **macellum** (covered market), artisanal quarters, necropolises, and organized residential blocks (*insulae*) with planned streets
- From the end of the **3rd century AD**, the colony entered decline; its monuments fell into ruin and provided building materials reused across the Lake Geneva region, including in Nyon's medieval **Tour Cesar** and **Porte Sainte-Marie**

## 2. The Roman Amphitheatre (Amphitheatre romain)

- Discovered in **1996** during construction on the **Rue de la Porcelaine**, the amphitheatre was a sensational archaeological find — the **sixth amphitheatre exposed in Switzerland** (after Avenches, Vindonissa, Augst, Martigny, and Bern)
- Built in the **early 2nd century AD**, likely during the reign of Emperor **Trajan**, as suggested by inscriptions found on site
- Arena dimensions: **50 meters** (long axis) by **36 meters** (short axis), placing it mid-sized among Swiss amphitheatres
- Researchers reconstructed approximately **17 rows of seating tiers**, capable of accommodating **several thousand spectators**; front rows were narrower, reserved for notable citizens
- Two entrances (one toward the city, one toward the lakeside road to **Lousonna/Lausanne-Vidy**) and two animal holding chambers (*carceres*) positioned opposite each other
- Spectacles included **gladiator fights**, **venationes** (animal hunts), exhibitions of wild animals, and executions of condemned prisoners
- After being abandoned at the end of the Roman era, the site was used as a **quarry** and was completely filled in by embankments in the **17th century** — hidden for over 300 years
- Restoration occurred between **1998 and 2001**; the site is classified as a **monument of national importance**

## 3. The Roman Museum (Musee romain de Nyon)

- In **1974**, the spectacular discovery of the **foundations of the forum basilica** occurred during the demolition of a house on Rue Maupertuis — the basilica had been built in the **second half of the 1st century AD**
- The city of Nyon decided to build a museum directly on these foundations; the **Musee romain** was inaugurated in **September 1979**
- The museum sits within the basilica's ancient walls — a true *musee de site* (site museum) where visitors walk among the original Roman foundations
- Exhibits combine archaeological artefacts, models, and immersive digital devices including films, animated images, and 3D reconstructions of the Colonia Iulia Equestris
- Notable finds include the **Artemis Mosaic**, discovered in **1932**, and numerous architectural elements from the forum complex
- On the **Esplanade des Marronniers** (Chestnut Tree Promenade), three **Roman columns** from the cryptoporticus of the sacred area of the second forum (built around **50 AD**) stand in the open air — originally **7.5 meters tall**; they were unearthed beneath the Rue Delaflechere and re-erected in **1958** to celebrate **2,000 years of the city of Nyon**

## 4. The Chateau de Nyon (Chateau de Nyon)

- The castle was originally built in the **late 12th century** by the **Lords of Prangins** (*seigneurs de Prangins*) to control trade routes along Lake Geneva
- In **1293**, **Amadeus V, Count of Savoy**, and his brother **Louis I, Baron of Vaud**, conquered the city by besieging it from both land and lake; Louis I then made Nyon a center of his power, establishing a **court** and a **mint** that struck coins for the lords of Vaud (c. **1286-1350**)
- After the **Bernese conquest of 1536**, the castle became the residence of the **Bernese bailiff** (*bailli bernois*); it received its present appearance largely during the **16th century**, reflecting Gothic, Renaissance, and later 18th-century design elements
- Since **1888**, the castle has housed a **historical museum** — one of Switzerland's earliest
- In **1947**, following a major exhibition of Nyon porcelain, it became the **Musee historique et des porcelaines** (History and Porcelain Museum)
- After six years of renovation, the museum expanded in **2006** to occupy the entire building
- Curiosities include **prison cells** on the top floor that remained in use until **1979**, and a **lock of Napoleon Bonaparte's hair** dated **1821**
- The castle's five towers offer panoramic views over Lake Geneva and the Jura mountains

## 5. Nyon Porcelain (Porcelaine de Nyon)

- **Jacques Dortu** (1749-1819), a painter and porcelain manufacturer of French Protestant origin, arrived in Nyon in spring **1781** with his father-in-law **Ferdinand Muller** to establish a porcelain factory
- Nyon became one of only **two porcelain manufactories** in 18th-century Switzerland (the other in Zurich, **1763-1813**)
- The factory lacked the essential raw material — **kaolin** — which had to be imported from **Saint-Yrieix near Limoges**, France
- The **golden age** of production was **1788-1795**, when Dortu's pieces rivaled the finest European centres; the factory supplied aristocratic tables across Switzerland, Russia, and the courts of the **Queen of Naples**, the **Queen of Spain**, and reportedly **Marie Antoinette** of France
- Pieces bore a distinctive trademark and often featured neoclassical designs intended for patricians and notables
- Financial difficulties caused by the **French Revolution** and the **Napoleonic wars** forced the factory to close definitively in **1813** after 32 years of operation
- Today the finest collection of Nyon porcelain is displayed at the **Chateau de Nyon**, where fakes produced in France in the late 19th century attest to the enduring desirability of the originals

## 6. Savoy, Bern, and the Reformation (1293-1798)

- From the late 13th century, Nyon was controlled by the **House of Savoy** — the Counts (later Dukes) of Savoy used the castle as their administrative centre for the **Pays de Vaud**
- In **1293**, Amadeus V confirmed town rights previously granted by **Aymon de Prangins** and extended further freedoms to Nyon's citizens
- In **1536**, Bernese troops marched through on their way to support Geneva; Nyon **surrendered without a fight** and was raised to the status of an administrative centre of a **Vogtei** (bailiwick) of Bern
- The **Reformation** was imposed: the church of **Saint-Jean-Baptiste**, first mentioned in **1346** and a pilgrimage site housing relics of the **Theban Legion** martyrs, was ordered **destroyed by Bern in 1537**
- The 13th-century **Franciscan monastery** (*couvent des Freres Mineurs*) near the lakeside was converted into a **hospital** in 1537 — the same building that later became the **Musee du Leman**
- Bernese rule lasted **262 years**, ending in **1798** during the Vaud Revolution inspired by the French Revolution
- From 1798 to 1803, Nyon was the district capital of the **Canton de Leman** in the **Helvetic Republic**; after 1803, it became part of the newly created **Canton de Vaud**

## 7. The Musee du Leman (Lake Geneva Museum)

- Founded in **1954** by **Edgar Pelichet**, head of archaeology for the Canton de Vaud, in the former **hospital of Nyon** (built 1756-1758, originally the converted Franciscan monastery)
- Inaugural opening: **5 July 1954**, with just **120 m2** and three rooms covering fishing, sailing, and birds
- In **1968**, the museum nearly closed when Pelichet retired, but **local residents mobilized** to save it
- Reopened in **1975** as the **Maison du Leman** under new director Andre Jeanneret, expanded to **230 m2**
- In **1985**, a major expansion added French-speaking Switzerland's **largest aquariums** — visitor numbers jumped from 9,984 to **24,159**
- The collection now includes approximately **14,000 objects** and **100,000 written documents**, including more than **50 ships and boats** and **150 ship models**
- Notable acquisitions: **Jacques Piccard** donated his personal archives in **1999**, followed by the archives of his father **Auguste Piccard** in 2008 — both pioneers of deep-sea exploration

## 8. UEFA — The House of European Football

- **UEFA** (Union of European Football Associations), the governing body of European football, has been headquartered in Nyon since **1995**
- UEFA moved from Bern to Nyon, initially occupying temporary offices in the **Providentia insurance building** (1995-1999) while permanent headquarters were built
- The **House of European Football** (*Maison du football europeen*) was inaugurated on **22 September 1999** at Route de Geneve 46
- The campus expanded with the **La Clairiere** building (inaugurated **October 2010**) and the **Bois-Bougy** building (opened **March 2012**)
- Approximately **800 staff** work across the three-building campus
- Since **2010**, UEFA has managed the **Colovray sports complex** opposite its headquarters, featuring a **4,000-capacity stadium** that hosts the **UEFA Youth League finals** annually
- In **2000**, UEFA established the **Foundation for the Development of Sport in Nyon**, which by 2022 had supported **119 projects** with grants totalling **1.6 million Swiss francs**

## 9. The Paleo Festival (Paleo Festival Nyon)

- Born in the post-Woodstock era, the festival was created by a handful of music lovers who founded the **Folk Club de l'Escalier** association in **1976**
- The first edition, the **"Premier Folk Festival"**, was held in the **village hall** in Nyon with just **1,800 spectators**
- From **1977 to 1989**, the festival moved to **Colovray**, on the shores of Lake Geneva, with two stages; by the end of the decade it drew **90,000 visitors** and involved **1,000 collaborators**
- In **1990**, it relocated to the **Plaine de l'Asse**, its current 84-hectare site north of Nyon, sparking a "creative and scenic explosion"
- Renamed from **"Nyon Folk Festival"** to **"Paleo Festival Nyon"** (the name "Paleo" referencing the nearby archaeological heritage)
- Today the festival features **7 stages**, over **200 stands**, around **60 bars**, and draws approximately **250,000 attendees** over six days in late July
- It is **Switzerland's largest open-air festival** and one of the **biggest in Europe**; it won **Best Major European Festival** in **2025**
- The festival approaches its **50th edition in 2027**, having grown from a village hall to a European cultural landmark while maintaining its volunteer-driven, community spirit

## 10. Geography — La Cote, Lake Geneva, and the Alps

- Nyon lies on the **northwestern shore of Lake Geneva** (*Lac Leman*), at an elevation of **403 meters**, roughly **halfway between Geneva (25 km) and Lausanne (35 km)**
- The town is part of the **La Cote** region ("The Slope"), where terraced vineyards descend from the Jura foothills to the lakeshore — the **largest wine-producing region** in the Canton de Vaud, covering some **2,000 hectares**
- The **Nyon Appellation** alone covers **270 hectares** with white varieties (Chasselas, Chardonnay, Pinot Gris) and reds (Gamay, Pinot Noir, Garanoir, Gamaret)
- From the **Esplanade des Marronniers** and the castle terrace, visitors enjoy a panoramic sweep from the **Jura mountains** behind to **Mont Blanc** and the **French and Swiss Alps** across the lake
- On clear days, the **Jet d'Eau** of Geneva is visible to the southwest
- The **Promenade des Vieilles Murailles** (Old Walls Promenade) follows the line of the medieval fortifications, offering views over terraced gardens, the harbour, and the lake
- Nyon is the gateway to the **Parc naturel du Jura vaudois** (Jura Vaudois Nature Park) and the nearby ski village of **Saint-Cergue**

## 11. The Esplanade des Marronniers and the Roman Columns

- The **Esplanade des Marronniers** (Chestnut Tree Promenade) is Nyon's most iconic viewpoint, shaded by mature chestnut trees and framed by the three re-erected **Roman columns**
- The columns belonged to the **cryptoporticus** of the sacred precinct of the second Roman forum, built around **50 AD**
- Originally **7.5 meters tall** including their sculpted capitals, the columns were discovered beneath the Rue Delaflechere and placed on the esplanade in **1958** to mark the **bimillenary** (2,000th anniversary) of the city
- Every Sunday in summer, the esplanade hosts **free open-air concerts** — a local tradition of music and dancing under the chestnut trees with the lake and Alps as backdrop
- The promenade connects to the **Bourg-de-Rive** park, which descends in terraces to Nyon's **harbour** and the CGN steamboat landing for cruises across Lake Geneva

## 12. Notable People and Events

- **Germaine de Stael** (1766-1817), the celebrated French-Swiss novelist and political philosopher, held her famous intellectual salon at the **Chateau de Coppet**, just **8 km south of Nyon**; exiled by Napoleon, she gathered the great minds of Europe into the **"Groupe de Coppet"**, a hotbed of liberal opposition to the Empire
- **Jacques Piccard** (1922-2008), the Swiss oceanographer and engineer, had close ties to the Lake Geneva region; his personal archives are held at Nyon's **Musee du Leman**; in **1964**, his tourist submarine **Mesoscaphe "Auguste Piccard"** took **33,000 passengers** beneath Lake Geneva during **Expo 64**
- The **Visions du Reel** international documentary film festival, founded in **1969**, takes place annually in Nyon and is one of the world's leading documentary film events
- Nyon's population has grown from a small medieval town to approximately **21,700 inhabitants** (2020), reflecting its status as a vibrant commuter town between Geneva and Lausanne
- The **Chateau de Prangins**, the largest 18th-century chateau in the canton, lies just outside Nyon and houses the **Swiss National Museum's** French-speaking branch

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The lost legionnaire of Noviodunum** — A Roman cavalry veteran arriving in 45 BC to settle Caesar's new colony gets separated from his legion; a child helps him find his way through the half-built forum, thermal baths, and newly planted vineyards to the amphitheatre where his comrades await

2. **The columns that remember** — The three Roman columns on the Esplanade des Marronniers have watched 2,000 years pass; one night they whisper their memories to a child: the roar of the amphitheatre, the Savoy siege of 1293, the Bernese bailiff, the porcelain painters, and the first Paleo concert

3. **The porcelain painter's secret** — A child visiting the Chateau de Nyon touches a porcelain cup and is transported to 1790, the golden age of the manufacture; Jacques Dortu needs help: the kaolin shipment from Limoges has gone missing, and without it, the queen's dinner service cannot be finished

4. **The gladiator's cat** — Hidden beneath the Rue de la Porcelaine, a stray cat falls through a crack into the buried Roman amphitheatre and discovers it is not empty: ghostly gladiators still perform for phantom crowds; a child must convince them that 2,000 years have passed and help them find peace

5. **The UEFA football that rolled through time** — A football kicked from the UEFA campus in Nyon rolls down the hill, through the castle gate, past the Roman columns, and into Lake Geneva; a child chases it and at each landmark, the ball bounces into a different century of Nyon's history

6. **The chestnut tree concert** — Every Sunday the Esplanade des Marronniers hosts free music under the chestnut trees; one summer evening, a child's melody summons a Roman musician, a medieval troubadour, and a Paleo rock star, and they must play together before the last chestnut leaf falls

7. **The castle's five towers** — Each of the Chateau de Nyon's five towers holds a secret from a different era: a Savoy knight's oath, a Bernese prisoner's carved message, a porcelain figurine that comes alive, Napoleon's lock of hair that whispers French, and a telescope pointed at Mont Blanc; a child must visit all five before the castle clock strikes midnight

8. **The lake that kept a secret** — Inspired by Jacques Piccard's submarine adventures, a child boards a magical vessel at Nyon's harbour and descends into Lake Geneva, discovering Roman coins, a sunken Savoy galley, a porcelain cup thrown overboard during the Revolution, and a message in a bottle from Germaine de Stael herself
