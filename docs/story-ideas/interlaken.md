## 1. Geography — Between the Lakes

- The name **Interlaken** derives from the Latin **"inter lacus"** — literally "between the lakes"
- The town sits on the **Bödeli**, a flat alluvial plain at **568 meters** above sea level, between **Lake Brienz** (Brienzersee) to the east and **Lake Thun** (Thunersee) to the west
- The **Aare river** flows through the town, connecting the two lakes — Lake Brienz sits **2 meters higher** than Lake Thun, so the Aare flows from east to west
- After the last ice age, both lakes were a single body of water known as **Wendelsee** ("Lake Wendel"). Over millennia, debris from the rivers **Lütschine** and **Lombach** cascading down from the mountains created the Bödeli, splitting the lakes apart — a process completed by roughly the **10th century**
- The Bödeli is shared by three communities: **Interlaken**, **Unterseen** (to the west), and **Matten** (to the south)
- To the south rises the famous trio of peaks: **Eiger** (3,967 m), **Mönch** (4,110 m), and **Jungfrau** (4,158 m)

## 2. The Augustinian Monastery — The Town's Origin

- In **1133**, Baron **Seliger von Oberhofen** founded an **Augustinian monastery** on the Bödeli between the two lakes
- Holy Roman Emperor **Lothair II** took the monastery under his protection that same year — the first documented mention
- The monastery belonged to the **diocese of Lausanne**
- From the monastery's Latin name — **"inter lacus"** (between the lakes) — the settlement and eventually the town took its name
- During the **13th century**, the monastery's influence spread throughout the Aare and Gürbe valleys; it gained authority over **two dozen churches** and numerous villages, becoming the **largest religious landholder** in the region
- A **women's convent** was added in **1257**
- At its peak (a **1310 document**): **30 priests**, **20 lay brothers**, and **350 women** lived at the monastery
- By **1472**, decline had set in: only the provost, prior, **9 canons**, **7 novices**, and **27 nuns** remained
- During the **Protestant Reformation**, the monastery was **secularized in 1528** by the city of Bern
- Bern converted the monastery lands into the **Interlaken bailiwick** (administrative district); part of the building became the district headquarters, part an indigent hospital
- Today the **castle church** and monastery buildings still stand — the church dates partly to the **14th century** and is one of Interlaken's oldest landmarks

## 3. The Birth of Alpine Tourism — Grand Tour and Famous Visitors

- Interlaken is one of **Switzerland's oldest summer tourist resorts**, attracting visitors since the late **18th century**
- The first Unspunnenfest in **1805** (see below) drew over **3,000 visitors** from across Western Europe and gave the initial impetus to modern tourism in the Bernese Oberland
- **Lord Byron** visited in **September 1816** during a ten-day trek through the Bernese Oberland with companion John Cam Hobhouse. He reached Interlaken by boat from Lake Brienz on **September 26**. The Staubbach Falls at nearby Lauterbrunnen moved him deeply — he described the cascade as "the tail of a white horse streaming in the wind." His alpine experiences inspired the poem **"Manfred"**
- **Felix Mendelssohn** first visited as a **14-year-old** in **1822** on a family trip to Lucerne and the Bernese Oberland, composing two Swiss symphonies inspired by alpine folk melodies. He returned multiple times: in **1831**, **1842** (when he wrote that Interlaken was "the finest of all in this unbelievably beautiful country"), and finally in **1847** after the death of his sister Fanny, when he painted **13 watercolours** of the Interlaken area and composed his **Quartet in F minor** as a requiem
- **Mark Twain** crossed the Swiss border in **August 1878** with Reverend Joseph Twichell. His account of Interlaken and the Jungfrau appeared in **"A Tramp Abroad"** (1880), where he described the giant form of the Jungfrau rising "cold and white into the clear sky" from his hotel window
- **Steamship services** began on Lake Thun in **1835** and Lake Brienz in **1839**, making Interlaken reliably accessible
- The **Victoria-Jungfrau Grand Hotel** opened in **1865** after Eduard Ruchti commissioned architects Friedrich Studer and Horace Edouard Davinet to redesign the former Pension Victoria. It offered unprecedented luxury including telephone, hydraulic lift, and electric light. In **1899**, the Victoria and adjacent Hotel Jungfrau (built 1864) were merged into the grand complex standing today

## 4. The Höhematte — The Protected Green Heart

- The **Höhematte** is a vast **14-hectare** (35-acre) green meadow in the centre of Interlaken, offering an unobstructed view of the Jungfrau
- Originally owned by the **Augustinian monastery**, the meadow became open land after secularization
- In **1860**, local citizens banded together to **buy the land** to ensure it remained open and publicly accessible
- In **1864**, a legally binding covenant was established: **no trees may be felled, no buildings constructed**, and the land cannot be divided — a protection that holds to this day
- The main avenue **Höheweg** runs along one side, lined with grand hotels; the Höhematte stretches along the other, creating the iconic Alpine panorama that defines Interlaken's town centre
- Today the meadow is used for festivals, paragliding landings, and serves as the town's "green lung"

## 5. The Jungfraujoch — Top of Europe

- At **3,454 meters** above sea level, the **Jungfraujoch** railway station is the **highest railway station in Europe** — marketed as the **"Top of Europe"**
- The Swiss industrialist **Adolf Guyer-Zeller** conceived the railway on **August 27, 1893**, while hiking above Mürren. He sketched the route that night in his hotel room
- Construction licence granted **December 21, 1894**; ground-breaking **July 27, 1896**
- The **9.3-kilometre** line runs from **Kleine Scheidegg** (2,064 m) to the Jungfraujoch, mostly inside a **7-kilometre tunnel** bored through the Eiger and Mönch mountains to protect the line from snow and avalanches
- Three intermediate tunnel stations were built: **Rotstock**, **Eigerwand** (with windows carved into the Eiger's north face, opened **1903**), and **Eismeer** (3,160 m, opened **1905**)
- Construction took **16 years**: workers struck **6 times**, management changed **8 times**, and **30 workers died** — mostly from blasting accidents. The workforce was primarily Italian immigrants using manual tools
- Breakthrough to the Jungfraujoch on **February 21, 1912**; the first train with tourists ran five months later in **July 1912**
- At the summit: the **Sphinx Observatory** (3,571 m) with panoramic views toward Italy, France, and Germany; an **Ice Palace** carved into the glacier; and views of the **Aletsch Glacier**, the longest glacier in the Alps
- The **Swiss Alps Jungfrau-Aletsch** region was inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** in 2001

## 6. The Harder Kulm — Interlaken's House Mountain

- **Harder Kulm** at **1,322 meters** is Interlaken's local mountain ("Hausberg"), offering panoramic views of both lakes, the town, and the Eiger-Mönch-Jungfrau trio
- The concession to build a funicular was granted in **1890**, but it took **15 years** to find financing
- Construction began in **1905**; the **Harderbahn funicular** opened on **15 May 1908**, built with the support of Lausanne banker **Ernest Chavannes** and engineers Gaston Boiceau and Henri Muret
- The track is **1,435 meters** long with a gradient of up to **64%**, climbing **734 vertical meters** in about **10 minutes**
- The castle-like **Bergrestaurant Harder Kulm** at the summit, with its turret and red tiled roof, resembles a fairytale castle
- The **"Zwei-Seen-Steg"** (Two Lakes Bridge) viewing platform features a glass floor section — visitors can see both Lake Thun and Lake Brienz simultaneously
- New centenary coaches were delivered in **2008** for the funicular's 100th anniversary
- An **alpine wildlife park** was opened near the base station in **1913**

## 7. Schynige Platte — Heritage Railway and Alpine Garden

### The Railway
- The idea of a railway to Schynige Platte was born in **1880**; a federal concession was granted after a decade of planning
- The first train with special guests reached the summit on **5 May 1893**; the line opened to the public on **14 June 1893**
- The **7.3-kilometre** rack railway runs from **Wilderswil** (584 m) to **Schynige Platte** (1,987 m), climbing **1,420 vertical meters** with a maximum gradient of **25%**
- Opened with **steam traction**; **electrified in 1914** (1,500 V DC overhead supply)
- Built to **800 mm gauge** using the **Riggenbach rack** system
- The railway still uses **vintage rolling stock** — electric locomotives from the early industrial era, wooden-bench coaches, no electronic signals (all points operated by hand, trains waved off by railway managers personally)
- Operates only in summer (June to October), with **52-minute** journeys and services every 40 minutes

### The Alpine Botanical Garden
- Founded by the **Schynige Platte Alpine Garden Society** on **10 June 1927**, after chemist **Dr. R. Jenzer** and hotelier **W. Hofmann** campaigned for the project
- The garden was fenced off in **1928** (over **8,000 square metres**), ending centuries of alpine pasture use, and opened to the public in **1929**
- Displays over **600 species** of plants native to the Swiss Alps, presented in their **natural plant communities** — described as the first botanical garden worldwide to use this approach
- Since **1932**, an annual **alpine-botanical course** has been held in collaboration with the **University of Bern's Institute of Plant Sciences**

## 8. The Unspunnenfest — Swiss Tradition and a Stolen Stone

### The Festival's Origin
- The first **Unspunnenfest** was held on **17 August 1805** in the meadows near the ruins of **Unspunnen Castle**, organized by Bern's mayor **Niklaus Friedrich von Mülinen** and Interlaken's chief magistrate **Franz Ludwig Thormann**
- **Political context**: After the collapse of the **Helvetic Republic** (1798–1803), the Bernese Oberland had lost its status as an independent canton under Napoleon's **1803 Act of Mediation**, shifting political power back to the urban population. The festival was conceived as an act of **reconciliation between city and countryside**
- Over **3,000 herdsmen and guests** from across Western Europe attended — the festival sparked a revival of the **alphorn**, folk songs, and the beginnings of tourism in the Oberland
- A second festival followed in **1808**; modern editions have been held roughly every **12 years** since **1946** (most recently **2017**)

### Traditional Sports
- **Schwingen** (Alpine wrestling): Contestants wear special shorts over their trousers; one hand grips the opponent's belt, the other grips the bottom of his shorts. The aim is to throw the opponent onto his back without releasing the grip
- **Steinstossen** (stone throwing): Competitors hurl the **Unspunnen Stone**, an **83.5 kg** block of Aare granite from the Hasli valley

### The Saga of the Unspunnen Stone
- The **original stone** from 1805 was lost; the **1808 festival** used a replacement (76 kg)
- On **3 June 1984**, members of the **Bélier Group** (young Jura separatists) stole the 1808 stone from the **Interlaken Museum of Tourism** as a political protest over the disputed Jura border
- During captivity, the Béliers **vandalized** the stone, chiselling **European stars**, their coat of arms, and the date of the **1992 EEA referendum** into it — reducing its weight and making it unusable for competition
- The stone was returned in **2001** via a private initiative
- In **2005**, the stone was stolen **again** — this time from the lobby of the **Victoria-Jungfrau Hotel**. The thieves left behind a paving stone painted with the Jura coat of arms
- As of 2024, the stone **has not been recovered** — investigative reports suggest it may be buried in the Jura soil

## 9. The Tellspiele — William Tell in the Woods

- In **1912**, local teacher **August Flückiger** in Matten bei Interlaken initiated an open-air performance of Friedrich Schiller's **"Wilhelm Tell"** with his pupils and the local **Dramatische Gesellschaft** (Dramatic Society)
- Performances ran **1912–1914**, then **1931–1939**, and **every year since 1947** (interruptions only during the world wars)
- The **Tellspielverein** (Tell Play Association) was established in **1930** to manage the productions
- Performed in the **Rugen woods** in Matten — a natural forest amphitheatre
- The ensemble consists of approximately **200 amateur actors**, with speaking roles, musicians, equestrians, and mute extras; live animals including **horses, goats, and cows** are part of the production
- Key scenes include the **swearing of the Rütli Oath** and the famous **shooting of the apple**
- By **1995**, the theatre had reached **1,000 performances**; today there have been over **1,250 performances** with more than **2 million spectators**
- It is the longest-running open-air theatre production in Switzerland

## 10. The Kursaal — From Whey Spa to Congress Centre

- In **1858**, a group of **33 citizens** acquired the site of the former Augustinian monastery to build a **whey spa** (Molkenkuranstalt) and casino with a view of the Jungfrau
- Construction was leased to French Baron **Azène du Plessis**; the Kursaal opened on **20 July 1859** with facilities for reading, games, dining, music, and balls
- **One month later** it was closed due to a **federal gambling ban** — Baron du Plessis fled Interlaken
- The **"Kurhausgesellschaft Interlaken"** was officially established in **1862** to manage the property
- **1873**: A **music pavilion** was erected in the garden; relocated in **1892** to reveal the Jungfrau view
- **1883**: Gambling returned in limited form with the **"jeu des petits chevaux"** (horse game), generating crucial revenue
- **1889**: First **electric illumination** installed
- **1899–1910**: Major expansion by Neuchâtel architect **Paul Bouvier** — concert hall, auditorium, and theatre added
- **1920**: Federal popular initiative banned gambling again; the prohibition took effect in **1925**
- **1980**: A modern **congress building** was inaugurated; the complex was added to the **federal list of historic monuments**
- **2002**: The Federal Council granted a **Type B casino licence**, restoring gaming to the Kursaal after decades

## 11. Adventure Capital of Europe

- Interlaken has earned the title **"Adventure Capital of Europe"** — the vision of establishing the town as an adventure hub was first put forward in **1985**
- The enormous peaks, rivers, lakes, and deep valleys create an ideal outdoor playground for **paragliding**, **canyoning**, **bungee jumping**, **skydiving**, **river rafting**, and **hang gliding**
- Between **2000 and 2007**, backpacker bookings in Interlaken surged from **59,000** to over **100,000** per year; by 2007, the town recorded roughly **800,000 overnight stays**
- **27 July 1999**: A catastrophic **canyoning accident** in the **Saxetbach Gorge** near Saxeten killed **21 young people** from Australia, New Zealand, England, South Africa, and Switzerland. A flash flood caused by a collapsed natural dam swept through the gorge during a commercial canyoning excursion. The tour company **Adventure World** had ignored storm warnings
- The tragedy led to a **federal suspension** of all canyoning expeditions and, eventually, new **risk-sport regulations** covering canyoning, bungee jumping, and whitewater rafting. Over **30 firms** joined the oversight partnership **"Safety in Adventures"**

## 12. The Jungfrau Trio — Eiger, Mönch, and Jungfrau

- The three peaks visible from Interlaken form one of the most iconic mountain panoramas in the world
- **Eiger** ("Ogre"): **3,967 m** — first ascent **11 August 1858** by Charles Barrington with guides. Its nearly **1,800-meter north face** (Eigerwand) was called "the last problem of the Alps" and was not conquered until **1938** by an Austrian-German expedition
- **Mönch** ("Monk"): **4,110 m** — first ascent **15 August 1857** by Christian Almer, Christian Kaufmann, Ulrich Kaufmann, and Sigismund Porges
- **Jungfrau** ("Virgin"): **4,158 m** — first ascent **3 August 1811** by the **Meyer brothers** of Aarau and two chamois hunters from Valais
- The names may derive from the monastery connection: the **Jungfrau** (Virgin) and **Mönch** (Monk) relate to the Augustinian monks and nuns who once looked up at these peaks; the **Eiger** stands apart as the "threatening one"

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The lake that split in two** — A water spirit living in the ancient Wendelsee watches in wonder as the rivers slowly divide its home into two lakes, and a village grows on the new land between them
2. **The monks between the lakes** — A young novice at the Augustinian monastery in 1257 discovers a secret passage connecting the two lakes and must decide whether to share it or keep the monastery's peace
3. **The train through the mountain** — A child rides the Jungfrau Railway in 1912 on its very first journey, peering through the Eigerwand windows into the Eiger's heart and meeting the ghost of a tunnel worker who wants his story remembered
4. **The stolen stone** — A child in Interlaken discovers the missing Unspunnen Stone and must outwit the tricksters who stole it, following clues from the 1805 festival across the Bernese Oberland
5. **Tell in the woods** — During a Tellspiele rehearsal, a child actor playing Tell's son accidentally fires a real arrow and is transported back to 1307, where the real William Tell needs help
6. **The two-lakes bridge** — A child afraid of heights must cross the glass-floored Zwei-Seen-Steg on Harder Kulm, but discovers that each lake holds a different magical world visible only from above
7. **Mendelssohn's last watercolour** — In 1847, a grieving Mendelssohn paints the Jungfrau and a child from the village watches; together they discover that music and painting can heal even the deepest sorrow
8. **The Höhematte pact** — In 1860, the citizens of Interlaken must save their beloved meadow from being built upon; a child rallies the town, and the meadow itself reveals why it must stay green — it is the dreaming place of the mountains
