## 1. Origins — From Bronze Age to Medieval Borgo

- The oldest archaeological finds near Ascona date to the **Late Bronze Age**; graves with equipment point to the **1st–2nd century AD**, likely associated with a Roman villa
- Roman-era Ascona lay on trade routes linking the northern Alps to the **Po Valley**
- A fortified settlement is first documented in **1186** as **"castrum quod dicitur Sconae"** (Latin: "the castle called Sconae"); in **1224** it appears as **"burgus de Schona"** (the borough of Schona)
- The **etymology** of the name is uncertain — possibly from Latin *scanum* ("alluvial sediment"), from *ab abscondito* ("hidden place"), or from an early Romance word *asculà* ("uncultivated pasture")
- In the Middle Ages, Ascona had a **rights market** and **solid fortifications** erected by local aristocratic families
- Four medieval castles defended the town from the surrounding heights: **San Michele**, **San Materno**, **dei Grillioni** (or Ghiriglioni), and **dei Carcani** — connected, according to legend, by underground passages
- The town formed a **village cooperative** (vicinanza) together with Ronco and Castelletto
- Until the early 20th century, the local economy depended on **crops, livestock, and fisheries** — Ascona was fundamentally a **fishing village** (villaggio di pescatori) on the northern shore of Lake Maggiore

## 2. Geography — Switzerland's Lowest Point

- Ascona sits at **196 metres above sea level**, making it **Switzerland's lowest-lying town** (la località più bassa della Svizzera)
- Lake Maggiore's surface lies at **193 m**, the absolute lowest point in all of Switzerland
- The town developed on the **delta of the Maggia river** (delta della Maggia), a wide alluvial plain shared with neighbouring Locarno
- The **Maggia** rises from the Pizzo Cristallina and flows **56 kilometres** through the Sambuco, Lavizzara, and Maggia valleys before reaching the lake; its catchment area covers approximately **930 km²**
- The delta's **wetland nature reserve**, established in **1985** and protected by cantonal law since **1990**, is one of the most important estuaries in the Insubric region
- The **Mediterranean microclimate** — over **2,300 hours of sunshine** per year — allows **palm trees, camellias, oleanders, and subtropical vegetation** to thrive, giving Ascona its distinctly southern character

## 3. The Borgo and the Lungolago — Waterfront and Old Town

- The **Borgo** (old town) is a tight web of narrow lanes lined with **colourfully painted houses** — the architectural signature of the former fishing village
- At its heart stands the **Chiesa dei Santi Pietro e Paolo** (Church of Saints Peter and Paul), documented as a parish since **1264**, enlarged in the **16th century**, with a tall rectangular stone **campanile** that serves as the town's iconic landmark
- Inside the church: three important canvases by **Giovanni Serodine** (Ascona, 1600–Rome, 1630), a Swiss-Italian Baroque painter influenced by Caravaggio — including the *Road to Emmaus* and the *Sons of Zebedee*, donated to the parish in **1633** by the painter's father and brother
- The **Piazza Giuseppe Motta**, the car-free lakeside promenade (lungolago), is Ticino's most celebrated waterfront — lined with **palm trees, café terraces, and Belle Époque hotels**
- In **1925**, an extensive **redistribution of land ownership** allowed non-locals to buy property in Ascona; by **1934**, landowners included 299 Ticinesi, 88 other Swiss, 41 Germans, 35 Italians, and 31 other foreign nationals — reflecting the international character that tourism had already brought

## 4. Monte Verità — The Mountain of Truth

### Founding (1900)
- In the autumn of **1900**, five idealists settled on the hill of **Monescia** above Ascona and renamed it **Monte Verità** ("Mountain of Truth")
- The founders: **Henri Oedenkoven** (25, from Antwerp, son of an industrialist), pianist **Ida Hofmann** (from Montenegro), and brothers **Karl Gräser** (ex-officer) and **Gustav (Gusto) Gräser** (artist) from Transylvania, joined by **Lotte Hattemer**
- Their philosophy: a **"Christian-communist"** cooperative based on **vegetarianism** (Vegetarismus), **nudism** (Nacktkultur), **theosophy**, life in the open air, and the emancipation of women — a radical rejection of industrial bourgeois society

### The Anarchists and Reformers (1900–1920)
- Anarchist physician **Raphael Friedeberg** moved to Ascona in **1904**, attracting other anarchists: **Erich Mühsam**, **Prince Peter Kropotkin**, psychoanalyst **Otto Gross**, and activist **Gustav Landauer**
- The colony operated as a **sanatorium** (Naturheilsanatorium) frequented by theosophists, social reformers, writers, and artists
- **Hermann Hesse** visited repeatedly; **Isadora Duncan** danced on the hillside
- In **1920**, founders Oedenkoven and Hofmann departed for **Brazil**, attempting to recreate Monte Verità in the tropics

### Laban and the Birth of Modern Dance (1913–1919)
- In **1913**, choreographer **Rudolf von Laban** established a **Summer School for the Arts** on Monte Verità
- Students practised barefoot, often nude, in the open air — developing what became known as the **"New Dance"** (Ausdruckstanz / danza espressiva)
- **Mary Wigman**, **Sophie Taeuber**, **Katja Wulff**, and **Suzanne Perrottet** trained under Laban
- In the summer of **1917**, the dance drama **"Sang an die Sonne"** ("Song to the Sun") was performed at the conclusion of a large vegetarian and pacifist congress — a landmark event in the birth of modern European dance

### The Bauhaus Era — Baron von der Heydt (1926–1964)
- In **1926**, German-Swiss banker and art collector **Baron Eduard von der Heydt** purchased the Monte Verità complex
- He commissioned architect **Emil Fahrenkamp** to build an elegant hotel in the **Bauhaus style** (constructed **1927–1929**)
- Von der Heydt displayed his extensive collection of **modern art and non-European art** in the hotel and its park
- The site attracted **Bauhaus luminaries** — Walter Gropius, Josef Albers, and Herbert Bayer visited
- After von der Heydt's death in **1964**, the property passed to the **Canton of Ticino**
- Today: **Casa Anatta** (built in theosophist style by the original colonists) houses a **history museum** (since **1981**); the Bauhaus hotel hosts **international conferences and cultural events**

## 5. Chiesa di Santa Maria della Misericordia — Late-Gothic Frescoes

- The church dates to **1399**, according to a **Gothic inscription** painted in the south-east corner of the choir
- The facade features a smooth **Gothic granite portal** preceded by a three-arched atrium (atrio a tre arcate)
- The bell gable (campanile a vela) bears the date **1488** in its plinth; the belfry and cone spire are **terracotta**, in late Lombard Gothic style
- Above the portal: a fresco of **Our Lady of Mercy** (Madonna della Misericordia), attributed to the **school of Maestro Domenico** (c. 1490–1510)
- The interior has a **single rectangular nave** covered by a **painted coffered ceiling** (soffitto a cassettoni)
- Above the high altar: a **1519 polyptych** depicting the **Maria Assunta** (Assumption of Mary), by **Antonio Gaia**, an artist from Ascona
- The church is considered home to one of Switzerland's **best-preserved late-Gothic fresco cycles** — biblical scenes and saints covering the walls and vault

## 6. Collegio Papio — Renaissance Cloister

- Founded by the testamentary will of **Bartolomeo Papio** (Ascona, 1526–Rome, 1580), a local man who made his fortune in Rome serving the **Orsini family**
- Papio bequeathed his palace and property to the municipality for the erection of a **seminary**
- **Cardinal Carlo Borromeo**, Archbishop of Milan, championed the project as a bulwark against the Protestant Reformation spreading from the north
- The college was built in **1585** in **Renaissance style**, designed by architect **Tibaldo Pellegrino di Valsolda**, adjacent to the Chiesa di Santa Maria della Misericordia
- Through a portal whose tympanum features a bas-relief of the **Madonna della Misericordia**, one enters the large **quadrangular cloister** (chiostro) with a portico on the lower floor and **loggias** on the upper floor
- The cloister rests on **28 Tuscan-style columns** (colonne toscane) of **red granite** quarried from **Baveno**
- Under the arcades: **27 bas-reliefs** in stucco, marble, and granite depicting coats of arms of the college's protectors — including **Pope Gregory XIII** and **Carlo Borromeo**
- A **shell-shaped niche** displays a bust of Bartolomeo Papio
- The college remained a **Pontifical College** until **1965**, when the Holy See donated it to the **Diocese of Lugano**

## 7. The Bohemian Colony — Artists, Writers, and Exiles

- Ascona's magnetic pull on artists began with Monte Verità but extended far beyond the hill

### Marianne Werefkin and the Museum of Modern Art
- **Marianne Werefkin** (1860–1938), Russian-born Expressionist painter and key figure of the **Blaue Reiter** (Blue Rider) movement, arrived in Ticino in **1918** fleeing World War I
- In **1922**, Werefkin and other resident artists donated works with the aim of creating a **Municipal Museum** — the foundation of today's **Museo Comunale d'Arte Moderna** (housed in a **16th-century palazzo** on Via Borgo)
- In **1924**, Werefkin founded the artists' group **"Der Grosse Bär"** (The Great Bear / L'Orsa Maggiore), named after the constellation
- The **Marianne Werefkin Foundation** now holds approximately **90 paintings**, **170 sketchbooks**, and hundreds of drawings — the world's most important collection of her work
- Fellow artists **Alexej Jawlensky**, **Paul Klee**, **Arthur Segal**, and **Cuno Amiet** also contributed works to the museum

### Hans Arp and the Dada Connection
- **Hans (Jean) Arp** arrived in Ascona in **1915**, drawn by the Monte Verità circle and his interest in theosophy and Eastern mysticism
- Arp developed his signature **elementary organic forms** on the shore of Lake Maggiore — sketching broken branches, roots, and stones cast up by the water, then simplifying them into "fluid ovals"
- With **Otto van Rees**, Arp painted a mural for a **children's school** in Ascona
- In **1959**, Arp and his wife **Marguerite Hagenbach** purchased a house in nearby **Locarno-Solduno**, where the **Fondazione Marguerite Arp** now preserves his late works

### Erich Maria Remarque
- In **August 1931**, **Erich Maria Remarque** — author of *All Quiet on the Western Front* — purchased the villa **Casa Monte Tabor** in **Porto Ronco** (a district of Ronco sopra Ascona)
- He fled Germany after Nazi protests forced the banning of the film of his novel
- The villa became a cultural salon: guests included **Thomas Mann**, **Carl Zuckmayer**, **Arthur Koestler**, **Ernst Toller**, **Joseph Roth**, **Franz Werfel**, and **Else Lasker-Schüler**
- Remarque lived in the villa until his death in **1970**

### Other Notable Figures
- **Carl Gustav Jung** frequented Monte Verità and the Eranos conferences in nearby Moscia
- **Paul Klee** contributed his painting *The Red House* (Das rote Haus) to the Ascona museum collection
- **Hugo Ball** (co-founder of Dada) and **Emmy Hennings** lived their final years in nearby municipalities

## 8. Isole di Brissago — Botanical Paradise on the Lake

- Two islands in the Swiss part of Lake Maggiore, near Brissago and Ascona: **San Pancrazio** (the larger, visitable island) and **Sant'Apollinare** (the smaller, wild island)
- In the **13th century**, a **convent of nuns** occupied the islands; for centuries afterward they lay abandoned
- In **1885**, Russian-born **Baroness Antoinette de Saint Léger** and her husband **Richard Fleming** purchased the islands (then called **"Isole dei conigli"** — Rabbit Islands)
- The Baroness transformed San Pancrazio into a **botanical garden** and **cultural centre**, hosting painters **Daniele Ranzoni**, **Filippo Franzoni**, and **Giovanni Segantini**, and composer **Ruggero Leoncavallo** (between **1886 and 1914**)
- After World War I, she also hosted **Rainer Maria Rilke**
- Fleming departed in **1897**; the Baroness continued alone until selling to Hamburg merchant and art collector **Max Emden** in **1927**
- Emden built a **Palazzo-style villa** (designed by Berlin architect **Alfred Breslauer**), added a Roman bath, orangery, and dock
- In **1949**, a consortium of the **Canton of Ticino** (50%), the municipalities of Ascona, Brissago, and Ronco sopra Ascona, plus the **Swiss Nature Protection League**, purchased the islands from Emden's son in Chile
- On **2 April 1950**, the **Parco Botanico del Canton Ticino** opened to the public
- Today the garden hosts approximately **2,000 plant species** from Mediterranean and subtropical regions across five continents, receiving over **90,000 visitors** annually

## 9. Castello San Materno — From Fortress to Art Museum

- The oldest of Ascona's four medieval castles, with origins in the **6th–8th century** (early medieval / alto medioevo)
- Located on the heights above the town, it once defended and controlled navigation on the upper **Verbano** (Lake Maggiore)
- Only fragmentary remains of the original fortification survive, near the **oratory of San Materno**
- In **2014**, after careful restoration, the castle reopened as the **Museo Castello San Materno**, housing the art collection of the **Kurt and Barbara Alten Cultural Foundation**
- The collection features works from the **Worpswede artist colony** and periods of **German Impressionism and Expressionism**: paintings by **Paula Modersohn-Becker**, **Otto Modersohn**, **Fritz Overbeck**, and **Hans am Ende**

## 10. JazzAscona — New Orleans on Lake Maggiore

- Founded in **1975** in Lugano as **"Festa New Orleans"** by **Hannes Anrig** (artistic director until 1984)
- Relocated to **Ascona in 1985**, transforming the lakeside promenade into an open-air jazz stage
- Devoted to **historical styles of jazz**, particularly **New Orleans jazz** — the only festival of its kind in Europe
- In **2004**, renamed **"JazzAscona – New Orleans & Classics"**
- In **2015**, the festival reached an official agreement with the **City of New Orleans**; it is the **only event outside the USA** to be officially supported by New Orleans
- Mayor **LaToya Cantrell** later travelled to Ascona for a formal **sister city agreement** between Ascona and New Orleans
- The 10-day festival (late June to early July) features over **200 concerts**, **55 bands**, late-night jam sessions, and **Creole cuisine from Louisiana** — all offered **free of charge** along the Piazza Giuseppe Motta
- Under artistic director **Nicolas Gilliet** (2003–2019), the festival reached its peak international profile

## 11. The Maggia Delta — Nature Between Two Towns

- The **delta della Maggia** forms the wide alluvial plain on which both Ascona and Locarno were built
- At **198 metres** above sea level, it constitutes the **lowest region of Switzerland**
- The delta is **constantly expanding** into Lake Maggiore as the Maggia deposits sediment
- About **10 hectares** of the estuary are shared between Locarno (93%) and Ascona (7%)
- The wetland is periodically submerged by floods from both the lake and the river — a dynamic, shifting landscape
- Since **1990**, the delta has been included among the **protected areas of the Canton of Ticino** (aree protette del Cantone Ticino)
- The nearby **Bolle di Magadino** (on the opposite shore) is a **Ramsar wetland** of international significance

## 12. Notable Historical Facts

- **1512–1513**: Swiss Confederate forces conquered Ascona (along with Locarno and other Ticinese territories) from the **Duchy of Milan** after the **Battle of Novara**
- Ascona became part of the **Baliaggi Ultramontani** (Bailiwicks Beyond the Mountains), governed by rotating Swiss bailiffs for nearly **300 years**
- **1803**: Napoleon's **Act of Mediation** unified the region into the **Canton of Ticino**
- **1882**: The opening of the **Gotthard Railway Tunnel** transformed access to the Lago Maggiore region, launching Ascona's slow transition from fishing village to resort
- **1925**: The nearby **Locarno Treaties** (Trattati di Locarno) were signed in Locarno — a landmark attempt at European peace after World War I, earning the lead negotiators the **Nobel Peace Prize**
- **2025**: Ascona was named **Swiss Village of the Year** (Dorf des Jahres), celebrated for its authenticity, Mediterranean charm, and ability to blend tradition with innovation

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The dancers of Monte Verità** — A child discovers bare footprints glowing on the hillside above Ascona; following them uphill, they meet the ghost of a young dancer from 1917 who needs a partner for the final performance of "Song to the Sun" before the memory fades forever
2. **The painter's sketchbook** — In the Museo Comunale d'Arte Moderna, a child touches Marianne Werefkin's sketchbook and is pulled into a vivid Expressionist Ascona where colours are alive, the lake is purple, and the Borgo's houses rearrange themselves like a living painting
3. **The Baroness's garden** — On the Isole di Brissago, a child discovers that one of the 2,000 plant species blooms only once a century; following the Baroness's spirit through the garden at midnight, they must find and protect the flower before sunrise
4. **The secret of the cloister** — Behind one of the 27 bas-reliefs in the Collegio Papio cloister, a child discovers a hidden message from Bartolomeo Papio himself — leading to a treasure hunt through Ascona's four medieval castles connected by legendary underground passages
5. **Giovanni's last canvas** — In the Chiesa dei Santi Pietro e Paolo, a child notices that a figure in Serodine's painting is missing; stepping into the Baroque canvas, they must travel the Road to Emmaus alongside the painted pilgrims to return the lost figure before the church bell strikes midnight
6. **The jazz boat** — During JazzAscona, a child sitting on the Piazza Giuseppe Motta hears trumpet notes rising from beneath the lake; following the music in a small boat, they discover a ghostly New Orleans riverboat anchored below the surface, where jazz musicians from every era play an eternal jam session
7. **The mountain of truth** — A child camping on Monte Verità finds that the old Bauhaus hotel comes alive at night, each room a portal to a different era: 1900 (the vegetarian utopians), 1915 (Hans Arp sketching on the lakeshore), 1917 (dancers in the meadow), 1929 (the Baron's art collection) — and they must find the "truth" the mountain was named for to return home
8. **The fisherman's last catch** — An old fisherman on the Borgo waterfront tells a child the story of when Ascona was just a village of nets and boats; the child falls asleep and wakes in medieval Ascona, where they must help defend the castrum from invaders using only what a fishing village has — nets, boats, and cunning
