## 1. Founding and Early Origins

- The toponym is first recorded in **824 AD** as **Postclave**, in a Carolingian diploma referring to a donation of the local parish church to the **Abbey of Saint-Denis** near Paris
- The name likely derives from the Latin **post clavem** ("beyond the key/pass"), referring to the valley's position beyond the **Bernina Pass**
- Together with the **Veltlin** (Valtellina), the Val Poschiavo was donated by **Charlemagne** (748-814) to the **monastery of Saint-Denis** — linking this remote Alpine valley to one of the most powerful Frankish abbeys
- In the **12th and 13th centuries**, actual lordship shifted to the feudal lords of **Matsch-Venosta** (the Vogte von Matsch), who administered the valley on behalf of the Bishop of Chur
- As early as the **12th century**, the municipalities (**comuni**) of **Poschiavo** and **Brusio** had their own independent administrative structures — an early form of Alpine self-governance
- Following the subjugation of Como and Valtellina to Milan in **1335**, Val Poschiavo fell under the rule of the **Visconti** dynasty in **1350**

## 2. The Three Leagues and Swiss Integration

- In **1408**, seeking protection from Milanese domination, the people of Val Poschiavo voluntarily submitted to the jurisdiction of the **Bishop of Chur** and joined the **Caddea League** (Gotteshausbund / Lega Caddea), one of the Three Leagues of the Grisons
- In **1524**, the valley was fully incorporated into the **Free State of the Three Leagues** (Freistaat der Drei Bunde / Stato libero delle Tre Leghe)
- The **Reformation** arrived in Poschiavo between **1547 and 1549**, brought by **Giuseppe della Rovere** and the former papal nuncio **Pier Paolo Vergerio**, who established a **printing press** in Poschiavo in **1549** to publish Protestant tracts
- Initially, both denominations shared the parish church of **San Vittore**; the Reformed community was forced to flee persecution in **1623** during the turmoil of the **Bundner Wirren** (Graubunden troubles)
- After the turmoil subsided, a formal agreement in **1642** gave the Reformed community the right to build its own church, the **Chiesa della Santissima Trinita**, in exchange for renouncing all claims to shared use of Catholic churches and chapels in the valley
- This **Protestant-Catholic coexistence** became a defining feature of Poschiavo — both communities have lived side by side for nearly **four centuries**, a remarkable record of religious tolerance in the Alps
- In **1797**, when Napoleon annexed the Valtellina to the **Cisalpine Republic**, Val Poschiavo **rejected annexation** and remained loyal to the Grisons — a decisive moment in the valley's Swiss identity
- Through the **1803 Act of Mediation**, Val Poschiavo became part of the **Canton of Graubunden** and the Swiss Confederation
- Local liberal figures **Franz and Stefano Ragazzi**, together with **Tommaso Lardelli**, founded the weekly newspaper **Il Grigione Italiano** — championing Italian-language rights within the predominantly German-speaking canton

## 3. Geography — From Glaciers to Chestnuts

- **Val Poschiavo** (German: **Puschlav**) stretches approximately **25 km** from the **Bernina Pass** (2,328 m) in the north to the Italian border at **Campocologno** (553 m) in the south
- This dramatic **1,775-metre altitude drop** creates an extraordinary climate gradient: from **high-alpine glacial landscapes** at the summit to **Mediterranean-influenced vegetation** (chestnuts, vines, even palms) in the lower valley
- The valley is drained by the **Poschiavino** river, which flows from the **Lago Bianco** at the Bernina summit through the entire valley into Italy
- The town of **Poschiavo** lies at **1,014 m** above sea level, sheltered in a natural basin surrounded by peaks exceeding **3,000 m**
- Val Poschiavo is one of the **four Italian-speaking valleys of Graubunden** (alongside Val Mesolcina, Val Calanca, and Val Bregaglia), making it an **Italian-language enclave** within the predominantly German- and Romansh-speaking canton
- Alongside standard Italian, locals speak **Pus'ciavin**, a Lombard dialect used in family life, folk songs, and storytelling

## 4. Lago di Poschiavo — The Valley's Mirror

- **Lago di Poschiavo** (Lej da Pus-ciav) is a natural lake at **962 m** elevation in the heart of the valley, **2.5 km** long, with a surface area of **1.98 km²** and a maximum depth of **85 m**
- The lake was formed naturally by **massive rock slides** from both sides of the valley, which met to create a natural dam
- It is both fed and drained by the **Poschiavino** river
- Between **1904 and 1907**, the lake was converted into a **reservoir** for the **Campocologno hydroelectric power station** — at the time the **largest high-pressure hydroelectric plant in Europe**, with a drop height of **418 m**
- In operation, the water level can vary by up to **7 m**, giving a usable storage capacity of **15.1 million cubic metres**
- The village of **Le Prese**, on the lake's western shore, developed as a small spa resort in the **19th century** — today a peaceful base for walks around the lake (the **Giro del Lago** trail)
- The company that built the power station, founded on **14 June 1904**, evolved into **Repower AG**, still headquartered in the region

## 5. The Bernina Railway — UNESCO World Heritage

- The **Bernina railway line** was built between **1908 and 1910** by the **Bernina-Bahngesellschaft (BB)**, financed by the investors of the **Kraftwerke Brusio** hydroelectric company, without cantonal subsidy
- The first section (**Tirano to Poschiavo**) opened on **1 July 1908**; the full line to St. Moritz opened on **5 July 1910**
- The line climbs from **429 m** (Tirano) to **2,253 m** (Ospizio Bernina) — the **highest railway crossing of the Alps** — without the aid of **rack-and-pinion** (cogwheel), using adhesion traction alone on gradients of up to **7%**
- The **61 km** Bernina line includes **13 tunnels**, **52 viaducts and bridges**, and passes through landscapes ranging from Mediterranean to high-alpine
- The **Brusio spiral viaduct** (Kreisviadukt von Brusio), opened **1 July 1908**, is a nine-arched stone viaduct that forms a complete **360-degree loop** to manage the steep descent — **110 m** long with a radius of **70 m**, one of the most iconic railway structures in Europe
- **Alp Grum** station (2,091 m), accessible only by train or on foot, offers a breathtaking **180-degree panorama** of the **Palu Glacier**, **Lago di Palu**, and the entire Val Poschiavo
- In **1943**, the financially struggling BB was taken over by the **Rhaetian Railway (RhB)**
- In **July 2008**, the Albula and Bernina lines were jointly inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** under the name **"Rhaetian Railway in the Albula / Bernina Landscapes"** — recognized for their outstanding engineering and harmonious integration into the Alpine landscape
- The famous **Bernina Express** panoramic train runs from **Chur** (or Davos/St. Moritz) to **Tirano**, passing through Poschiavo as a highlight of the journey

## 6. The Spaniolenviertel — Palazzi of the Returning Emigrants

- From the **17th century onward**, many Poschiavini (people of Poschiavo) emigrated for economic reasons, travelling to **Spain, Italy, France, England, Poland, Russia, Australia**, and the Americas
- They became renowned as **confectioners, pastry chefs (Zuckerbacker/pasticceri), and cafe owners** — in **Madrid, Barcelona, and Porto**, legendary confectioneries and restaurants were run by Poschiavo families, often under the name **"Suisse"**
- After ten or twenty years abroad, the most successful emigrants returned home and proudly displayed their wealth by commissioning **magnificent palazzi** on the southern outskirts of the town
- The **Quartiere dei Palazzi** (Palazzi quarter), popularly called the **Spaniolenviertel** ("Spanish Quarter"), is a striking **120-metre-long row of colourful villas** built to plans by Venetian architect **Giovanni Sottovia** in the second half of the **19th century**
- Notable among the returning emigrants was **Antonio Semadeni**, a pastry chef who made his fortune in **Poland** and later served as **Swiss consul in Warsaw**; he built the magnificent **Casa Console** in **1856**, today the **Museo d'Arte Casa Console**, housing over **100 paintings** including works by **Carl Spitzweg**
- The emigration tradition continued well into the **20th century**: the family histories of many Poschiavini include branches in Marseille, London, and South America — the book **"The Baker's Daughter"** by Louise Johncox traces her family back to **Carlo Bernardo Forer**, born in Poschiavo in **1839**
- The name **"Hotel Suisse"** or **"Cafe Suisse"** became a hallmark of Poschiavini establishments across southern Europe — a badge of Swiss quality and precision applied to the art of pastry-making
- Even today, the **Hotel Suisse** in Poschiavo's centre (on the Piazza Comunale) carries the name as a reminder of this proud tradition

## 7. Chiesa Collegiata di San Vittore Mauro and the Historic Centre

- The **Chiesa collegiata di San Vittore Mauro** is the main Catholic parish church of Poschiavo, combining **Romanesque, late Gothic, and Baroque** architectural styles
- The **Romanesque bell tower** was erected in the first half of the **13th century** and remains the most striking element of the building
- Construction of the church began in the **12th century**; the Baroque interior was essentially completed in **1653**, with a carved Baroque portal dating from **1700**
- Fragments of **late Gothic paintings** from the early **1500s** survive on the ceiling; the interior features ribbed vaults, Gothic windows, and a carved altar with figures of saints
- The **Piazza Comunale** (Plazza da Cumun) is the heart of the old town (**borgo**), overlooked by San Vittore, the **Oratorio di Sant'Anna** with its ossuary chapel (Beinhaus), the Baroque **Cappella di Santa Maria Presentata**, and an ancient **defensive tower**
- The **Museo Poschiavino**, founded in **April 1950**, is housed in the **Palazzo de Bassus-Mengotti** (built **1655**) and documents the valley's history, emigration, and daily life; its annex, **Casa Tome**, dates to **1357/58** and is one of the **oldest preserved farmhouses in the Alpine region**
- **Ten buildings** in Poschiavo are listed as **Swiss Heritage sites of national significance**

## 8. The Cavaglia Glacier Garden — Marmitte dei Giganti

- At **Cavaglia** (1,693 m), at the foot of the Bernina massif and the **Piz Palu**, lies the **Giardino dei Ghiacciai** (Glacier Garden), one of the natural wonders of Val Poschiavo
- The garden contains a remarkable collection of **marmitte dei giganti** ("giants' pots") — cylindrical holes of considerable size ground into the bedrock over thousands of years by gravel and debris transported by pressurized **glacial meltwater**
- These potholes were carved by the retreating **Palu Glacier** during and after the last ice age
- The site is accessible by a short walk (approximately **10 minutes**) from the **Cavaglia railway station** on the Bernina line
- Since **July 2021**, a new pathway through the **Cavaglia Gorge** allows visitors to discover additional potholes and erosion formations
- Entry is **free of charge**; guided visits are offered on **Tuesdays, Thursdays, Saturdays, and Sundays** starting at 14:00 from the station
- The combination of the UNESCO railway, the glacier garden, and the dramatic Alpine scenery makes Cavaglia a highlight of the Bernina journey

## 9. Notable People of Poschiavo

- **Guido Fanconi** (1892-1979), born in Poschiavo, became one of the **founders of modern paediatrics**; as professor and head of the **Kinderspital** (Children's Hospital) of the University of Zurich from **1929**, he made it one of the most renowned children's hospitals in the world; two conditions bear his name — **Fanconi syndrome** (1936, a renal tubular disorder) and **Fanconi anaemia** (1927, a hereditary bone marrow failure)
- **Wolfgang Hildesheimer** (1916-1991), a German-Jewish writer regarded as the most important writer of the **absurd** in twentieth-century German literature, discovered Val Poschiavo in **autumn 1956** and settled there in **1957** with help from **Friedrich Durrenmatt**; he lived in Poschiavo for over **three decades**, received **honorary citizenship** in **1982**, and wrote major novels including **Tynset** (1965) and **Marbot** (1981) while living in the valley
- **Pier Paolo Vergerio** (1498-1565), former papal nuncio turned Protestant reformer, established a **printing press** in Poschiavo in **1549** and used it to publish tracts against Rome — making tiny Poschiavo a flashpoint of the **Reformation in the Italian-speaking Alps**
- **Giovanni Sottovia**, Venetian architect, designed the famous **Palazzi row** and founded the **Poschiavo Italian Workers Society** (Societa Operaia Italiana) in **1867**
- **Evelina Raselli** (born **1992** in Poschiavo), Swiss women's ice hockey forward and **bronze medallist** at the **2014 Winter Olympics** in Sochi — a modern sporting hero from the valley

## 10. 100% Valposchiavo — The Organic Valley

- Since **2012**, the **100% Valposchiavo** project has been transforming the valley into a global pioneer of **organic farming** (agricoltura biologica)
- Over **95%** of agricultural land in the valley is now cultivated by **Bio Suisse-certified organic farms** — a figure unique in Switzerland and worldwide
- More than **150 products** have obtained the **100% Valposchiavo** label, produced by over **60 farms** and processed locally
- **13 restaurants** in the valley participate, serving dishes made exclusively from local organic ingredients
- The agricultural diversity ranges from **cereal growers and cheesemakers** to **herb growers, butchers, and fruit producers** — reflecting the valley's extraordinary climate gradient from Alpine to Mediterranean
- The project represents a deliberate strategy of **territorial rural development**, positioning Val Poschiavo as a model for sustainable Alpine agriculture in the 21st century

## 11. The Bernina Pass — Ancient Trade Route

- The **Bernina Pass** (Passo del Bernina, **2,328 m**) connects the **Engadin** valley with Val Poschiavo and, beyond, with **Tirano** in the Italian Valtellina
- The pass has been used since **prehistoric and Roman times** as a trade and transit route, initially as rudimentary **mule paths** suited to pack animals
- It gained strategic importance in **1512** when the Three Leagues conquered the Valtellina; the Bernina became a key route between Switzerland and the **Republic of Venice**, facilitating trade and diplomacy
- By the mid-**16th century**, French messengers regularly travelled between **Lyon and Venice** via the Albula, Bernina, and Aprica passes
- The Bernina was one of the main routes for exporting the famous **Valtellina wine** northward into the Engadin and beyond
- Construction of the modern **pass road** began in **1842** and was completed in **1865**, finally connecting Val Poschiavo by carriage road to the rest of the canton
- The pass remains the **highest paved road in the Eastern Alps** and is typically open from **June to October**
- At the summit, the **Lago Bianco** (White Lake, 2,234 m) and **Lej Nair** (Black Lake) lie side by side — one feeding the Inn river northward to the Danube and Black Sea, the other feeding the Poschiavino southward to the Adda, Po, and Adriatic — a striking **continental divide**
- Temporary **hospices** (ospizi) sheltered travellers at the summit in medieval times; the tradition continues with the modern **Ospizio Bernina** restaurant at the railway's highest station

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The pastry chef who came home** — A child in Poschiavo discovers that the colourful Palazzi houses each hold a hidden recipe from a different country; following clues left by a returning confectioner, they bake their way through Spain, Poland, and France to unlock the secret of the grandest villa
2. **The spiral viaduct's secret** — On the Bernina Express, a child notices the train going in a full circle on the Brusio viaduct; the loop is actually a time portal, and each revolution takes them deeper into the railway's construction in 1908, where they must help the engineers solve the impossible gradient
3. **The giants' kitchen** — At the Cavaglia Glacier Garden, a child peers into a marmitte dei giganti and falls in; deep underground, ancient glacier spirits are still grinding their enormous pots, cooking stone soup from the minerals of the Alps, and need a human helper to taste-test the recipe
4. **The two churches of Poschiavo** — A child discovers that the Catholic bell tower of San Vittore and the Reformed chiesa della Trinita ring their bells in a secret code at midnight; deciphering the code reveals a friendship pact made in 1642 between two children — one Catholic, one Protestant — hidden inside the tower wall
5. **The train that climbed without teeth** — A child riding the Bernina railway asks why the train has no cogwheel; the locomotive itself answers, telling the proud story of how it was the first to climb the highest Alpine crossing on smooth rails alone, and challenges the child to a race to Alp Grum
6. **The lake that breathes** — A child at Lago di Poschiavo notices the water level rising and falling like breathing; following the Poschiavino river upstream, they discover that the lake is alive — formed when two ancient rockslides shook hands across the valley — and it sighs seven metres with every season
7. **Dr. Fanconi's smallest patient** — A child in Poschiavo finds the old house where baby Guido Fanconi was born in 1892; inside, a magical stethoscope lets them hear the heartbeats of children around the world whom Fanconi's discoveries would one day save
8. **The writer in the valley** — A child meets the ghost of Wolfgang Hildesheimer wandering through Poschiavo at night, unable to sleep (just like the narrator of his novel Tynset); together they walk the moonlit borgo, and Hildesheimer shows the child how to turn sleeplessness into stories
