## 1. Geography — Where the Lake Becomes the Rhine

- Stein am Rhein lies in the eastern part of the canton of **Schaffhausen**, at the precise point where the **Rhine flows out of the Untersee** — the western arm of Lake Constance (Bodensee)
- Lake Constance has two parts: the **Obersee** (Upper Lake, ~473 km²) and the **Untersee** (Lower Lake, ~63 km²), connected by the Seerhein. The **High Rhine** begins at Stein am Rhein, flowing westward toward the Rhine Falls at Schaffhausen
- The town sits on **both banks** of the Rhine, with the historic old town on the north bank and the railway station on the south
- Nearby cities: **Schaffhausen** (~20 km west), **Konstanz** (~20 km east), **Zürich** (~55 km southwest)
- The surrounding landscape includes the **Seerücken** hills, vineyard-covered slopes, and the broad, glassy surface of the Rhine — still lake-calm at this point before it picks up speed downstream
- Population: approximately **3,800** inhabitants (2024) — a remarkably small town for its outsized cultural significance

## 2. Roman Tasgetium — A Fortress at the Rhine Crossing

- In the Roman period, the settlement at this Rhine crossing was called **Tasgetium** — a name recorded by the geographer **Claudius Ptolemaeus** (2nd century AD) as "Taxgaition," deriving from a Celtic personal name, Tasgetius
- The civilian settlement (vicus) lay on the south bank in present-day **Eschenz** (canton Thurgau), while the fortress stood on the north bank in present-day Stein am Rhein
- A **wooden bridge** spanned the Rhine here: dendrochronological analysis of bridge piles discovered in 1986 dates the first bridge construction to **81–82 AD**
- The **Late Roman fortress** was built under Emperors **Diocletian and Maximian around 294 AD** as part of the Danube-Iller-Rhine Limes — a vast defensive line following the empire's military reorganisation. The fortress had a diamond-shaped layout measuring **88 × 91 meters**, protected by **twelve towers** including four polygonal corner towers
- An estimated **100–200 soldiers** plus civilians occupied the fort for three to four generations before it was abandoned **shortly after the year 400**
- In **September 1969**, workers laying heating pipes discovered two skeletons and a glass bowl fragment — this led to the excavation of a **necropolis of 83 graves**. The grave goods include exceptional Roman glassware, notably the **Jagdschale** (hunting cup, made in the Rhineland between 260–290 AD) and a **double glass jug** from Cologne (early 4th century)

## 3. The Founding of the Town — Emperor Henry II and St. George's Abbey

- Around **1005–1007**, Emperor **Henry II** (973–1024) relocated a Benedictine monastery from its original site on the **Hohentwiel** (a volcanic hill near Singen in Germany) to Stein am Rhein
- At that time, Stein am Rhein was little more than a **small fishing village** at the Rhine crossing
- Henry II gave the abbots extensive rights: **market privileges, minting rights, and toll collection** — transforming the settlement into a prosperous trading town
- The monastery's strategic position — at the intersection of major **road and river routes** — made it an economic and political anchor for the region
- The town grew steadily around the monastery, developing its characteristic tight medieval layout with half-timbered houses along narrow streets leading to the central market square

## 4. Kloster St. Georgen — The Benedictine Monastery

- **Kloster St. Georgen** is one of the **best-preserved medieval monastery complexes in Switzerland**
- The abbey church is dedicated to **Saint George** and features Romanesque elements with high round-arched windows and stone walls adorned with frescoes
- The main monastery buildings date from the **13th to 15th centuries**; the late Gothic **cloister** was built between **1400 and 1480**
- The last abbot, **David von Winkelsheim** (elected 1499, died 1526), completed the monastery's architectural programme. He commissioned the **Festsaal** (banquet hall, built around **1515**) — a reception room for high-ranking guests decorated with a fresco cycle that counts among the **earliest Renaissance paintings north of the Alps**
- The monastery's paintings are by **Thomas Schmid** and **Ambrosius Holbein** (brother of the more famous Hans Holbein). Ambrosius painted two death-dance scenes on the window reveals of the Festsaal
- During the **Reformation in 1525**, the monastery was secularised and its assets taken over by **Zürich**. The last abbot David von Winkelsheim was suspected of collusion with the Habsburgs and imprisoned, but escaped to **Radolfzell**, where he died shortly after in 1526
- From the Reformation until the end of the 18th century, the buildings served as the **residence of the Zürich bailiffs**
- In **1926**, the **Gottfried Keller Foundation** acquired the complex with support from the canton and city. Since **1945**, it has been owned by the **Swiss Confederation** and operated as a museum by the Federal Office of Culture

## 5. Burg Hohenklingen — The Castle Above the Town

- **Hohenklingen Castle** stands at **590 meters above sea level** on the wooded ridge above Stein am Rhein, offering panoramic views over the old town, the Rhine, and the Untersee
- Around **1200**, **Walter von Klingen** erected a **residential tower** on the site. By **1220**, a ring wall and palace were added; between **1250 and 1280**, a gate, keep, and further palace extensions followed
- The castle was the **first toll castle on the Rhine** — the Lords of Klingen controlled river traffic and collected tolls, while also serving as **protectors (Vögte) of St. George's Abbey** and the townspeople
- **Walther III von Klingen** (c. 1220–1284), a descendant of the castle's builders, was a **Minnesänger** (lyric poet) — eight of his songs are preserved in the famous **Codex Manesse** manuscript. The miniature depicts him as the victor of a joust
- Around **1460**, battlements for **firearms** were installed, reflecting the military modernisation of the era
- The castle played a strategic role during the **Swabian War (1499)** and the **Thirty Years' War (1618–1648)**
- In the **15th century**, the city of Stein am Rhein purchased the castle. From the 15th to 19th century, it served as the **residence of Zürich's bailiffs**
- Major renovations: **1895–1897** (total renovation of the dilapidated structure) and **2005–2007** (restoration including a modern lift)
- Remarkably, the castle has been **spared from war damage** throughout its entire history. Today it houses a **restaurant** with one of the finest views in eastern Switzerland

## 6. The Painted Facades — Switzerland's Most Beautiful Town Square

- The **Rathausplatz** (Town Hall Square) is the heart of Stein am Rhein and is often called **Switzerland's most beautiful town square** — every building is adorned with magnificent painted facades
- The facade paintings date primarily from the **Renaissance** (1520s), with some from the **Baroque period** and restorations around **1900**
- Most of the original frescoes were painted by the German artist **Thomas Schmid** (also written Schmede) between **1520 and 1525**, making this the **world's largest concentration of Renaissance facade frescoes**
- The paintings depict **biblical stories, mythological scenes, everyday life, moral tales**, and references to the businesses housed within — each house tells its own story on its walls
- **Zum Weissen Adler** (White Eagle): Considered the most beautifully decorated building. Features the **oldest preserved Renaissance mural in Switzerland** (c. 1520), with scenes from Boccaccio's Decameron and the Bocca della Verità. The centrepiece is a white eagle, and the facade includes a depiction of Hercules fighting the Hydra
- **Haus zur Sonne** (House of the Sun): The oldest hotel in the town, built c. 1520 under Mayor Johann Jakob Meyer. Its main fresco shows a large laughing sun overlooking a man hiding in a barrel while being interrogated by soldiers
- **Zum Rothen Ochsen** (Red Ox): The oldest tavern in town, first documented in **1395**. Features a red ox fresco alongside medieval village scenes — including a woman conversing with God
- **Steinerner Trauben** (Stone Grapes): At no. 11 on the square. Its main fresco depicts two grey-haired men in ancient Greek dress carrying a large vine of heavy purple grapes
- **Vordere Krone** (Front Crown): At Rathausplatz 7, a heritage-listed building. Its crown fresco was painted in **1734**
- Owners of frescoed buildings **must maintain the paintings at their own expense** in the same condition as the originals — a civic obligation that has preserved this extraordinary ensemble for centuries

## 7. The Rathaus (Town Hall)

- The **Rathaus** was built between **1539 and 1542** as a multi-purpose building for merchants and the town council, also serving as a cloth, grain, and department store
- It received its present form in **1898–1900**: the market hall was enclosed, and a bay window and exterior paintings were added
- The history paintings are by **Carl von Häberlin** (1832–1911) from Stuttgart, depicting scenes from Stein am Rhein's history including the **Battle of Murten**. Coats of arms and figures were painted by **Christian Schmidt** from Zürich
- Inside, the **Gothic Great Council Chamber** houses a famous **collection of coats of arms from 1542** and a weapons collection (15th–18th centuries)
- The smaller council chamber features **rococo gilded panelling** installed in **1766**
- The small council hall has hosted continuous city council meetings **since 1542** — nearly 500 years of unbroken civic governance

## 8. The Town Gates — Untertor and Obertor

- The medieval fortifications of Stein am Rhein included a **city wall** (now largely absorbed into the row of houses) and **two principal gates**
- **Untertor** (Lower Gate): First documented in **1367**, it stood at the Rhine end of the old town, controlling access from the river and the bridge. Outer bailey trapdoors were added in **1520**. The original gate was **destroyed in the 1945 bombing** but was **rebuilt true to the original**, featuring clock faces on both sides
- **Obertor** (Upper Gate): Built in **1360** as part of a fortification expansion project. A three-storey structure with embrasures for arrows and projectiles, it also served as a watchtower. The Obertor was **destroyed in a town fire in 1688** but was restored shortly afterward. Its clock face is decorated with historic frescoes

## 9. The Wakker Prize of 1972 — A Pioneering Award

- In **1972**, Stein am Rhein became the **very first winner** of the **Wakkerpreis**, awarded by the **Schweizer Heimatschutz** (Swiss Heritage Society)
- The prize was made possible through a bequest from Geneva businessman and real estate entrepreneur **Henri-Louis Wakker**, who died in 1972
- The jury praised Stein am Rhein for caring for an **"extraordinary and unique townscape in exemplary fashion"** ("zu einem aussergewöhnlichen, ja einzigartigen Stadtbild in beispielhafter Weise Sorge getragen"), noting that nowhere else in Switzerland could one find **so many outstanding individual buildings in such a small space**
- The award was worth **CHF 20,000**, but its true value lay in **public recognition** — putting Stein am Rhein on the map as a model for heritage preservation
- Crucially, the jury emphasised that Stein am Rhein was **not a museum but a living community** — its preservation was organic, driven by residents, authorities, and expert committees working together
- The Wakkerpreis has been awarded annually ever since, with the 50th anniversary celebrated in 2022

## 10. The 1945 Bombing — A Tragic Misunderstanding

- On **22 February 1945**, American bombers of the **USAAF** accidentally bombed Stein am Rhein, a neutral Swiss town
- The bombing killed **9 people** (4 women and 5 children) and seriously injured **15 others**
- The pilot later reported that during the bombing he had seen **"red squares with large white crosses on the roofs"** — the Swiss neutrality markings — but, like many American pilots at the time, **did not recognise the Swiss flag**
- The **Untertor** (Lower Gate) was destroyed in the attack, later rebuilt true to the original
- The incident was part of a broader pattern of accidental Allied bombings of Swiss targets during WWII. Allied investigations found that bad weather broke up formations over France and strong winds **nearly doubled the bombers' ground speed**, causing navigational confusion
- The accidental bombings ended after US Chief of Staff **George Marshall** met the Swiss in Geneva in **March 1945** and agreed to full compensation for the damage

## 11. Wine Growing on the Slopes

- The steep vineyards on the **Wolkenstein hills** descend to the Rhine between Stein am Rhein and Hemishofen, covering approximately **32 hectares**
- The soils here are **shallow, sandy-gravel** — distinct from the limestone-rich Klettgau region further west in the canton
- The Schaffhausen wine region as a whole covers approximately **480 hectares**, with **60% planted in Pinot Noir** (locally called **Blauburgunder**) — the undisputed signature grape
- Wine production in the region dates back to the **Roman period** but was developed extensively by **monks during the medieval era**, when Schaffhausen was regarded as one of Switzerland's most important wine-producing areas
- Several local wineries (e.g., **Weinbau Leibacher**, **Weinbau Trutmann**) offer tastings and vineyard walks through the vines above the Rhine

## 12. Notable Historical Timeline

- **81–82 AD**: First wooden bridge across the Rhine at Tasgetium (dendrochronological dating)
- **c. 294 AD**: Late Roman fortress built under Diocletian and Maximian
- **c. 400 AD**: Roman fortress abandoned
- **1005–1007**: Emperor Henry II relocates monastery from Hohentwiel to Stein am Rhein
- **c. 1200**: Walter von Klingen builds the first residential tower at Hohenklingen
- **1360**: Obertor (Upper Gate) built
- **1367**: First mention of the Untertor (Lower Gate)
- **1395**: First documented mention of the Roter Ochsen tavern
- **1459**: Stein am Rhein enters treaty with Zürich and Schaffhausen
- **1484**: Annexed by Zürich
- **1499**: David von Winkelsheim elected last abbot; castle reinforced during the Swabian War
- **c. 1515**: Festsaal of the monastery completed with Renaissance frescoes
- **1520–1525**: Thomas Schmid paints the Renaissance facade frescoes on the Rathausplatz
- **1525**: Monastery secularised during the Reformation
- **1539–1542**: Rathaus (Town Hall) built
- **1688**: Obertor destroyed by fire and rebuilt
- **1895–1897**: Major renovation of Hohenklingen Castle
- **22 February 1945**: Accidental American bombing kills 9, destroys the Untertor
- **1972**: First Wakkerpreis awarded to Stein am Rhein

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The walls that tell stories** — A child discovers that the painted figures on the Rathausplatz houses come alive at night, each facade telling its own tale; the child must hear all the stories before dawn to break an old curse
2. **The Roman glass bowl** — A child finds a glowing shard of Roman glass near the Rhine and is transported back to Tasgetium in 294 AD, where soldiers are building the fortress and a bridge across the river
3. **The last abbot's secret room** — In the monastery museum, a child discovers a hidden door behind a fresco in the Festsaal, leading to David von Winkelsheim's private study where the abbot left a final riddle before fleeing to Radolfzell
4. **The minnesinger of Hohenklingen** — Walther von Klingen's ghost still haunts the castle, singing his old songs from the Codex Manesse; a child must learn the words to his lost ninth song to set his spirit free
5. **The sun that laughed** — The laughing sun on the Haus zur Sonne winks at a passing child and invites them into the painted world, where every house facade is a portal to a different century of the town's history
6. **The Wakker Prize treasure hunt** — When the town receives a mysterious letter claiming the original Wakker Prize trophy has been hidden somewhere in the old town, a child must solve clues at each painted house, both gates, the castle, and the monastery to find it
7. **The bridge builder of Tasgetium** — A child befriends the Roman engineer who built the first wooden bridge across the Rhine in 82 AD, helping to solve the puzzle of how to anchor wooden piles in the swift current
8. **The white eagle and the red ox** — The heraldic animals from the painted houses escape their facades during a thunderstorm and run through the old town; the child must coax each animal back to its correct wall before the frescoes fade forever
