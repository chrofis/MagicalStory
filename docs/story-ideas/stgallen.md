## 1. The Legend of Gallus — The Irish Monk and the Bear (612 AD)

- **Gallus** (c. 550–c. 640), also known as **Saint Gall**, was an Irish monk from **Bangor**, one of twelve companions who joined the missionary **Saint Columbanus** on his journey across continental Europe
- Around **612**, when Columbanus crossed the Alps into Italy, Gallus fell ill and was left behind near **Arbon** on Lake Constance, nursed back to health by a local deacon named **Hiltibod**
- Once recovered, Gallus retreated into the wild **Steinach valley** (Steinachtal) and established a simple **hermitage** (Einsiedelei) — a prayer cell in the forest
- **The Bear Legend**: According to the beloved 9th-century account, when Gallus sat by his campfire one night, a **wild bear** emerged from the forest. Rather than fleeing, Gallus calmly commanded the bear to **fetch wood for the fire**. The bear obeyed, and in return Gallus gave the animal **a loaf of bread**, ordering it never to harm humans or animals again
- This tale is depicted on a famous **9th-century ivory tablet** and remains central to St. Gallen's identity — the bear appears on the **city's coat of arms** to this day
- Gallus lived as a hermit in the Steinach valley for nearly **30 years**, dying around **640** (his saint's day is **October 16**)
- His grave became a pilgrimage site, and the settlement that grew around it took his name: **Sankt Gallen**

## 2. The Abbey of St. Gall — From Hermitage to European Powerhouse

- In **719**, the Alemannic priest **Saint Otmar** (Othmar) transformed Gallus's modest hermitage into a proper **Benedictine monastery**, becoming its first abbot
- In **747**, the monastery officially adopted the **Rule of Saint Benedict** (Regula Benedicti)
- Under Abbot **Gozbert** (816–837), the abbey entered its **golden age** — a period of extraordinary artistic and intellectual output
- The abbey became an **independent principality** (Fürstabtei) between the 9th and 13th centuries, with the **Prince-Abbot** (Fürstabt) wielding both spiritual and secular power over a significant territory
- For centuries it was one of the **chief Benedictine abbeys in Europe**, a centre of learning, manuscript production, and the arts
- Abbot **Ulrich Rösch** (1463–1491) consolidated abbey territories into a cohesive **abbey state** (Klosterstaat)
- In **1805**, the canton replaced the abbey as governing authority — the monastery was **secularized** (Klosteraufhebung)
- The **Diocese of St. Gallen** was founded in **1823**, and the former abbey church became the **cathedral** (Kathedrale)
- In **1983**, the **Stiftsbezirk** (Abbey District) was inscribed as a **UNESCO World Heritage Site** — one of Switzerland's first three — recognized as "a perfect example of a great Carolingian monastery"

## 3. The Abbey Library (Stiftsbibliothek) — Pharmacy of the Soul

- The **Stiftsbibliothek** is one of the **oldest monastic libraries in the world**, and the third-oldest library still in operation (after the Biblioteca Capitolare di Verona and the library of Saint Catherine's Monastery on Mount Sinai)
- Above the ornate Baroque portal, carved in **1781** by **Franz Anton Dirr**, is the Greek inscription **ΨΥΧΗΣ ΙΑΤΡΕΙΟΝ** (Psyches Iatreion) — meaning **"Healing Place of the Soul"** (Seelenapotheke), echoing an inscription at the ancient library of **Ramesses II** in Thebes
- The magnificent **Rococo hall** was designed by architect **Peter Thumb** and built between **1758 and 1767**, with ceiling frescoes by **Josef Wannenmacher** and joinery by **Brother Gabriel Loser**
- The collection holds nearly **170,000 volumes**, including **2,100 manuscripts** dating from the 8th to 15th centuries and **1,650 incunabula** (books printed before 1500)
- The core of the collection is its corpus of **Early Medieval manuscripts** from the 8th to 11th centuries — most produced in the abbey's own **scriptorium** (Schreibstube)
- In **2017**, the library received UNESCO **Memory of the World** status for its written heritage spanning over **1,300 years**
- Today the library attracts more than **150,000 visitors per year** and operates as a modern research library with a focus on the Middle Ages

## 4. Famous Manuscripts — Treasures of the Scriptorium

- **The Plan of Saint Gall** (c. 820–830): The **only surviving major architectural drawing** from the roughly 700-year period between the fall of Rome and the 13th century. Drawn in red and brown ink on **five parchments sewn together** (113 × 78 cm), it depicts an ideal Benedictine monastery with church, workshops, brewery, infirmary, stables, and a building for bloodletting — designed to house **110 monks, 115 lay visitors, and 150 workers**. Dedicated to Abbot Gozbert, it was never actually built. Catalogued as **Codex Sangallensis 1092**
- **The Irish Gospels of St. Gall** (Codex Sangallensis 51): Among the **oldest Latin Gospel manuscripts** in the Western world, brought by Irish monks — beautifully illuminated with Celtic interlace patterns
- **Manuscript B of the Nibelungenlied**: One of the three primary medieval manuscripts of the great German heroic epic, kept in the abbey library
- **The Evangelium Longum**: A late 9th-century illuminated manuscript with an intricately carved **ivory cover** created by the monk **Tuotilo**
- All manuscripts are indexed as **Codices Sangallenses** and many are digitized through the **e-codices** virtual manuscript library of Switzerland

## 5. The Abbey's Great Scholars — Notker, Tuotilo, and Ekkehart

- The abbey's scriptorium produced not only manuscripts but **extraordinary scholars and artists**
- **Notker Balbulus** ("the Stammerer", c. 840–912): A Benedictine monk, composer, poet, and **librarian** at St. Gall. He authored the **Liber Hymnorum**, an important collection of early musical sequences, and the **Gesta Karoli Magni**, an early biography of Charlemagne. He helped make St. Gall one of the most important centres of **early medieval music**
- **Tuotilo** (d. c. 913): A monk, musician, and gifted ivory carver who created the celebrated cover of the Evangelium Longum. Educated alongside Notker and **Ratpert**, all three were composers
- **Ekkehart IV** (c. 980–1060): A scholar and chronicler who wrote the **Casus Sancti Galli** (History of St. Gall), a vivid account of life in the monastery
- Together, these monks ensured that St. Gall became a **beacon of Carolingian and Ottonian culture**, rivalling the great centres of Aachen and Reichenau

## 6. The Stiftsbezirk and Baroque Cathedral

- The **Stiftsbezirk** (Abbey District) is the ensemble of buildings surrounding the former monastery, forming the heart of the UNESCO World Heritage Site
- The current **cathedral** (Kathedrale) was built between **1755 and 1767**, commissioned by Prince-Abbot **Cölestin Gugger von Staudach** and designed by **Peter Thumb** of Vorarlberg — the same architect who built the library hall
- It is considered one of the **last monumental sacred buildings of the late Baroque era** in Europe
- The interior features lavish **frescoes** by Josef Wannenmacher, elaborate stucco work, and the **most complete historic set of church bells** in Switzerland
- The Baroque complex was built to accommodate both the Benedictine abbey and the **residence of the Prince-Abbot**, though it served this dual purpose only a few decades before the abbey's dissolution in **1805**

## 7. The Toggenburgerkrieg — When the Abbot's Power Crumbled (1712)

- The **Toggenburgerkrieg** (Toggenburg War), also called the **Second War of Villmergen**, was a Swiss civil war fought from **12 April to 11 August 1712** — simultaneously a religious war, a war for hegemony, and a subjects' uprising
- The trigger: **Prince-Abbot Leodegar Bürgisser** attempted to reassert traditional feudal rights over his Reformed (Protestant) subjects in the **County of Toggenburg** (Grafschaft Toggenburg), which had belonged to the abbey's principality since **1460**
- Beginning in **1707**, Bürgisser ordered the men of **Wattwil** to perform **corvée labour** (Frondienst) on road building. Wattwil's residents refused, claiming earlier exemption
- The conflict escalated: on one side stood the **Catholic inner cantons** and the Prince-Abbot; on the other, the **Reformed cantons** of Zürich and Bern, allied with the Toggenburg subjects
- The war ended with **victory for the Reformed side** at the **Peace of Aarau** (11 August 1712), which ended the political hegemony of the Catholic cantons that had existed since **1531**
- For the abbey, this was a decisive blow to the **Prince-Abbot's temporal power**, foreshadowing the eventual secularization of 1805

## 8. Vadian and the Reformation in St. Gallen

- **Joachim von Watt** (1484–1551), known by his humanist Latin name **Vadian**, was born in St. Gallen into a family of wealthy **linen merchants** (Leinwandhändler)
- He studied at the **University of Vienna** under the famous humanist **Conrad Celtis**, becoming a renowned scholar, geographer, and poet laureate
- Returning to St. Gallen, he was elected **Bürgermeister** (mayor) in **1526** and led the city's conversion to **Protestantism**
- He managed to maintain St. Gallen's Reformed status even after the Catholic victory in the **Second War of Kappel** (1531)
- A statue of Vadian stands at the central **Marktplatz**, and his name graces the city's main street (**Vadianstrasse**) — he remains the city's most celebrated historical figure after Gallus himself

## 9. The Textile Empire — City of a Thousand Stitches

- St. Gallen's textile tradition stretches back to the **Middle Ages**, when the abbey monks began producing **linen cloth** (Leinentuch). By the 15th century, hand-woven linen and intricate embroidery had become a major industry
- St. Gallen merchants traded linen **throughout Europe**, making the city a major commercial hub. The linen trade flourished until the **early 18th century**, when it was gradually replaced by **cotton production**
- Around **1850**, embroidery replaced cotton as the dominant industry, fuelled by the invention of **hand-embroidery machines** in the mid-1840s that could produce multiple stitched motifs simultaneously
- By the late 19th century, St. Gallen embroidery adorned the courts of Europe — a ball gown made with needlepoint lace is said to have belonged to **Empress Eugénie** (wife of Napoleon III)
- St. Gallen also pioneered **chemical lace** (Ätzspitze/Guipure): embroidery stitched onto a base material that is then chemically dissolved, leaving a free-standing lace fabric
- Around **1910**, embroidery was the **largest export branch of the Swiss economy** — **18% of total Swiss export value**. More than **50% of world embroidery production** came from St. Gallen
- **World War I** brought catastrophic collapse: demand for luxury textiles vanished overnight. St. Gallen's population dropped from **75,482 to 64,079** between 1910 and 1930 due to mass emigration
- The absolute low point: embroidery exports fell from **5,899 tonnes in 1913** to just **640 tonnes in 1935**
- Today, Eastern Swiss textile companies remain **global market leaders**, having diversified into haute couture, medical textiles, and filter technology. The **Textilmuseum St. Gallen** preserves this extraordinary heritage

## 10. The Old Town — 111 Erker and Painted Facades

- St. Gallen's **Altstadt** (Old Town) is famous for its **111 beautifully preserved Erker** (oriel windows) — ornate bay windows projecting from upper-floor facades
- These elaborately carved and brightly painted oriels were **status symbols** (Statussymbole) of wealthy textile merchants, built during a construction boom between **1650 and 1720**
- Many oriels bear **individual names**: the **Kamel-Erker** (Camel Oriel), the **Pelikan-Erker** (Pelican Oriel), the **Schwanen-Erker** (Swan Oriel), and the poetically named **"Allbier zum blauen Himmel"**
- The finest examples are concentrated on four streets: **Spisergasse**, **Marktgasse**, **Kugelgasse**, and **Schmiedgasse**
- The oriels tell stories through their carvings — depicting legends, mythical figures, historic events, and allegories of wealth and trade
- Combined with the **painted facades** (bemalte Fassaden) of the merchant houses, the Old Town creates one of the most distinctive streetscapes in Switzerland

## 11. Geography, the Bratwurst, and OLMA

- St. Gallen sits in a **valley at approximately 700 metres above sea level** in northeastern Switzerland, in a region where four countries' borders meet: Switzerland, Germany, Austria, and Liechtenstein
- The city lies between **Lake Constance** (Bodensee) to the north and the **Alpstein massif** to the south, crowned by the **Säntis** (2,502 m) — the landmark peak of the region, visible from the city on clear days
- The **St. Galler Bratwurst** is a culinary institution, with its recipe first recorded in the **1438 statutes of the Butchers' Guild** (Metzgerzunft). Made from **veal (50%+), bacon, spices, and fresh milk**, it holds **PGI** (Protected Geographical Indication) status
- The cardinal rule: **"Ohne Senf!"** (without mustard). Requesting mustard with a St. Galler Bratwurst is considered a serious cultural faux pas — locals insist the sausage's delicate flavour needs no masking
- The **OLMA** (Schweizer Messe für Landwirtschaft und Ernährung) is Switzerland's **largest public fair**, held annually in October since **1943** (growing from an agricultural exhibition first held in **1941** during wartime supply shortages). It draws **370,000–390,000 visitors** per year
- Star attraction since **1997**: the **Säulirennen** (pig race), where trained female pigs race toward a feeding trough before **3,000 cheering spectators**

## 12. Notable People Connected to St. Gallen

- **Saint Gallus** (c. 550–c. 640): Irish monk, founder of the hermitage, city's namesake and patron saint
- **Saint Otmar** (c. 689–759): First abbot, transformed the hermitage into a Benedictine monastery
- **Notker Balbulus** (c. 840–912): Monk, composer, poet, and librarian — one of the great minds of the Carolingian Renaissance
- **Tuotilo** (d. c. 913): Monk, musician, and ivory carver of the Evangelium Longum
- **Joachim von Watt / Vadian** (1484–1551): Humanist scholar, reformer, and Bürgermeister who brought the Reformation to St. Gallen
- **Ulrich Varnbüler** (1474–1545): Diplomat and imperial official, immortalized in a famous woodcut by **Albrecht Dürer**
- **Hans-Adam II** (b. 1945): Reigning Prince of Liechtenstein, alumnus of the **University of St. Gallen** (HSG) — one of Europe's leading business schools, founded in 1898

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The bear who built a fire** — The legend of Gallus retold from the bear's perspective: a curious bear encounters a strange, calm man in the forest who asks for firewood instead of running away — and offers bread in return
2. **The library where books heal** — A child discovers the Greek inscription above the abbey library door and steps inside to find that each ancient manuscript holds a cure: one book mends broken bones, another heals homesickness, another fixes a broken friendship
3. **The Plan that was never built** — A child finds the 1,200-year-old Plan of Saint Gall and is magically transported into the ideal monastery it depicts — exploring the brewery, the herb garden, the infirmary, and the scriptorium, meeting the monks who dreamed it up
4. **The embroidery that dressed a queen** — A young girl in 1900s St. Gallen learns to operate an embroidery machine, and the lace she creates travels across Europe to adorn the gown of an empress — following the fabric's journey from the Steinach valley to a Parisian ballroom
5. **The 111 windows that tell stories** — Each oriel window in the Old Town holds a frozen scene; when a child touches the carved Camel Oriel, the camel comes alive and leads a tour through the streets, with each named oriel revealing a different chapter of the city's history
6. **Notker's song** — A shy boy who stammers (like the real Notker Balbulus) discovers that when he sings instead of speaks, his words flow perfectly — and his melodies echo through the abbey, inspiring the monks to create the first musical sequences
7. **The great Bratwurst debate** — A child from Zürich accidentally asks for mustard at a St. Gallen sausage stand, sparking a humorous culinary adventure where the city's butchers teach the true art of the Bratwurst through the centuries, from 1438 to today
8. **The pig who won the race** — At the OLMA fair, a small runt piglet named Rosa is not expected to win the famous Säulirennen — but with encouragement from a farm child, she outruns them all to the feeding trough, becoming the unlikely champion of Switzerland's biggest fair
