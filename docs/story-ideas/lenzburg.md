## 1. Geography — The Seetal and Swiss Mittelland

- Lenzburg lies in the **Seetal** (lake valley), about **3 km south of the Aare river**, in the canton of **Aargau** in the central **Swiss Mittelland**
- The town sits on the small river **Aabach**, at the northern edge of the Seetal, roughly **30 km west of Zürich**
- Lenzburg is the capital of the **Lenzburg District** and has a population of approximately **11,000**
- The prominent **Schlossberg** (castle hill) rises steeply above the town, giving the castle its commanding position over the valley
- Nearby: **Lake Hallwil (Hallwilersee)**, a picturesque glacial lake stretching south through the Seetal — no building is permitted on its banks, preserving its natural landscape
- In **1938**, Sir **Malcolm Campbell** set a water speed record of **210.66 km/h** on Lake Hallwil in his Bluebird K3 speedboat
- The **Seetal railway** connects Lenzburg to Lucerne, and Lenzburg station is a hub on the Zürich–Bern main line
- Lenzburg sits at the heart of the **Lenzburg Castle Region**, encompassing five historic castles: Lenzburg, Hallwyl, Wildegg, Brunegg, and Habsburg
- Approximately **half the municipality's 1,132 hectares** are covered in forest

## 2. Prehistoric and Roman Roots

- A **Neolithic grave field** of the **Cortaillod culture** was discovered on the **Goffersberg** (close to the castle hill), dating from **4300–3500 BCE** — excavated in 1959/60, with 16 stone cist graves
- Both Goffersberg and the **Lindfeld** site are listed as **Swiss heritage sites of national significance**
- At the **Lindfeld**, a **Roman vicus** (small settlement) of about **500 inhabitants** was discovered — it existed from the first quarter of the 1st century AD for approximately 200 years before being abandoned in the 3rd century
- The name of the settlement was probably **Lentia**
- A **Roman theatre** was uncovered in **1964** during motorway construction — **64 meters wide**, built between **50 and 150 AD**, with seating for **4,000 to 5,000 spectators**
- This theatre was unique among the vici of Aargau (Frick, Laufenburg, Baden, Vindonissa) — its disproportionate size compared to the settlement suggests a **regional ceremonial function**
- The first documentary mention of the name Lenzburg: **924** as "de Lencis"

## 3. The Counts of Lenzburg — A Powerful Medieval Dynasty

- The **Counts of Lenzburg** were one of the most important comital families in the **Duchy of Swabia** during the 11th and 12th centuries, controlling large portions of the **Aargau** and **Zürichgau**
- A charter dated **1036** names **Ulrich I ("the Rich")**, Count of Aargau, as the Emperor's Vogt in Zürich and overseer of the abbeys of **Beromünster** and **Schänis**
- In the same year, Ulrich I convinced Emperor **Henry III** to grant Schänis Abbey, Beromünster, and his county **imperial immediacy** — a powerful privilege placing them directly under the Emperor
- The **residential tower**, the castle's oldest surviving building, was constructed by the Counts around **1100**
- The first definite record of the castle itself dates to **1077**, in connection with **Ulrich II**
- **Ulrich IV**, the last Count of Lenzburg, was a close confidant of the Holy Roman Emperors: he joined Emperor **Lothar III's** Italian campaign in 1136/37, fought in the **Second Crusade** (1147–49) with King Conrad III, and was permanently at the court of **Frederick Barbarossa** from 1152
- When Ulrich IV died without an heir on **5 January 1173**, he bequeathed his lands to Emperor Frederick Barbarossa — ending the dynasty
- Barbarossa distributed the Lenzburg territories among the houses of **Kyburg**, **Zähringen**, and **Hohenstaufen**

## 4. Schloss Lenzburg — 1,000 Years on a Hilltop

- **Schloss Lenzburg** is one of the **oldest and most important hilltop castles** in Switzerland, with over 1,000 years of documented history
- The fortress is encircled by a wall and comprises **seven buildings** dating from the 12th to early 18th centuries, arranged in a **horseshoe pattern** around a courtyard, showcasing Gothic and Baroque elements
- After the Lenzburg line died out (1173), the castle passed through a succession of powerful dynasties:
  - **Kyburg dynasty** (from c. 1230, through marriage) — they also established the fortified market settlement at the foot of the castle hill that became the town of Lenzburg
  - **Habsburg dynasty** (from 1273) — Rudolf I, Count of Habsburg (later King of the Romans), seized the estate and held court there in 1275; **Duke Frederick II of Habsburg** built the **Knights' Hall (Rittersaal)** around **1340**
  - **Bernese rule** (1444–1798) — after the Swiss Confederation's conquest of Aargau in **1415**, Bern secured the castle; the first **Bernese Landvogt** (bailiff) took residence in **1444**, governing the district
- The most famous Landvogt: **Adrian I von Bubenberg** (served 1457–1461), who later became Schultheiss (mayor) of Bern and the hero of the **Battle of Murten (1476)** against Charles the Bold of Burgundy
- Fortifications were strengthened during the **Reformation** and the **Thirty Years' War** (1618–1648)
- **1798**: French occupation of Canton Aargau ended feudal use of the castle

## 5. The Dragon Legend — Ritter Bertram and Ritter Guntram

- According to the **founding legend** of Lenzburg Castle, a fearsome dragon terrorized the local peasants from a cave beneath the hilltop
- Two noble brothers — **Ritter Bertram** and **Ritter Guntram** — confronted and slew the dragon
- As a reward, they were granted the title of **Counts of Lenzburg** and permission to build their fortress on the **Drachenfelsen** (dragon rock)
- A partially gilded **dragon sculpture** on the **north gable of the Landvogtei (Bailiwick) building** commemorates this legend — first documented in **1594**
- The modern continuation of the legend: Museum Aargau imagines the dragon mother laid an **egg** in her final moments, which rolled down the hillside and lay hidden for centuries
- After "three years, nine months, and five days," a dragon hatchling named **Fauchi** emerged — today the beloved **castle dragon** (Schlossdrache) of Lenzburg
- The **Dragon Research Station** (Drachenforschungsstation) inside the castle lets young visitors examine dragon artifacts, learn about "dragon species," and explore Fauchi's cave

## 6. The Ghost Legends — The Phantom Bell and the Sod Maid

- Beyond the dragon myth, two ghost stories haunt the castle:
- **The Phantom Bell**: A 12th-century bell in the bailiff's courtyard is said to ring on **full moon nights** without human intervention. One version tells of a bone from a murder victim attached to the bell's rope — when the killer, an elderly beggar, pulled it, blood splashed upon him
- **The Sod Maid (Sodmaitli)**: This apparition appears each year on **Corpus Christi Eve** (late May or June). A grieving woman in a trailing gown, clutching a pale child to her breast, wanders through the castle gardens toward the ancient **sod well** (a sealed shaft well). At midnight, she lets out a cry and drops the child into the watery depths
- The legend says the tragedy was born of **forbidden love** — the maid drowned her child to hide her shame
- She can only be redeemed if a **pure maiden catches her tears in a jug** before they touch the ground — but when one brave girl once attempted it, the tears proved impossibly heavy, and the jug slipped from her grasp

## 7. The Castle's Famous Private Owners (1860–1956)

### The Wedekind Family (1872–1893)
- In **1872**, **Friedrich Wilhelm Wedekind** purchased Schloss Lenzburg for **90,000 francs**
- Wedekind had emigrated to San Francisco after the failure of the **March Revolution of 1849** and made a fortune speculating in land during the **California Gold Rush**
- Returning to Europe in 1864, he emigrated again — this time to Switzerland — in protest against Prussian domination of the German Empire
- His **six children** spent their childhoods in the castle, among them the playwright **Frank Wedekind** and the opera singer **Erika Wedekind**

### Augustus Edward Jessup (1893–1911)
- In **1893**, the Wedekind family sold the castle for **120,000 francs** to **Augustus Edward Jessup**, an American industrialist from Philadelphia and long-time resident of England
- Jessup was married to **Mildred Marion Bowes-Lyon**, a relative of the Bowes-Lyon family (the family of the future **Queen Mother Elizabeth**)
- He undertook a **comprehensive renovation**, restoring the castle to its medieval condition while installing modern comforts: central heating, plumbing, and electricity — spending half a million francs from his personal fortune

### The Ellsworth Family (1911–1956)
- In **1911**, American industrialist **James W. Ellsworth** acquired the castle
- He bequeathed it to his son **Lincoln Ellsworth** in **1925** — the famous **polar explorer** who led the first Arctic and Antarctic air crossings
- Lincoln Ellsworth accompanied **Roald Amundsen** on the **1925 North Pole flight attempt** using two Dornier Wal flying boats
- On **23 November 1935**, Ellsworth and pilot Herbert Hollick-Kenyon completed the **first trans-Antarctic flight** from Dundee Island to the Ross Ice Shelf, discovering the **Ellsworth Mountains** — Antarctica's highest range
- Lincoln married **Mary Louise Ulmer** in 1933, dividing their time between Schloss Lenzburg and New York
- After Lincoln's death in **1951**, his widow sold the castle in **1956** for **500,000 francs** to a foundation established by the **City of Lenzburg and Canton of Aargau** — returning it to public ownership
- The castle opened as **Museum Aargau** in **1987**

## 8. Frank Wedekind — Playwright of Scandal

- **Benjamin Franklin Wedekind** (24 July 1864, Hannover – 9 March 1918, Munich), known as **Frank Wedekind**, was not born in Lenzburg but spent his **formative childhood years** there (1872–1884) after his family moved to the castle
- His father Friedrich Wilhelm gave him the name "Franklin" in honor of **Benjamin Franklin** — reflecting his American years
- Frank Wedekind became one of the most provocative German playwrights of the fin de siècle, a direct **forerunner of Expressionism** and the **Theatre of the Absurd**
- Major works: **"Frühlings Erwachen" (Spring Awakening, 1891)** — a shocking critique of sexual repression among adolescents; **"Erdgeist" (Earth Spirit, 1895)** and **"Die Büchse der Pandora" (Pandora's Box, 1904)** — the Lulu plays featuring the iconic, tragic character Lulu
- His sister **Erika Wedekind** (1868–1944) became one of the **first celebrated coloratura sopranos in Germany**, performing over a thousand times in cities from Moscow to London — though her father had forbidden her from training as a singer, and she only studied music after his death

## 9. The Medieval Old Town — Rathausgasse and Heritage

- The **Altstadt** (old town centre) at the foot of the Schlossberg is of **national importance** as a Swiss heritage site
- The town was founded in the 13th century by the **Counts of Kyburg**, who established a fortified market settlement below the castle
- On **20 August 1306**, Lenzburg received its **town charter** from Count Frederick the Fair
- The layout is horseshoe-shaped: the wide **Rathausgasse** (Town Hall Lane) and parallel streets to north and south, with the **Kirchgasse** running perpendicular at the lower end
- The **Rathaus** (Town Hall) at Rathausgasse 16 is listed as a **heritage site of national significance**
- In total, **nine sites** in Lenzburg carry this designation, including: the castle, the Rathaus, the Müllerhaus (Bleicherain 7), Sonnenberg (Schlossgasse 50), Villa Malaga, and the two archaeological sites (Goffersberg and Lindfeld)
- The **Stadtkirche** (city church), just outside the old gate, dates to the **16th century**
- In **1415**, Lenzburg surrendered to the advancing Bernese army on **20 April** — the town was not destroyed and retained its city rights

## 10. The Stapferhaus — European Museum of the Year

- The **Stapferhaus** was founded in **1960** by Pro Helvetia, Pro Argovia, the Canton of Aargau, and the City of Lenzburg as a centre for intellectual debate and contemporary cultural issues
- Named after **Philipp Albert Stapfer** (1766–1840), the education minister of the **Helvetic Republic** who initiated comprehensive school surveys and is considered a key figure in the founding of Canton Aargau
- From 1960 to the early 1990s, the Stapferhaus was housed **inside Lenzburg Castle** itself, hosting conferences and debates
- Its first major exhibition in **1994** was **"Anne Frank and Us"**, curated under Hans Ulrich Garner
- In **2018**, the Stapferhaus moved into a purpose-built **new building next to Lenzburg railway station** — designed for flexible, interactive exhibition spaces
- In **2020**, it won the **European Museum of the Year Award** — recognized for both its innovative building design and its exhibition work
- The Stapferhaus addresses big questions of the present through immersive, sensory exhibitions: past themes have included time, money, home, and fakes
- Exhibitions are presented in **German, French, and English**

## 11. Schloss Hallwyl — The Moated Castle Nearby

- **Hallwyl Castle** (Schloss Hallwyl) lies just north of **Lake Hallwil**, in the municipality of **Seengen**, about 10 km south of Lenzburg
- One of the most important **moated castles** (Wasserschlösser) in Switzerland, built on **two islands** in the River Aabach
- Founded by the **Lords of Hallwyl** in the late **12th century** — originally a residential tower with a dry moat, converted to a water moat in the early 14th century
- In **1415**, during the Confederation's conquest of Aargau, the castle was **burned by Bernese troops** but immediately rebuilt and expanded
- **Wilhelmina von Hallwyl** established the Hallwil Foundation in **1925** to preserve the castle; the Canton of Aargau acquired it in **1994**
- Today it houses a **museum with 11 themed exhibitions** spanning the Middle Ages to the modern age
- The castle can only be entered via a **drawbridge** — crossing the Aabach into a setting of remarkable medieval atmosphere

## 12. Cultural Traditions — Jugendfest and Freischarenmanöver

- The **Jugendfest** (Youth Festival) is Lenzburg's most cherished tradition, dating back **over 400 years** to the 16th century
- Originally a school celebration called **"der Schulknaben Königrych"** (the schoolboys' kingdom), where students were fed in the town hall after a procession
- The festival takes place annually on **the second Friday in July**, beginning at 6:00 AM with cannon fire (Tagwache)
- Activities include morning ceremonies with speeches and singing, a **festive procession** through the old town, games for schoolchildren, evening banquets, a **children's lantern procession**, and **fireworks launched from the castle**
- Graduating students receive commemorative silver coins called **Jugendfesttaler** (since 1972)
- Every **two years** (even-numbered years), the **Freischarenmanöver** (Freischaren Maneuvers) take place — a tradition since **1852**: colorfully costumed and fearfully made-up "Freischaren" troops stage a mock battle against the **cadets** (the youth), and each time suffer a **shameful defeat**
- Other notable cultural events: the **Gauklerfestival** (international street arts festival, held annually in the old town since **1993**) and the **Lenzburgiade** (international open-air festival for classical and world music)

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The dragon of Lenzburg hill** — Retelling of the founding legend from the dragon's perspective: she hides her last egg before Bertram and Guntram arrive, and centuries later Fauchi hatches and must learn to live among humans
2. **The polar explorer's castle** — A child discovers Lincoln Ellsworth's old expedition maps in the castle attic; following the clues leads to an imaginary Antarctic adventure across frozen castle courtyards and the Seetal valley
3. **The Sod Maid's tears** — A brave child tries to catch the ghost's tears in a jug on Corpus Christi Eve, solving the ancient riddle that has kept the Sod Maid wandering the castle gardens for centuries
4. **The Counts' last crusade** — Ulrich IV, the last Count of Lenzburg, sets off for the Second Crusade with Emperor Barbarossa; a child from the castle kitchen stows away and must find courage in a foreign land
5. **Fauchi and the Dragon Research Station** — Fauchi the castle dragon accidentally awakens other "dragon species" hidden across the five castles of the Lenzburg region, and a child must help return them to their lairs before the museum opens
6. **The Jugendfest battle** — A child joins the cadets for the Freischarenmanöver and discovers the mock battle is based on a real medieval siege; time-travelling back to 1415 when Bern's army arrived
7. **The singer in the tower** — Young Erika Wedekind secretly practices her voice in the castle tower at night, defying her father's ban; the notes drift down to the old town and wake the dragon sculpture on the Landvogtei gable
8. **The phantom bell of Lenzburg** — On a full moon night, the 12th-century bell rings by itself; a child follows the sound through the castle's seven buildings, each from a different century, to uncover an ancient secret
