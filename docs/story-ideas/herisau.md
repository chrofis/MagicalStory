## 1. Geography — Rolling Hills Between Saentis and St. Gallen

- Herisau is the **capital and largest town** of the half-canton **Appenzell Ausserrhoden** (Appenzell AR) in northeastern Switzerland, with a population of approximately **15,700** — about one-third of the entire canton's roughly 55,000 inhabitants
- The town lies along the **Glatt River** (locally called the **Glatt**), just southwest of the city of **St. Gallen**, at an elevation of roughly **770 m above sea level**
- Area: **25.2 km squared**, of which 56.8% is agricultural land and 27.1% is forested — a landscape of gentle, rolling green hills (locally: **Huegel**) characteristic of the Appenzell **Hinterland**
- Herisau sits open to views of the **Saentis** (2,502 m) to the south — the highest peak of the **Alpstein** massif — and the **Lake Constance** (Bodensee) region to the north
- Two ruined medieval castles — **Rosenberg** and **Rosenburg** — stand on the hills above the town, built around **1150-1200** by the noble **Rorschach family**, ministeriales (knights) in service to the Abbot of St. Gallen
- Herisau was named **Alpine Town of the Year 2003**, praised for balancing tradition with innovation

## 2. Early History — From Herinisauva to Freedom from the Abbey

- First documented mention: **837**, under the name **"Herinisauva"** — likely derived from a personal name (Herin) and the Old High German **ouwa** (watered meadow)
- A church on the site is recorded in **907**, dedicated to the Holy Savior (later rededicated to **St. Laurentius**)
- For centuries, Herisau lived under the feudal authority of the powerful **Abbey of St. Gallen** (Fuerstabtei St. Gallen)
- In **1084**, Herisau was destroyed during power struggles around the monastery
- In **1248 and 1249**, the town was destroyed again — this time **by the monastery itself**, to enforce loyalty
- During the **Appenzell Wars** (Appenzeller Kriege, **1401-1429**), the people of the region fought for independence from the Abbey. In **1403**, the **Rosenburg Castle** above Herisau was attacked and burned by Appenzeller rebels — a symbolic act of defiance against feudal rule
- Between **1517 and 1518**, Herisau finally **purchased its freedom** (Loskauf) from the monastery — a momentous achievement after centuries of subordination
- The town hall (**Rathaus**) was built in **1601**, but just five years later, in **1606**, a devastating fire destroyed much of the town

## 3. The Landteilung of 1597 — When Appenzell Split in Two

- After the **Reformation** reached the region around **1529-1530**, the outer communities (Ausserrhoden) converted to **Protestantism**, while the inner communities (Innerrhoden) remained **Catholic**
- Tensions deepened over foreign policy: in **1588**, Catholic Innerrhoden was invited to join a military alliance with **Habsburg Spain**. In **1596**, Innerrhoden signed the treaty **without Ausserrhoden's consent** — the breaking point
- On **2 June 1597**, the citizens of Ausserrhoden voted for separation; on **15 June 1597**, Innerrhoden confirmed the split
- On **8 September 1597**, the formal **Landteilungsbrief** (partition letter) was sealed, mediated by six neutral cantons — three Catholic (Luzern, Schwyz, Nidwalden) and three Protestant (Zurich, Glarus, Schaffhausen)
- This **peaceful partition** was remarkable for its era — while religious wars ravaged Europe, Appenzell resolved its confessional divide through negotiation and a handshake
- Ausserrhoden, the larger and more populous half, chose **Herisau as its capital** — where the cantonal government and parliament (**Kantonsrat**) sit to this day (the judiciary is based in Trogen)
- Between **1798 and 1803**, during the Helvetic Republic, both Appenzell half-cantons were merged into the larger **Canton Saentis**, with Herisau briefly serving as capital

## 4. The Landsgemeinde — Open-Air Democracy Until 1997

- The **Landsgemeinde** (open-air cantonal assembly) was Appenzell Ausserrhoden's most distinctive democratic tradition — citizens gathered annually to vote by raising their right hands (**Handmehr**)
- Unlike Innerrhoden, which held its Landsgemeinde in a single location (Appenzell village), Ausserrhoden's assembly **alternated between two villages**: **Hundwil** (odd years) and **Trogen** (even years)
- The **Landsgemeindeplatz** in Hundwil is listed as a **heritage site of national significance**
- The last Ausserrhoder Landsgemeinde took place on **27 April 1997** in Hundwil
- On **28 September 1997**, a ballot vote abolished the tradition by **11,623 to 9,911 votes** — a narrow margin reflecting deep ambivalence about ending a centuries-old democratic ritual
- Today, only **two Swiss cantons** still practice the Landsgemeinde: **Appenzell Innerrhoden** and **Glarus**

## 5. The Textile Industry — Linen, Cotton, and World-Famous Embroidery

- From the **16th century onward**, Herisau and the surrounding Ausserrhoden region became major centres for **Leinwandproduktion** (linen production) — virtually every home was used for weaving, spinning, or embroidery as a cottage industry (**Heimarbeit**)
- By the **17th century**, the Herisau region was among the **most densely populated areas in Europe**, driven by the booming linen trade
- Influential merchant families — the **Wetters** and **Tanners** of Herisau, the Schlaepfers of Speicher, the Zellwegers of Trogen — established trading houses that built the linen economy into an international enterprise
- The first textile printing works opened along the **Glatt River** in **1737**, fostering industrial quarters equipped with bleacheries and finishing facilities
- In the **early 18th century**, **cotton** gradually replaced linen; spinning and weaving spread across the region alongside farming
- The invention of the **hand embroidery machine** by **Josua Heilmann** in **1828** shifted production toward embroidery; **Isaak Groebli's Schiffli embroidery machine** (1863) brought the industry to its peak
- By **around 1910**, more than **half of the world's embroidery production** came from Eastern Switzerland — embroidery represented roughly **one-fifth of total Swiss exports**
- **World War I (1914-1918)** ended the golden era of embroidery, devastating the region's economy almost overnight
- Appenzell Ausserrhoden was **Switzerland's most densely populated canton around 1800**, a direct result of the textile boom

## 6. The Grubenmann Master Builders — Timber Genius from the Hinterland

- The **Grubenmann family** from neighbouring **Teufen** (just 6 km from Herisau) produced some of the most celebrated engineers and master builders (**Baumeister**) of 18th-century Europe
- The patriarch **Ulrich Grubenmann** (1668-1736) lived in **Gstalden**, Appenzell Ausserrhoden, and fathered three sons who became legendary: **Jakob** (1694-1758), **Johannes** (1707-1771), and **Hans Ulrich** (1709-1783)
- All three were village carpenters (**Zimmermeister**) who combined the **arch and truss principles** in timber construction, making it possible to build longer and better wooden bridges than ever before
- **Hans Ulrich** built the famous **Rhine bridge at Schaffhausen** (1755) — two spans of **52 m and 59 m**, one of the longest timber bridges in Europe at the time
- **Johannes** built the **Reichenau bridge** in Graubuenden (1757) with a single span of **67 m**
- Together they built the **Wettingen bridge** over the Limmat (1778) — a **60 m span** believed to be the **first timber bridge to employ a true arch**, a milestone in structural engineering
- Their connection to Herisau is direct: in **1741**, **Johannes Grubenmann** added the distinctive **octagonal spire** to the tower of the **Reformed Church in Herisau**, enhancing its silhouette against the Alpstein skyline
- The Grubenmanns also built or remodelled several **Reformed churches** across Switzerland, including those at **Wadenswil** (1764-1767), **Teufen** (1776-1778), and **Trogen** (1779-1781) — their column-free interiors with enormous unsupported roof spans rank among the most important Protestant sacred buildings in Switzerland
- Tragically, both the Schaffhausen and Wettingen bridges were **burned by French troops in 1799** during the Napoleonic Wars
- Today, the **Zeughaus Teufen** (Grubenmann-Museum) houses the **Grubenmann-Sammlung** — wooden bridge models, original documents, and woodworking tools — preserving the family's extraordinary legacy

## 7. Robert Walser — The Writer Who Died in the Snow

- **Robert Walser** (1878-1956), born in Biel, is today considered **one of the most important Swiss writers of the 20th century** — admired during his lifetime by Hermann Hesse, Franz Kafka, and Walter Benjamin
- His major works include the novels **Geschwister Tanner** (1907), **Der Gehuelfe** (1908), and **Jakob von Gunten** (1909), as well as the novella **Der Spaziergang** (The Walk, 1917)
- After a mental breakdown in **1929**, Walser entered the **Waldau asylum** in Bern; in **June 1933**, he was transferred — **against his will** — to the psychiatric clinic in **Herisau** (today the Psychiatrisches Zentrum Appenzell Ausserrhoden, on the Krombach site)
- He spent the last **23 years** of his life in Herisau, ceasing to write entirely and living in near-complete anonymity
- From **1936**, the journalist and literary admirer **Carl Seelig** (1894-1962) began visiting Walser regularly, becoming his friend, legal guardian, and literary executor. Seelig documented their excursions in **Wanderungen mit Robert Walser** (Walks with Walser, 1957) — a book that did more to revive Walser's reputation than any other
- On **Christmas Day, 25 December 1956**, Walser set out on one of his customary solitary walks. A group of **children found his body in the snow** on the Wachtenegg near Herisau — his right hand on his chest, his left arm outstretched, a black hat lying nearby. He had died of a **heart attack**, aged 78
- The police photograph of Walser's body in the snow has become one of the most iconic images in Swiss literary history — eerily echoing scenes from his own fiction
- The **Robert-Walser-Pfad**, created by writer **Peter Morger** in **1986**, is **Switzerland's first literary trail** — an 8 km loop (approx. 2.5 hours) connecting the Walser Fountain in town, the psychiatric clinic, the cemetery, and the Museum Herisau, with plaques bearing quotations from Walser's work

## 8. Hermann Rorschach — The Inkblot Test Was Born Here

- **Hermann Rorschach** (1884-1922), the Swiss psychiatrist who created the world-famous **inkblot test** (Tintenkleckstest), spent the final and most productive years of his career in Herisau
- In late **1915**, Rorschach was appointed **associate director** of the Herisau asylum (Kantonale Heil- und Pflegeanstalt). The hospital held around **300 patients** but had only **two psychiatrists**
- Beginning in **1918**, Rorschach revived an earlier interest in inkblots as diagnostic tools. He showed cards to **288 mentally ill patients** and **117 "normal" control subjects** — nurses, medical students, children, and others — all at the Herisau hospital
- The results were published as **Psychodiagnostik** in **1921** — the foundational text of projective personality testing
- On **2 April 1922**, Rorschach died of **peritonitis** (probably from a ruptured appendix) in Herisau, aged just **37** — barely a year after his masterwork's publication
- The Rorschach inkblot test became one of the most widely used psychological assessment tools in the world — and it was developed entirely within the walls of the Herisau clinic

## 9. The Saentis — Giant of the Alpstein and Its Legend

- The **Saentis** (2,502 m) towers over Herisau to the south — the highest peak of the **Alpstein** massif and the dominant mountain of the entire Appenzell region, visible on clear days from as far as southern Germany
- According to local **Appenzell folklore**, the mountain is named after a **giant called Saemtis** (or Saentis) who once lay sleeping in the valley: the **Schwendibach valley** was his bed, the **Meglisalp** with its soft alpine grass was his pillow, and where he pricked his elbows when getting up, the **Seealpsee** (lake) was formed
- The giant disappeared and never returned, but the mountain keeps his name — a legend deeply connected to the Appenzeller sense of landscape and identity
- Near the summit, the **Wildkirchli** (Wild Chapel) caves sit at about 1,500 m — three interlinked caves where **hermit monks** lived and worshipped for two centuries (until **1853**). In medieval times, the large bones of **cave bears** found there were believed to be dragon bones
- In **1903-1908**, archaeologist **Emil Baechler** discovered Mousterian **flint tools** in the caves — the first evidence of **Neanderthal** presence in the Alps, dating to approximately **40,000 BC**
- The **Saentis weather station**, established in **1882**, is one of the highest in Switzerland and has recorded some of the country's most extreme weather events

## 10. Living Traditions — Bloch, Silvesterchlausen, and the Alpabzug

- **The Herisauer Bloch**: On the Monday following Ash Wednesday (**Blochmontag**), men in traditional Appenzell dress pull a massive felled tree trunk — the **Bloch** — through the streets of Herisau, starting at 4:30 a.m. The origin lies with forest owners who thanked their workers with a gift of a fir tree. At the end of the day, the trunk is sold to the highest bidder in the village square to be made into shingles or furniture said to bring good luck. The Bloch tradition survives only in the **Appenzeller Hinterland**
- **Silvesterchlausen** (New Year's Mummers): On both **31 December** (Gregorian) and **13 January** (Julian calendar — the **"Alter Silvester"**), groups of men don elaborate costumes and masks, ring huge cowbells (**Schellen**), and go from house to house singing a slow, haunting yodel (**Zäuerli**) to wish families a happy new year. Three types exist: the **Schoene** (Beautiful), the **Schoe-Wueschte** (Pretty-Ugly), and the **Wueschte** (Ugly). The tradition is documented since **1663** and is part of Switzerland's **intangible cultural heritage**. Herisau participates alongside Urnaesch, Hundwil, Waldstatt, and Teufen
- **Viehschau and Alpabzug**: Each autumn (mid-September to mid-October), farmers return from the mountain pastures in the **Alpabzug** (alpine descent). The opening ceremony features farmers walking **through a richly decorated wooden arch** with their herds — some in the traditional **Sennisch** costume of yellow trousers and red waistcoat. A jury evaluates animals; the finest cows are decorated with **wreaths of paper flowers** (Chranzchue). The evening culminates in celebrations with **singing, yodelling, and dancing** at a local inn

## 11. Music, Folk Art, and the Appenzell Dialect

- **Appenzeller Streichmusik** (string music) is the region's signature folk sound: a quintet of **two violins, a Hackbrett (hammer dulcimer), cello, and double bass**. In Herisau, the ensemble **Streichmusik Edelweiss** (founded in Trogen in 1913, renamed Edelweiss Herisau in 1986) has cultivated this tradition for over a century
- The wordless yodel known as **Zauerli** (in Ausserrhoden) or **Rugguusseli** (in Innerrhoden) is among the world's most celebrated yodel forms — sung in the **lower register** at a slow, elongated tempo, often accompanied by **Talerschwingen** (rolling a five-franc coin around the rim of a ceramic bowl to create a droning resonance)
- **Bauernmalerei** (farmer painting): **Bartholomaeus Laemmler** (1809-1865), born in Herisau, pioneered the creation of **Sennenstreifen** — long, narrow panoramic paintings of alpine cattle drives on paper or wood, produced as coloured woodcuts. He is considered one of the most important early figures in Appenzeller naive art
- The **Appenzeller Mundart** (dialect) is distinctive within Swiss German. In the Hinterland around Herisau, the bee is called **"Biili"** (versus "Iime" elsewhere in the canton), and the "r" sound is pronounced almost like an "a". The dialect has been gradually influenced by neighbouring St. Gallen speech, though older speakers preserve the authentic Hinterland sound

## 12. Notable People

- **Hermann Rorschach** (1884-1922): Psychiatrist; developed the inkblot test at the Herisau asylum (see section 8)
- **Robert Walser** (1878-1956): Writer; spent his final 23 years at the Herisau psychiatric clinic (see section 7)
- **Johannes Baumann** (1874-1953): Born in Herisau; member of the **Swiss Federal Council** (1934-1940), head of the Federal Department of Justice and Police, **President of the Confederation in 1938**
- **Hans-Rudolf Merz** (born 1942): Born in Herisau; member of the **Swiss Federal Council** (2004-2010), **President of the Confederation in 2009**. Herisau is thus the birthplace of **two Federal Councillors** — remarkable for a town of 16,000
- **Santiago Roth** (1850-1924): Born in Herisau as Kaspar Jakob Roth; emigrated to Argentina in 1866 and became one of South America's most important **paleontologists**, discovering megafauna fossils in the Pampas and Patagonia
- **Bartholomaeus Laemmler** (1809-1865): Pioneer of Appenzeller naive art and Sennenstreifen (see section 11)
- **Jakob Heierli** (1853-1912): Swiss archaeologist and prehistorian, born in Herisau
- **Annette Gigon** (born 1959): Acclaimed Swiss architect, born in Herisau

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The inkblot that came alive** — A child visiting the old Herisau hospital discovers one of Rorschach's original cards; the inkblot transforms into a creature that shows what you feel — and the child must figure out what the inkblot sees in *them*
2. **The writer in the snow** — On Christmas Day, a child follows footprints in the snow through Herisau and meets the ghost of Robert Walser, who takes them on one last walk past the psychiatric clinic, through the woods, to the places that inspired his stories — each stop revealing a tiny, beautiful observation about the world
3. **The bridge that wouldn't burn** — A child travels back to 1799 as French soldiers set fire to the great Grubenmann bridge at Schaffhausen. Inside the flames, the spirit of Johannes Grubenmann appears and teaches the child to rebuild: arch, truss, and the courage to span what seems impossible — because every bridge starts with believing the other side is worth reaching
4. **The Landsgemeinde that wouldn't end** — A child time-travels to the last Landsgemeinde of 1997 in Hundwil and discovers that one vote is missing; they must travel back through the centuries — 1597 (the great split), 1518 (freedom from the Abbey), 1403 (the Appenzell Wars) — to find the lost voter and complete democracy's final open-air act
5. **The giant's pillow** — A child hiking near the Saentis discovers a colossal footprint in the Meglisalp grass. Following the legend, they meet the sleeping giant Saemtis, who asks for one last bedtime story before he turns into the mountain forever — and the child must decide what kind of mountain he will become
6. **The Bloch that rolled away** — On Blochmontag at 4:30 a.m., the great tree trunk breaks free from the men pulling it through Herisau and rolls through the dark streets on its own, gathering speed. A child chases it past the Reformed church, down to the Glatt, and into the hills — discovering that the Bloch is trying to return to the forest where it grew up
7. **The Sennenstreifen painter** — Bartholomaeus Laemmler's panoramic painting of an Alpaufzug comes to life; a child steps into the long, narrow world of the Sennenstreifen and must help a painted cow reach the alpine pasture before the paint dries and the picture freezes forever
8. **The Wueschte and the Schoene** — On Alter Silvester (13 January), a shy child joins the Silvesterchlausen procession in an Ugly mask (Wueschte), only to discover that when the slow Zauerli yodel begins, the masks swap — the Ugly become Beautiful and the Beautiful become Ugly — and the child must sing their way back to their true face before the last cowbell rings
