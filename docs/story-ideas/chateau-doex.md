## 1. Geography — The Pays-d'Enhaut (the "High Country")

- Château-d'Oex (pronounced "sha-TOH-day") sits at approximately **1,000 meters** altitude in the upper valley of the **Sarine river** (German: Saane), in the canton of **Vaud** in western Switzerland
- The name means "Castle of Oex" — **château** (castle) plus **Oex**, probably derived from the Celtic **ogo** meaning "high land," mirroring the German name **Hochgau** for the district
- First mentioned in **1115** under the spellings **Oit, Oyz, Oix, and Oyez**
- The municipality is the **largest in land area** in the canton of Vaud, with a population of approximately **3,487** (2020)
- The **Pays-d'Enhaut** (literally "the high country") is a traditional pre-Alpine valley comprising three villages: **Château-d'Oex**, **Rougemont**, and **Rossinière**, nestled between the Vaud Alps and the Bernese Oberland
- The region lies within the **Parc Naturel Régional Gruyère Pays-d'Enhaut**, one of Switzerland's 20 parks of national importance, covering **632 km²** across three cantons (Vaud, Fribourg, Bern) and ranging from **374 m** on Lake Geneva to **2,548 m** at the Tarent peak
- The Sarine river flows through a dynamic **alluvial zone** (zone alluviale) south of the village, a landscape of braided channels and shifting gravel beds
- Many local place names preserve Celtic roots: **combe** (valley), **joeur** (forest), **man** (rock)

## 2. Medieval History — The Counts of Gruyère and Bernese Rule

- The Pays-d'Enhaut was incorporated into the **County of Gruyère** (Comté de Gruyère) by the 10th century
- The **Cluniac Priory of Rougemont**, founded between **1073 and 1085** at the request of Count **Guillaume I of Gruyère**, was the first and only convent in the Vaud Alps — its Romanesque **Church of Saint-Nicolas** still stands today
- At the beginning of the **14th century**, the castle at **La Motte** was rebuilt for the Counts of Gruyère; another stronghold likely stood on the rocky spur known as **Château Cottier** — giving the village the "château" in its name
- Within the county, Château-d'Oex formed a **feudal castellany** (châtellenie) alongside Rossinière, governed by the Counts of Gruyère as vassals of the **House of Savoy**
- In **1388**, the villagers threw off the obligation to serve the nobility; in **1403**, against the wishes of the Count, they joined a limited alliance with **Bern**
- In **1554**, the last Count of Gruyère, **Count Michel**, declared bankruptcy (la faillite), and his lands were divided between his principal creditors: the cantons of **Bern** and **Fribourg**
- In **1555**, Bern received the **upper part of the county**, including Château-d'Oex, which became part of the German-speaking **bailiwick of Saanen** (bailliage de Gessenay)
- The **Protestant Reformation** was imposed under Bernese rule from **1555** — the local church converted to the Reformed faith, and the parish also included L'Etivaz until **1713**
- Rights and freedoms were henceforth based on **Bernese law** rather than the older Land Law of Moudon (Coutume de Moudon)
- Under Bernese administration, the economy experienced a strong **upswing**: common land was divided and sold, and alpine pastures were leased to private cheesemakers who produced **Gruyère cheese** — sold via the Col de Jaman pass at the market in **Vevey** and exported onward to Lyon

## 3. The Great Fire of 1800 and Rebuilding

- On a fateful night in **July 1800**, fire swept through Château-d'Oex and **destroyed most of the wooden houses** on the central hill — dramatically altering the town's appearance
- The houses had been built in timber, crowded together on the hilltop
- The **Temple** (village church), originally built in the **Gothic style in 1450**, was severely damaged; its timber framework and interior galleries had to be entirely rebuilt
- Thanks to the efforts of **Dean Philippe-Sirice Bridel** (le doyen Bridel), a prominent local pastor and writer, enough money was raised to finance reconstruction
- The municipality hired **masons from Savoy**, **carpenters from the Simmental**, and **plasterers from the lower Gruyère lands** to rebuild in **stone** — a fundamental shift from the all-timber tradition
- This was one of **three major fires** across three centuries that shaped the region's building evolution, each time destroying much of the existing wooden architecture

## 4. Traditional Chalets — Pays-d'Enhaut Wooden Architecture

- The **chalet** (from Franco-Provençal for "herder's hut") is the quintessential building of the Pays-d'Enhaut — heavy timber construction with wide eaves, carved facades, and inscribed quotations or blessings
- Authentic pre-1850 chalets were built **almost entirely of timber**, easily distinguished by their **ornamental carved facades** bearing quotations or greetings
- Roofs featured distinctive **"fish scale" patterns** (écailles de poisson) thanks to the use of **shale tiles** (ardoises); facades were also adorned with **tavaillons** (hand-split wooden shingles), a craft still practiced by a few local artisans
- Pays-d'Enhaut chalets were **taller and more decorative** than those of neighboring villages, reflecting regional prosperity from agriculture and cheese exports
- The **Grand Chalet de Rossinière**, built between **1752 and 1756** for farmer, notary, and judge **Jean David Henchoz**, is the **largest inhabitable wooden building in Switzerland** — with **500 m² of ground surface**, **5 floors**, **113 windows**, and a painted inscription of **2,800 letters**
- Henchoz intended the cellar for **600 wheels of cheese** in their aging stage, and the ground floor for trade with merchants from Bulle, Vevey, and Lyon
- The Grand Chalet later became a fashionable hotel; guests included **Victor Hugo**, **Léon Gambetta**, and **Alfred Dreyfus**
- In **1977**, the painter **Balthus** (Count Balthazar Klossowski de Rola, 1908–2001) acquired the Grand Chalet and lived there until his death, painting and receiving fellow artists

## 5. Découpage — The Art of Paper Cutting (l'art du découpage)

- **Découpage** (German: Scherenschnitt) is the art of cutting intricate lace-like patterns from paper with scissors or a craft knife — a tradition rooted in the Pays-d'Enhaut since the **18th century**
- The two founding masters of Swiss découpage both lived in this valley:
  - **Johann Jakob Hauswirth** (1809–1871): Born in Saanen, worked as a farm laborer, woodcutter, and charcoal maker in the border region between the Bernese Oberland and the Pays-d'Enhaut. Self-taught, he created exquisite Alpine scenes — cattle drives, flower arrangements, village celebrations — which he gave or traded to the farmers who employed him. He died in poverty in **L'Etivaz** but is now considered the **father of Swiss paper cutting**
  - **Louis Saugy** (1871–1953): Born in **Gérignoz** near Château-d'Oex, worked as a postman in Rougemont for 25 years. His cuttings depicted the **montée à l'alpage** (ascent to the alpine pastures), embellished with bouquets and hearts. He pioneered the technique of **assembling previously cut sections** to create more complex works. He was filmed at work on Christmas Eve **1950**, footage later used in the documentary *La Nature au Bout des Ciseaux*
- The **Musée du Pays-d'Enhaut** (Grand Rue 107, Château-d'Oex) serves as the **Swiss Centre for Paper Cut-outs** (Centre Suisse du Papier Découpé), housing permanent collections of Hauswirth, Saugy, and contemporary artists
- Découpage is listed in the **Swiss Federal Inventory of Living Traditions** (Lebendige Traditionen) under the entry **"Art of découpage in the Pays d'Enhaut, in Saanenland and in Simmental"** — cantons of Vaud and Bern
- The **Association Suisse des Amis du Découpage sur Papier** maintains over **500 members** nationwide, though no Swiss school formally teaches the art

## 6. L'Etivaz AOP — Alpine Cheese of the Pays-d'Enhaut

- **L'Etivaz** is a hard Alpine cheese (fromage d'alpage) made by hand from **raw milk** (lait cru) exclusively between **10 May and 10 October**, in copper cauldrons heated over **wood fires** (feux de bois), at altitudes between **1,000 and 2,000 meters**
- Named for the hamlet of **L'Etivaz** in the municipality of Château-d'Oex, the cheese is the **ancestor and artisanal predecessor of Gruyère**
- In the **1930s**, a group of **76 Gruyère-producing families** felt that government regulations were compromising cheese quality. They withdrew from the Gruyère program and created their own cheese under the L'Etivaz name
- They founded a **cooperative in 1932**; the first cheese cellars were built in **1934**, and the cooperative was officially inaugurated on **24 May 1935**
- L'Etivaz was the **first Swiss product** to receive the AOP label (Appellation d'Origine Protégée) in **January 2000**
- Today, over **3,000 cows** across approximately **134 mountain chalets** (chalets d'alpage) produce the milk; the cooperative's cellars have expanded to a capacity of **30,000 wheels** (after renovations through 2012)
- The mountain pastures feature exceptional flora — **arnica**, **gentians** — contributing distinctive flavors to the cheese
- After **6 months** of maturation, approximately **1,000 wheels** are selected for the premium designation **"Rebibes"** — an extra-aged specialty

## 7. The International Hot Air Balloon Festival (Festival International de Ballons)

- In **February 1979**, the Château-d'Oex Tourist Office organized the first **International Hot Air Balloon Week** (Semaine Internationale de Montgolfières) with the collaboration of **Hans Bücker** — **12 balloons** from **5 countries** attended
- The actor **David Niven**, who lived in Château-d'Oex, sponsored this inaugural event, helping raise the region's international profile
- The valley's **microclimate** — sheltered by surrounding mountains, with stable winter thermals — makes it ideal for balloon flying
- The festival has been held annually in January ever since, growing into one of the **largest hot air balloon events in Europe**
- **1994**: Hosted the **4th World Hot-Air Ballooning Championship**, the first winter mountain competition of its kind in Switzerland, with 16 pilots from 9 countries
- **2017**: Pilots accumulated over **800 flight hours** with **611 take-offs**; the Night Glow (Nuit des Ballons) returned after a five-year absence
- **2023**: The 43rd edition (after a two-year pandemic pause) featured **60 balloons** completing **379 take-offs** totaling **498 flight hours** across nine festival days
- The festival typically draws **20,000–50,000 spectators** from approximately **15 countries**, organized by a committee of 20 members and 60 volunteers

## 8. Breitling Orbiter 3 — First Non-Stop Balloon Flight Around the World

- On **1 March 1999**, at 8:05 GMT, the **Breitling Orbiter 3** launched from Château-d'Oex with pilots **Bertrand Piccard** (Swiss psychiatrist) and **Brian Jones** (English balloonist)
- After **19 days, 21 hours, and 47 minutes** aloft, they landed in the **Egyptian desert near Dakhla** on **21 March 1999** — completing the **first non-stop balloon circumnavigation of the globe**
- Total distance: **45,633 km** (28,350 miles); maximum altitude: **11,737 m** (38,507 ft); maximum speed: **228 km/h** (142 mph)
- The balloon was a **Cameron R-650 Rozière** — a hybrid design combining a helium gas cell within a hot-air envelope, standing **55 meters tall** when fully inflated, built by Cameron Balloons of Bristol, England
- Two earlier attempts also launched from Château-d'Oex: **Orbiter 1** (12 January 1997, ditched in the Mediterranean after 6 hours due to fuel leak) and **Orbiter 2** (28 January 1998, landed in Myanmar after 9 days when China denied overflight permission)
- The flight set **7 world records** simultaneously, including longest aviation flight by distance and duration
- The gondola is now displayed at the **Smithsonian National Air and Space Museum** in the Udvar-Hazy Center near Washington, D.C., alongside the Wright Flyer and the Apollo 11 command module — a gift from the Breitling Watch Company
- Bertrand Piccard is the third generation of a Swiss explorer dynasty: his grandfather **Auguste Piccard** (1884–1962) was the first person to reach the stratosphere by balloon; his father **Jacques Piccard** (1922–2008) co-piloted the bathyscaphe Trieste to the deepest point of the ocean, the **Mariana Trench** (10,911 m), in 1960

## 9. The MOB Railway (Montreux–Oberland Bernois)

- The **Montreux Oberland Bernois Railway** (Chemin de fer Montreux–Oberland bernois, MOB) is a **metre-gauge** (1,000 mm) electrified railway connecting **Montreux** on Lake Geneva to **Zweisimmen** in the Bernese Oberland — **62.4 km** of scenic mountain railway
- Founded on **26 June 1899** by the **Dufour brothers**, prominent hoteliers from Les Avants, aiming to link the Riviera to the Alpine hinterland for tourism
- The line opened in stages: **Montreux–Les Avants** (17 December 1901), **Les Avants–Montbovon** (1 October 1903), **Montbovon–Château-d'Oex** (19 August 1904), and onward to **Gstaad** (20 December 1904)
- The railway passes through the heart of the Pays-d'Enhaut, climbing from the shores of **Lake Geneva** (372 m) through vineyards, mountain forests, and Alpine pastures
- By **1916**, it became possible to travel by train from **Montreux to Lucerne** with two changes (at Zweisimmen and Interlaken), forming the route later known as the **GoldenPass Line**
- On **11 December 2022**, the **GoldenPass Express** began direct service between Montreux and Interlaken Ost, using innovative gauge-changing technology to bridge the metre-gauge MOB and the standard-gauge BLS networks — a century-old dream finally realized

## 10. Alpine Farming and Transhumance (l'inalpe et la désalpe)

- **Transhumance** — the seasonal migration of livestock between valley farms and high Alpine pastures — has been practiced in the Pays-d'Enhaut since at least the **12th century**
- The annual cycle connects three altitudinal zones: the **valley floor** in winter, intermediate pastures called **à-primiers** in May, June, and September, and the high **alpages** (up to 2,000 m) in July and August
- During the summer months, herders live in the **chalets d'alpage** (mountain dairy huts), producing cheese daily from fresh milk — this is how L'Etivaz AOP is made
- The **inalpe** (montée à l'alpage) in spring marks the festive ascent of decorated cattle to the high pastures, accompanied by **alphorn** players and flag throwers
- The **désalpe** (descente de l'alpage) in autumn celebrates the return: cows descend wearing enormous **bells** (sonnailles) and colorful **floral headdresses** (couronnes de fleurs)
- The herders wear traditional costume: the **bredzon** (a dark blue smock) for men, with tools of the trade — milking stools, cheese molds, wooden buckets
- This seasonal rhythm is the foundation of the region's economy, identity, and landscape — the open pastures, scattered chalets, and terraced hillsides are all products of centuries of transhumance

## 11. World War I Internment — The First Trainload

- During World War I, neutral Switzerland accepted **68,000 sick or injured prisoners of war** from both sides of the conflict for medical care and convalescence
- Château-d'Oex was the **first community chosen** to host internees — its letter offering to help was the first received by the Swiss government
- On **30 May 1916**, the first contingent of **700 British soldiers** arrived by train to cheering crowds; witnesses reported that many soldiers were "crying like children" and some fainted with emotion at the reception
- British soldiers were concentrated in areas popular with English-speaking tourists before the war, principally **Château-d'Oex** and **Mürren**; Germans were placed in German-speaking regions; French and Belgians in francophone areas
- Between January 1916 and August 1919, Switzerland interned **37,515 French**, **21,000 German**, **4,326 Belgian**, and **4,081 British** soldiers

## 12. Notable People and Connections

- **David Niven** (1910–1983): The English film actor and writer bought a chalet in Château-d'Oex in **1960** and lived there for **23 years** until his death, dividing his time between the village and Cap Ferrat on the Côte d'Azur. He sponsored the first balloon festival in 1979. Neighbors included **Deborah Kerr**, **Peter Ustinov**, and **Noël Coward**
- **M. C. Escher** (1898–1972): The Dutch graphic artist lived in Château-d'Oex from **4 July 1935** to **1937**, fleeing Fascist Italy after his son was forced to join the Balilla Youth Movement. His youngest son Arthur needed mountain air for tuberculosis. Escher befriended local painter **John Paschoud** and held a joint exhibition of 38 woodcuts and lithographs on **6 January 1937** in Paschoud's studio
- **Mike Horn** (born 1966): The South African-born Swiss explorer arrived in Château-d'Oex by chance in **1990**, met his wife Cathy in a village café, and has maintained his residence there ever since. Known for solo circumnavigation of the equator (2001) and the Arctic Circle (2004), and the first winter trek to the North Pole without motorized transport (2006)
- **Balthus** (Count Balthazar Klossowski de Rola, 1908–2001): The French-Polish painter acquired the Grand Chalet in Rossinière in **1977** and lived there until his death, making the Pays-d'Enhaut an artists' destination once again
- **Bertrand Piccard** (born 1958): While not a resident, his three launches from Château-d'Oex (1997, 1998, 1999) cemented the village's place in aviation history; he later piloted **Solar Impulse** on the first solar-powered flight around the world (2015–2016)

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The balloon that flew around the world** — A child watches from the snow-covered meadow as the Breitling Orbiter 3 inflates at dawn; stows away in a dream and circles the globe in 20 days, seeing deserts, oceans, and the Egyptian landing from the gondola window
2. **The paper cutter's gift** — Retelling of Johann Jakob Hauswirth's life: a poor woodcutter thanks the farmers who shelter him by cutting intricate Alpine scenes from a single sheet of black paper; a child discovers one of his lost cuttings hidden inside a chalet wall
3. **The cheese that climbed the mountain** — A child joins the inalpe, helping herd cows up to the alpine pasture; learns to make L'Etivaz cheese over a wood fire in a copper cauldron, and discovers why the mountain flowers make the cheese taste different from any other
4. **David Niven and the first balloon** — Winter 1979: a child befriends the famous actor who lives in the big chalet, and together they watch 12 colorful balloons rise over the snow-covered valley for the very first time
5. **The soldiers who cried** — May 1916: a child from Château-d'Oex stands at the train station as 700 injured British soldiers arrive; despite not sharing a language, friendship forms through shared meals, mountain walks, and the universal language of kindness
6. **Escher's impossible chalet** — 1936: a child peeks into the Dutch artist's studio and discovers drawings where staircases go up and down at the same time, fish become birds, and the Pays-d'Enhaut chalets fold into impossible geometry
7. **The three Piccards** — A child at the balloon museum learns about grandfather Auguste in the stratosphere, father Jacques at the bottom of the ocean, and grandson Bertrand circling the earth — three generations, three frontiers: sky, sea, and around the world
8. **The Grand Chalet's 113 windows** — A child exploring the largest wooden house in Switzerland discovers that each of the 113 windows shows a different scene from the building's history: a cheese merchant in 1756, Victor Hugo writing at a desk, Balthus painting in the attic
