## 1. Geography — A Village in a Cauldron of Stone

- **Leukerbad** (French: **Loeche-les-Bains**) sits at **1,411 meters** above sea level in the upper Dala Valley, canton of **Valais**, southwestern Switzerland
- The village lies in a dramatic natural amphitheatre — a high Alpine basin enclosed on three sides by sheer **limestone cliffs** rising to over 2,500 meters, with the towering **Gemmiwand** (Gemmi Wall) to the north
- Population: approximately **1,500** permanent residents, though the village swells with visitors year-round
- Access from the Rhone Valley below is via a serpentine mountain road climbing from the town of **Leuk** (Loeche), or by dramatic descent from the **Gemmi Pass** (2,346 m) on foot or by cable car
- The **Dala River** flows through the village and carves the spectacular **Dalaschlucht** (Dala Gorge) on its way down to the Rhone
- The surrounding peaks — **Torrenthorn** (2,998 m), **Daubenhorn** (2,942 m), **Majinghorn**, and **Rinderhorn** — form a ring of stone that traps warmth and shelters the village from northern winds

## 2. The Thermal Springs — Water from the Deep

- Leukerbad is the **largest thermal spa resort in the Alps** — no other Alpine location has a greater supply of natural thermal water
- **65 thermal springs** exist in the village, of which **8 remain in active use** today
- The springs deliver over **3.9 million litres** of thermal water per day, emerging at temperatures up to **51 degrees C**
- **Geological journey**: The water begins as precipitation at **2,300–3,000 meters** altitude, seeps underground through porous limestone, descends to approximately **500 meters below sea level**, is heated by geothermal energy, enriched with **calcium and sulphate** (1,800–2,000 mg/l dissolved solids), plus sodium, strontium, iron, and fluoride, then rises back to the surface — a journey of over **40 years**
- The characteristic **rust-red fango (mud)** visible on rocks near the springs results from the high iron content
- The springs emerge primarily near the **Majinghorn** and **Torrenthorn** mountains, many of them visible in the Dala Gorge where rust-brown streaks stain the rock faces

## 3. Roman and Medieval Origins

- Habitation in the Leukerbad area dates to approximately the **4th century BC**, evidenced by graves and ceramics
- The Romans knew the springs as **"Balnea Leucensia"** — traces of Celtic culture and objects from the times of emperors **Vespasian** and **Diocletian** confirm Roman use of the thermal waters
- **5th century**: Burgundian migrations reshaped the region; **9th century**: Alemanni invasions contributed to the bilingual character of the Valais (French and German)
- **1229**: First documented mention of the village, then called **"Boez"** — French was the locally spoken language at this time
- **1315**: Leukerbad became an **autonomous municipality**; the oldest known document about the village already mentions the baths
- **1449**: A trail connecting **Leuk** (in the valley below) to Leukerbad was formally established
- By the **16th century**, German had replaced French as the local language, brought by the **Walser migrations** — the Walliserdeutsch dialect spoken today is a legacy of this shift

## 4. Cardinal Matthaeus Schiner — The Bishop Who Made Leukerbad Famous

- **Matthaeus Schiner** (c. 1465–1522), born to peasant parents in Muehlebach, Upper Valais, rose to become one of the most powerful figures in European politics
- Educated at cathedral schools in **Sion** and **Como**, fluent in German, Latin, French, and Italian
- Appointed **Bishop of Sion** in **1499**; created **Cardinal** in March **1511**
- In **1501**, Schiner acquired the **rights to the hot springs** in Leukerbad, invested in the baths, and had a magnificent inn built
- He liked to ride from his castle in **Leuk** to the soothing springs to recover from the stresses of politics, often hosting European dignitaries
- Schiner promoted Leukerbad's thermal cures on his diplomatic travels throughout Europe — this marks the **beginning of modern spa tourism** in the village
- His broader career was extraordinary: he served as **Papal Minister of War** under Pope Julius II, directed Swiss mercenary forces in the Italian Wars, and helped expel the French from northern Italy
- After the catastrophic **Battle of Marignano (1515)**, where approximately 10,000 Swiss soldiers died, Schiner's political power collapsed
- He died of plague in **Rome in 1522**, having narrowly missed election as **Pope** — French cardinals blocked his candidacy
- He remains one of the most remarkable figures in Valais history: a peasant's son who nearly became leader of all Christendom

## 5. The Gemmi Pass — The Terrifying Cliff Path

- The **Gemmi Pass** (2,346 m) is a high mountain crossing linking **Leukerbad** (Valais) with **Kandersteg** (Bernese Oberland) — one of the few historical routes connecting the Rhone Valley to the north
- **Roman coins** found along the trail confirm it has been in use for many hundreds of years; soldiers, pilgrims, and traders have crossed it since antiquity
- The southern descent is the dramatic part: the path drops nearly **900 meters** down the sheer **Gemmiwand** — a vertical rock wall approximately **550 meters** (1,800 feet) high
- In **1739–1741**, a band of **Tyrolean workmen** carved the current mule path into the cliff face in a series of extraordinary **zigzags** — an engineering marvel of the 18th century
- Before this improvement, the path was even more perilous — yet it was still used as a vital trade route
- German writer **Wolfgang Menzel** described it as: *"The boldest structures in the world do not give a greater idea of human courage and human strength than this suspended path in the air"*
- Wealthy travellers historically used **"Gemmi-Waegeli"** — special carriages — or were carried in litters by porters
- A **cable car (Gemmibahn)** was first opened in **1957** (after a licence application in 1908), renovated in 1975, and rebuilt with a modern cabin in **2012**, carrying passengers from 1,411 m to 2,350 m
- At the summit: the **Berghotel Wildstrubel**, panoramic views of the Matterhorn, Monte Rosa, Weisshorn, and Dent Blanche, plus the dark waters of **Daubensee** (lake)

## 6. Mark Twain and the Gemmi Descent (1878)

- **Mark Twain** crossed the Gemmi Pass in the **summer of 1878**, descending to Leukerbad with his companion "Mr. Harris" (Joseph Twichell) — the journey is recounted in **Chapter XXXV** of *A Tramp Abroad* (1880)
- Twain described the descent: *"the most remarkable road I have ever seen. It wound its corkscrew curves down the face of the colossal precipice — a narrow way, with always the solid rock wall at one elbow, and perpendicular nothingness at the other"*
- He noted the hazards of meeting mule trains on the narrow path — *"the mule prefers the outside"* — forcing walkers to press against the cliff wall
- Twain also described the **Hotel Schwarenbach** high on the pass as sitting *"on a lonely spot among the peaks, where it's swept by the trailing fringes of the cloud rack and is rained on and snowed on and pelted and persecuted by the storms nearly every day of its life"*
- In Leukerbad itself, Twain visited the communal thermal baths: *"There are several of these big bathing houses, and you can always tell when you are near one of them by the romping noises and shouts of laughter"*
- Twain also memorably noted the difficulty of reaching the village: *"You must come on foot or be carried"*

## 7. Goethe, Maupassant, and the Literary Pilgrims

- **Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** arrived in Leukerbad on **9 November 1779**, aged 30, travelling with **Duke Carl August** of Saxe-Weimar
- They took a demanding path from **Sitten (Sion)** through the village of **Inden** and arrived around three in the afternoon
- Goethe took daily hour-long baths in the thermal waters and wrote admiringly of them in his *Letters from Switzerland*: *"This water has not the slightest sulphurous odour... it is very hot and famous for its good powers"*
- Of the Gemmi approach he noted: *"The path to Inden is cut into the steep rockface which encloses this amphitheatre... It is not a dangerous path, it just looks terrifying"*
- A trail called **"Goethe's Path"** still exists, and the **Haus Goethe** in the village honours his visit
- **Guy de Maupassant** visited the Schwarenbach Inn on the Gemmi in **1877** and later wrote the chilling short story **"The Inn" (L'Auberge)**, published in **1886** — a tale of two mountain guides left to guard the inn through winter, slowly driven mad by isolation, snow, and an invisible menace
- **Pablo Picasso** crossed the Gemmi Pass in **1933** — his signature is preserved in the Schwarenbach guest books
- **Jules Verne** visited Leukerbad in **1873**
- **Alexandre Dumas** and **Vladimir Lenin** also stayed in the village at different times
- **Arthur Conan Doyle** referenced the Gemmi route in the Sherlock Holmes story *The Final Problem* — Holmes uses the pass to reach Meiringen in the Bernese Oberland

## 8. James Baldwin — Stranger in the Village (1951–1953)

- **James Baldwin**, the African-American novelist and essayist, first came to Leukerbad in **1951** at age 27, nearly suffering a breakdown in Paris
- His partner, Swiss painter **Lucien Happersberger**, brought him to Happersberger's family chalet in the village to recover
- Baldwin spent the **winters of 1951–1952 and 1952–1953** in Leukerbad, working in near-total isolation — the village had *"no movie house, no bank, no library, no theater; very few radios"*
- In the chalet, he completed his first novel, **Go Tell It on the Mountain** (published 1953), listening to records by **Bessie Smith** and **Fats Waller**
- Baldwin was the **first Black person** many villagers had ever seen — children shouted **"Neger! Neger!"** in the streets, adults touched his hair and skin with curiosity
- He channelled these experiences into the landmark essay **"Stranger in the Village"**, published in **Harper's Magazine** in **October 1953** and later collected in *Notes of a Native Son* (1955)
- The essay's most famous line: *"This world is white no longer, and it will never be white again"*
- Baldwin drew a devastating parallel: while Leukerbad's curiosity was innocent, the same word on American lips carried centuries of hatred — *"I am a stranger here. But I am not a stranger in America"*
- In **2018**, Swiss-Haitian artist **Sasha Huber** permanently installed a portrait of Baldwin — made entirely of **metal staples** on a wooden window shutter — on his former chalet in Leukerbad
- The **Leukerbad International Literary Festival**, founded in **1996**, carries forward the village's deep literary heritage — the only destination literary festival in Switzerland, drawing 35–40 international authors annually

## 9. The Dala Gorge — Where the Earth Breathes Heat

- The **Dalaschlucht** (Dala Gorge) begins less than **one kilometre** from the village square — a narrow, moss-covered canyon carved by the Dala River
- The **Thermal Springs Footbridge** (Thermalquellen-Steg) spans approximately **600 meters** through the gorge, standing about **4 meters** above the riverbed
- Along the route, thermal water seeps from cracks and fissures in the rock walls, leaving **rust-brown streaks** from the iron content — visitors can feel the warmth radiating from the stone
- A **21-meter-long bridge** crosses to a **35-meter-high waterfall** within the gorge
- The **Thermal Springs Route** (Thermalquellen-Weg) is a 2.5 km walking path through the village and along the gorge, with information panels explaining the geology and history of the springs
- The gorge is where the underground thermal waters first become visible — a place where the 40-year journey of the water from alpine precipitation to heated mineral spring can be witnessed at the surface

## 10. Avalanches — The Village That Refused to Die

- Leukerbad's dramatic position beneath towering cliffs has made it vulnerable to **avalanches** for centuries
- **1518**: An avalanche killed **61 people** — one of the earliest recorded disasters
- **17 January 1718**: A massive avalanche destroyed **every house** in the village, killing **52 people** — exactly 200 years after the first catastrophe
- Between the 16th and 18th centuries, several major avalanches struck, but the inhabitants **rebuilt each time**
- **1829–1831**: The **Werimauer** was constructed — considered **Switzerland's first avalanche wall**, a massive stone barrier designed to deflect avalanche flows away from the village
- The Werimauer still stands today and is featured on a historical **avalanche trail hike**, where visitors can learn about the centuries-old struggle of the Walliser mountain community against the forces of nature
- The village's repeated destruction and rebuilding is a testament to the stubbornness and resilience of the Valaisan people — and to the irresistible draw of the thermal springs

## 11. The Modern Thermal Baths — Burgerbad and Alpentherme

- **Leukerbad Therme** (formerly **Burgerbad**): Originally built in **1968**, permanently remodelled from 1980, today features **10 thermal pools**, a modern sauna area, and Switzerland's largest Alpine water slide (182 feet) — it is the **largest thermal bath in the Alpine region**
- **Walliser Alpentherme & Spa**: Opened in **1993**, replacing the traditional **Lorenzbad** — features an infinity-edge thermal pool with views of the surrounding peaks, a **Valais Sauna Village** (over 300 square meters), and a Roman-Irish bath ritual
- **Therme 51 degrees**: Named for the maximum spring temperature, a boutique spa recognised as a **Swiss medicinal bath**, with pools at 36–42 degrees C, Finnish and alpine herbal saunas
- In total, over **250 different treatment options** are available across the village's thermal facilities
- Leukerbad is also an established training centre for **physiotherapy** — its thermal waters have been used for medical rehabilitation for centuries
- **1889**: Leukerbad became one of the first electrified villages in the canton, when the **first power station in the Valais** was built to serve the spa infrastructure

## 12. Via Ferrata and Climbing — Vertical Adventures

- The **Daubenhorn Via Ferrata** is the **longest via ferrata in Switzerland** — a route of over **2,000 meters of steel cable** and **216 meters of ladders**, some of them overhanging
- Difficulty: **K5–K6** (extremely difficult), requiring 5–7 hours, excellent fitness, and strong nerves
- Highlights include passage through a **cave crevice with a waterfall**, two **suspension bridges**, and the spectacular "Underworld" detour
- A shorter version (K4–K5) covers the first section with its exposed ladder series
- The **Tolen climbing area** near the village offers **23 routes** (up to grade 6b), including 11 beginner and youth routes
- Combined with the thermal baths, Leukerbad offers a unique formula: **extreme vertical adventure by day, hot mineral water by night**

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The water that remembers** — A child dips into the thermal pool and the water shows visions of its 40-year journey: falling as snow on the peaks, sinking through rock layers, heating in the deep earth, rising to the surface — the child follows the water's memory back through time
2. **The mule on the Gemmi Wall** — A stubborn mule on the 1741 cliff path refuses to move, blocking the entire trail; a child must befriend the frightened animal and guide it down the terrifying zigzags while traders, pilgrims, and porters wait impatiently above
3. **Baldwin's typewriter** — A child exploring an old Leukerbad chalet finds a vintage typewriter that types stories on its own; each page reveals a scene from the village's past through the eyes of a stranger — echoing Baldwin's experience of being the first outsider to see this world differently
4. **Twain's corkscrew** — Mark Twain's hat blows off the Gemmi precipice and a child chases it down the spiralling cliff path, tumbling through chapters of history at every switchback: Roman traders, medieval pilgrims, Goethe sketching the view, Picasso signing the guest book
5. **The cardinal's cure** — Cardinal Schiner, exhausted from wars and papal politics, rides to Leukerbad to heal; a village child becomes his unlikely guide to the springs and shows him that the water's real magic is not in curing the body but in slowing down
6. **The avalanche wall** — In 1718 an avalanche buries the entire village; a child and a mountain dog must dig through snow to find survivors, guided by the faint warmth of the thermal springs seeping up through the frozen ground
7. **Maupassant's ghost at the Schwarenbach** — A child stranded overnight at the lonely Schwarenbach Inn on the Gemmi hears strange sounds in the snow; the inn's guest book comes alive with stories from Maupassant, Twain, and Picasso, each warning about a different danger — but only the child can figure out which story is real
8. **The rust-red river** — A child following the iron-stained waters through the Dala Gorge discovers a hidden spring that no one has found before; the water is warmer and older than any other, and it holds the memory of the very first people who bathed here in Roman times
