## 1. Geography — The Dead-End Valley at the Foot of the Wildstrubel

- Adelboden lies at **1,350 m** elevation on a sunny terrace at the upper end of the **Engstligen valley** (locally: **Entschligental**) in the western **Bernese Oberland**, Canton of Bern
- It is a classic **dead-end valley** (**Sackgasse**) — only one road leads in from **Frutigen** to the north; no pass road crosses out the other side
- The municipality covers **87.6 km²**, ranging from **1,045 m** (valley floor of the Engstlige river) to **3,243 m** (Grossstrubel summit)
- Land use: **41.4%** farmland, **18.2%** forest, **36.2%** unproductive mountain terrain (rock, ice, scree)
- The Engstlige river flows northward through the valley, joining the **Kander** at Frutigen, which eventually reaches the Aare
- The village sits on a natural terrace looking south toward the **Engstligen Falls** and the great wall of cliffs below Engstligenalp
- Population: approximately **3,343** (2020), a community shaped by farming and tourism
- The municipality encompasses numerous scattered **Bäuerten** (traditional hamlets/school districts): **Innerschwand** (today the village centre), **Ausserschwand**, **Stiegelschwand**, **Hirzboden**, and **Boden** — an ancient pattern of dispersed alpine settlement

## 2. Name and Early History — "In Valle Adelboden"

- First mentioned in historical records in **1409** as **"in valle Adelboden"** (in the valley of Adelboden)
- In **1453**, recorded as **"Adelboden alias silva"** — emphasising the forested character of the area
- The **etymology** likely derives from Middle High German **"adel"** (noble) + **"boden"** (ground), meaning "noble ground"; a local theory links it to **Adelgras** (*Poa alpina*), a prevalent alpine grass species
- The alps **Engstligenalp** and **Silleren** belonging to Adelboden first appeared in records in the **13th century**
- **1400**: Adelboden came under the authority of Bern, administered by the **Bernese castle of Frutigen** (Kastlanei Frutigen)
- In the **15th century**, the remote farming community petitioned for its own church — the request was initially **refused** by the monastery at Interlaken and the Bishop of Lausanne
- **Twelve independent men** built the church at their own risk; when finished, **56 property owners** pledged their entire properties as surety to fund a priest's salary
- During the **Reformation** in the 1520s–1530s, Adelboden joined the Protestant movement; the Catholic priest **fled over the Hahnenmoospass** to Catholic Fribourg

## 3. The Village Church — Giacometti Windows and Medieval Frescoes

- The **Reformed Church of Adelboden** (Reformierte Kirche) dates from **1433**, built by those twelve determined men against official opposition
- The church has experienced **only minor structural changes** since construction — one of the best-preserved medieval churches in the Bernese Oberland
- The **entrance frescoes** (Vorhalle) are particularly notable, with well-preserved medieval wall paintings
- In **1937**, the Swiss artist **Augusto Giacometti** (1877–1947) created the **choral stained-glass windows** — luminous, colour-saturated works that brought modern art into the 500-year-old church
- Giacometti, from **Stampa in Grisons**, was a cousin of Giovanni Giacometti (father of the famous sculptor Alberto Giacometti); Augusto was one of Switzerland's first abstract painters and a master of stained glass, with commissions also at the **Grossmünster** in Zurich (1933) and the **Fraumünster** (1945)
- A brass **memorial plaque** in the church archway commemorates **Camp Moloney**, the American WWII internment camp (see section 5)
- Classical music **concerts** are regularly held inside the church, taking advantage of its intimate medieval acoustics
- The church remains the spiritual heart of a community that is **95% German-speaking** and historically **66% Reformed Protestant**

## 4. The Rise of Tourism — From Farmhouse to Grand Hotels

- **1873**: Teacher **Christian Hari** (1834–1897) accommodated the first guests in his farmhouse on the Schlegeli, at the suggestion of Pastor **Karl Rohr** from Bern — founding tourism in Adelboden
- **1878**: Hari built the **Stöckli** guesthouse to handle growing international demand
- **1884**: Opening of the **Frutigen–Adelboden road** along the Engstlige river connected the village to the outside world, ending centuries of relative isolation and giving a strong boost to tourism
- **1901**: The **Bellevue Parkhotel** was built — one of Adelboden's first purpose-built grand hotels
- **1903**: **Sir Henry Lunn** (1859–1939), British travel pioneer and founder of Lunn Poly, organised the **first packaged winter sports holiday** to Adelboden through the **Public Schools Alpine Sports Club**, booking entire hotels for English upper-class guests
- **1904**: The **Hotel Adler** was built with electric light and running water in all rooms — a luxury for the era
- By **1914**, the Hotel Hari complex alone had **nine buildings** with roughly **130 guest rooms**; under **Johannes Hari** (1864–1938), Christian's son, the hotel reached **200 beds**
- The **1930s–1940s** brought hardship: world wars and the Depression caused tourism to collapse; hotels housed **internees and war-displaced children** during the 1940s
- Post-war recovery transformed Adelboden into a major **year-round alpine resort** with over **200 km of ski slopes** (Adelboden-Lenk area) and **300 km of hiking trails**

## 5. World War II — Camp Moloney and the Interned Airmen

- During World War II, neutral Switzerland interned foreign military personnel who crossed its borders; the remote, dead-end valley of Adelboden was chosen as an ideal internment site — surrounded by mountains, with only one road in
- **1940**: The Swiss military began converting Adelboden's largely empty tourist **chalets and hotels** into internment facilities
- The camp primarily housed **American airmen** whose bombers were damaged over Germany and France and diverted to Swiss airspace; it was the **first internment camp in Switzerland for American personnel**
- The Americans named it **"Camp Moloney"** (also spelled Maloney) after **Sergeant Joseph F. Maloney**, a ball turret gunner who was the first American serviceman to die in Switzerland, killed during an emergency landing on **October 1943**; he was buried at **Bad Ragaz** cemetery
- Conditions were austere: hot water only once every ten days, limited coal for heating, and rations of **black bread, potatoes, and watery soup**; meat (usually blood sausage) only once a week
- As the air war intensified in **1944–1945**, the number of interned airmen grew; enlisted men went to **Adelboden or Wengen**, officers to **Davos**
- Some internees attempted escape; the American Legation in Bern covertly aided escape efforts
- Today a **brass memorial plaque** (approximately 60 cm x 60 cm) in the archway of the village church commemorates the camp and the airmen who were interned there
- The episode remains a little-known but important chapter connecting this small Oberland village to the global conflict of the 20th century

## 6. The Chuenisbärgli — World Cup Giant Slalom on the Legendary Slope

- The **Chuenisbärgli** is a World Cup ski course on natural terrain above Adelboden, opened in **1955**
- **1956**: Dr. **Fred Rubi**, former ski racer and course director, organised the inaugural **"Internationale Adelbodner Skitage"** (International Adelboden Ski Days) — 36 racers in sweaters and wool caps before 2,000 spectators
- **1967**: The giant slalom was included in the very **first FIS Alpine Ski World Cup season**
- Adelboden is one of only **three places** (with **Wengen** and **Kitzbühel**) that have hosted races **every year** since the World Cup began in 1967
- **2002**: The GS start was moved to the top of the former ski lift, adding the fearsome **"Kanonenrohr" (cannon barrel)** section — gradient up to **60%** through more than 60 gates
- The course drops from **1,730 m** at the start to the village finish area, making it one of the most demanding GS courses in the world
- **2006**: The current format was established — **Giant Slalom on Saturday, Slalom on Sunday**
- Today: approximately **35,000–40,000 spectators** attend the weekend; **1,700 volunteers** organise the event; budget of **CHF 7.5 million**; broadcast to **15 countries**
- **1998**: The World Cup was saved when **450 trucks of snow** were hauled to the course; since **1999**, artificial snow-making equipment ensures reliable conditions
- **January 10, 2026**: **Marco Odermatt** won his **fifth consecutive** Adelboden giant slalom — surpassing **Ingemar Stenmark's** record of four consecutive wins (1978–82) to stand alone in Chuenisbärgli history
- The finish-area atmosphere is described as a **"boiling witch's cauldron"** (brodelnder Hexenkessel) — a sea of red-and-white Swiss flags

## 7. The Engstligenalp — Largest High Plateau in the Western Swiss Alps

- The **Engstligenalp** is a vast limestone plateau at **1,900–2,000 m** elevation south of Adelboden
- It measures roughly **1 km x 2 km** (oval form) covering approximately **7 km²** — the **largest high plateau in the western Swiss Alps**
- Surrounded by peaks: the **Wildstrubel** (SW), **Lohner massif**, and **Bunderspitz**
- Since **1996**, classified as a **Swiss landscape of national importance** (BLN No. 1513: Engstligenalp and Entschligefäll, covering 2,494 hectares)
- **Alpine pasture tradition** dates to the **Middle Ages**; today the alp is owned by a cooperative of about **100 farmers** from Frutigen and Adelboden
- From late June to mid-September, **500 cattle** of the **Simmental breed** (the big brown-and-white **Fleckvieh**) graze on the plateau, continuing a practice that predates written records
- The **Alpaufzug** (cattle ascent) and **Alpabzug** (cattle descent) along the steep, narrow rocky path are among Switzerland's most spectacular alpine traditions
- Traditional **cheesemaking** (Alpkäserei) is still practised on-site in timber huts with copper pots
- **14th century**: Four huts existed on Engstligenalp; records mention **robbers** stealing cattle and burning structures
- **12 September 1937**: The **first aerial cableway for passenger transport in Canton Bern** was inaugurated to Engstligenalp, financed entirely by the **Müller family** — four-seater cabins carried tourists summer and winter
- **1963**: A second lift with 12-seater cabins replaced the original; **2016**: two new modern gondolas opened
- **Globi adventure area** on the plateau — a children's playground themed around the beloved Swiss cartoon character

## 8. The Engstligen Falls — Switzerland's Second-Highest Waterfall

- The **Engstligen Falls** (Engstligenfälle) cascade approximately **600 m** from the high plateau of Engstligenalp into the valley — the **second-highest waterfall in Switzerland**
- The falls consist of **two main drops**: Engstligen Fall I (**97 m**) and Engstligen Fall II (**165 m**), with cascades between them making up the total drop
- Under **cantonal nature protection since 1948** — one of the earliest protected natural monuments in the Bernese Oberland
- The water source is the Engstlige river, fed by snowmelt and springs on the plateau above
- The falls can be reached on foot from Adelboden in approximately **90 minutes**, or viewed from above via the Engstligenalp cable car, which passes directly over the cascades
- In winter, the falls partially **freeze into enormous ice columns** — a dramatic sight against the cliff face
- A new **Waterfall Loop Trail** (3 km, ~70 min, medium difficulty) offers viewpoints including a selfie station

## 9. The Wildstrubel — Dominant Peak and Glacier Massif

- The **Wildstrubel** (3,244 m) is the dominant mountain closing the valley to the south, straddling the border between cantons **Bern and Valais**
- It forms a broad, heavily glaciated massif approximately **15 km wide**, extending between the **Rawil Pass** and the **Gemmi Pass**
- Three nearly equal summits along a **3.5 km ridge**: Wildstrubel/Lenkerstrubel (3,244 m), Middle Peak (Mittelgipfel, 3,243 m), and Grossstrubel (3,243 m)
- **First ascent**: probably by **Jakob Tritten** and **Dr. M. Schmid** in the summer of **1855**; a documented ascent by **Edmund von Fellenberg** and Tritten followed on 16 August **1856**
- **1858**: **Leslie Stephen** (1832–1904), prominent Victorian alpinist and father of Virginia Woolf, made an early ascent of the Wildstrubel during the **Golden Age of Alpinism**
- The **Plaine Morte Glacier** on the south side is one of the largest plateau glaciers in the Alps
- The mountain is climbed as an **alpine hike or ski tour** rather than a technical climb, though glacier crossings are required
- Since 2022, the **Wildstrubel by UTMB** trail-running event circles the entire massif: the flagship **Wild 110** covers 113 km with 6,000 m elevation gain, crossing two cantons and two language regions via the Gemmi and Rawil passes

## 10. The Tschentenalp — Adelboden's Local Mountain

- The **Tschentenalp** (1,950 m) is Adelboden's **Hausberg** (local mountain), reachable by cable car in **7 minutes** from the village
- A popular **paragliding** launch site — tandem flights with certified pilots take off from a meadow 10 minutes above the mountain station, landing in Oey in the valley
- Over **30 km of hiking trails** of varying difficulty radiate from the summit station
- Home to the **Giant Swing** (Riesenschaukel) — a large swing set against the panorama of the Bernese Alps
- The **Schwandfeldspitz** viewpoint is only 20 minutes from the top station
- In winter, the Tschentenalp served as a backup race venue for the World Cup — snow was relocated here **11 times** between 1959 and 1994

## 11. The Cholerenschlucht and Pochtenkessel — Gorge and Rock Cauldron

- The **Cholerenschlucht** (Choleren Gorge) is a narrow river gorge where the **Tschentebach** has carved through massive limestone over thousands of years, creating smooth, rounded erosion forms and potholes only a few metres wide
- Accessible via a system of **metal walkways and stairs** bolted into the rock walls — the gorge is about **100 m long**
- The circular hike is **2.5 km** with 110 m elevation gain, taking approximately **45 minutes**
- At the lower entrance: the **Jungbrunnen** ("Fountain of Youth") — a natural pool for cooling off
- Nearby lies the **Pochtenkessel** — a dramatic natural **rock funnel** where the Engstlige and Otterebach rivers converge in a deep, hollowed-out amphitheatre; equipped with bridges and staircases since **2004**
- Both are **free to enter** (donation box for maintenance); open **June to October**
- Not recommended after heavy rain due to flash-flood risk

## 12. Alpine Traditions — Wood Carving, Flower Trails, and Vogellisi

- **Wood carving** (Holzschnitzerei) is a deep-rooted craft in the Bernese Oberland; in Adelboden, the **Trummer family** has practised the art since **1945**, passing skills across generations
- Historically, carving was a pastime for **herdsmen and shepherds** (Sennen und Hirten) during long alpine summers, evolving over decades into an artistic craft
- The **Adelboden Flower Trail** (Blumenweg, open May–October, ~45 min) teaches visitors to identify alpine wildflowers — **gentian, alpine anemone, marigold, saxifrage** — along marked paths with botanical illustrations
- The **Vogellisi Theme Trail** (Vogellisi-Erlebnisweg, 3.5 km, ~70 min) on the Sillerenbühl is a children's adventure path based on the story of **Vogellisi**, a girl who can speak to birds and knows every mountain herb's healing power
- Written by Adelboden native **Annemarie Stähli** and illustrated by **Karin Widmer** (published **2017**), the story is set at the beginning of the 20th century, when Adelboden was transforming from an impoverished farming village into a tourist destination
- **Folkloric evenings** (Folklore-Abende) are organised in summer (June–August), featuring **yodelling, Schwingen** (Swiss wrestling), **alphorn**, and folk dancing — a living showcase of Oberland culture
- **Local dialect**: Adelbodnertütsch, belonging to the **Highest Alemannic** (Höchstalemannisch) dialect group — one of the most conservative German dialects in Switzerland

## 13. Notable Facts and Modern Adelboden

- The village retains its traditional character: **no high-rise buildings**, only traditional **chalet-style architecture** (Bernese Oberland Chalet) with carved wooden balconies, flower boxes, and wide overhanging roofs
- The **Adelboden-Lenk ski area** spans over **200 km of groomed slopes** across interconnected mountains
- One of **Europe's longest suspension bridges** spans the Engstligen river near the village
- The municipality's economy rests on three pillars: **agriculture** (especially cattle farming), **tourism** (summer hiking, winter skiing), and **local industry** (wood building companies, mineral water source)
- **Fred Rubi**, the legendary course director who organised World Cup racing from 1956, stepped down in **1995** after nearly four decades; a new four-member organising committee took over

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The girl who spoke to birds** — Inspired by the Vogellisi legend: a shy child discovers she can understand the alpine choughs and marmots on the Tschentenalp, who guide her to a lost healing herb before winter comes
2. **The five gates of the Chuenisbärgli** — On the morning of the World Cup, a child sneaks onto the empty race course and discovers that each gate is a portal to a different era of Adelboden's history: 1409 (first settlers), 1873 (first tourist), 1937 (first cable car), 1967 (first World Cup)
3. **The waterfall that froze the giant** — A legend of the Engstligen Falls: a giant once blocked the water to steal the plateau for himself; the falls froze him into the cliff face, and every spring the thawing ice reveals his outline
4. **500 bells on the mountain** — The Alpaufzug from a young Simmental calf's perspective: the terrifying climb up the narrow rocky path to the vast plateau of Engstligenalp, where 500 cattle bells ring together for the first time
5. **The clockmaker's gorge** — In the Cholerenschlucht, a child finds a centuries-old water wheel still turning; following its mechanism deeper into the rock, they discover a hidden workshop where a forgotten craftsman once carved the village's first clock
6. **The noble ground** — A tale of the 12 stubborn men who built Adelboden's church in 1433 against the wishes of the bishop and the monastery, told from the perspective of the youngest builder's child who carried stones up the hill
7. **The airmen in the chalets** — Set in winter 1943: an Adelboden girl befriends a young American airman interned at Camp Moloney; she smuggles him warm bread and teaches him the word "Jungbrunnen," while he teaches her to fold paper aeroplanes that fly over the mountains
8. **The first winter guests** — Set in 1903: an Adelboden farm girl watches in amazement as Sir Henry Lunn's English guests arrive with strange wooden planks strapped to their feet, and she becomes the first local child to try skiing
