## 1. Geography — The Reuss Valley and the Urnersee

- Altdorf lies in the lower **Reuss Valley** (Reusstal) on the eastern bank of the Reuss river, at an elevation of about **458 meters** above sea level
- The town sits roughly **2 kilometers south** of the **Urnersee**, the southernmost arm of Lake Lucerne (Vierwaldstättersee)
- Dramatic mountain scenery surrounds the town: peaks rise steeply to over **1,700 meters** at the Eggberge above Altdorf
- The Eggberge sun-terrace at **1,400 meters** offers panoramic views and serves as a year-round excursion destination
- Altdorf occupies a strategic position at the northern approach to the **Gotthard Pass** — anyone crossing the Alps via the Gotthard had to pass through Uri territory
- The Reuss flows northward through the town into the Urnersee, which connects via Lake Lucerne to the cities of Brunnen, Luzern, and beyond
- The municipality covers **10.21 square kilometers**, including the flat alluvial plain along the river

## 2. Capital of Canton Uri — A Founding Canton

- Altdorf is the **capital** (Hauptort) of Canton Uri, one of the **three founding cantons** of the Swiss Confederation alongside Schwyz and Unterwalden
- On **1 August 1291**, representatives of the three cantons signed the **Federal Charter** (Bundesbrief), a treaty of mutual recognition and military assistance — now regarded as the founding document of Switzerland
- The charter was rediscovered in the Schwyz archives only in **1758**, centuries after its creation
- August 1 is celebrated as **Swiss National Day** in commemoration
- The formal recognition of 1291 as the founding date only came at the end of the 19th century — conveniently coinciding with Bern's 700th anniversary celebrations in **1891**
- Uri had earlier received an imperial charter of freedom from King Henry VII in **1231**, granting the valley community direct imperial status (Reichsfreiheit) and exempting it from Habsburg feudal authority
- Uri's fierce independence earned it a reputation as the **"wild canton"** — its people resisted outside authority with legendary stubbornness

## 3. The Rütli Oath — Birthplace of Switzerland

- The **Rütli** (also Grütli) is a meadow on the western shore of the Urnersee, near Seelisberg — directly across the lake from Altdorf's region
- According to tradition (dated to **1307** by the chronicler Aegidius Tschudi), representatives of Uri, Schwyz, and Unterwalden gathered here to swear an oath of mutual defense against Habsburg tyranny — the **Rütlischwur**
- The Rütli Oath is the **founding myth** of Switzerland, dramatically depicted in Friedrich Schiller's play and in the frescoes of the Tellskapelle
- In **1859**, the meadow was purchased by a private organization and donated to the Swiss Confederation the following year, making it a **national monument**
- The **Swiss Path** (Weg der Schweiz), a 34-kilometer hiking trail around the Urnersee, begins at the Rütli
- Annual celebrations on August 1 are held at the Rütli, organized since **1860** by the Schweizerische Gemeinnützige Gesellschaft (SGG)

## 4. The William Tell Legend — In Full Detail

- **William Tell** (Wilhelm Tell) is Switzerland's legendary national hero — an expert crossbowman from the canton of Uri
- The first written record of the Tell legend appears in the **White Book of Sarnen** (Weisses Buch von Sarnen), compiled around **1474** by Hans Schriber, state secretary of Obwalden. The manuscript was rediscovered only in **1856**

### The Hat Edict
- **Hermann Gessler**, the Habsburg bailiff (Landvogt), placed his hat atop a pole in the main square of Altdorf and decreed that all passersby must bow before it as a sign of submission to Habsburg authority

### The Apple Shot
- On **18 November 1307** (traditional date), Tell walked through the square with his young son **Walter** and refused to bow before the hat
- Gessler arrested Tell but offered him a chance to save his life: he must shoot an **apple** off his son's head with his crossbow from a distance of **120 paces** (some sources say 70 or 80)
- Tell placed Walter against a tree, took aim, and **split the apple cleanly** without harming the boy

### The Second Arrow
- Gessler noticed Tell had concealed a **second crossbow bolt** under his coat and demanded to know its purpose
- Tell replied that if the first shot had killed his son, the second arrow would have been for **Gessler himself**
- Enraged, Gessler ordered Tell arrested and transported by boat across Lake Lucerne to his dungeon at **Küssnacht**

### Tell's Leap (Tellensprung)
- During the boat crossing, a violent storm struck the Urnersee; the guards freed Tell to steer the vessel to safety
- Near the **Tellsplatte** (at the foot of the Axenberg cliffs near Sisikon), Tell leapt from the boat onto a rocky ledge and escaped into the mountains
- A chapel has stood at this site since **1388**; the current **Tellskapelle** was built in **1879–1880** and decorated with four frescoes by Basel painter **Ernst Stückelberg** (1880–1882)

### The Revenge at the Hohle Gasse
- Tell ambushed Gessler in the **Hohle Gasse**, a narrow sunken lane between Küssnacht and Immensee, and killed the tyrant with a single crossbow bolt
- A **Tellskapelle** chapel at the Hohle Gasse, rebuilt in its current form around **1638**, commemorates the assassination
- The site was renovated in **2004/05** with an accessible path and interactive pavilion

### Historical Authenticity
- There is **no documentary evidence** that a person named Wilhelm Tell ever existed, nor that a Habsburg bailiff was assassinated in central Switzerland in this period
- The legend is widely regarded as a **founding myth** — a symbolic narrative expressing Swiss values of freedom, resistance to tyranny, and self-determination

## 5. The Tell Monument (Telldenkmal)

- The bronze statue by Zurich-born, Solothurn-based sculptor **Richard Kissling** was unveiled on **28 August 1895** at the Rathausplatz in Altdorf
- It depicts Tell in peasant clothing with his crossbow, his arm protectively around his son **Walter**, who gazes admiringly upward
- Dimensions: Tell stands nearly **4 meters** tall; the figure of Walter is **2.4 meters** high
- In **1887**, the Federal Council authorized financial support for monuments of national character; in **1890**, the Federal Art Commission approved a Tell memorial in Altdorf
- A competition was held in **March 1891**; Kissling's model was selected as the winner
- The inauguration on 27–28 August 1895 was attended by **three Federal Councillors**, representatives of all cantonal governments, and national dignitaries
- Total cost: approximately **143,000 Swiss francs**, funded through donations and cantonal contributions; the bronze was cast by **Frères Thiebaud** in Paris
- The year **1307** is engraved on the monument's base

## 6. The Türmli — Altdorf's Medieval Tower

- The **Türmli** (little tower) stands directly behind the Tell Monument at the Rathausplatz — one of Altdorf's oldest buildings
- Foundations date to the **mid-13th century**; a second storey was added shortly after as living quarters
- In **1517**, the canton of Uri acquired the tower and transformed it into a prestigious **watchtower with a clock**
- Destroyed in the village fire of **1693**, quickly rebuilt with a fifth and sixth storey added by the Uri painter **Karl Leonz Püntener**
- The clockwork mechanism dates to approximately **1690**, probably by **Johann Peter Landtwing**, a clockmaker from Zug
- In **1895**, painter **Hans Sandreuter** added a fresco depicting a Bürglen scene on the rear facade
- The Türmli was restored in **2011** (interior, new staircase) and the clockwork received conservation work in **2016**
- Open daily 9:00–19:00; visitors can climb to the summit for views across central Switzerland

## 7. The Gotthard Pass — Uri's Alpine Gateway

- The **St. Gotthard Pass** (2,107 m) connects **Andermatt** (Canton Uri) in the north to **Airolo** (Canton Ticino) in the south — one of the most important Alpine crossings in European history
- Although locally known in antiquity, the route was not widely used until the early **13th century**, when the first wooden bridge was built across the **Schöllenen Gorge** around **1220**
- A chapel honoring **St. Gotthard** was consecrated at the summit in **1230**, accompanied by a hospice operated by the Humiliati order (first recorded in **1237**)
- For centuries, trade caravans exchanged cattle, cheese, and timber from the north for grain, wine, rice, and salt from the south
- Uri's control of the northern approach gave the canton enormous **strategic and economic power**
- In **1403**, Uri made the upper Leventina (the valley south of the pass) a protectorate, extending its influence into Italian-speaking territory
- The **Pfaffenbrief** of **1370** — an agreement among the confederates — explicitly guaranteed peace along the route from Zurich to the Gotthard
- The first **railway tunnel** through the Gotthard opened in **1882** (15 km); the **Gotthard Base Tunnel** opened in **2016** as the world's longest rail tunnel at **57 km**

## 8. The Devil's Bridge and the Schöllenen Gorge

- The **Schöllenen Gorge** (Schöllenenschlucht) is a dramatic canyon carved by the upper Reuss between **Göschenen** and **Andermatt** — the critical bottleneck on the Gotthard route
- Enclosed by sheer granite walls, the gorge required spectacular bridges and tunnels to traverse
- The most famous crossing is the **Teufelsbrücke** (Devil's Bridge) — the name first recorded in **1587**
- **The legend**: The people of Uri wanted to build a bridge across the gorge but could not manage it. The **Devil** appeared and offered to build the bridge on condition that the **first soul to cross** would belong to him. The cunning Uri folk sent a **goat** across first. The enraged Devil seized a massive boulder to destroy the bridge, but an **old woman scratched a cross** on the rock, causing the Devil to flee and abandon both rock and bridge
- The first stone bridge was built in **1595**, replacing an earlier wooden structure
- In September **1799**, retreating French troops destroyed the bridge during the battle against Suvorov's Russian forces
- A replacement stone bridge was designed by cantonal engineer **Karl Emanuel Müller** and took **10 years** to construct

## 9. The Suvorov Monument — Russia in the Alps

- In September **1799**, during the Wars of the Second Coalition, Russian Field Marshal **Alexander Suvorov** led approximately **21,000 men** across the Alps through Canton Uri to engage French revolutionary forces
- On **24 September 1799**, Suvorov's forces drove the French from the **Gotthard Hospice**
- The following day, French troops under General **Claude Jacques Lecourbe** filled the Urnerloch tunnel with rubble and blew up the Devil's Bridge with gunpowder
- Suvorov's soldiers improvised a crossing using **wooden beams lashed together with officers' uniform bands** — suffering significant casualties in the process
- The **Suvorov Monument** was erected between **1895 and 1898** according to plans by architect **Alexander Werschinsky**, with approval from the Swiss Confederation
- The 12-meter-high granite cross is carved into the rock face in the Schöllenen Gorge, near the Devil's Bridge
- The plot of land on which it stands is **perpetually owned by the Russian Federation** — though it is not an extraterritorial zone
- The monument is registered as a **Swiss cultural asset of national importance**

## 10. The Tellspiele — Schiller's Drama Comes Home

- **Friedrich Schiller** published his verse drama **"Wilhelm Tell"** in **1804** — a five-act play set against the Swiss struggle for independence from the Habsburgs
- The premiere was staged in **Weimar** under the direction of **Johann Wolfgang von Goethe** on **17 March 1804**
- The earliest documented Tell performance in Altdorf predates Schiller by centuries: **1512** saw "Ein hübsch Spyl gehalten zu Uri in der Eidgenossenschaft" (a fair play held in Uri)
- In **1898**, the **Tellspielgesellschaft** was founded by the Männerchor Altdorf to bring Tell performances back to his legendary homeland
- The first modern production opened on **25 June 1899** before **1,200 spectators** at the Schützenmatte, directed by **Gustav Thiess** (then director of the Stadttheater Luzern)
- A purpose-built wooden **Tellspielhaus** (Tell playhouse) hosted productions from 1899 to 1913 (91 performances total)
- The wooden theatre was demolished in **1915**; in **1924–1925**, a new masonry Tellspielhaus with **1,000 seats** was inaugurated
- Since 1899, the Tellspielgesellschaft has staged new interpretations of Schiller's Tell approximately every **four years**, each reflecting the spirit of its time
- Since **1988**, **Franziska Kohlund** became the first female director of the Tellspiele, pioneering contemporary interpretations

## 11. The Tell Museum and the Tell Chapel at Bürglen

- **Bürglen**, a village just east of Altdorf, is the legendary **birthplace** of William Tell
- The **Tell-Museum** is housed in the **Wattigwilerturm** on the Postplatz in Bürglen, founded in **1956** as the Tell-Museumsgesellschaft Uri and operating since **1966**
- It holds the most comprehensive collection of Tell-related materials from **six centuries**: over **500 items** including documents, paintings, prints, and objects
- The museum features an important collection of works by Basel painter **Ernst Stückelberg** (1831–1903), who set up his studio in Bürglen
- A **Tell Chapel** stands on the spot where Tell's house allegedly stood — first built in **1582**, with paintings from **1588** representing the **oldest known picture cycle** of the Tell myth in the oldest preserved Tell chapel
- The chapel was rebuilt in **1879/80** in its current form

## 12. The Bannwald and the Fires of Altdorf

- The **Bannwald** (protection forest) rises above Altdorf on the steep slopes behind the town — a vital shield against rockfalls, avalanches, and landslides
- The community of Altdorf first appears in records from **1366** regarding ancient usage rights and commons in the Bannwald
- The term "Bannwald" dates to the medieval period, referring to forests with **restricted usage rights** — cutting trees was prohibited to maintain the forest's protective function
- Forest management measures were applied from the **14th century** and rigorously enforced by the **17th century**
- Archaeological finds in the Bannwald include **La Tene era** bronze axe-heads and iron tools from the **3rd century BCE** — the earliest evidence of settlement in Altdorf
- Major fires devastated Altdorf repeatedly: in **1400**, **1693**, and **1799**
- After the **1693 fire**, streets were widened and straightened, though houses were still rebuilt in wood with shingle roofs weighted by heavy stones
- The catastrophic fire of **1799** — coinciding with the devastation of the Napoleonic wars — finally led the municipality to mandate construction in **stone and tile**
- The town church and town hall were rebuilt in **neoclassical style** after 1799
- The population did not recover to pre-1799 levels until the **mid-19th century**

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The boy who wouldn't bow** — Tell's son Walter tells the story: the hat on the pole, the apple, the second arrow, and how his father's courage freed a nation
2. **The Devil's goat** — Retelling of the Teufelsbrücke legend: Uri villagers need a bridge across the terrifying gorge, and a clever child suggests the trick with the goat
3. **The Rütli oath by moonlight** — A child accidentally travels back to 1291 and witnesses three determined mountain folk swear an oath of freedom on the meadow above the lake
4. **The ghost of the Bannwald** — The ancient forest above Altdorf whispers warnings; a child discovers why the trees must never be cut — they hold the mountain in place
5. **The Türmli clock strikes thirteen** — At the stroke of thirteen, the Türmli's 1690 clockwork opens a portal; a child visits Altdorf before the great fire, meets the clockmaker from Zug
6. **Suvorov's impossible bridge** — A child witnesses Russian soldiers lashing together a bridge from scarves and planks across a destroyed gorge in a snowstorm, learning about courage from an unexpected source
7. **The Tell Monument comes alive** — On the night of August 1, the bronze Tell and Walter step down from their pedestal and walk through modern Altdorf, astonished by what Switzerland has become
8. **The white pages of Sarnen** — A child discovers a mysterious old book in a dusty archive that tells stories everyone claims are just legends — but the book insists they are true
