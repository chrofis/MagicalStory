## 1. Founding — The Counts of Neuchâtel and the Island Town

- The town of Aarberg was founded between **1220 and 1225** by **Count Ulrich III of Neuchâtel**, who had recently acquired rulership over the Seeland region and needed a central location from which to govern
- Aarberg is first mentioned in documents in **1236** as *Arberc*, and in **1267** as *opidum de Arberch* (town of Aarberg)
- The site was chosen on a **natural island** formed by the Aare and the Kleine Aare (Little Aare) flowing around a spur of rock — a strategic position controlling a key **bridge crossing** over the Aare
- **Schloss Aarberg** (Aarberg Castle) was built around **1220** simultaneously with the town, to control the bridge and serve as the count's residence
- Ulrich III's son, **Ulrich IV**, founded the **Counts of Aarberg** as a distinct branch of the Neuchâtel dynasty. He held the lordships of Arconciel, Illens, and Aarberg, and before **1251** exchanged Strassberg with his brother Berthold for the lordship of Valangin
- The Counts of Aarberg subsequently ruled Valangin until **1592**, building the castle and fortified walls there
- In **1358**, Count **Peter von Aarberg**, in deep financial difficulty, began seeking a buyer for the town. After years of failed attempts, he sold Aarberg and all his ruling rights to the city of **Bern** in **1377–1379**, ending the dynasty's direct rule
- Bern installed a **Vogt** (bailiff) in Aarberg Castle. From **1358 to 1830**, roughly **100 Vögte** occupied the castle as Bernese administrators

## 2. Geography — The Aare Island and the Seeland

- Aarberg lies **20 km northwest of Bern** on the river Aare, at the heart of the **Seeland** (lake district) in the western Swiss Plateau
- The old town was built on an **island** between the main Aare and the Kleine Aare, which flowed around it on both sides — the town grew up along the edges of the island with a large open plaza in the center
- The Seeland is part of the **Drei-Seen-Land** (Three Lakes Region), defined by Lake Biel/Bienne, Lake Neuchâtel, and Lake Murten — Aarberg sits at the eastern edge of this region
- The surrounding flatland, known as the **Grosses Moos** (Great Marsh), was historically a vast swampy floodplain. Before the 19th-century water corrections, the landscape between Aarberg and Solothurn suffered from poor drainage and recurring devastating floods due to the low gradient of the Aare
- The Seeland's economy is dominated by **agriculture**, particularly vegetable production — over **500 farms** contribute about one-fifth of Switzerland's vegetable output, earning the region the nickname **"Gemüsegarten der Schweiz"** (vegetable garden of Switzerland)
- Today Aarberg is the **administrative center** of the Seeland administrative district (Verwaltungskreis Seeland), which replaced the old Aarberg district in **2010**
- Population: approximately **4,626** (as of 2020)

## 3. The Great Fires and the Rebirth in Stone

- Initially, Aarberg was an **unwalled town** with two rows of **wooden houses** arranged around a narrow central plaza
- Devastating fires struck the town in **1419** and **1477**, destroying large parts of the settlement including the castle
- After the fires, Aarberg was **rebuilt in stone**. The houses were set back approximately **10 meters** from their original positions, creating the town's distinctive wide, **lens-shaped central plaza** (Stadtplatz) — one of the most impressive town squares in the Bernese Seeland
- The current **Aarberg Castle** was constructed between **1608 and 1610** by the city of Bern as the new seat of the bailiff, replacing the fire-damaged medieval structure. Today it serves as the **town hall**, the district seat, and the cantonal police station
- The **Reformed Church** stands on the site of the former castle. A church was built here in **1484**; after it burned, it was rebuilt in **1575–1576** in the **late Gothic style** and has served the Swiss Reformed congregation ever since. Bern adopted the **Reformation in 1528**, and Aarberg followed

## 4. The Stadtplatz — A Medieval Trading Powerhouse

- The central market plaza of Aarberg was one of the **largest trans-shipment centers in Switzerland** during the Middle Ages
- The town's position at a key **Aare bridge crossing** made it a natural hub for regional trade. Many transport companies and inns — including the **Tavernen Krone** (Crown) and **Tavernen Falken** (Falcon) — grew up around the square
- The yearly market was first recorded in **1271**. By **1478**, it had become a major regional event attracting **salt, iron, steel, and cloth merchants** from across the region
- Markets expanded over the centuries: **1507** — two markets per year; **1681** — three per year; **1759** — quarterly markets
- Today the Stadtplatz retains its historic character with **old cobblestones** and a unique backdrop of houses, and hosts events including the **Stars of Sounds** music festival (held annually since 2011 on the town square) and the **Chlouser-Märit** (St. Nicholas Market) on the first weekend of Advent

## 5. The Covered Wooden Bridge — Aarberg's Landmark

- The town's most iconic landmark is its **60-meter-long covered wooden bridge** (Gedeckte Holzbrücke), dating from **1566–1569**
- The bridge was constructed of **spruce trunks** and features medieval stone pillars visible from underneath
- It was **reinforced in 1786** and **renovated in 1973**
- Aarberg's entire reason for existence was the **Aare bridge crossing** — the town controlled river traffic and overland trade routes from its island position
- After the **First Jura Water Correction** (1868–1891) diverted the Aare away from Aarberg through the new Hagneck Canal, the main river no longer flows through the town. The bridge now spans only the remnant **Alte Aare** (Old Aare), a narrow waterway — making the grand 60-meter bridge **strikingly oversized** for its current stream
- The covered wooden bridge and the **Liechti House** are listed as **Swiss heritage sites of national significance**. The entire old town of Aarberg is part of the **Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites (ISOS)**

## 6. The Jura Water Correction — Remaking the Landscape

- The **First Juragewässerkorrektion** (Jura Water Correction, **1868–1891**) was one of the largest hydraulic engineering projects in 19th-century Switzerland and fundamentally transformed Aarberg and the entire Seeland
- Before the correction, the Aare flowed from Aarberg northward past Büren an der Aare, bypassing Lake Biel. The river split into multiple channels after Aarberg, and the low-lying Seeland was a marshy, disease-ridden swampland subject to constant flooding
- The plan, developed by Grisons engineer **Richard La Nicca**, called for diverting the Aare from Aarberg into Lake Biel via a new canal
- Construction of the **Hagneck Canal** began in **1875**. The greatest challenge was cutting through the **Seerücken hills** — a trench **900 meters long** and **34 meters deep**, requiring the excavation of approximately **one million cubic meters** of material, all done **by hand without machines**
- On **16 August 1878**, the waters of the Aare first flowed into Lake Biel through the new canal
- The **Second Juragewässerkorrektion** (**1962–1973**) made further corrections under engineer Robert Müller
- **Result**: The drained Seeland became Switzerland's most important **vegetable-growing region** — the "Gemüsegarten der Schweiz" (vegetable garden of Switzerland), with over **60 varieties of vegetables** cultivated, including the prized white asparagus
- For Aarberg itself, the correction was bittersweet: while it ended centuries of flooding, the town **lost its river**. The grand covered bridge now spans only the trickle of the Alte Aare

## 7. The Sugar Factory — Switzerland's Sweet Spot

- The **Zuckerfabrik Aarberg** is the oldest and most important sugar refinery in Switzerland, and one of only **two sugar factories** in the country (the other being in Frauenfeld)
- A first sugar factory was established in Aarberg before **1899**, but because too few sugar beets were grown in the region, it **went bankrupt** after only about ten years. The **Berner Kantonalbank** saved it by purchasing the company at auction
- The factory came to an abrupt end in **January 1912** when a devastating **fire** destroyed the facility
- The current **Zuckermühle und Raffinerie Aarberg AG (ZRA)** was founded on **16 November 1912** and rebuilt on the same site
- The factory supplied Swiss-made sugar through both **World Wars** and radical changes in agriculture — a symbol of national self-sufficiency
- In **1963**, a second Swiss sugar factory opened in **Frauenfeld** (Canton Thurgau)
- In **1997**, the two companies merged into **Zuckerfabriken Aarberg und Frauenfeld AG (ZAF)**, later renamed **Schweizer Zucker AG (SZU)**
- Today each factory processes approximately **10,000 tonnes of sugar beet per day** during the autumn campaign season
- The sweet smell of beet processing during the campaign months (September–December) is a defining sensory experience of Aarberg — locals say you can smell the factory from kilometers away
- Guided tours of the sugar factory are offered through Biel Seeland Tourism
- The factory is a major local employer and a source of civic pride — Aarberg is inseparable from its identity as Switzerland's sugar town

## 8. The Alte Aare — A River Reborn as Nature Reserve

- After the Jura Water Correction diverted the main Aare through the Hagneck Canal, the former riverbed between Aarberg and Büren an der Aare became a **remnant waterway** known as the **Alte Aare** (Old Aare)
- Over the following century, nature reclaimed the abandoned floodplain, creating a unique **riparian landscape** with oxbow lakes, dense floodplain forests, and open water areas
- The area is now a **nationally protected wetland**: the **Alte Aare: Aarberg-Lyss** section covers **194 hectares**, and the **Alte Aare: Lyss-Dotzigen** section covers **190 hectares**
- The nature reserve is a valuable habitat for **numerous bird species**, amphibians, and aquatic plants — it is listed as an important **birding site** in Switzerland
- A popular hiking route, the **Alte Aare trail from Aarberg to Büren an der Aare**, follows the old river course through the reserve
- The contrast is remarkable: a landscape that was once dangerous swampland, then an engineered canal system, has now become one of the Seeland's most treasured natural areas

## 9. Local Traditions and Cultural Life

- The **Chlouser-Märit** (St. Nicholas Market) is Aarberg's annual highlight, traditionally held on the **first weekend of Advent** (Friday through Sunday in early December). The medieval Stadtplatz fills with market stalls offering traditional Swiss delicacies, while visitors enjoy dance performances, fire shows, weapons displays, and the visit of **Samichlous and Schmutzli** (the Swiss St. Nicholas and his companion). A fairy tale tent and children's carousel entertain younger guests
- The **Stars of Sounds** open-air music festival has been held annually on the Stadtplatz since **2011**, drawing international acts to the intimate setting of the medieval town square
- The **Evangelisch-Reformierte Kirchgemeinde Aarberg** (Reformed Church parish) continues to serve the community from the late Gothic church built in 1575–1576
- The local **Bernese German dialect** (Berndütsch) spoken in Aarberg and the surrounding Seeland municipalities carries unique idioms shaped by the region's marshy heritage and agricultural traditions
- The **sugar campaign** (September–December), when the factory processes the region's sugar beet harvest, remains a defining rhythm of town life — the sweet smell of processing beets permeates the town for months
- Aarberg's gastronomic culture reflects its agricultural setting: the Seeland is known for **white asparagus** in spring and a wide variety of root vegetables, and the Stadtplatz restaurants offer regional specialties in a historic setting

## 10. Notable Historical Facts

- **1220–1225**: Town founded by Count Ulrich III of Neuchâtel
- **1236**: First documentary mention as *Arberc*
- **1339, 1382, 1386**: Aarberg was **besieged but never taken** — a testament to its island fortification
- **1377–1379**: Sold to **Bern** by Count Peter von Aarberg
- **1419, 1477**: Devastating fires; town rebuilt in stone
- **1528**: Aarberg adopted the **Protestant Reformation** with Bern
- **1608–1610**: Current castle built as Bernese bailiff's seat
- **1803**: Aarberg became capital of the **Bezirk (district) of Aarberg**
- **1878**: Aare diverted through the Hagneck Canal — Aarberg loses its main river
- **1912**: Sugar factory refounded after fire; begins continuous production
- **2010**: Aarberg district replaced by the new **Verwaltungskreis Seeland**, with Aarberg as administrative center
- The entire **old town** is listed in the **ISOS** (Inventory of Swiss Heritage Sites of National Importance)
- **Nesa von Aarberg** (recorded 1398, died before 1438), a Swiss nun from a Neuchâtel ministerial family, served as **Regelmeisterin** (head of discipline) of the Third Order of Saint Francis in Basel in **1405** — one of the few documented medieval women connected to the Aarberg name, and aunt of **Rudolf Hofmeister**, Schultheiss (mayor) of Bern
- The town's siege record is remarkable: besieged in **1339, 1382, and 1386** but **never taken** — the island position and river defenses proved impregnable

## Story Ideas (brief — generatable on the fly)

1. **The bridge that lost its river** — A child walks across the enormous covered wooden bridge and wonders why it is so big for such a tiny stream. The bridge itself tells the story: how the mighty Aare once roared beneath it, how engineers dug a canal by hand through a hill 34 meters deep, and how one day in 1878 the water simply flowed away to Lake Biel, leaving the bridge stranded over a trickle

2. **The island that wasn't afraid** — In 1339, an army arrives to besiege Aarberg, but the town sits on an island between two arms of the Aare. A child living on the island helps the townspeople defend their bridges, learning that Aarberg was besieged three times but never conquered. The river itself was their greatest wall

3. **The sweet smell of Aarberg** — A child follows a mysterious sweet scent drifting over the Seeland and discovers the sugar factory, where mountains of sugar beets are transformed into white sugar. Inside, the factory tells its own story: a first factory that went bankrupt, a fire that destroyed everything, and the stubborn people who rebuilt it to make Switzerland's own sugar

4. **The count who sold his town** — Count Peter von Aarberg has no money left. In 1358, he puts his entire town up for sale — castle, bridge, market, and all. For twenty years, nobody wants to buy it. A child helps the count find a buyer, and finally the city of Bern agrees. The child realizes that sometimes letting go is the bravest thing a ruler can do

5. **The canal diggers** — In 1875, thousands of workers arrive at Aarberg with nothing but shovels and wheelbarrows. They must dig a canal through a hill 34 meters deep — one million cubic meters of earth, all by hand. A child befriends one of the workers and watches as the impossible project takes shape, until the day the Aare flows into Lake Biel for the first time

6. **The market on the island** — It is 1478, and Aarberg's yearly market is the biggest event in the Seeland. Salt merchants from the west, iron traders from the north, cloth sellers from Bern — they all cross the covered bridge to reach the great Stadtplatz. A child running errands for an innkeeper discovers that every object in the market tells the story of a different part of Switzerland

7. **The swamp that became a garden** — A child living in the Seeland in 1860 knows only mud, mosquitoes, and floods. Then the engineers arrive and begin to drain the marshes and dig canals. The child returns as a grandparent to find the same land covered in carrots, cabbages, and white asparagus — Switzerland's vegetable garden, born from a swamp

8. **The old river's secret** — Walking along the Alte Aare nature trail, a child discovers that the quiet, overgrown waterway was once the main course of a powerful river. Herons, kingfishers, and frogs now live where boats and barges once traveled. The old river whispers its memories: of floods, of the town on the island, and of the day the water was sent away through a mountain
