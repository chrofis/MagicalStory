# Scene Output Format (SCENE + METADATA per page)

Every page outputs TWO blocks after its `TEXT:` block: a `SCENE:` paragraph (prose, 250–350 words, consumed directly by the image model) and a `METADATA:` JSON block (structured fields consumed by the pipeline). The SCENE prose is the image prompt — there is no Haiku expansion step downstream.

## SCENE prose rules

Write one continuous paragraph, 250–350 words. Open with the main subject and the main action. Weave each named character's physical description in on first mention — age look, build, hair, eyes, distinctive features, clothing — taken verbatim from the `CHARACTER DETAILS` block above. Do not invent traits (beards, hairstyles, eye colors) not listed there; do not drop traits listed there (glasses, facial hair, distinctive marks). Flowing language, not a labeled list. Name recurring Visual Bible elements as they appear and interleave their identifying detail.

- **Plain concrete verbs only.** Prefer `holds`, `sits on`, `reaches for`, `walks toward`, `gazes at`. BANNED: `hovers`, `cradled`, `perched upon`, `rests gently against`. Image models render fancy words literally. Skip aesthetic buzzwords: `dramatic`, `ethereal`, `haunting`, `cinematic`, `breathtaking`, `stunning` — they carry no visual information.
- **Positions + depth explicit for every character.** Name which corner or band of the frame (left foreground, center midground, far right background). Include depth words (near, close, distant, small) so the image model can stage properly.
- **Facing direction explicit for every character.** State who or what each character faces — the reader, another named character, the crowd, a specific object, a direction (left edge / right edge). Without this, the image model picks randomly and one figure gets back-turned for compositional variety even when the scene calls for a front-facing shot. "Stands with arms raised" is underspecified; "stands with arms raised, facing the reader" is enough.
- **Honor `perspective` and `shot` fields from the hint you write.** For `perspective: over-the-shoulder`, the camera sits behind that character's shoulder: the OTS character fills the near foreground (large, back view, shoulder + head + held object visible), the subject they watch goes in midground or background (smaller, distant). Never invert — an OTS character in the background reads as a different shot entirely. For `perspective: back view`, shoulders/head/hips/both feet turned away, heels visible, toes pointing away. For `shot: ultra-wide` or "small silhouette in the distance" compositions, describe the character as explicitly tiny — "roughly a sixth the frame height", "a small silhouette in the wave-trough".
- **Forbidden side.** `textPosition` names a corner that must stay character-free. Derive the side (right/left for corners, full row for `*-full`) and stage every character on the opposite side. The corner is also where the calmest surface of the scene should naturally sit (sky, wall, ground, foliage) — keep that area uncluttered.
- **One moment, one focal point.** Single instant. One main action draws the eye. No time-lapse ("opens door, walks in, sits").
- **Pick the moment in motion.** When the page text chains physical actions, depict the one with the most motion — mid-leap, mid-swing, mid-throw — not the static pose that follows.
- **Characters are active.** Every character does something specific. Never standing-and-smiling. Eyes look at what they interact with, not at the camera.
- **Still frame, not a verb-stream.** Describe the pose, not the motion or thought. No "walks briskly", "about to reach", "thoughtful", "distracted". Use the body position the reader sees in the frozen moment.
- **Physically possible.** Two hands = max two items.
- **No age collectives** — never "the children", "the kids". Use names or "all three", "the group".
- **Never describe** weapons touching a character's body, characters bound or held by another, or wounds / blood.
- Every `objects[]` entry with a CHR* ID must appear by name in the prose with a position and one identifying visual detail, even at background depth. No folding into "crowd" or "background blur".
- When the hint's `background` field names unnamed supporting figures (crowd, villagers, townsfolk, soldiers, onlookers, passersby), render them in the prose with one sentence naming count (or "dense crowd"), rough action (standing, watching, bowing, moving past), and placement (far midground, both flanks, background). Do not drop them.
- Everyday scene objects on the ground, at a character's feet, in hand, or on a surface need a size anchor in the prose — "a fist-sized apple", "a forearm-length crossbow", "a palm-sized cup", "an arm-length arrow". Without the anchor, image models render objects compositionally prominent and they come out 3-5× natural size.

## METADATA JSON schema

```
METADATA:
{
  "characters": [
    { "name": "Name", "position": "left foreground|center background|...", "clothing": "standard|winter|summer|costumed:type" },
    { "name": "Name", "position": "right background", "clothing": "standard", "depth": "background", "perspective": "back view" }
  ],
  "objects": ["LOC001", "ANI001"],
  "interactions": [
    { "character": "Name", "object": "wooden ladder", "where": "climbs" },
    { "character": "Other", "object": "ART001", "where": "gazes at" }
  ],
  "background": "busy crowd in hats and coats",
  "setting": "indoor|outdoor",
  "time": "morning|midday|afternoon|sunset|night",
  "weather": "sunny|cloudy|rainy|snowy|n/a",
  "shot": "close-up|medium|wide|ultra-wide",
  "framingPattern": "solo-before|solo-after|over-the-shoulder",
  "textPosition": "top-left|top-right|bottom-left|bottom-right|top-full|bottom-full",
  "textZoneDescription": "5–15 words naming the saturated, high-contrast surface in that corner (e.g. 'deep evening sky in rich indigo', 'dark green pine foliage', 'saturated wet cobblestones')",
  "emptyScenePrompt": "2–4 sentences describing the background environment only."
}
```

**Character fields:**
- Every named character visible in the scene — foreground, midground, or background — belongs in `characters[]`, even if the prose or `background` field only names them once. If you mention a character by name anywhere in the prose or metadata, include them here with `clothing`.
- A figure without a story name still belongs in `characters[]` (and gets a placeholder name like `Soldier1`, `Guard2`, `Innkeeper`) whenever the page text shows that figure performing a specific physical action on a named character or Visual Bible object — freeing, handing, taking, pointing, opening, lifting, striking, embracing. Anonymous extras stay in `background` only when they're scene atmosphere with no named-action role.
- `position` (required): combined horizontal + depth phrase — `left foreground`, `center background`, `right midground`, etc.
- `clothing` (required): must match an available avatar category exactly.
- `depth` (optional, default `foreground`): camera distance, NOT frame position. `foreground` = large and close, filling ~1/3+ of frame height. `midground` = head and torso visible, middle band. `background` = small/distant; a tiny silhouette roughly 1/6 of frame height or less. If the prose calls the character "tiny", "small", "distant", "silhouette", or gives a fractional size, `depth` MUST be `background`. If `shot` is `ultra-wide` and the character is named as a single distant figure, `depth` MUST be `background`.
- `perspective` (optional, default `front`): `front`, `side`, `back view`, `over-the-shoulder`.

**objects[]** — Visual Bible IDs. **When a landmark has numbered photo variants** (e.g. `LOC003.1`, `LOC003.2`, `LOC003.3`), emit the specific variant whose description matches the scene's `shot` and perspective — not the bare `LOC003`. Default to `.1` only if no variant is a clear match.

**interactions[]** — one entry per essential character-to-object or character-to-character contact. Each entry is a simple A-verb-B form: `character` (the subject), `object` (the target — Visual Bible ID, character name, or plain noun like 'rope', 'ladder'), `where` (a short verb phrase, 1-4 words: 'climbs', 'gazes at', 'holds', 'sits on', 'leans against', 'kneels beside', 'in pocket', 'around neck'). Pose details — neck angle, gaze direction, body-part positioning ('arms at sides', 'fingers curled', 'feet on third rung') — do NOT belong in `where`; leave them in the prose. Include every contact the plot depends on: what the character holds, wears, or carries; what surface they stand, sit, kneel, crouch, climb, push off, or land on; a weapon being gripped; a pivotal hand on another character. If the text describes movement FROM one thing TO another, BOTH origin and destination get an entry. Omit incidental contact (a hand resting on a stall edge, a foot near a prop, a shoulder brushing a wall). Test: could the story be told identically without the contact being visible? If yes, leave it out. Every person named in `interactions[]` must appear in this scene's `characters[]`.

**Interactions at a distance.** When the verb implies action across a gap rather than touching contact ('shoots', 'aims at', 'throws at', 'calls to', 'watches from afar', 'spots across', 'runs toward across', 'gestures across', 'looks at' over distance), `where` must include an explicit separation phrase: 'across the room', 'a few paces apart', 'across the square', 'across the field', 'down the valley', 'from one rooftop to another'. Without it the image renders the two figures inside touching distance and the dramatic moment collapses. For these scenes `shot` MUST be `ultra-wide` so both endpoints fit visibly in the frame at the named separation, and the prose must place each figure at the correct end of the frame.

**emptyScenePrompt** (required): Describe ONLY the background environment — no named main or secondary characters, no tracked animals, no carried/handheld artifacts. Fixed immobile artifacts that anchor the scene (a pole-with-banner, a throne, a monument, a pedestal, an altar, a signpost) ARE allowed and, when the page text centers on them, MUST be described as the focal point — "prominent at the center, dominating the foreground composition," not "a pole stands somewhere." Name the artifact's key visual marker (the hat on top, the inscription). Location, architecture, natural landscape, lighting, weather, camera angle. Leave the foreground open and uncluttered. NEVER write hypothetical references like "where children would stand" — image models render those literally.

**Prop placement vs landmark.** When a separate prop shares a depth band with a real landmark, position the prop relative to the landmark (in front of, beside, between camera and landmark) — not by an unanchored frame slot. SCENE prose and `emptyScenePrompt` use the same relative phrasing.

The `emptyScenePrompt` MUST MIRROR the SCENE prose's composition geometry — do not reinvent it. The generated empty scene becomes the style anchor the main image composites onto, so if their geometries disagree, the main image will fail at compositing (aim directions, target placements, perspective lines won't line up). Carry across verbatim from SCENE:
- **Direction of any path, river, road, corridor, shoreline, horizon, or major perspective line.** If SCENE says *"path stretches from lower-left foreground to upper-right background"*, the `emptyScenePrompt` uses the same direction phrasing.
- **Vanishing point / opening location** as a concrete frame position (upper-right, center-right, lower-center, etc.), not a vague side.
- **Reserved spots for distant composited figures or objects.** If SCENE says *"a tiny mounted figure at far right background in a sliver of pale light"*, the `emptyScenePrompt` says *"leave an open spot at the upper-right background, ~70% frame width, for a small distant figure — the path's vanishing point should land there"*. Name the frame position, don't just say "at the end of the path".
- **Lighting direction** consistent with `time` + `weather` (e.g., *"late afternoon sunlight rakes in from the upper right, long shadows cast toward the lower left"*).
- **Dominant diagonals / perspective lines** from SCENE.
A reader comparing SCENE and `emptyScenePrompt` side by side must see the same geometry described twice — same path direction, same vanishing point, same reserved corners — just with named characters absent from the empty version.

**Anonymous extras follow the setting.** When the setting is inherently populated — market, street, plaza, square, festival, school, gathering, village, town — include a background crowd (villagers, passersby, onlookers, soldiers, townsfolk) in both the scene prose and `emptyScenePrompt`, unless `shot` is `close-up`. Paint them as atmospheric density and silhouettes — no detailed faces, no individual names. Name a colour mix (at least 3 tunic/coat/dress colours) and at least two garment shapes across the crowd so it reads as a varied populace, not a uniform robed mass. For `close-up` shots, intimate/indoor/domestic settings (bedroom, kitchen, forest clearing, solitary room), or inherently empty outdoor locations (rural path, mountain top): the background stays empty of figures.

**Text zone rule.** Story text is rendered in WHITE and overlaid on the image at `textPosition`. Pick `textPosition` so the matching corner is the visually calmest region, AND so it can read as a saturated, high-contrast surface for white text to sit on — deep evening sky, dark foliage, rich wall, saturated water, deep cobblestones, dark earth. Not pale sky, not empty white, not a solid black rectangle. Calmness matters more than colour: saturated green, deep blue, rich brown all work. `textZoneDescription` names that surface in 5–15 words. The chosen surface must NATURALLY OCCUPY that corner in the scene's geometry — for `top-*` use sky, cliff face, upper foliage, mountainside, ceiling, cave roof; for `bottom-*` use cobblestones, floor, water, grass, sand, shadow, dark earth, low foliage. Vertical architectural surfaces (walls, facades, doors) only work when the camera angle actually places that surface across the chosen corner — narrow alleys, against-the-wall framing, interior shots. If the surface wouldn't naturally fill that corner, the model paints an awkward floating panel. Both `emptyScenePrompt` AND the SCENE prose must describe that corner as a natural part of the scene (e.g. "the deep evening sky fills the upper-right, unbroken and smooth"). Never write "dark rectangle", "negative space", "text box", "blank area", or "for text" — describe what's there, not what it's for.

- **CHARACTER COUNT** - Aim for 1-3 characters per scene. Use 4-5 only for gathering moments (reunion, group discovery, classroom). Never exceed {MAX_CHARACTERS_PER_SCENE}. Never pad.
  - The description must ONLY mention characters from the characters array.

  - GOOD: solo moment → 1 character in array

A character standing still on a rock after a jump, looking at scenery, is the wrong moment — show the jump itself. Aftermath framing (character still, consequence visible) is valid only when the page text itself is already an aftermath (a character reacting to something that just finished). Back view fits observation moments (a character watching something happen), not the character's own climactic action. The reader needs to see posture, weapon held, face or profile — body language carries the moment.
- **ANTAGONISTS MUST APPEAR WHEN STORY-PRESENT** - When the page text describes antagonists actively pressuring, chasing, restraining, or surrounding the main character, they must be visible in the illustration. Put them in the scene hint's `background` field (count + role + placement) if they're not main characters. Blurred, distant, or small in the frame is fine — absent is not. - **VISUAL ONLY** - Describe what the CAMERA sees. ❌ "excitedly runs" ✅ "runs with arms spread wide". No emotions, only body language.

  - **Streak limit:** never more than 3 consecutive pages in the same half (top or bottom).

  Pick what naturally suits each scene — close-up with a clean sky above → `top-*`; outdoor with open ground below → `bottom-*`; wide/ultra-wide with a clean horizon band → `top-full` or `bottom-full`.

**Scene Variety (IMPORTANT for great illustrations):**
- **SIMPLE ACTIONS** - Characters in natural, stable poses: sitting, standing, walking, looking. If it would be hard to capture in a single photo, simplify it.
- **SIMPLE SCENES PREFERRED** - Prefer 1-2 central characters per scene. If a group moment is needed, keep 1-2 characters in foreground and use `"depth": "background"` or `"perspective": "back view"` for others.
- **EVERY CHARACTER VISIBLE** - Each primary character must appear in at least 2 scene hints across the story.
- **VARY EXPRESSIONS** - Not every scene is happy. Match expression to the moment: wonder, concentration, worry, surprise, determination. "Smiling" on at most 2-3 pages in the entire story.
- **NO TWO CONSECUTIVE SCENES ALIKE** - Each scene must differ from the previous one in at least 2 of: shot type, character count, perspective. If page 3 is a medium shot of 2 characters facing each other, page 4 CANNOT be another medium shot of 2 characters facing each other.

**Scene Types That WORK WELL (use these):**
- **SOLO DISCOVERY** — One character alone, exploring or reacting (close-up or medium). Best quality images.
- **CHASE / PURSUIT** — Two characters far apart, both small, moving in the same direction. One left, one right.
- **CROWD SCENE** — Main character in foreground, nameless background crowd (market, festival, school). Use `"setting"` description for the crowd, don't list them as characters.
- **OVER-THE-SHOULDER** — Camera behind one character, looking at another. Use `"perspective": "over-the-shoulder"`.
- **BACK VIEW** — Character walking away from camera into a landscape. Use `"perspective": "back view"`.
- **BIRD'S EYE** — Looking down at tiny characters in a large space. Use `"shot": "ultra-wide"`.
- **REACTION CLOSE-UP** — Just the face filling the frame, strong emotion. 1 character only.
- **DISTANCE SHOT** — Two characters far apart with empty space between them. Either pairing works: one `foreground` + one `background`, OR both `background` (tiny figures across an open square / field / hall — preferred for aiming, shooting, throwing, calling, watching across distance).
- **For any beat where two characters interact across open distance (aim, shoot, throw, call, watch from afar), use DISTANCE SHOT — never two `foreground` characters left/right.**

**Scene Types That DON'T WORK (avoid):**
- 4+ named characters all facing the camera (group photo effect — AI makes them identical)
- 3+ characters at similar size all in foreground (faces blur together)
- Two characters having a conversation while ALSO holding objects AND gesturing (too complex)
- Same composition repeated: if two scenes both show "2 people talking face to face", one must change

**SCENE PLAN (required — write this in the ---SCENE PLAN--- section BEFORE writing pages):**
Before writing any pages, plan the visual composition for each page. For a 10-page story, distribute roughly:
- 3× solo character scenes (close-up or medium)
- 2× two characters interacting (medium, varied perspectives)
- 2× environment-focused (wide/ultra-wide, characters small in landscape)
- 1× crowd scene (main character + nameless crowd in setting description)
- 1× over-the-shoulder or back view
- 1× unique angle (bird's eye, through a window, looking up)
Scale proportionally for longer/shorter stories. The plan ensures variety BEFORE writing — no need to fix afterwards.

# Character Portrayal Guidelines

- Write in third person (he/she/they)
- Let readers discover personality through what characters DO, not what they ARE told
- Your characters must feel like real people, not cardboard cutouts.
- Do not repeat the visual traits of the character in the text. Max, the red-haired boy is fine for the scene hint, do not repeat this in the story text.

**SHOW, don't TELL:**
- NEVER mention trait words directly (don't write "She was brave" or "He was shy") SHOW traits through actions, dialogue, and behavior
- Instead of "Max was curious" → Show Max asking unexpected questions, peeking behind doors, wondering aloud
- Instead of "Emma was shy" → Show Emma hiding behind her mom, speaking quietly, avoiding eye contact
- Instead of "Leo was brave" → Show Leo stepping forward when others hesitate, volunteering for the scary task

**Each character should:**
- Have a unique voice in dialogue (different speech patterns, word choices)
- React differently to situations based on their personality
- Show their traits through ACTIONS, not descriptions
- Grow and change through the story

**AVOID:**
- Repeating character traits - if a character is "curious", don't keep writing "she was curious" or "her curiosity led her to..."
- Mentioning the same trait more than once in the entire story
- Generic dialogue that any character could say
- Characters who all react the same way

**BANNED GESTURES (AI clichés - NEVER use these):**
- "Hand on shoulder" - COMPLETELY FORBIDDEN, not even once
- "Ruffled hair" / "tousled hair"
- "Patted on the back"
- "Nodded knowingly" / "nodded wisely"

**Instead, show support/connection through:**
- Actions: standing closer, walking side by side, sharing food, helping with a task
- Expressions: a warm smile, a wink, raised eyebrows, eyes lighting up
- Fun gestures: fist bump, high five, thumbs up, silly salute, pinky promise
- Body language: leaning in, sitting next to, making eye contact, a quick nudge
- Each gesture may appear ONCE in the entire story - then use a different one

**HUMOR (no jokes or puns):**
