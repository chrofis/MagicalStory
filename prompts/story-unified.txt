# Scene Output Format (SCENE + METADATA per page)

Every page outputs TWO blocks after its `TEXT:` block: a `SCENE:` paragraph (prose, 250–350 words, consumed directly by the image model) and a `METADATA:` JSON block (structured fields consumed by the pipeline). The SCENE prose is the image prompt — there is no Haiku expansion step downstream.

## SCENE prose rules

Write one continuous paragraph, 250–350 words. Open with the main subject and the main action. Name each character explicitly on first mention, THEN weave the physical description in (e.g. "CharacterE — a slim grade-school boy with short straight brown hair, brown eyes, rounded child features — sits cross-legged…"). Never substitute a description for the name ("A slim grade-school boy sits cross-legged…" is wrong because the image model can't map that figure to the named character's reference photo). Use the traits from `CHARACTER DETAILS` verbatim; don't invent (beards, hairstyles, eye colors) and don't drop (glasses, facial hair, distinctive marks). Flowing language, not a labeled list. Name recurring Visual Bible elements as they appear and interleave their identifying detail.

When mentioning clothing, write out the full outfit from `clothingRequirements[name][category].description` (e.g. "wearing a navy striped polo and khaki shorts"). Do not abbreviate to the category label ("wearing his standard clothes", "in her winter clothes") — that is the metadata key, not an outfit description.

- **Plain concrete verbs only.** Prefer `holds`, `sits on`, `reaches for`, `walks toward`, `gazes at`. BANNED: `hovers`, `cradled`, `perched upon`, `rests gently against`. Image models render fancy words literally. Skip aesthetic buzzwords: `dramatic`, `ethereal`, `haunting`, `cinematic`, `breathtaking`, `stunning` — they carry no visual information.
- **Positions + depth explicit for every character.** State each character's depth (foreground, midground, background) and their spatial relation to a named anchor — another character, a Visual Bible object, the boat they stand on, the wall behind them, the path they follow. Prefer relational phrasing (`on the boat`, `behind CharacterD`, `a few paces ahead of him`, `at the rock's edge`) over absolute frame slots. Use left/right only when (a)<!-- TEXT_OVERLAY_BEGIN --> it determines text-zone clearance, (b)<!-- TEXT_OVERLAY_END --> a character's left/right placement must persist across pages for continuity (sitting in a vehicle, lead in a procession), or (c) `shot: ultra-wide` chase / distance compositions where horizontal placement matters. Otherwise leave LR out — image models follow `behind CharacterD` more reliably than `right midground` and don't horizontal-flip relational phrasing the way they flip LR slots.
- **Facing direction explicit for every character.** State who or what each character faces — the reader, another named character, the crowd, a specific object. Prefer `facing [named target]` over `facing left/right`. Without this, the image model picks randomly and one figure gets back-turned for compositional variety even when the scene calls for a front-facing shot. "Stands with arms raised" is underspecified; "stands with arms raised, facing the reader" is enough.
- **Body-facing vocabulary (discrete only).** Pick one of: `facing the camera`, `facing left`, `facing right`, `back view`. Never write fractional turns ("three-quarters right", "two-thirds toward", "45-degree", "angled three-quarters"). Image and repair models can't resolve fractional angles — `facing right` is rendered correctly, `three-quarters right` is rendered as front-facing or random. If a character glances back at someone behind them, write `back view, glancing over the shoulder toward [target]` — pick the over-the-shoulder side that points toward the target. **`facing left/right` is for camera-perpendicular shots only.** When the character's body axis aligns with the depth axis (looking INTO or OUT OF the picture), use `back view` for into-depth and `facing the camera` for out-of-depth — never `facing left/right`, which is rendered as camera-right/-left and points the body at the picture edge instead of into the scene.
- **Foreground-shooter / background-target compositions.** When a character in foreground or midground aims at, points at, shoots at, or gazes at a target whose `depth` is `background` (a tiny distant figure, the far end of a path, the vanishing point, a horizon target), the discrete facing MUST be `back view` — or `back view, head turned over the [left|right] shoulder toward [target]` for a glance-back — NOT `facing left/right`. `facing right` is rendered as camera-right (perpendicular to the depth axis), which makes the held weapon, gaze line, or pointing finger lead to the picture edge instead of toward the receding target. Held aimed objects (bolt in groove, arrow on string, sword tip, outstretched finger) follow the same rule: write `the bolt points at the small distant rider in the upper-right background` — name the target AND its frame position. Never `aimed right`, never `aimed down the path` — those directional phrases lose the depth axis and the model renders the aim sideways.
- **Age = apparent-age category.** Use the `Looks:` category from CHARACTER DETAILS verbatim (`school-age girl`, `teenager`, `middle-aged man`, `kindergartner`). Never write the numeric age in years and never substitute a different category — the eval and the avatar photo are both anchored to the apparent-age bucket; switching buckets in prose creates a mismatch the repair loop can't satisfy.
- **Honor `perspective` and `shot` fields from the hint you write.** For `perspective: over-the-shoulder`, the camera sits behind that character's shoulder: the OTS character fills the near foreground (large, back view, shoulder + head + held object visible), the subject they watch goes in midground or background (smaller, distant). Never invert — an OTS character in the background reads as a different shot entirely. For `perspective: back view`, shoulders/head/hips/both feet turned away, heels visible, toes pointing away. For `shot: ultra-wide` or "small silhouette in the distance" compositions, describe the character as explicitly tiny — "roughly a sixth the frame height", "a small silhouette in the wave-trough".
- **`behind` is ambiguous when ANY character is `back view` or `over-the-shoulder`.** With the camera behind that character, "behind X" splits into two contradictory readings: "between camera and X" (foreground, closer to viewer) vs "further from camera than X" (background, deeper into the scene). Image models pick one arbitrarily — usually wrong. For OTHER characters in such scenes, NEVER write `behind [back-view-char]`. Pick the unambiguous phrase for what you mean: write `closer to the camera than [name], in the foreground` for the camera-side case, or `further along the [bridge/path/room] past [name], deeper into the scene` for the scene-side case. Same applies to `position` metadata — `"position": "behind Daniel"` is not allowed when Daniel is back-view; replace with `"position": "between Daniel and the camera"` or `"position": "at the far end of the bridge past Daniel"`. The depth field must agree: camera-side ⇒ `foreground` or `midground`; scene-side ⇒ `background`.
<!-- TEXT_OVERLAY_BEGIN -->
- **Forbidden side.** `textPosition` names a corner that must stay character-free. Derive the side (right/left for corners, full row for `*-full`) and stage every character on the opposite side. The corner is also where the calmest surface of the scene should naturally sit (sky, wall, ground, foliage) — keep that area uncluttered.
<!-- TEXT_OVERLAY_END -->
- **One moment, one focal point.** Single instant. One main action draws the eye. No time-lapse ("opens door, walks in, sits").
- **Pick the moment in motion.** When the page text chains physical actions, depict the one with the most motion — mid-leap, mid-swing, mid-throw — not the static pose that follows.
- **Characters are active.** Every character does something specific. Never standing-and-smiling. Eyes look at what they interact with, not at the camera.
- **Still frame, not a verb-stream.** Describe the pose, not the motion or thought. No "walks briskly", "about to reach", "thoughtful", "distracted". Use the body position the reader sees in the frozen moment.
- **Physically possible.** Two hands = max two items.
- **No age collectives** — never "the children", "the kids". Use names or "all three", "the group".
- **Never describe** weapons touching a character's body, characters bound or held by another, or wounds / blood.
- Every `objects[]` entry with a CHR* ID must appear by name in the prose with a position and one identifying visual detail, even at background depth. No folding into "crowd" or "background blur".
- When the hint's `background` field names unnamed supporting figures (crowd, villagers, townsfolk, soldiers, onlookers, passersby), render them in the prose with one sentence naming count (or "dense crowd"), rough action (standing, watching, bowing, moving past), and placement (far midground, both flanks, background). Do not drop them.
- Everyday scene objects on the ground, at a character's feet, in hand, or on a surface need a size anchor in the prose — "a fist-sized apple", "a forearm-length crossbow", "a palm-sized cup", "an arm-length arrow". Without the anchor, image models render objects compositionally prominent and they come out 3-5× natural size.

**`sceneIntent`** (required): 2-3 present-tense sentences naming the single moment the image depicts. Every named person in `sceneIntent` MUST appear in `characters[]` with a `position`. Every entry in `characters[]` MUST be doing something visible in `sceneIntent` or in an `interactions[]` entry. The sentences answer: who is doing what to whom and where (sentence 1), what each character physically holds / reaches for / gazes at (sentence 2), setting + lighting + mood (sentence 3). Pick ONE temporal moment — bolt mid-flight OR aftermath, not both. Do not stitch cause+effect ("X shoots Y, the horse stands riderless" = two moments; pick the one the image will render). Do not include characters who appear in adjacent paragraphs of the page text but are not physically present at this chosen moment.

**Coherence self-check (mandatory before returning each page):**
1. Does the prose visually describe what `sceneIntent` says? If `sceneIntent` says "X shoots Y" but the prose only describes Y's empty horse with no Y on it, REWRITE the prose to place Y in the frame.
2. Is every person named in `sceneIntent` listed in `characters[]` AND described in the prose with appearance + position?
3. Does the prose describe ONE continuous moment? Mid-flight projectile + already-fallen victim = two moments. Pick one and rewrite.
4. Do `background`, `interactions`, and the prose all describe the same temporal moment? A "riderless horse" plus an "aiming at the rider" interaction is contradictory.
If any check fails, rewrite the prose AND fix the metadata before returning. Do not return a page with internal contradictions.

**Character fields:**
- Every named character visible in the scene — foreground, midground, or background — belongs in `characters[]`, even if the prose or `background` field only names them once. If you mention a character by name anywhere in the prose or metadata, include them here with `clothing`.
- A figure without a story name still belongs in `characters[]` (and gets a placeholder name like `Soldier1`, `Guard2`, `Innkeeper`) whenever the page text shows that figure performing a specific physical action on a named character or Visual Bible object — freeing, handing, taking, pointing, opening, lifting, striking, embracing. Anonymous extras stay in `background` only when they're scene atmosphere with no named-action role.
- `position` (required): a short phrase placing the character in the scene. Prefer relational phrasing tied to a named entity — `on the boat's rim`, `behind CharacterD`, `a few paces ahead`, `at the rock's edge`, `across the bench from CharacterE`. Use left/right only when it carries cross-page continuity (a character sits to CharacterD's right in the train and must stay there next page), or when the page is an `ultra-wide` chase where horizontal placement matters. Always pair with a `depth` field.
- `clothing` (required): must match an available avatar category exactly.
- `depth` (optional, default `foreground`): camera distance, NOT frame position. `foreground` = large and close, filling ~1/3+ of frame height. `midground` = head and torso visible, middle band. `background` = small/distant; a tiny silhouette roughly 1/6 of frame height or less. If the prose calls the character "tiny", "small", "distant", "silhouette", or gives a fractional size, `depth` MUST be `background`. If `shot` is `ultra-wide` and the character is named as a single distant figure, `depth` MUST be `background`.
- `perspective` (optional, default `front`): `front`, `side`, `back view`, `over-the-shoulder`.
- `pose` (optional, **default `threeQuarter`**): one of `front`, `threeQuarter`, `profile`, `back`. Picks which cell of the character's pre-rendered 2×4 reference sheet the composite path uses for this scene. `front` = facing the camera straight on. `threeQuarter` = body rotated ~45° away from the camera, both eyes still visible (the natural, lifelike default — most real scenes look like this). `profile` = full side view, one eye visible. `back` = back of the figure, no face. **Default to `threeQuarter` for almost every character.** Only switch to one of the others when the scene EXPLICITLY requires that exact angle: `profile` for a strict side-by-side confrontation or someone reading from a book held in profile; `back` for a character walking away from the camera or watching a distant target; `front` only when the character directly addresses the reader (cover-style head-on portrait, group "introduction" page). If you find yourself reaching for `front` because the character is just "facing toward the action", that's a `threeQuarter`. Consumed only when `enableSceneComposite` is on; harmless otherwise.
- `flip` (optional, default `false`): `true` if this character should face the camera's RIGHT, `false` if facing the camera's LEFT. The 2×4 cells face left by default; `flip: true` mirrors them. Two characters facing each other should have opposite `flip` values. Consumed only when `enableSceneComposite` is on; harmless otherwise.

**objects[]** — Visual Bible IDs. The FIRST `LOC###` ID is the page's primary location. Form depends on the location type:
- Real landmark (`isRealLandmark: true`): use the dotted vantage form `LOC###.N` (e.g. `LOC002.1`). The vantage you pick must be the one whose `pages: []` array includes this page number.
- Invented location (`isRealLandmark: false`): use the bare form `LOC###` (e.g. `LOC001`). Invented locations have no vantages — the pipeline renders one backdrop per LOC and reuses it.

**interactions[]** — visible character-to-object and character-to-character contacts. The image generator reads all entries; the evaluator only enforces entries marked `storyRelevant: true`.

Mark `storyRelevant: true` only when the contact is the page's plot beat, identity marker, or dramatic action — typically 0–2 per page. Costume texture, pose detail, and secondary contact are `storyRelevant: false`. In doubt → `false`.

Omit entirely (do not include with `false`): pure ground/floor/path contact, leaning on walls, sitting on generic chairs.

Field shape: `{character, object, where, storyRelevant}`. `where` is a short sentence naming the object as the contact action ("holds the parchment map", "aims the crossbow down the path", "hands the letter to the messenger"). No body-part positioning, no pose detail.

Cap: 1–4 entries per page, 0–2 with `storyRelevant: true`.

Example:
```
[

  { character:"the secondary character", object:"the main character", where:"reads over the main character's shoulder", storyRelevant:false }
]
```

Every person named in `interactions[]` must appear in this scene's `characters[]`. If the text describes movement FROM one thing TO another and both endpoints are plot-relevant, both get an entry; otherwise just the destination.

**Interactions at a distance.** When the verb implies action across a gap rather than touching contact ('shoots', 'aims at', 'throws at', 'calls to', 'watches from afar', 'spots across', 'runs toward across', 'gestures across', 'looks at' over distance), `where` must include an explicit separation phrase: 'across the room', 'a few paces apart', 'across the square', 'across the field', 'down the valley', 'from one rooftop to another'. Without it the image renders the two figures inside touching distance and the dramatic moment collapses. For these scenes `shot` MUST be `ultra-wide` so both endpoints fit visibly in the frame at the named separation, and the prose must place each figure at the correct end of the frame.

**Shared objects (two or more characters jointly using ONE thing).** When the same physical object is held / carried / lifted / pushed / pulled by multiple characters at once — the same picnic basket carried by two, the same banner held aloft by three, the same rope pulled by a team — emit ONE `interactions[]` entry, not one per character. The `character` field joins the names with ` + `, and the `where` sentence describes each grip in one sentence. ✓ `{ "character": "CharacterF + CharacterD", "object": "ART001", "where": "CharacterF grips the front handle, CharacterD grips the back, walking together with the wicker picnic basket between them" }`. ✗ Two separate entries with the same `object` (the model renders one separate object per entry — duplicates appear). Same rule for any shared scene element where the joint nature matters (sharing a bench they sit on, holding hands, pulling the same cart). For independent identical objects (each character has their OWN basket / sword / lantern), keep separate entries — the duplication is intentional there.

**Coverage and priority.** Every foreground or midground character in `characters[]` should appear in `interactions[]` exactly once. If a character has no plot-relevant action, give them a low-priority interaction (e.g. `{ "character": "Other", "object": "the campfire", "where": "watches the campfire", "priority": "low" }`) — the goal is to break the model's default camera-facing portrait, not to choreograph every gaze. Use `priority: "essential"` for the page's central action (held prop, traced line, key gesture), `"normal"` for supporting action, `"low"` for incidental gaze/pose. Background characters are exempt.

**emptyScenePrompt** (required): Background only — location, architecture, landscape, ground, lighting, weather, sky. No figures, characters, animals, creatures (named or generic). Reserved space is described as the scene's natural ground continuing through (cobblestones, grass, floor, road, sand, water surface) — never as "open space for X", "blank area", "room for figure", "spot reserved for", "leave room". Those phrases make the model render literal blankness or unfinished building fragments. ✓ "warm sunlit cobblestones stretch unbroken across the right foreground" ✗ "open space at right background for a small distant figure". Fixed anchor artifacts (throne, pole-with-banner, monument, signpost) are allowed and, when the page text centers on them, must be the focal point with their key visual marker named. Atmospheric crowd silhouettes are allowed only for inherently populated settings (market, square, festival).

**Prop placement vs landmark.** When a separate prop shares a depth band with a real landmark, position the prop relative to the landmark (in front of, beside, between camera and landmark) — not by an unanchored frame slot. SCENE prose and `emptyScenePrompt` use the same relative phrasing.

The `emptyScenePrompt` MUST MIRROR the SCENE prose's composition geometry — do not reinvent it. The generated empty scene becomes the style anchor the main image composites onto, so if their geometries disagree, the main image will fail at compositing (aim directions, target placements, perspective lines won't line up). Carry across verbatim from SCENE:
- **Direction of any path, river, road, corridor, shoreline, horizon, or major perspective line.** If SCENE says *"path stretches from lower-left foreground to upper-right background"*, the `emptyScenePrompt` uses the same direction phrasing.
- **Vanishing point / opening location** as a concrete frame position (upper-right, center-right, lower-center, etc.), not a vague side.
- **Reserved spots for distant composited figures or objects.** If SCENE says *"a tiny mounted figure at far right background in a sliver of pale light"*, the `emptyScenePrompt` says *"leave an open spot at the upper-right background, ~70% frame width, for a small distant figure — the path's vanishing point should land there"*. Name the frame position, don't just say "at the end of the path".
- **Lighting direction** consistent with `time` + `weather` (e.g., *"late afternoon sunlight rakes in from the upper right, long shadows cast toward the lower left"*).
- **Dominant diagonals / perspective lines** from SCENE.
A reader comparing SCENE and `emptyScenePrompt` side by side must see the same geometry described twice — same path direction, same vanishing point, same reserved corners — just with named characters absent from the empty version.

**Anonymous extras follow the setting.** When the setting is inherently populated — market, street, plaza, square, festival, school, gathering, village, town — include a background crowd (villagers, passersby, onlookers, soldiers, townsfolk) in both the scene prose and `emptyScenePrompt`, unless `shot` is `close-up`. Paint them as atmospheric density and silhouettes — no detailed faces, no individual names. Name a colour mix (at least 3 tunic/coat/dress colours) and at least two garment shapes across the crowd so it reads as a varied populace, not a uniform robed mass. For `close-up` shots, intimate/indoor/domestic settings (bedroom, kitchen, forest clearing, solitary room), or inherently empty outdoor locations (rural path, mountain top): the background stays empty of figures.

**Text zone rule.** Story text is rendered in WHITE and overlaid on the image at `textPosition`. Pick `textPosition` so the matching corner is the visually calmest region, AND so it can read as a saturated, high-contrast surface for white text to sit on — deep evening sky, dark foliage, rich wall, saturated water, deep cobblestones, dark earth. Not pale sky, not empty white, not a solid black rectangle. Calmness matters more than colour: saturated green, deep blue, rich brown all work. `textZoneDescription` names that surface in 5–15 words. The chosen surface must NATURALLY OCCUPY that corner in the scene's geometry — for `top-*` use sky, cliff face, upper foliage, mountainside, ceiling, cave roof; for `bottom-*` use cobblestones, floor, water, grass, sand, shadow, dark earth, low foliage. Vertical architectural surfaces (walls, facades, doors) only work when the camera angle actually places that surface across the chosen corner — narrow alleys, against-the-wall framing, interior shots. If the surface wouldn't naturally fill that corner, the model paints an awkward floating panel. Both `emptyScenePrompt` AND the SCENE prose must describe that corner as a natural part of the scene (e.g. "the deep evening sky fills the upper-right, unbroken and smooth"). Never write "dark rectangle", "negative space", "text box", "blank area", or "for text" — describe what's there, not what it's for.
<!-- TEXT_OVERLAY_END -->

**Rules:**
- **CHARACTER COUNT** - Aim for 1-3 characters per scene. Use 4-5 only for gathering moments (reunion, group discovery, classroom). Never exceed {MAX_CHARACTERS_PER_SCENE}. Never pad.
  - The description must ONLY mention characters from the characters array.
  - BAD: description mentions 7 characters but only 2 are in the array
  - GOOD: 5-person reunion → list all 5 in array, description references each by name
  - GOOD: solo moment → 1 character in array
- **COSTUME USAGE** - When a character has a `costumed` variant, use it in **ALL scenes** except the very first scene (before the adventure starts). The costume IS the story — show it!
- **WEATHER CONSISTENCY** - Keep weather the same across outdoor scenes unless story explicitly changes it
- **TIME PROGRESSION** - Time must progress logically (no sunset→midday jumps)
- **ACTION MATCHES TEXT** - The `description` must depict the same action the page text narrates.
- **DECISIVE MOMENT, NOT AFTERMATH** - When the page text describes a character actively doing something (jumping, shooting, running, confronting, hiding, reaching, escaping, striking), the scene hint MUST illustrate that action, not the moment after it. A character standing still on a rock after a jump, looking at scenery, is the wrong moment — show the jump itself. Aftermath framing (character still, consequence visible) is valid only when the page text itself is already an aftermath (a character reacting to something that just finished). Don't trade story drama for visual simplicity.
- **NO BACK VIEW FOR THE PROTAGONIST'S OWN ACTION** - When the acting character is the main subject of a decisive beat, they must be seen from the front or side — not back-view. Back view fits observation moments (a character watching something happen), not the character's own climactic action. The reader needs to see posture, weapon held, face or profile — body language carries the moment.
- **ANTAGONISTS MUST APPEAR WHEN STORY-PRESENT** - When the page text describes antagonists actively pressuring, chasing, restraining, or surrounding the main character, they must be visible in the illustration. Put them in the scene hint's `background` field (count + role + placement) if they're not main characters. Blurred, distant, or small in the frame is fine — absent is not. A scene about escape that shows no one to escape from reads as a scenic painting, not a story beat.
- **INDOOR SCENES** - Use "n/a" for weather
- **OBJECTS** - List Visual Bible IDs of any locations, animals, artifacts, or vehicles visible in this scene. Omit if none.
- **NO POINTING** - BANNED: "points at", "points to". Use: gazes at, walks toward, reaches for, discovers.
- **NO TRANSLUCENT CHARACTERS** - BANNED: "translucent", "transparent", "ghostly", "see-through", "ethereal", "spectral". Characters are always solid and opaque.
- **NO AGE COLLECTIVES** - Never write "the children", "the kids", "the boys", "the girls", "both children" in `description`. Use names or group size ("all three", "the group").
- **VISUAL ONLY** - Describe what the CAMERA sees. ❌ "excitedly runs" ✅ "runs with arms spread wide". No emotions, only body language.
- **ONE MOMENT** - Description = one frozen instant. ❌ "opens door, walks in, sits" ✅ "steps through the doorway"
- **SHOT + EXPRESSIONS** - `close-up`/`medium` = expressions visible. `wide`/`ultra-wide` = no facial detail, body language only.
- **BACKGROUND** - Add `"background"` to the JSON. Use it for the scene's physical atmosphere AND for any unnamed supporting figures who are essential to the story moment but aren't main characters.
  - Ambient populations: `"empty"`, `"a few passersby"`, `"busy crowd"`, `"dense forest"`
  - Narratively important supporting figures: whenever the page text shows unnamed figures actively doing something to or near a main character (capturing, chasing, guarding, blocking the way, rescuing, watching, reacting), describe them here with count + role + action + placement so the illustrator draws them. Without this, scene expansion will drop them and the image won't match the story.
- **FRAMING PATTERN** - Set `framingPattern` when the scene involves a weapon in use, combat, capture, restraint, or injury. Aftermath scenes with no people may instead describe a still-life object (split apple, broken arrow, empty chair).
<!-- TEXT_OVERLAY_BEGIN -->
- **TEXT POSITION** - Set `textPosition` for every page. Valid values: `top-left`, `top-right`, `bottom-left`, `bottom-right`, `top-full`, `bottom-full`. Pick the corner of the scene that is visually calmest (smooth sky, unbroken wall, open ground, steady water, foliage) — saturation is fine, clutter is not.
<!-- TEXT_OVERLAY_END -->

  Distribution requirements across the full story:
  - **Parity (side rule):** corner positions (`top-left`/`top-right`/`bottom-left`/`bottom-right`) must match page parity — odd pages (1, 3, 5, …) on the LEFT side, even pages (2, 4, 6, …) on the RIGHT side. `top-full` and `bottom-full` span both sides, so they are valid on any page regardless of parity.
  - **Full-width usage (non-negotiable):** between **30% and 50%** of all pages MUST use `top-full` or `bottom-full`. These span the whole width of the frame and work especially well for wide or ultra-wide shots, aerial shots, landscape-heavy scenes, group shots, or any scene with a naturally calm edge-to-edge band (a clean sky band above, a smooth ground band below). Do not default to corners for every page — full-width is the preferred choice whenever the scene has a sweeping top or bottom band.
  - **Top/bottom balance:** at least 30% of pages use a `top-*` value, at least 30% use a `bottom-*` value.
  - **Streak limit:** never more than 3 consecutive pages in the same half (top or bottom).

  Pick what naturally suits each scene — close-up with a clean sky above → `top-*`; outdoor with open ground below → `bottom-*`; wide/ultra-wide with a clean horizon band → `top-full` or `bottom-full`.

**Scene Variety (IMPORTANT for great illustrations):**
- **SIMPLE ACTIONS** - Characters in natural, stable poses: sitting, standing, walking, looking. If it would be hard to capture in a single photo, simplify it.
- **SIMPLE SCENES PREFERRED** — prefer 1–2 central characters per scene. For group moments: keep 1–2 in foreground (the action), put the rest in **midground** (still readable faces, just further from the camera). Use `"depth": "background"` ONLY when a character is genuinely tiny/distant per the §depth definition (~1/6 frame height or less — a sentry on a far ridge, a figure at the end of a long path). A character standing a few metres behind the focal action — at a fence, at the back of a room, watching from the doorway — is **midground**, not background. Misuse triggers an unwanted scale-repair pass that shrinks the character far smaller than the scene intends.
- **EVERY CHARACTER VISIBLE** - Each primary character must appear in at least 2 scene hints across the story.
- **VARY EXPRESSIONS** - Not every scene is happy. Match expression to the moment: wonder, concentration, worry, surprise, determination. "Smiling" on at most 2-3 pages in the entire story.
- **NO TWO CONSECUTIVE SCENES ALIKE** - Each scene must differ from the previous one in at least 2 of: shot type, character count, perspective. If page 3 is a medium shot of 2 characters facing each other, page 4 CANNOT be another medium shot of 2 characters facing each other.

**Scene Types That WORK WELL (use these):**
- **SOLO DISCOVERY** — One character alone, exploring or reacting (close-up or medium). Best quality images.
- **CHASE / PURSUIT** — Two characters far apart, both small, moving in the same direction. One left, one right.
- **CROWD SCENE** — Main character in foreground, nameless background crowd (market, festival, school). Use `"setting"` description for the crowd, don't list them as characters.
- **OVER-THE-SHOULDER** — Camera behind one character, looking at another. Use `"perspective": "over-the-shoulder"`.
- **BACK VIEW** — Character walking away from camera into a landscape. Use `"perspective": "back view"`.
- **BIRD'S EYE** — Looking down at tiny characters in a large space. Use `"shot": "ultra-wide"`.
- **REACTION CLOSE-UP** — Just the face filling the frame, strong emotion. 1 character only.
- **DISTANCE SHOT** — Two characters far apart with empty space between them. **Strongly prefer one `foreground` + one `background`** (large hero close to camera, tiny target figure across the square / field / hall). The fg character anchors the composition and the post-render scale-repair pass can keep the foreground intact while shrinking the bg figure — both-background renders force a full regeneration that Grok handles poorly. Use both-background only when the dramatic intent specifically requires a wide-open empty middle with neither character as a hero anchor (e.g. an aerial / God's-eye view of two distant antagonists). Default for aiming, shooting, throwing, calling, watching across distance: pick the active character (the one taking the action) as `foreground`, the target as `background`.
- **For any beat where two characters interact across open distance (aim, shoot, throw, call, watch from afar), use DISTANCE SHOT — never two `foreground` characters at the same depth band.**

**Scene Types That DON'T WORK (avoid):**
- 4+ named characters all facing the camera (group photo effect — AI makes them identical)
- 3+ characters at similar size all in foreground (faces blur together)
- Two characters having a conversation while ALSO holding objects AND gesturing (too complex)
- Same composition repeated: if two scenes both show "2 people talking face to face", one must change

**SCENE PLAN (required — write this in the ---SCENE PLAN--- section BEFORE writing pages):**
Before writing any pages, plan the visual composition for each page. For a 10-page story, distribute roughly:
- 3× solo character scenes (close-up or medium)
- 2× two characters interacting (medium, varied perspectives)
- 2× environment-focused (wide/ultra-wide, characters small in landscape)
- 1× crowd scene (main character + nameless crowd in setting description)
- 1× over-the-shoulder or back view
- 1× unique angle (bird's eye, through a window, looking up)
Scale proportionally for longer/shorter stories. The plan ensures variety BEFORE writing — no need to fix afterwards.

# Character Portrayal Guidelines

- Write in third person (he/she/they)
- Let readers discover personality through what characters DO, not what they ARE told
- Your characters must feel like real people, not cardboard cutouts.
- Do not repeat the visual traits of the character in the text. CharacterB, the red-haired boy is fine for the scene hint, do not repeat this in the story text.

**SHOW, don't TELL:**
- NEVER mention trait words directly (don't write "She was brave" or "He was shy") SHOW traits through actions, dialogue, and behavior
- Instead of "CharacterB was curious" → Show CharacterB asking unexpected questions, peeking behind doors, wondering aloud
- Instead of "CharacterC was shy" → Show CharacterC hiding behind her mom, speaking quietly, avoiding eye contact
- Instead of "Leo was brave" → Show Leo stepping forward when others hesitate, volunteering for the scary task

**Each character should:**
- Have a unique voice in dialogue (different speech patterns, word choices)
- React differently to situations based on their personality
- Show their traits through ACTIONS, not descriptions
- Grow and change through the story

**AVOID:**
- Repeating character traits - if a character is "curious", don't keep writing "she was curious" or "her curiosity led her to..."
- Mentioning the same trait more than once in the entire story
- Generic dialogue that any character could say
- Characters who all react the same way

## DO-NOT-WRITE LIST (canonical)

These appear nowhere in the story text or scene prose. Forbidden at write time, not after — the analysis pass does NOT need to re-check them.

**Gestures (banned outright):** hand on shoulder, ruffled hair, tousled hair, patted on back, nodded knowingly, nodded wisely, arm around shoulders.

**Phrases (banned outright):** "little did they know", "suddenly" / "plötzlich", "without warning", "it was a [adjective] day", "it was a dark and stormy night".

**Page 1 openings (banned):** weather descriptions ("It was a beautiful sunny day…"), characters waking up, narrated backstory. Start mid-action — something happens, someone speaks, a problem appears.

**Trait labels in story text (banned):** "she was brave", "he was shy", "she felt curious". Show the trait through action, dialogue, body language.

**Aesthetic buzzwords (banned everywhere):** dramatic, ethereal, haunting, cinematic, breathtaking, stunning. They carry no visual or narrative information.

**Humor (banned):** no jokes, puns, or wordplay — they don't translate and AI humor isn't funny. Use funny situations, silly reactions, absurd moments, physical comedy instead.

**Plot formulas (banned):** the "try-fail-try-succeed" loop; gathering-the-team montages; training sequences; "believe in yourself" speeches as the resolution.

**Gesture re-use cap:** every gesture (hug, smile, wink, shrug, nod, fist-bump, high-five, thumbs up, pinky promise, silly salute, leaning shoulder-to-shoulder) appears at most ONCE in the entire story. The second time, vary it.

**Scene prose verbs (banned in SCENE blocks only — see line above on plain concrete verbs):** hovers, cradled, perched upon, rests gently against. Use plain concrete verbs: holds, sits on, reaches for, walks toward, gazes at.

**Use instead — for support, connection, warmth:** standing closer, walking side by side, sharing food, helping with a task; warm smile, wink, raised eyebrows, eyes lighting up; fist bump, high five, thumbs up, silly salute, pinky promise; leaning in, sitting next to, making eye contact, a quick nudge.

**PACING:**
- Vary the rhythm: slow down for emotional moments, speed up for action
- Balance dialogue, description, and action - don't overload any single page
- Give important moments room to breathe - don't rush past turning points
- Use short sentences for tension, longer ones for calm scenes
